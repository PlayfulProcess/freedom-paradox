# Chapter 4: Open as in Business

In January 1998, Netscape did something no major software company had ever done. It announced that it would release the source code for Navigator, its web browser — the product that had defined the early internet and that was now being crushed by Microsoft's Internet Explorer.

Netscape was losing the browser wars. Microsoft had bundled Internet Explorer with Windows, giving it an insurmountable distribution advantage. Netscape's market share was collapsing. Opening the source code was a Hail Mary — and it sent shockwaves through the technology world.

A group gathered in Palo Alto, California to discuss how to capitalize on the moment. The attendees were some of the most influential figures in the free software community: Eric Raymond, who had written "The Cathedral and the Bazaar" and whose ideas had directly influenced Netscape's decision. Bruce Perens, who had authored what would become the Open Source Definition. Michael Tiemann, who had built one of the first companies around free software. Jon "maddog" Hall, a longtime free software advocate. Larry Augustin and Sam Ockman.

The meeting had a practical question: How do we make this moment count? How do we convince other companies to follow Netscape's lead?

The answer came from Christine Peterson, a researcher at the Foresight Institute. The problem, she argued, was the name. "Free software" was confusing. In English, "free" means both "without cost" and "without restriction." Stallman's painstaking distinction — "free as in speech, not free as in beer" — required a paragraph of explanation every time. Worse, "free" scared businesspeople. It sounded anti-commercial, anti-profit, ideological.

Peterson suggested a new term: "open source."

The group debated it. The term stuck. And in that renaming, something fundamental shifted.

---

"Open source" was a marketing decision. This is not a criticism — or not only a criticism. It was a *strategic* rebranding executed with remarkable skill, and it worked.

The word "free" carried baggage that the word "open" did not. "Free" implied ideology, radicalism, the FSF, Stallman's uncompromising moral stance. "Open" implied transparency, collaboration, pragmatism, good engineering. "Free" was a philosophy. "Open" was a methodology.

In February 1998, Eric Raymond and Bruce Perens founded the Open Source Initiative to promote the new term and steward its definition. Linus Torvalds endorsed it the following day — a critical stamp of legitimacy from the most famous developer in the world.

In April 1998, Tim O'Reilly organized the "Open Source Summit" — a gathering of the leaders of major projects. Torvalds was there. Larry Wall, creator of Perl. Brian Behlendorf, co-founder of the Apache web server. Guido van Rossum, creator of Python. The summit was a coming-out party for the rebranded movement, and it placed the emphasis squarely on *practical excellence* — these projects produced software that corporations actually depended on.

The pitch to business was straightforward: open source produces better software, faster, at lower cost. You get transparency (you can inspect the code), reliability (thousands of eyes finding bugs), and no vendor lock-in (you're never beholden to a single company's roadmap). These are *business* arguments. The Four Freedoms didn't come up much.

Richard Stallman watched this unfold and refused to participate. He has consistently maintained, for nearly three decades now, that "open source" deliberately strips out the ethical dimension that gives the movement its meaning. The open source camp, he argues, asks: "How do we make better software?" The free software camp asks: "How do we respect users' freedom?" These are different questions with different answers.

---

Was Stallman right? The honest answer is: both yes and no, and the ratio changes depending on when you ask the question.

In the short term — roughly 1998 to 2020 — the open source rebranding was an unqualified triumph. It opened the floodgates of corporate participation. Companies that would never have touched "free software" — with its whiff of anti-capitalism — embraced "open source" enthusiastically. IBM invested a billion dollars in Linux. Google built its entire infrastructure on open-source tools. Microsoft, once the movement's arch-nemesis, became one of the largest contributors to open-source projects and eventually acquired GitHub for $7.5 billion.

The business case worked because it was true. Open-source software genuinely was better for many purposes — more secure, more reliable, more adaptable. The "bazaar" model of development, stripped of its countercultural trappings, turned out to be a superior engineering methodology for infrastructure software. The enterprise world didn't need to believe in the Four Freedoms to see the value in Linux, Apache, and PostgreSQL.

The numbers tell the story. Today, virtually every Fortune 500 company relies on open-source infrastructure. GitHub hosts over 400 million repositories. Linux runs the internet. The rebranding unlocked all of this.

But something was lost. By framing open source as a *methodology* rather than an *ethic*, the movement surrendered the vocabulary it would later need to confront the hardest questions. When the discussion is about efficiency and quality — "open source produces better software" — there is no principled basis for saying "some things should not be opened." Methodologies don't have moral limits. Ethics do.

This distinction barely mattered when the technology in question was web servers, compilers, and databases. It matters enormously when the technology is artificial intelligence capable of mass surveillance and autonomous warfare. The 1998 decision to prioritize pragmatism over principle was perfectly rational in its context. But the context has changed.

---

The rebranding also changed who the movement attracted — and who led it.

Stallman's free software movement was, for all its flaws, rooted in an ethical vision accessible to anyone. You didn't need to be a programmer to understand that users should have freedom. The open source movement, by contrast, was built for and by an engineering elite. Its arguments were technical. Its language was corporate. Its heroes were CTOs and VCs, not philosophers and activists.

This isn't inherently bad. Technical excellence matters. Corporate adoption brought resources, stability, and reach that the free software movement alone could never have achieved. But it created a cultural shift: the movement's center of gravity moved from "what is right" to "what works" — and from there, inevitably, to "what pays."

Eric Raymond and Bruce Perens, the co-founders of the OSI, represented this shift. Raymond's "Cathedral and the Bazaar" was fundamentally a *management* argument: here's why decentralized development produces better outcomes. Perens wrote the Open Source Definition — a technical standard for what qualifies as an open-source license. Both contributions were valuable. Neither was about ethics.

---

The result of the rebranding was a paradox that would take two decades to fully manifest.

Open source won. It became the default infrastructure of the digital world. It enabled a generation of startups to build billion-dollar companies on free foundations. It proved that collaboration at scale could produce extraordinary results.

But open source also became a tool — a strategy to be deployed when it served corporate interests and set aside when it didn't. The same companies that championed open source for their infrastructure projects kept their most valuable software locked and proprietary. Google open-sourced Android and Chromium; it did not open-source its search algorithm or ad-targeting systems. Facebook open-sourced React; it did not open-source its news feed algorithm.

This is the world the 1998 rebranding made possible. A world where "open source" is a business decision, not a moral commitment. A world where the question isn't "Is this right?" but "Does this serve our interests?"

For two decades, that was fine. Infrastructure software benefits from being open. The more people use Linux, the better Linux gets. The incentives were aligned.

Then came AI. And for the first time, the thing being potentially "opened" wasn't infrastructure that makes everyone's life easier. It was a technology that could, in the wrong hands, make everyone's life dramatically worse. Suddenly, the question Stallman had been asking since 1983 — the *ethical* question, the question about freedom and responsibility and what humans owe each other — was the only question that mattered.

And the movement that had spent twenty-five years cultivating a vocabulary of efficiency, quality, and business value found itself without the words it needed.
