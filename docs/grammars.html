Part I: The Crisis
Chapter 01

When Stories Lose Their Homes

In the first edition of the Brothers Grimm's Kinder- und Hausmärchen, published on December 20, 1812, a woodcutter's wife proposes abandoning her children in the forest. Not a stepmother. The biological mother. She speaks plainly: "Early tomorrow morning we will take the two children out into the thickest part of the woods, make a fire for them, and give each of them a little piece of bread, then leave them by themselves and go off to our work. They won't find their way back home, and we'll be rid of them."

The father protests weakly. He submits. The children are led into the forest.

This is Hansel and Gretel as it was first told — or, more precisely, as it was first written down, by Dortchen Wild, the Grimms' neighbor's daughter, who would later marry Wilhelm. In the 1810 manuscript, the children didn't even have names. They were simply "Little Brother" and "Little Sister." The story had no gingerbread house yet, no duck to carry them across water, no religious consolation. It had a mother who decided that her children's deaths were preferable to the family's starvation.

The tale was not a fantasy. The Great Famine of 1315–1321 had devastated Europe five centuries earlier, and the folklore it produced endured because the conditions it described kept recurring. Maria Tatar, in The Hard Facts of the Grimms' Fairy Tales, confirms that "child abandonment was a cruel social reality among the impoverished, even as late as 1806, when the Grimm brothers began collecting their tales." John Boswell's The Kindness of Strangers documents that abandoning children was not a fairy-tale trope but a routine survival strategy across centuries of European history. The woodcutter's wife was not a monster. She was making a calculation that countless real mothers had made.

The gingerbread house — when it appeared — was the cruelest detail of all. In a world of famine, a house made of food was a weapon. It weaponized the child's deepest desire against them. The very thing they craved was the trap. The witch understood what hungry children would do when they encountered plenty: they would eat, and in eating, they would be consumed.

Now consider telling this story to a child in 2026 who has never known hunger.

· · ·

The gingerbread house is no longer a fantasy. It is a description of the average child's Tuesday. The American child lives surrounded by abundance, targeted by algorithmic lures designed to exploit desire, inhabiting a world where plenty itself is the pathology. The tale is more relevant than ever — but its meaning has inverted. The child hearing it cannot access the original terror because the material conditions that gave the tale its power have disappeared from their experience. The starvation trap reads as a set piece. The witch reads as a Halloween costume. The abandonment reads as something that happens in other centuries, to other people.

This is what I want to call mythic displacement: not that the story is false, but that the context which made it meaningful has dissolved. The story still exists. It is told in picture books, animated films, gingerbread-house decorating kits at Christmas. But it is homeless — severed from the lived conditions that produced it, circulating in a world that can no longer hear what it was trying to say.

And we did this to it deliberately.

· · ·

D.L. Ashliman at the University of Pittsburgh has tracked the precise changes the Brothers Grimm made across seven editions, from 1812 to 1857. The mutations form a genealogy of softening.

In the first edition, the mother is the biological mother. She and the father share blame equally. In the second edition, published in 1819, the father becomes "more introspective and milder" — the line "It would be better to share the last bit with the children" was added. The tale grows, in Ashliman's words, "more dramatic, more literary, and more sentimental." By the fourth edition, in 1840, the Grimms introduce the word "stepmother" for the first time, though they retain "mother" in some passages. In the fifth edition, they add the famine as explicit context — "a way to rationalize the parents' decision," as Jack Zipes notes — as though the tale needed explaining rather than witnessing. And in the seventh and final edition of 1857, the wife is referred to once as "the stepmother," twice as "the mother," and about a dozen times as "the woman." Even after forty-five years of revision, the Grimms could not fully erase the biological mother. She persists in the text like a stain that will not wash out.

The pattern is consistent across every revision: the tale was progressively softened, sentimentalized, moralized, and de-historicized. The biological mother became a stepmother. The reluctant father became a victim. Religious consolation was inserted where none had existed. The material reality — starvation, the brutal arithmetic of too many mouths and not enough bread — was rationalized rather than confronted.

Zipes's explanation is the most direct: the Grimms "held motherhood sacred." Marina Warner's is the most devastating: Wilhelm's edits moved "from blaming both parents for agreeing to abandon the children in the first edition to explaining away the father's complicity in the final edition where the woman, no longer the natural mother but a stepmother, forces the father to comply." The father was innocent. The mother was replaced. The story became bearable — and in becoming bearable, it lost the capacity to bear witness.

The scholarly fault line over fairy tales runs deeper than editorial choices. It is a dispute about what stories are.

Bruno Bettelheim, in The Uses of Enchantment (1976), reads the gingerbread house as oral regression — the child's fantasy of a world made entirely of food, representing the desire to return to the breast. The witch is the "bad mother" projected outward. The tale's function is psychological and universal: it helps children work through separation anxiety, oedipal conflict, and the fear of abandonment. For Bettelheim, the tale's meaning transcends its historical context. It is a dream that all children dream.

Robert Darnton, in The Great Cat Massacre (1984), insists that fairy tales are primary sources. He disputes psychological interpretations "on the grounds that they fail to pay close enough attention to the narrative content and known historical context of the stories." The world of the fairy tale is the world of the peasant storyteller: "the omnipresence of hunger, the hazards of life on the road, the burden of children in poor households." The gingerbread house is not a symbol. It is a trap built from the specific fantasies of people who were starving.

Warner's critique is the most useful for the argument this book is making. She argues that psychological interpretations and Disney adaptations "leech the history out of fairy tales" and give them "a falsely archetypal and universal character." Warner reconnects the tales to their historical conditions — showing that the "absent mother" of fairy tales arises from demographic reality: women died in childbirth, widowers remarried, and the conflicts between stepmothers and stepchildren were material, not merely psychological.

The dispute matters because it is not really about fairy tales. It is about whether meaning can survive decontextualization. Bettelheim says yes — the psychological core persists regardless of material conditions. The historicists say no — strip the material conditions and you strip the meaning. This book sides with the historicists, but with a complication: the psychological dimension is real. Children do work through fear of abandonment by hearing Hansel and Gretel. But they work through it differently when abandonment is a lived possibility than when it is an abstraction. Context is not merely decorative. Context is constitutive of meaning.

In 1920, a son was born to a man who had recently returned from the Battle of the Somme.

A.A. Milne had been commissioned as an officer in the 4th Battalion, Royal Warwickshire Regiment, and fought in one of the bloodiest engagements in human history — over three million combatants, one million casualties. He came home with what was then called shell-shock. In his autobiography, It's Too Late Now, he wrote that it made him "almost physically sick" to think of "that nightmare of mental and moral degradation, the war." At home, he was triggered by buzzing bees, popping balloons, champagne corks. He moved his family from London to the countryside to escape the noise.

His son, Christopher Robin, was born into this silence. And the stories Milne wrote for him — Winnie-the-Pooh, published in 1926 — are widely understood as a father's attempt to process trauma through the creation of a counter-world. The Hundred Acre Wood is a forest where the worst thing that can happen is running out of honey. Each character carries a dimension of what the father was living through: Piglet's anxiety, Eeyore's depression, Tigger's impulsive energy, Rabbit's need for control, Owl's confabulation, and Pooh himself — gentle, slightly bewildered, dissociated from danger. The pastoral world was not an escape from reality. It was a therapeutic landscape built by a man who could not yet speak directly about what had happened to him.

The stories worked — as therapy, as literature, as a father's gift to his son. And then they became famous.

Christopher Robin resented it for the rest of his life. In his autobiography, The Enchanted Places (1974), he described the poem "Vespers" — published in Vanity Fair when he was a small child — as having brought him "more toe-curling, fist-clenching, lip-biting embarrassment than any other." As the books' popularity exploded, he was bullied at school, paraded before media, treated as a marketing artifact rather than a child. After his father's death in 1956, Christopher saw his mother only once in the remaining fifteen years of her life. The intimate play between father and son — the material that produced one of the world's most beloved children's stories — was extracted from its relational context and turned into a global commodity. The story meant to heal a father's trauma became the source of a son's.

· · ·

The original Pooh, Piglet, Eeyore, Kanga, and Tigger — Christopher Robin's actual stuffed animals — now sit in a glass case at the New York Public Library's main branch on Fifth Avenue. They were donated by the publisher E.P. Dutton in 1987. Millions of tourists visit them. Most know the Disney version. Almost none know about the Somme.

Walter Benjamin, writing in 1935, gave us the vocabulary for what happened. In "The Work of Art in the Age of Mechanical Reproduction," he distinguished between an artwork's cult value — its meaning within the ritual context that produced it — and its exhibition value — its meaning as an object of display. Mechanical reproduction, Benjamin argued, destroys the artwork's aura: "that which withers in the age of mechanical reproduction is the aura of the work of art." The aura is the object's embeddedness in a specific time, place, and tradition — its here and now. Reproduction strips this away.

Christopher Robin's stuffed animals had cult value. They were mediators between a traumatized father and his bewildered son — objects of play through which a man who could not speak about war found a way to be present for his child. Behind glass at the New York Public Library, they have exhibition value. The tourists photograph them. The gift shop sells replicas. The cult value — the meaning that required those specific hands, that specific relationship, that specific wound — is gone.

The pattern extends wherever you look. Egyptian tomb objects, designed to serve the dead in the afterlife, sit in museum vitrines as decorative curiosities. Dan Hicks, in The Brutish Museums, argues that the Benin Bronzes — looted by British forces in 1897 — constitute "a systematic extension of warfare across time." Their continued display in Western museums is not neutral curation. It is ongoing violence. Amy Lonetree, in Decolonizing Museums, insists that indigenous objects behind glass are "living entities" — their decontextualization is not a completed historical act but an ongoing source of grief for the communities from which they were taken.

The display case is not a neutral frame. It is a technology of displacement.

Joseph Campbell, whose universalism this book will elsewhere resist, made one claim that is difficult to argue with: "Myth cannot be exported into another land or another time." In The Masks of God, he argues that mythology must be "transparent to transcendence" within the specific cultural context where it operates. When myths are displaced from their originating conditions, they become literature, entertainment, or ideology — but they cease to function as living mythology.

Mircea Eliade's The Sacred and the Profane names the larger process: modernity is characterized by progressive desacralization — the evacuation of the sacred from everyday experience. Sacred time, which was cyclical and mythic, gives way to profane time, which is linear and historical. Sacred space, which was specific and charged, gives way to profane space, which is homogeneous and interchangeable. The Grimms' tales originally participated in a world where forests were genuinely dangerous, witches were genuinely feared, and the boundary between the natural and supernatural was genuinely porous. The tale's sacred function — to orient the child within a cosmos of meaningful dangers — depended on the shared belief that such a cosmos existed. Desacralization does not falsify the tale. It evacuates the cosmological context that made it function.

Alan Dundes, the UC Berkeley folklorist, coined the term "autobiographical ethnography" to describe what folklore does: it is a culture's way of describing itself to itself. The stories a culture tells are constitutive of its identity — the culture knows itself through its stories. When the stories lose their connection to lived experience, the culture loses a mode of self-knowledge.

And Ngũgĩ wa Thiong'o, in Decolonising the Mind, extends the argument to its most radical form: colonial languages do not merely replace indigenous languages — they replace the entire mythic infrastructure that indigenous languages carried. To think in English is to inherit English myths, English categories, English ways of organizing experience. The indigenous myths carried by Gikuyu or Yoruba are not merely translated. They are displaced. The language that could hold them has been replaced by a language that cannot.

· · ·

I want to be precise about what mythic displacement is and what it is not.

It is not the same as cultural appropriation, though the two can overlap. Cultural appropriation involves taking from another culture. Mythic displacement includes a culture's broken relationship to its own inherited stories.

A modern German parent telling Hansel and Gretel to a well-fed child is not appropriating. The tale belongs to that tradition. But it may still be displaced — the material conditions that gave the tale its meaning no longer exist in the teller's or the listener's world. The American parent reading Paul Bunyan, the Japanese parent reading Momotarō, the Brazilian parent telling the Saci-Pererê — all are offering tales whose constitutive contexts have dissolved, to varying degrees, in the world the child actually inhabits.

This distinction matters because it prevents the argument from collapsing into a simpler critique. The crisis is not that we are stealing each other's stories. The crisis is that even within cultures that own their stories, the stories have become homeless. Warner's historicist approach shows this clearly: displacement happens within traditions across time, not just between traditions across space. The Grimms themselves displaced their tales — softening them, moralizing them, sentimentalizing them across seven editions. Disney displaced them further. The attention economy displaces them again — algorithmically distributing fragments of stories without any of the relational context that made them meaningful.

Zipes names the economic mechanism: fairy tales "violated" by consumer capitalism. The stories are not borrowed or stolen. They are commodified — turned from communal practice into individual product.

· · ·

What happens to the psychological architecture of a story when the conditions that produced it no longer exist?

Can a myth survive its own success — can universal distribution destroy what made a story locally powerful?

If context is constitutive of meaning, what does it mean that we now consume stories with no context at all — algorithmically surfaced, stripped of origin, optimized for engagement?

And the deepest question, the one that will occupy the rest of this book: are we mythically homeless — not because we lack stories, but because the stories we have no longer belong to the world we inhabit?

Chapter 02

The Degradation of the Hearth

For most of human history, families shared their stories around fire. The fire was not a metaphor. It was a technology — the original technology of gathering, the thing that created the first circle. A teller, embedded in the same material conditions as the listeners, told a story that the community already knew, at a time and in a place that the community recognized as appropriate for that story. This is what Joseph Campbell means by "living mythology." Stories had owners, seasons, occasions, authorized tellers. Gregory Cajete of the Tewa nation has documented that indigenous oral traditions "have always been integrated with drawing, arts, and practical education" — not extractable content but embedded practice.

The fire created the conditions for mythic belonging. Everyone heard the same story. Everyone heard it together. The teller could see the listeners' faces and adjust — slowing for fear, pausing for wonder, repeating for the child who asked again. The story was not consumed. It was participated in.

Then came the screen.

· · ·

In the mid-twentieth century, television replaced the hearth as the focus for family gatherings. Cecelia Tichi, in Electronic Hearth, describes the "teleconsciousness" that begins with "a continuous reprioritizing of attention in the habitat of the 'always on' TV." The family gathered, but the gathering had changed. The teller was gone — replaced by a broadcast signal that could not see the listeners' faces. The story was no longer local, seasonal, or community-specific. It was national, continuous, and commercially motivated.

What was preserved: the family still gathered. They still shared a narrative. They still had common symbolic vocabulary. In 1963, ninety-six percent of Americans watched coverage of Kennedy's assassination. Everyone could reference Walter Cronkite, or The Wizard of Oz, or the moon landing. Dayan and Katz, in Media Events, call these moments temporary ritual unity — the broadcast event as shared sacrament, experienced simultaneously by millions.

What was lost: Neil Postman saw it first. The screen is "so saturated with our memories of profane events, so deeply associated with the commercial and entertainment worlds that it is difficult for it to be recreated as a frame for sacred events." The medium was already compromised. But the gathering persisted. A family watching the same show, in the same room, at the same time, still shares something. The content may be degraded. The togetherness is real.

· · ·

On May 1, 1969, Fred Rogers — a Presbyterian minister who had been ordained with the explicit charge of conducting his ministry through mass media directed at children — testified before the U.S. Senate Commerce Subcommittee. President Nixon's administration had proposed cutting federal funding for public broadcasting from twenty million dollars to ten million. Senator John Pastore, chairing the hearing, was known as gruff and impatient. He had never heard of Rogers or his show.

In approximately six minutes of testimony, Rogers explained that six thousand dollars — his per-show budget — pays for less than two minutes of cartoons. "Two minutes of animated, what I sometimes say, bombardment." He described the show's emphasis on helping children manage feelings, particularly anger. He recited the lyrics to "What Do You Do with the Mad That You Feel?" — a song teaching children that having angry feelings is normal, and what matters is what you do with them.

Pastore's response: "I'm supposed to be a pretty tough guy, and this is the first time I've had goosebumps for the last two days. I think it's wonderful. Looks like you just earned the $20 million."

Congressional funding for PBS subsequently increased from nine million dollars to twenty-two million.

Rogers did not lobby by citing audience numbers. He described a philosophy of care and demonstrated it through his own presence. The medium was the message. And the message was that television — the degraded hearth — could still serve the child's inner life, if the people designing it chose to serve the child rather than exploit them.

· · ·

The research would later confirm what Rogers knew intuitively.

Dimitri Christakis, editor-in-chief of JAMA Pediatrics, and his colleagues found that early television exposure was associated with attentional problems at age seven — but that this association was specific to non-educational viewing and to viewing before age three. Educational programming, including Rogers-style content, showed no adverse cognitive outcomes. In a study coding eighty-five popular children's shows for pacing, Mighty Morphin' Power Rangers scored 41.90 — among the fastest — and Mister Rogers' Neighborhood scored 14.95 — the slowest of all shows measured.

The distinction was not how much children watched. It was what they watched. And what Rogers built was the opposite of everything that caused harm: slow-paced, realistic, directly addressed to the child, grounded in real activities and genuine emotions. He asked questions and paused for responses — creating contingent interaction decades before touchscreens. As Christakis observed: Rogers understood that asking questions would make children attend, and the children would answer, "and then invariably the answer was, 'That's right!'" The child felt heard. Through a screen. By a man they had never met.

The Daniel Tiger research extended the finding into the next generation. Texas Tech researchers enrolled 127 preschoolers in a controlled study. Children who watched Daniel Tiger's Neighborhood — the direct descendant of Rogers's show — exhibited higher empathy, better emotion recognition, and more confidence in social situations than the control group. But the empathy scores were only significant in the group where parents participated in active mediation — watching together and discussing the content.

The co-viewing was the hearth. Not the screen. The screen was the occasion. The gathering was the medicine.

· · ·

Then the gathering ended.

The personal screen dissolved even the degraded togetherness that television had preserved. "Gone are the days of the family gathered around a single television set." Each family member now consumes their own algorithmically curated stream. A study from Bengkulu found that "family members are frequently physically present but psychologically detached" — teenagers communicating with parents only through "practical reminders about schedules and schoolwork rather than deep conversations about values, emotions, or aspirations."

This is what the researchers call absent presence — bodies in the same room, attention in different worlds.

Christakis's developmental research reveals what is lost when attention fragments. Joint attention — shared awareness between caregiver and child — occurs hundreds of times daily in healthy development. The child points at a bird. The parent looks. The parent says the word. The child learns not just the word but the experience of being seen seeing. This is how language, empathy, and theory of mind develop. Engaging tablet apps reduce toddlers' responses to joint attention prompts. Background television decreases play quality and parent-child interaction — parents become slower to respond to bids for attention. The screen does not merely distract the child. It disrupts the relational infrastructure through which the child becomes human.

The co-viewing research contains one more finding worth sitting with. Co-viewing with siblings improves inhibitory control. Co-viewing with parents decreases cognitive flexibility. The mere presence of an adult does not help. The quality of the gathering matters, not just the fact of it. Simply sitting next to your child while both of you stare at separate screens is not co-viewing. It is parallel isolation.

· · ·

Anna Lembke, a Stanford psychiatrist and author of Dopamine Nation, provides the neurochemical explanation for why the personal screen is not merely a distraction but a clinical harm. Every dopamine hit — every notification, every like, every autoplay video — tips the brain's pleasure-pain balance toward pleasure. The brain compensates by tipping toward pain, recalibrating its baseline. With repeated high-dopamine stimulation — the kind that variable-reward interfaces are explicitly designed to produce — the brain requires more stimulation to feel normal. Withdrawal produces irritability, anxiety, insomnia, and craving. "The smartphone," Lembke writes, "is the modern-day hypodermic needle, delivering digital dopamine 24/7 for a wired generation."

The developmental implications are specific. More than twenty-five percent of American adults take psychiatric medication daily. Depression has increased in affluent countries over three decades while decreasing in South Asia. The Surgeon General's data: ninety-five percent of kids ages thirteen to seventeen use social media; those spending more than three hours per day face double the risk of depression and anxiety symptoms. The crisis is not metaphorical.

And the read-aloud — the most direct descendant of the firelit story — is disappearing. Only fifty-one percent of families with young children read aloud regularly. Storybooks contain fifty percent more rare words than prime-time television and college students' conversations. The neuroimaging evidence is direct: a 2025 fNIRS study showed that live book reading produces greater activation in the right hemisphere — the areas processing others' emotions, intentions, and shared attention — while screen-based storytelling produces more balanced bilateral activity that relies less on social engagement networks. The live human voice, reading a story to a child who is present, recruits brain architecture that screens do not.

The cross-cultural evidence confirms what the neuroscience suggests. Every major tradition examined — Brazilian contação de histórias, Japanese yomikikase (institutionalized in elementary schools as a nationwide practice with parent volunteers), West African evening storytelling under moonlight, the Igbo opening call "E nwerem akuko nga akoro unu" ("I have a story to tell you") — demonstrates that intentional curation of narrative intake is universal and structurally necessary. What varies is which stories, who has authority to tell them, when they are told, and whether the system is initiated or open. The crisis is not that stories are dying. Content has never been more abundant. The crisis is that the relational structures within which stories create meaning are dissolving.

A West African researcher notes: "The traditional moonlight storytelling culture of the agrarian society has all but disappeared. In its place, a more urban western-type society has taken root." The fire is gone. The moonlight is gone. What remains is the screen — and the screen, left to the market, will not serve the child. It will serve the shareholder.

· · ·

Tristan Harris calls it "human downgrading" — addiction, distraction, isolation, and polarization as mutually reinforcing harms. The platforms are designed not for meaning or development but for engagement. Autoplay, variable reward, infinite scroll — mechanisms structurally incompatible with contemplative practice. Shoshana Zuboff's Surveillance Capitalism names the economic logic: an "extractive attention economy that treats humans as resources to mine attention out of."

The children are the most vulnerable miners. Dark patterns — interfaces designed to manipulate — target them specifically. The infinite scroll has no natural stopping point because a stopping point would lose engagement. The notification is timed to recapture attention precisely when it wanders. The recommendation algorithm does not ask what the child needs. It asks what the child will watch next.

Rogers's six-thousand-dollar show made the opposite bet: that respecting the child's tempo, honoring the child's feelings, and trusting the child's capacity for genuine engagement would be enough. That you did not need to manipulate attention if you deserved it. That the medium could serve the child's development rather than extract from it.

He was right. But Rogers worked within broadcast television — a shared medium by definition. Everyone in the house heard the same show. The personal screen has no such constraint. Each family member feeds themselves their own stories. There is no shared symbolic ground. No common reference points. No ability to process experience through a shared narrative vocabulary.

The family in the same room, each on a different device, is the spatial image of what this chapter diagnoses.

· · ·

If the fire created mythic community, and the television created mythic family, and the personal screen creates mythic isolation — what creates mythic belonging now?

Fred Rogers proved the medium itself is neutral. But Rogers worked within a medium that gathered people by default. What does "Mr. Rogers for the algorithmic age" even mean when there is no shared channel?

Can parents curate shared symbolic worlds for their families — playlists of folk tales, tarot reflections, I Ching hexagrams that the family returns to together — creating a shared ritual space in the personal-screen era? Or has the infrastructure of personalization made that impossible?

And if a family cannot share a story, can a nation?

Chapter 03

The Polarization of the Polis

On the evening of November 22, 1963, approximately ninety-six percent of American households with television sets were tuned to the same broadcast. Walter Cronkite removed his glasses, glanced at the clock, and told the nation that its president was dead. The average American watched thirty-one hours of coverage over the next four days. There was nowhere else to look. Three networks carried the same story. The country grieved in the same room.

Sixty years later, no event commands that attention. Not because nothing matters, but because the room no longer exists. The 2024 presidential debates drew roughly seventy million viewers — a large number, but less than a quarter of the population, split across dozens of channels and platforms, each framing the same event through different narratives, each feeding different algorithmic streams to different audiences. By the next morning, two Americas had watched two different debates. The broadcast that once created temporary ritual unity now creates parallel realities.

Benedict Anderson, in Imagined Communities, argued that the nation itself is a narrative construction — made possible by shared media. The newspaper, the novel, and the broadcast created the conditions under which millions of strangers could imagine themselves as belonging to the same community. The ceremony was simultaneous consumption: every reader encountering the same news, every viewer watching the same event, each aware that millions of others were doing the same. The nation was, in Anderson's formulation, "an imagined political community" — imagined because its members will never meet most of their fellow members, yet each holds in their mind the image of their communion.

What happens when the ceremony ends? When the shared channel dissolves into a million personalized feeds? Not a crisis of information — there is more information than ever. A crisis of imagination. The ability to imagine that you and the stranger across town inhabit the same story.

· · ·

The previous chapter traced the dissolution of the family hearth — each member in the same room, each on a different screen, each consuming a different narrative stream. This chapter follows the same dissolution to its political conclusion. If a family cannot share a story, can a polis?

The media fragmentation research is unambiguous. Before talk radio, cable news, and the internet, citizens received what researchers call "a relatively consistent news package" — fostering "a common collective intelligence." The Georgetown media-and-politics research program documents the cascade: selectivity leads to fragmentation, fragmentation leads to echo chambers, echo chambers lead to polarization. Each person consumes different fragments of news content. The fragments are curated by algorithms optimized not for understanding but for engagement. And engagement, as Tristan Harris has documented, is maximized by outrage.

But the relationship between media and polarization is not as simple as "echo chambers cause division." A counterintuitive finding from Springer's political communication research suggests that polarization sometimes increases with more diverse exposure, not less — when the media itself is more polarized than the population it serves. The problem is not that people hear only their own side. It is that when they hear the other side, they hear a caricature designed to provoke. The algorithm does not show you what your neighbor actually thinks. It shows you the most extreme, most engaging version of what your neighbor's tribe has produced. Exposure to this distortion confirms your worst suspicions and hardens your position.

Robert Putnam, in Bowling Alone, documented the broader pattern: the decline of civic association correlated with the rise of privatized entertainment. Television replaced communal leisure with isolated consumption. The bowling league, the Elks Lodge, the PTA — the institutions where strangers practiced being in the same room with different people — dissolved as screens offered easier, more personalized alternatives. Smartphones and social media accelerated what television began.

The result is a polis that cannot disagree, because disagreement requires common ground to disagree on. You cannot argue about the meaning of a story you have not both heard.

· · ·

John Gottman's research at the University of Washington is the most important number in this chapter, and possibly in this book.

Forty years. Three thousand couples. Cameras measuring everything from facial expressions to heart rate. The finding: sixty-nine percent of relationship conflicts are perpetual problems that never get resolved. This held equally for happy and struggling couples. The difference between couples who thrive and couples who collapse was not whether they resolved their conflicts. It was how they managed the ones that would never be resolved.

If sixty-nine percent of conflicts are perpetual between two people who love each other, share a bed, and chose to build a life together — then permanent disagreement in diverse societies is not a bug. It is a defining characteristic of shared life. The purpose of political dialogue is not resolving disagreements but learning to live inside them with skill.

Jonathan Haidt's moral foundations research illuminates why the disagreements are permanent. Liberals weight two moral foundations most heavily: Care and Fairness. Conservatives additionally weight three others: Loyalty, Authority, and Sanctity. Neither framework is wrong — they process moral information through different evolutionary channels. Haidt's metaphor: the emotional intuition is the elephant, and rational argument is the rider. The elephant decides first. The rider constructs justifications after. You cannot argue someone out of a moral intuition because the intuition precedes the argument.

Tim Urban's vertical axis adds another dimension: the real divide is not horizontal (what you think) but vertical (how you think). A liberal scientist and a conservative scientist can productively disagree because they share epistemic norms — evidence, falsifiability, good faith. A liberal zealot and a conservative zealot cannot, because they have abandoned those norms in favor of tribal loyalty. The question is not what positions people hold but whether they hold them in a way that permits genuine encounter.

· · ·

The Talmudic tradition offers the oldest model of what this encounter looks like in practice.

"Both these and these are the words of the living God," the Babylonian Talmud declares of the rival schools of Hillel and Shammai — "but the law follows the House of Hillel." The reason given is not that Hillel's arguments were stronger. It is that the House of Hillel studied the arguments of Shammai before studying their own. They held the opposing position in their minds, gave it its full weight, and only then presented their own.

The tradition preserves minority opinions alongside majority rulings. Not as historical curiosities but as living resources — because "the minority view may become relevant in future circumstances." The Talmud is not a book of answers. It is a book of arguments, preserved in their full complexity, with the dissent as carefully recorded as the consensus.

The chavruta — the study partnership at the heart of Talmudic learning — enacts this structurally. Two students sit across from each other with the text between them. They are not seeking agreement. They are seeking understanding through productive disagreement. The practice assumes that truth emerges from the collision of perspectives, not from the triumph of one. As Rabbi Jonathan Sacks wrote: "In Judaism, argument is a form of worship."

What this requires is a shared textual ground. The chavruta works because both partners are reading the same text. They disagree about its meaning, but they are inside the same story. Without that shared ground, there is no chavruta. There are only two people shouting from different books.

· · ·

Structured dialogue initiatives demonstrate that shared narrative spaces work — when they can be created.

Ireland's Citizens' Assembly on abortion brought ninety-nine randomly selected citizens together with expert testimony and facilitated deliberation. The assembly recommended liberalizing abortion law. The subsequent referendum passed with sixty-six percent of the vote — in a country where the question had been deemed politically impossible to resolve.

James Fishkin's deliberative polls, conducted over thirty years in dozens of countries, show opinion changes averaging fifteen to twenty percentage points after structured deliberation — with some groups shifting as much as seventy percent. Intelligence Squared debates, which measure audience opinion before and after, record persuasion rates averaging forty-seven percent.

Braver Angels, the American depolarization organization, brings "reds" and "blues" into structured conversation with ground rules designed to prevent the usual dynamics. Participants consistently report increased understanding of the other side.

All of these work. All of them struggle with scale, self-selection, and sustained effect. They prove that humans can share a story and disagree inside it with skill. They cannot create the conditions under which millions of people share a story in the first place. The chavruta works in a room. It does not yet work at the scale of a civilization.

The structured dialogue movement is a symptom, not a cure. It demonstrates the need for shared narrative ground. It cannot provide that ground. The ground was once provided by shared media — the broadcast, the newspaper, the town square. Those institutions have fragmented. What replaces them?

· · ·

This chapter has moved from the family to the nation, from the hearth to the polis, from intimate narrative dissolution to political mythic collapse. The pattern is the same at both scales: without shared stories, there is no shared ground. Without shared ground, disagreement becomes combat rather than chavruta.

Polarization is not primarily an ideological problem. It is a mythic one. A polis needs shared stories the way a family needs shared stories — not because the stories resolve conflicts, but because they provide the common ground on which conflicts can be productively held. Gottman's sixty-nine percent teaches that most of life's essential tensions are permanent. The contemplative traditions teach that sitting with tension — rather than resolving it — is the practice. The structured dialogue research teaches that people can do this when they share a narrative space.

The question is whether such a space can be built — not by algorithm, not by broadcast, not by any of the mechanisms that produced the fragmentation in the first place, but by something older and simpler. By the grammar. By the finite symbolic vocabulary that generates infinite meaning through combination and interpretation. By the technology of encountering the unknown that humans have built independently, across civilizations, for thousands of years.

· · ·

Given Gottman's sixty-nine percent, should we stop trying to resolve our political conflicts and start learning to live inside them with skill?

Is there a political equivalent of the family hearth — a place where a community gathers around shared stories, not to agree but to practice the art of productive disagreement?

The Talmudic tradition preserved minority opinions alongside majority rulings because "the minority view may become relevant in future circumstances." Can a digital commons preserve disagreement without weaponizing it — can a platform hold opposing views the way the Talmud holds opposing rulings, as equally valid expressions of a living tradition?

And: what would it mean to address polarization at the mythic level — not with better arguments, not with better algorithms, not with better fact-checking — but with shared grammars? Finite symbolic vocabularies that both sides can enter, interpret differently, and leave with a richer understanding of the field they share?

Part II: The Grammars
Chapter 04

Machines for Constructing Stories

In the last quarter of the ninth century before the common era, anonymous diviners in the Western Zhou dynasty scratched numerical symbols onto oracle bones and bronze vessels. The symbols were binary: broken lines and unbroken lines, arranged in sets of six. Two line types, combined exhaustively, generated sixty-four figures. Each figure carried a name and a judgment. Together they formed a system that claimed to map the entirety of change — every possible configuration of yin and yang across six positions.

Twenty-five hundred years later and five thousand miles south, Yoruba Babalawos in the sacred city of Ile-Ife cast sixteen sacred palm nuts and read the marks that remained. Their system was also binary: single marks and double marks, arranged in sets of eight. Two mark types, combined exhaustively, generated two hundred and fifty-six figures. Each figure carried a name, a hierarchy, and an oral corpus of hundreds of verses encoding mythology, medicine, ethics, and philosophy. Together they formed a system that claimed to map the entirety of existence — every conceivable life situation contained within the Odu.

The structural parallel is extraordinary. The I Ching uses six binary positions — 2⁶ = 64 figures. Ifá uses eight binary positions — 2⁸ = 256 figures. Both generate their primary figures by pairing smaller units: the I Ching pairs eight trigrams, Ifá pairs sixteen tetragrams. Both attach a literary corpus to each figure. Both employ physical randomization — yarrow stalks or coins for the I Ching, palm nuts or divination chain for Ifá — to select figures, interpreting the result as communication between the human and the divine. Both present reality as fundamentally dynamic rather than static: the I Ching's changing lines transform one hexagram into another, while Ifá's prescribed ebo serves as the mechanism of transformation — changing misalignment into alignment.

Information-theoretically, a single tarot draw provides approximately 6.3 bits of entropy. A single I Ching hexagram provides 6 bits. Ifá's opele generates close to 8 bits of Shannon entropy per cast. These are not trivially different — Ifá encodes four times as many distinct states as the I Ching — but they occupy the same order of magnitude, the same neighborhood of informational richness. They are, in a precise mathematical sense, comparable machines for introducing structured randomness into human decision-making.

The scholarly consensus treats these as parallel but independent inventions. What is not debated is that something remarkable happened: across civilizations that had no contact with each other, human beings independently constructed symbolic architectures claiming to map the totality of reality through interconnected parts.

I call these systems grammars.

· · ·

Not grammars in the linguistic sense — not syntax rules for constructing sentences — but in a deeper sense: finite vocabularies of symbols that, through combination and interpretation, generate infinite meaning. A grammar, in the sense I mean, has five properties.

First, it has a finite symbolic vocabulary. Seventy-eight cards. Sixty-four hexagrams. Two hundred and fifty-six Odu. Ten Sefirot connected by twenty-two paths. The vocabulary is bounded. You can hold it in your hands, or at least in your memory. This finitude is not a limitation but the source of the system's power: because the vocabulary is finite, it can be learned, transmitted, and shared.

Second, the vocabulary is combinatorially generative. The meaning of any element depends on its relationships with other elements. A tarot card means one thing in the "past" position and another in the "future" position. An I Ching hexagram means one thing with no changing lines and another with three. The grammar generates meaning through juxtaposition, not through fixed assignment. This is what makes it a grammar rather than a dictionary: it has syntax, not just definitions.

Third, the grammar uses randomness as portal. The coin toss, the card draw, the palm nut cast, the birth moment — each introduces an element that the conscious mind did not produce. This randomness is not noise. It is the mechanism by which the grammar bypasses habitual thought and confronts the querent with something unexpected. Every grammar has a technology of randomness at its heart.

Fourth, the grammar requires a practitioner as participant. The diviner, the reader, the astrologer is not a detached observer decoding a message. They are an active participant in meaning-making, bringing their own experience, intuition, and context to the interpretation. The same card drawn by two different people in two different situations means two different things — not because the card changed, but because the act of interpretation is irreducibly personal. This is what distinguishes a grammar from a code. A code has one correct decryption. A grammar has infinite valid readings.

Fifth, the grammar models change rather than stasis. The I Ching is literally the "Book of Changes." Ifá's prescribed ebo transforms misalignment into alignment. Tarot's narrative spreads map temporal flow. Astrology tracks planetary motion through time. Every grammar is a technology of becoming, not being.

· · ·

The most vivid image of this convergence comes from Huayan Buddhism. The Avatamsaka Sutra describes Indra's Net: an infinitely vast net with a jewel at every node. Each jewel reflects every other jewel, and each reflection contains all the others — infinite reflections within reflections. The third patriarch Fazang demonstrated this with a hall of mirrors and a golden lion statue: every part of the lion is completely gold, and every point in the mirror hall reflects the entire hall.

The I Ching embodies this logic structurally. Through its changing lines, each hexagram enfolds pathways to every other hexagram. The system is genuinely holographic — each part contains the whole. Kabbalah's Tree of Life achieves the same through the central Sefirah, Tiferet, which connects directly to every other node, ensuring the graph is fully interconnected. Both systems model reality through the interplay of polar opposites at hierarchical levels — the Tree's three pillars mirror yin, yang, and their harmony.

David Bohm's implicate order — his distinction between the explicate order of separate things and the deeper implicate order where "everything is enfolded into everything" — represents the most rigorous modern scientific articulation of what the grammars have always modeled. The I Ching is a hologram of change. Ifá is a hologram of existence. Tarot is a hologram of human experience. Each fragment contains information about the whole.

None of this means these systems are "true" in the sense that they accurately predict the future. It means they are complete in a formal sense — they provide a vocabulary rich enough to describe any situation a human being might encounter, and a syntax flexible enough to generate meaningful interpretations of that situation. They are, in Italo Calvino's precise formulation, "machines for constructing stories." They work because the stories they construct are always about the person constructing them.

· · ·

The question is not why humans build grammars. The question is why we keep building them independently — and why they keep converging on the same structural properties.

One answer is cognitive. The human mind naturally categorizes, polarizes, and combines. We think in binaries and generate complexity by combining them. A system of six binary positions generates sixty-four states, which is enough to feel exhaustive without being overwhelming. A system of eight binary positions generates two hundred and fifty-six — pushing toward the limit of what a trained practitioner can hold in memory. These are cognitive sweet spots.

Another answer is mathematical. Ron Eglash, in African Fractals, traced a provocative chain: African binary divination → Islamic geomancy → European geomancy → Raymond Lull's logic machine → Leibniz → modern binary computing. The mathematics of grammars is not mysticism — it is combinatorics. Binary systems generate exhaustive possibility spaces efficiently. This is why digital computers use them. It is also why diviners use them.

But the deepest answer may be existential. Humans live in a world that exceeds our comprehension. We are finite beings confronting infinite complexity. We cannot process all the information available to us, cannot predict the consequences of our actions, cannot know what we do not know. Grammars are technologies for making this situation bearable — not by reducing complexity, but by providing a structured framework within which complexity can be encountered. The randomness of the draw is an honest acknowledgment of our ignorance. The finitude of the vocabulary is an honest acknowledgment of our cognitive limits. The requirement for interpretation is an honest acknowledgment that meaning is made, not found.

In this sense, grammars are humanity's oldest contemplative technologies. They predate meditation traditions, predate organized religion, predate writing itself — the proto-hexagram symbols on Shang dynasty oracle bones are among the earliest symbolic marks in the archaeological record. Before humans had books, before they had temples, before they had formal philosophies, they had grammars: structured systems for encountering the unknown and making meaning from the encounter.

· · ·

Part I of this book described a crisis: stories that have lost their homes, a hearth that has degraded, a polis that cannot share its myths. The grammars are not the answer to that crisis. They are evidence that the crisis is not new — that humans have always needed structured ways to encounter the unknown, and have always built them. The I Ching was constructed in a civilization facing its own upheavals. Ifá was created and maintained through centuries of displacement. Tarot accreted its meanings over six hundred years of cultural turmoil.

The question the next chapters take up is not what the grammars mean. It is how they survived — and what their survival teaches about what any shared symbolic system needs to persist.

Chapter 05

Survival

In 213 BCE, the Chancellor of China made his move. Li Si, the most powerful Legalist politician of his era, presented his memorial to Emperor Qin Shi Huang: scholars who "use the past to criticize the present" were dangerous to state unity. Private learning bred private judgment, and private judgment bred dissent. The emperor approved. The edict was brutal in its specificity: all privately held copies of the Classic of Poetry, the Classic of History, and the writings of the Hundred Schools of Philosophy were to be surrendered for burning. Public execution for anyone caught discussing the banned classics. Extermination of the entire clan for using history to criticize the present. Tattooing and forced labor on the Great Wall for anyone who failed to surrender their books within thirty days.

The edict concluded with a chilling instruction: "Those who wish to study should take government officials as their teachers." The state would be the sole source of knowledge.

Yet the burning was not total. The edict explicitly exempted texts on medicine, agriculture, and divination. The I Ching — by then already layered with the philosophical commentaries of the Ten Wings, already one of humanity's most profound cosmological documents — was swept into the protected category. Qin bureaucrats classified it as a divination manual, a practical tool for fortune-telling. The irony is profound. The Xi Ci Zhuan, the Great Commentary that articulated "One yin and one yang — this is called the Dao," the text that became the cornerstone of Chinese metaphysics for two millennia — survived because a bureaucrat saw only a fortune-telling handbook.

The Classic of Music, the sixth of the legendary Six Classics, was not so lucky. It was completely and permanently lost. Only seven fragments survive, quoted in other works. An entire tradition of pre-Qin musical knowledge, vanished.

How a civilization classifies its texts determines which ideas live and which die.

· · ·

The true catastrophe came three years later. The imperial library had preserved copies of every banned text — the burning targeted private holdings, not state collections. Those copies survived the edict. They did not survive the rebel general Xiang Yu, who sacked and burned the Qin palaces at Xianyang in 206 BCE. The second conflagration consumed the only remaining authorized copies of many works. Martin Kern of Princeton has argued that the 206 BCE palace burning was the real point of no return — that earlier texts frequently cite the Classics in ways that would have been impossible if the 213 BCE burning had truly been thorough.

What survived did so through two mechanisms: walls and memory.

In the late second century BCE, workers demolishing a wall at the Kong family home in Qufu — the ancestral home of Confucius — discovered manuscripts hidden within. Versions of the Classic of History, the Rites of Zhou, the Analects, written in pre-Qin seal script. These were transcribed by Kong Anguo, an eleventh-generation descendant of Confucius, and became the foundation of the "Old Text" tradition.

And there was Fu Sheng. A former Qin-era court scholar, he had hidden a copy of the Shangshu in his own home's walls. After decades of exile during the civil wars, he returned to find the manuscripts damaged — only twenty-eight or twenty-nine chapters out of roughly one hundred had survived. By the time Emperor Wen of Han sent an official to learn from Fu Sheng around 160 BCE, the old scholar was over ninety and nearly unintelligible. His daughter served as translator, converting his thick Shandong dialect into language the scribe could record.

The Classic of Poetry survived almost intact because its songs had been transmitted orally for centuries. Verse is far easier to memorize than prose.

· · ·

Five thousand miles south and eighteen centuries later, a different grammar faced a different catastrophe. The collapse of the Oyo Empire in the 1790s, the Fulani jihad of the 1830s, and inter-Yoruba civil wars generated massive numbers of enslaved Yoruba, some from the priestly class. They were shipped across the Atlantic in the holds of slave ships. Their temples were left behind. Their sacred objects — the divination trays, the palm nuts, the ivory tappers — were confiscated or lost. Everything material was stripped away.

But the grammar survived. Because Ifá's corpus is oral, not written. You cannot confiscate an oral encyclopedia. You cannot burn what exists only in memory. The Babalawos carried in their minds a system of two hundred and fifty-six Odu, each containing hundreds of verses encoding mythology, medicine, ethics, and philosophy — the entirety of Yoruba cosmological knowledge.

In Cuba, the enslaved Yoruba preserved Ifá within cabildos — mutual aid societies organized by African "nations." Orishas were syncretized with Catholic saints: Changó with Santa Bárbara, Yemojá with the Virgin of Regla. The syncretism was a survival strategy, not a theological compromise. By the time of the Cuban Revolution in 1959, approximately two hundred active Babalawos practiced on the island. By the 1990s, they numbered in the tens of thousands. UNESCO recognized the Ifá divination system as a Masterpiece of the Oral and Intangible Heritage of Humanity in 2005.

The I Ching survived through bureaucratic classification. Ifá survived through the irreducible capacity of human memory. Both strategies exploited the grammar's defining property: its finitude. Sixty-four hexagrams can be memorized. Two hundred and fifty-six Odu can be held in a trained mind. The survival of grammars is a function of their compactness — their capacity to encode maximum meaning in minimum structure.

· · ·

Tarot survived through a third mechanism: structural openness. There was no tarot author to execute, no tarot scripture to burn, no tarot church to dismantle. The seventy-eight-card deck originated as an Italian card game in the 1440s — luxury hand-painted commissions for the Visconti and Sforza dynasties of Milan. For over three hundred years, no occult significance was attached to the cards, nor any use made of them save for playing games. Michael Dummett, Oxford's Wykeham Professor of Logic, established this conclusively in his six-hundred-page The Game of Tarot.

The deck survived because card games propagate through use. They spread horizontally, through play, without requiring institutional support. When the meaning-system was later layered onto the cards — first by Court de Gébelin in 1781, then by Lévi, the Golden Dawn, Waite, and others — the physical infrastructure of seventy-eight cards was already ubiquitous across Europe. The container preceded the content.

And when Pamela Colman Smith illustrated the Rider-Waite-Smith deck in 1909, creating full pictorial narrative scenes for every card — an innovation no prior deck had attempted — she made the grammar self-interpreting. You no longer needed an expert to read the cards. The images themselves told stories. This reduced the need for institutional mediation and accelerated the deck's spread. Over one hundred million copies have been sold.

· · ·

The Maya codices provide the counterexample — what total destruction looks like. Of the thousands of books produced by Maya civilization — bark-paper manuscripts recording astronomy, history, ritual, and prophecy — exactly four survive. The rest were burned by Spanish friars, most notably Diego de Landa, who in July 1562 ordered the destruction of Maya manuscripts and images at Maní. His stated justification: they "contained nothing in which there were not to be seen superstition and lies of the devil." An entire civilization's written record, reduced to four fragments preserved in European libraries named after the cities where they ended up — Dresden, Paris, Madrid, and a fourth discovered in 2016.

The Maya had grammars. The Long Count calendar was a combinatorial system of extraordinary mathematical sophistication — base-20 arithmetic generating cycles within cycles, mapping time across thousands of years. The Tzolk'in ritual calendar combined twenty day-names with thirteen numbers to produce a 260-day cycle used for divination and ceremony. These systems had the five properties this book defined in Chapter 4: finite vocabulary, combinatorial generation, randomness as portal, practitioner as participant, modeling of change.

They did not survive. The manuscripts that encoded them were burned. The priests who carried them were killed or forcibly converted. The languages that gave them meaning were suppressed. What remains are fragments — reconstructed by modern scholars from the four surviving codices, from inscriptions on stone monuments, from colonial-era transcriptions like the Popol Vuh that were filtered through Christian frameworks.

The lesson is brutal. The grammars that survive are those that do not depend on institutions, manuscripts, or temples. The I Ching survived because a bureaucrat misclassified it. Ifá survived because it lived in memory. Tarot survived because it had no authority to execute. The Maya grammars were embedded in manuscripts, temples, and a priesthood — all of which could be targeted and destroyed.

· · ·

Each grammar's survival strategy teaches a different lesson. The I Ching: classification determines survival — be useful to the powerful, even if your true significance lies elsewhere. Ifá: orality is resilient — what lives in memory cannot be confiscated. Tarot: structural openness is its own defense — a system with no center cannot be decapitated.

Together, they demonstrate that the grammars which endure are those that can be compressed into the human body: memorized, carried, played, practiced. The manuscript can be hidden in a wall or burned in a palace. The oral tradition can cross an ocean in the mind of a single practitioner. The card deck can travel in a pocket. The grammar that depends on infrastructure — on temples, institutions, state support — is the grammar most vulnerable to destruction. The grammar that depends only on human beings is the grammar that survives.

· · ·

If the threat to grammars today is not burning but drowning — not destruction but dilution in an ocean of content — what survival strategy fits? A grammar's compactness was once its defense against confiscation. Is compactness still protective when the problem is not scarcity of stories but overwhelming abundance?

The Qin burning destroyed texts, but the Old Text / New Text controversy that followed shaped Chinese thought for centuries — the argument about what was authentic became as consequential as the originals. What arguments about authenticity are we already in, without recognizing them?

And the hardest question: if the grammars that survived were those that could be compressed into the human body, what happens when the body is replaced by the screen — when memory is outsourced, practice is gamified, and the grammar is consumed rather than carried?

Chapter 06

Tarot's Double Life

She drew every card. Seventy-eight full pictorial narrative scenes in approximately six months — an innovation no prior tarot deck had attempted. Where earlier pip cards showed only abstract arrangements of suit symbols, Pamela Colman Smith created full human stories for every card, making the deck immensely more readable, more intuitive, more alive.

She was paid a flat fee. She received no royalties, no copyright, and no credit. The deck was named for the publisher (Rider) and the commissioner (Waite). She died penniless in Bude, Cornwall, in 1951.

Over one hundred million copies of her deck have been sold. Her name was largely forgotten until scholars began recovering it in the twenty-first century. Whether the erasure was racialized, gendered, or both — Smith was of mixed heritage and never married — it reproduces precisely the colonial dynamic by which the labor of the marginalized creates value that accrues to the privileged. The most widely used tarot deck in history was drawn by a woman whose contribution was made invisible by the men who commissioned it.

This is the story of tarot in miniature: a system that generates extraordinary value while displacing the people who create that value. A system whose openness is simultaneously its greatest vulnerability and its greatest power. A system that lives a double life — captured and liberated, again and again, by the same structural feature.

· · ·

In 1781, Antoine Court de Gébelin, a French Protestant pastor and Freemason, published an essay claiming that tarot was a remnant of the ancient Egyptian Book of Thoth — sacred priestly wisdom encoded in symbolic images. He claimed "Tarot" derived from Egyptian words Tar ("path") and Ro ("royal"). He had no evidence for any of this. The Rosetta Stone would not be discovered for another eighteen years. Jean-François Champollion would not decipher hieroglyphics until the 1820s. Court de Gébelin could not read a single word of Egyptian. Later Egyptologists found nothing in the Egyptian language to support his etymologies.

The myth stuck because it was published in a prestigious work whose subscribers included Louis XVI.

What followed was a century of elaborate invention built on this fabrication. Éliphas Lévi linked the twenty-two Major Arcana to the twenty-two letters of the Hebrew alphabet and the Kabbalistic Tree of Life — despite never formally studying Hebrew and making frequent grammatical errors. The Hermetic Order of the Golden Dawn synthesized tarot, Kabbalah, astrology, alchemy, Hermeticism, and Egyptian mythology into a single comprehensive initiatory system. And in 1909, Arthur Edward Waite commissioned Smith to illustrate the deck that would carry his name but not hers.

Each generation of occultists built upon the fabrications of the last.

· · ·

Court de Gébelin's fabrication was not merely a historical error. It was an act of what Edward Said would later theorize as Orientalism: an "Orient" that exists to be decoded, systematized, and possessed by European intellects. His claims appeared seventeen years before Napoleon's Egyptian campaign, during a period of intense European colonial interest in Egypt. As Elliott Colla argues, "the genesis of western Egyptology went hand in hand with European imperialism."

Court de Gébelin created a colonial knowledge loop: Egyptian wisdom was claimed as the origin of tarot, but Europeans — not Egyptians — were positioned as the rightful interpreters and custodians of that wisdom. The colonized provide the raw material. The colonizer provides the framework. Authority flows in one direction.

The colonial dimensions compound through the nineteenth century. Lévi's appropriation of Kabbalah extracted Jewish mystical tradition from its communal, hermeneutic context and reframed it within a universalized Christian-Hermetic framework. As Boaz Huss documents, "mysticism is a modern discursive structure that was created within the context of European colonialism." Helena Blavatsky's Theosophical Society made the racial logic explicit. Her Secret Doctrine elaborated a theory of "root races" positioning the "Aryan race" as humanity's highest current form. Julian Strube's landmark article demonstrated that race was central to Theosophical doctrine yet has been consistently overlooked by most historians.

Strube's broader argument is decisive: "if occultism is modern, it is inherently intertwined with colonialism," reflecting modernity's rhetoric that "marginalizes all other epistemes that do not fit with the principles that aspire to build a 'totality of knowledge.'"

The Golden Dawn's Book T — which assigns each tarot card to a Hebrew letter, astrological sign, color, divine name, and position on the Tree of Life — is a miniature colonial encyclopedia of the world's esoteric traditions, reorganized under British authority.

· · ·

Here is the structural thesis of this chapter, and it is the hinge on which the entire book turns: the same openness that allowed grammars to survive is what made them available for colonial extraction.

A revealed tradition — one with an author-prophet, a scripture, an institutional church — is harder to colonize. You must contend with the authority that guards it. But an authorless system, a grammar with no center, no pope, no orthodoxy — this is maximally vulnerable to capture. Anyone can project anything onto it, and no one can say they are wrong.

Court de Gébelin projected Egypt onto Italian playing cards because there was no tarot authority to stop him. Lévi projected Kabbalah because there was no tarot tradition to contradict him. The Golden Dawn built their comprehensive synthesis because the cards, being authorless, offered no resistance.

This is not a reason to close the grammars. It is a description of the risk that comes with openness.

· · ·

Here is the turn.

Tarot has no author in the religious sense. Unlike the Bible, the Quran, or the Vedas, it claims no divine revelation, no prophetic authority, no institutional church. Unlike initiated traditions like Freemasonry or the Golden Dawn themselves, it requires no initiation, no lineage, no master. It is, in Calvino's formulation, "a machine for constructing stories" — a combinatorial semiotic system whose meanings emerge through juxtaposition and interpretation, not doctrine.

This structural openness has profound consequences. Because the esoteric meaning layered onto tarot was fabricated rather than revealed, it carries no binding authority. There is no tarot pope, no tarot scripture, no tarot orthodoxy that cannot be contested.

And so the same structural absence that allowed Court de Gébelin to project his Orientalist fantasies onto seventy-eight Italian playing cards also allows Christopher Marmolejo to resituate those cards within Aztec cosmology. It allows Cristy C. Road to remake them as queer punk revolution. It allows Jessica Dore to read them through dialectical behavior therapy. It allows anyone who picks up the cards to become an author of meaning.

· · ·

The live academic debate staged in the Anthropology of Consciousness journal between 2023 and 2024 puts the question precisely: can the master's tools dismantle the master's house?

Mike Sosteric's critique is structurally compelling. He argues that tarot's archetypal imagery — the hierarchical structures of Emperor, Hierophant, the rigid social order encoded in the court cards — normalizes and naturalizes power structures. "The master's archetypes from the master's picture book are so deeply ingrained" that using them "reinforces the archetype no matter how much you might resist."

Yvan Greenberg's response argues that tarot divination can serve as a "decolonizing methodology" by healing "Cartesian mental models that underlie modernity's hold on our society." The decolonial move is not to abandon tarot but to read it differently — to refuse the systematizer's authority.

Marmolejo's Red Tarot performs exactly this refusal. Drawing on Paulo Freire's critical pedagogy and bell hooks, he treats tarot reading as critical literacy: a means of subverting dominant power structures by resituating tarot imagery within non-Western cosmologies. His Death card works through the Hindu goddess Chhinnamasta. His High Priestess through the Aztec goddess Coyolxauhqui. The cards are the same. The projections are different. And because there is no tarot authority to declare his projections illegitimate, they stand.

Rachel Pollack, a trans woman who came out in 1971 — the same year she discovered tarot — authored Seventy-Eight Degrees of Wisdom, the foundational modern tarot text. Her insight: tarot offers LGBTQ+ people "a spirituality that we are otherwise shut out of," one that "is interactive, and relies on the very same journey of self-discovery and realisation that is built into the queer experience."

Jessica Dore, a licensed social worker, integrates tarot with dialectical behavior therapy, acceptance and commitment therapy, and mindfulness practice. Her Tarot for Change decolonizes tarot from its occultist gatekeeping while retaining its symbolic power — making it a tool for autonomous self-interrogation rather than initiated expertise.

The emerging HCI scholarship positions tarot not as archaic mysticism but as cutting-edge technology of imagination. Hong-An Wu locates tarot at "the worldmaking juncture where anti-oppression meets technological innovation." Stuart Candy and Jeff Watson's The Thing From The Future, developed at Carnegie Mellon, translates tarot's combinatorial logic into an explicit futures methodology — used at the UN Development Programme, IDEO, MIT Media Lab, and Stanford's d.School.

Aaron Talley frames tarot as a technology for Black futures: "For Black people, the future has never been promised… Tarot can predict the future; it also reminds us that we deserve futures in the first place."

· · ·

The framing that emerges is not "tarot despite its colonial history" but tarot because of its structural openness, which is the same feature that enabled its colonial capture.

A revealed tradition cannot be so easily colonized — but neither can it be so easily liberated. Tarot's lack of institutional authority is both its vulnerability (anyone can project anything onto it) and its emancipatory power (no one can claim final authority over it). This is the recursive logic: the same absence of foundation that made tarot available for colonial fabrication makes it available for decolonial reinvention.

"Gateway building, not gatekeeping" is not merely a slogan. It is a description of tarot's actual ontological structure.

· · ·

If a system's openness is both its vulnerability to colonial capture and its availability for liberation, what does ethical stewardship look like?

Is there a difference between a grammar that was always open — tarot, Italian playing cards that accumulated meaning through projection — and a grammar that is initiated and lineage-dependent — Ifá, transmitted through consecrated Babalawos? Where is the line between gateway building and gatekeeping? Between honoring a tradition and freezing it? Between making knowledge accessible and stripping it of its context — which is, after all, the very crisis this book began with?

These questions do not have stable answers. But they have a practice, which is what the chapters that follow explore.

Chapter 07

The Living Cosmos

The first time I sat with my birth chart — really sat with it, not as entertainment but as a question — Saturn was transiting my tenth house. I did not know this yet. What I knew was that everything I had built professionally felt like it was being dismantled from the inside. Not failing, exactly. Contracting. The institutions I depended on were withdrawing their support, one by one, with the impersonal precision of a tide going out.

A Jyotish practitioner looked at the chart and said: Saturn is doing what Saturn does. It takes away what is not yours. What remains afterward is what was always yours. The process is not punishment. It is clarification.

I did not believe this in any metaphysical sense. I am not someone who thinks Saturn, a gas giant eight hundred million miles away, is personally rearranging my career. But something happened when I attended to the idea — when I sat with the image of Saturn as a force of contraction, limitation, and discipline, and held it next to my actual experience. The image clarified the experience. The experience deepened the image. Something responded — not from the sky, but from a place inside the encounter between the symbol and the life.

This is what I want to understand: not whether the planets control us, but what happens when we attend to them as though they might.

· · ·

The Greek word for the zodiac — zoidia, from ζῷον, zōion — means "living thing." The same root gives us "animal" and "animism." The zodiac is literally the "circle of living beings."

When modern astrology reduced planets to psychological functions — Mars equals aggression, Venus equals love — it performed a disenchantment, stripping the cosmos of its aliveness. The planets became metaphors. The metaphors became personality types. The personality types became memes. Whatever relationship the ancients had with the night sky — a relationship mediated by prayer, fasting, mantra, and devotion — was flattened into a compatibility quiz.

Jyotish — the "eye of the Vedas" — never lost its living relationship with planetary beings. The Sanskrit word Graha means "to seize or grasp," reflecting how these forces influence human tendencies. But the Grahas are not impersonal forces. They are devas — beings with consciousness, agency, and the power to deliver karmic results. Traditional relationship with them develops through mantra, gemstone, fasting, charity, and devotion. This is not metaphorical. In the Vedic framework, chanting the beeja mantra of the Sun one hundred and eight times on a Sunday morning is a relational act — an address to a living intelligence, not a psychological exercise.

Each of the twenty-seven Nakshatras possesses a specific Shakti — a power. Ashwini carries the power to quickly heal. Bharani carries the power of transformation through death and rebirth. Krittika carries the power to burn and purify. Each Shakti has a "basis above," a "basis below," and a "result" — forming a triadic structure that maps onto tantric cosmology.

· · ·

The most explicit bridge between astrology and tantra runs through the Dashamahavidya — the Ten Great Wisdom Goddesses, central to Shakta Tantra. The Todala Tantra assigns each goddess to a specific Graha: Kali governs Saturn, Tara governs Jupiter, Tripura Sundari governs Mercury, Bhuvaneshvari governs Moon, Chinnamasta governs Rahu, Dhumavati governs Ketu, Bagalamukhi governs Mars, Matangi governs Sun, Kamala governs Venus. Worship of the corresponding Mahavidya is prescribed as a tantric remedial measure for planetary afflictions — qualitatively different from standard Vedic remedies because it involves full tantric sadhana.

The concept of Kala — both "time" and "death" — may be the deepest bridge. Mahakala, Great Time, is a form of Shiva representing eternity beyond cyclic time. Mahakali is his Shakti counterpart. Manifest time — kala — is what Jyotish maps. Unmanifest time — Mahakala — is what tantric realization aims to penetrate. The astrologer reads the clock. The tantric practitioner asks who is doing the reading.

This matters for the book's argument because it reveals what was lost when astrology was disenchanted. Modern Western astrology reads the clock and calls it insight. Jyotish reads the clock and asks about the nature of time itself. The grammar is the same — planets, houses, aspects, transits — but the depth of the inquiry is different by orders of magnitude. One produces a horoscope. The other produces a contemplative practice.

· · ·

The kriya/karma distinction makes this operational. In non-dual Shaiva Tantra, kriyā-śakti is one of the five powers of absolute Consciousness — "the Power of Acting." When this power operates through the contracted individual self — the ego believing itself to be a separate agent — the result is karma: bound, effortful action that creates impressions and binds awareness to the past. When the same power operates through an individual who has "recognized" their identity with the whole, the result is kriya: spontaneous, frictionless, divinely-aligned action that does not bind.

Parker Palmer reaches the same structural insight from within the Quaker tradition. His core thesis: "Before you tell your life what you intend to do with it, listen for what it intends to do with you." Palmer's concept of "functional atheism" — the belief that ultimate responsibility for everything rests with us, "a conviction held even by people who talk a good game about God" — is karma dressed in Western clothes. The ego believes it is the sole agent. Palmer's alternative — listening to what life wants to do through you — is kriya in Quaker language.

This convergence — a tenth-century Kashmiri philosopher, a twentieth-century American Quaker, a living tradition of Vedic astrology — is not coincidence. It is the grammars pointing at the same thing from different positions. The cosmos is alive. The appropriate response to a living cosmos is not prediction or control but attention and recognition. The grammar is the technology for making that attention structured and repeatable.

· · ·

Christopher Wallis, who holds an MPhil from Oxford and a PhD from Berkeley in Sanskrit, provides the metaphysical architecture for what the mathematics and the mythology point toward. His teaching, drawn from Abhinavagupta's Tantraloka, is that svātantrya-śakti — the Power of Autonomy — is the one fundamental quality of Consciousness, and it belongs exclusively to the whole, not to individual parts.

In the Pratyabhijña tradition, all phenomena — including planetary influences — are expressions of Cit-Shakti, consciousness-power. The planets are not external forces controlling us but aspects of awareness to recognize within ourselves. Practice becomes pratyabhijñā — recognition — rather than propitiation. You do not appease Saturn. You recognize, within your own experience of limitation and discipline and time, a quality of consciousness that the tradition has called Saturn.

Wallis's nonduality explicitly includes duality rather than negating it: "Instead of a nondual view that excludes duality and says duality is wrong, this is a nondual view that includes duality, as a valid level of experience, though not an absolute one." The third way does not eliminate the two poles but reveals them as expressions of a unity that encompasses both.

This framing resolves a tension that plagues most digital astrology: the fine line between insight and superstition. By grounding Jyotish in non-dual philosophy — where "the planets in the sky" and "the planets in consciousness" are recognized as non-different — a platform can offer genuine depth without the ethical problems of fortune-telling. The grammars become contemplative mirrors rather than predictive instruments.

· · ·

And this is not mysticism requiring special belief. It is a phenomenological claim about the structure of experience — compatible with Bohm's implicate order, with Merleau-Ponty's embodied perception, with Thomas Csordas's "somatic modes of attention." The claim is simple: when you attend to Saturn — in your chart, in the sky, in your experience of limitation and discipline and time — something responds. Not because Saturn is a god beaming influence at you. But because consciousness, attending to a quality of itself, recognizes what was always already there.

Grammars are not dead symbol systems. They are invitations to enter into living relationship with a responsive cosmos. Whether you frame that response as divine agency, as synchronicity, as the projective capacity of the human psyche, or as the implicate order unfolding into the explicate — the phenomenological experience is the same. You cast. Something is cast back. You attend. Something attends to you.

The grammar is the space where the meeting happens.

· · ·

If the grammars are alive — if they invite relationship rather than mere interpretation — what does it mean to host them on a digital platform? Can a screen mediate a living encounter with the cosmos, or does the medium inevitably flatten the relationship into information consumption?

Is there a difference between casting the I Ching with yarrow stalks in silence and tapping a button on a phone — and if so, is that difference essential or accidental? What would it mean to design technology that serves the aliveness of the grammars rather than domesticating them?

These are the questions that sit beneath the practical question the rest of this book takes up: not whether to build, but how to build without killing what you are building for.

Part III: The Hinge
Chapter 08

The Trap of Solving

Every chapter so far has ended with questions. The reader may be frustrated. Where are the answers? This chapter is about why the frustration is the point.

· · ·

Paul Tillich, in The Courage to Be (1952), describes what he calls "the God above God" — a term designed to irritate people on both sides of the theism-atheism divide. The God above God transcends both the believer's deity and the atheist's rejection of it. Tillich's critique of what he calls "theological theism" — God conceived as "a being beside others" — is that this God inevitably becomes an object of control: "This is the God Nietzsche said had to be killed because nobody can tolerate being made into a mere object of absolute knowledge and absolute control."

The atheist who rejects this God is correct. The believer who worships this God is worshipping a projection. Both are trapped within a binary — theism or atheism, belief or unbelief — that the ground of being transcends.

This is not agnosticism. It is the recognition that both poles of the binary arise within a field that encompasses them both. Tillich calls it "being-itself" — the ground, power, and abyss of being. You cannot stand outside it to affirm or deny it. You can only attend to it.

· · ·

Christopher Wallis, translating Abhinavagupta's Tantraloka, arrives at the same insight from a different continent and a different millennium. The one fundamental quality of Consciousness, Abhinavagupta teaches, is svātantrya-śakti — the Power of Autonomy. And this power belongs exclusively to the whole, not to individual parts.

The individual is the whole in contracted form. The same five powers that characterize the whole — awareness, bliss, will, knowledge, and action — exist in the individual but in limited, contracted versions. The path of pratyabhijñā — "recognition" — is the individual discovering that its apparent freedom was always the freedom of the whole expressing through it.

Wallis's nonduality explicitly includes duality rather than negating it: "Instead of a nondual view that excludes duality and says duality is wrong, this is a nondual view that includes duality, as a valid level of experience, though not an absolute one."

This is not mysticism requiring special belief. It is a structural claim: the part cannot possess the freedom that belongs to the whole. The individual cannot solve its way to liberation, because the solving — the attempt to fix, to manage, to control outcomes — is itself an expression of contracted consciousness. The part trying to function as the whole.

The operational distinction is between karma and kriya. Karma is action performed by a contracted self attempting to control outcomes. It binds because it originates from the illusion that the part is the author. Kriya is action that flows from recognition of the whole — spontaneous, appropriate, free. It does not bind because it does not originate from the illusion.

Parker Palmer reaches the same place from within the Quaker tradition. His core thesis: "Before you tell your life what you intend to do with it, listen for what it intends to do with you." Palmer's concept of "functional atheism" — the belief that ultimate responsibility for everything rests with us, "a conviction held even by people who talk a good game about God" — is karma in Western clothes. The ego believes it is the sole agent and must control outcomes. Palmer's alternative — listening to what life wants to do through you — is kriya in Quaker language.

Three traditions. Three continents. Three centuries. The same structural insight: the impulse to solve — to identify a problem, design a solution, implement it, evaluate it, and move on — is not merely ineffective at the level of totality. It is structurally incoherent. The part cannot fix the whole because the part does not possess the freedom the whole possesses.

· · ·

Now apply this to the crisis this book has been tracing.

Part I described a wound: stories that have lost their homes, a hearth that has degraded, a polis that may have lost the capacity for shared myth. The natural response — modernity's deepest reflex — is to identify this as a problem and solve it. Build a platform. Design an algorithm. Create content. Measure engagement. Iterate.

But this is the reflex that produced the crisis. The attention economy is the most sophisticated problem-solving apparatus in human history. It identified a problem (how do we capture and monetize human attention?), designed solutions (infinite scroll, autoplay, variable reward), implemented them at scale, and measured outcomes with extraordinary precision. The result is a civilization drowning in stories and starving for meaning. The solving worked. The solving is the problem.

Vanessa Andreotti, in Hospicing Modernity, names this pattern with precision. Modernity, she argues, is not a problem to be solved but a dying patient to be accompanied. Its "most important area of domination was the mental universe of the colonised" — and this includes the mental universe of those who critique it. The impulse to identify a crisis, propose an alternative, build it, scale it — this is modernity doing what it does, even (especially) in movements that claim to resist it. Andreotti calls it "low-intensity struggle activism" — activism that reproduces the very logic it claims to oppose.

Bayo Akomolafe's version is more radical: "What if the way we respond to the crisis is part of the crisis?" The categories we use to diagnose the problem — "mythic belonging," "ritual misplacement," "polarization" — are themselves products of the epistemological framework that produced the crisis. Naming the problem is not the first step toward solving it. It is a way of domesticating it within familiar frames.

A caveat is necessary. Andreotti and Akomolafe are provocative thinkers whose work has genuine force. But they do not operate with the scholarly rigor of a Christopher Wallis parsing Abhinavagupta's Sanskrit, or a Wande Abimbola transmitting four thousand ninety-six couplets of Ifá. Their claims are often more poetic than demonstrable, more gestural than evidenced. This does not make them wrong. But the reader should hold them as this book holds everything — as questions, not authorities.

What grounds the argument is not their poetry but the philosophical tradition behind it. Tillich's transcendence of the binary. Wallis's demonstration that freedom belongs to the whole. Palmer's practice of listening before acting. The trap of solving is not Andreotti's invention. It is the shared finding of contemplative traditions across civilizations — the recognition that the ego's attempt to manage reality is itself a contraction of the freedom it seeks.

· · ·

The cosmic topology provides the image.

The Planck mission measured the universe's curvature parameter as consistent with perfect flatness. But a flat universe could be infinite — like an endless plane — or finite — like a three-dimensional torus, where space wraps around on itself. The flatness you measure in your neighborhood tells you nothing about whether the universe is finite or infinite.

Local measurements cannot determine global shape. You cannot infer the topology of the whole from the geometry of the part. A flat torus is finite yet has no boundary — combining properties that binary thinking would call contradictory.

Applied to this book's argument: measuring screen time (a local measurement) tells you nothing about the global topology of a child's relationship to meaning. Measuring engagement (a local metric) tells you nothing about the global shape of a community's shared symbolic world. The attention economy measures locally — clicks, time on page, conversion rates. Grammars reveal topologically — the field within which the clicks occur, the shape of the space in which the metrics are generated.

You cannot solve the crisis of mythic belonging from inside the epistemological framework that produced it. You also cannot simply step outside that framework — you are in it, the way you are in the universe whose topology you are trying to determine. What you can do is make gestures. You can create conditions. You can build infrastructure and see what the community does with it.

· · ·

What a "gesture toward adaptive futures" means in practice.

Not a solution but a wager. Not a product but an experiment. Not certainty but — to use Tillich's word — courage. The courage to build something that might not work. That does not claim to have the answer. That holds questions without answering them.

The previous chapter described the grammars as alive. This chapter has argued that the impulse to manage, systematize, and deploy them — the impulse to solve with them — risks killing what makes them alive. The gesture is to create the conditions under which living encounter can happen, and then to step back. To love the clay in front of the children, as Margaret McFarland said, rather than to teach sculpting.

· · ·

Is it possible to build a technological platform without reproducing the technological logic that created the crisis? Can a digital commons genuinely serve contemplative practice, or does the medium inevitably shape the practice into something the attention economy can digest?

If the impulse to solve is part of the problem, what is the alternative to solving — and is naming that alternative just another form of solving?

These are the questions the final chapter lives inside. It does not escape them. It practices them.

Part IV: The Gesture
Chapter 09

Grammars of the Living World

Marsha Linehan developed the biosocial hypothesis — and the dialectical framework that grew from it — not from ideology but because it was effective, fit the data, and was compassionate. She had tried everything with patients who were suicidal, who had been failed by every existing treatment. Cognitive behavioral therapy alone was not enough. Psychodynamic therapy alone was not enough. She arrived at dialectics — holding opposites in creative tension, accepting reality while simultaneously working to change it — because the people in front of her needed it.

Three criteria guided her: Does it work? Does the evidence support it? Does it reduce suffering?

I want to apply those three criteria to a different problem. AI has arrived. Social structures are fraying. People are drowning in digital consumption but starving for authentic creation and connection. We need to build culture faster — not the culture of content and consumption, but the culture of meaning-making, of contemplative practice, of people creating together rather than consuming alone.

What organizational form is effective, fits the evidence, and is compassionate?

· · ·

Effective.

The evidence that open, community-maintained infrastructure outperforms closed alternatives at scale is now overwhelming. Linux runs virtually every server on the internet, every Android phone, one hundred percent of the world's top five hundred supercomputers. Wikipedia is the world's encyclopedia. Ghost — the publishing platform founded by John O'Nolan as a nonprofit under MIT license — generates nearly nine million dollars annually with no investors, no owners, zero percent transaction fees, and all revenue reinvested.

The key structural insight, documented by researchers analyzing forty-four open-source developer tools, is that control of distribution and operational infrastructure matters more than control of code. Projects controlling service-layer infrastructure achieve valuations orders of magnitude higher than community-funded alternatives with comparable community engagement.

This is the open-core model: open-source the foundation, sustain the platform through hosted services. Ghost does this. The foundation is free and modifiable. The service layer generates revenue. The mission is protected by the legal structure — a nonprofit foundation that legally prevents being sold, pivoted, or pressured by shareholders.

· · ·

Fits the evidence.

In 2008, the anthropologist Christopher Kelty published Two Bits: The Cultural Significance of Free Software — itself under a Creative Commons license, embodying the principles it described. Kelty's central contribution was the concept of the recursive public:

A recursive public is a public that is vitally concerned with the material and practical maintenance and modification of the technical, legal, practical, and conceptual means of its own existence as a public. It is a collective independent of other forms of constituted power, capable of speaking to existing forms of power through the production of actually existing alternatives.

What distinguishes a recursive public from an interest group, a corporation, a church, or a movement is its focus on the radical modifiability of its own terms of existence. The community does not just use the medium to communicate. It creates and maintains that medium as a form of political action.

Kelty identified five interlocking practices: sharing source code, conceiving open systems, writing copyright licenses, coordinating collaboration, and proselytizing. He asked: do these practices extend beyond software?

I am asking the same question for contemplative practice.

· · ·

The five components, modulated:

Sharing grammars modulates sharing source code. The grammars — tarot decks, I Ching hexagrams, Nakshatras, Dhammapada verses, children's stories, wellness exercises — are the "source code" of this platform. They are created by people, not algorithms. They are shareable, forkable, modifiable. Anyone can create a grammar. Anyone can contribute to an existing one. Everything is publicly and freely available.

Conceiving openness in sacred systems modulates conceiving open systems. This is the hardest modulation. "Open" means something different when the content is a Yoruba divination system transmitted through initiated lineages than when it is a software protocol. The CARE Principles for Indigenous Data Governance — Collective Benefit, Authority to Control, Responsibility, Ethics — provide the ethical framework. Some grammars are structurally open (tarot, I Ching) and belong in the commons. Others are initiated, oral, and lineage-dependent (Ifá) and should be represented only with the explicit involvement and authority of tradition-holders. The principle is: gateway building, not gatekeeping — but the gateway must be built with the community's consent.

Writing the license modulates writing copyright licenses. Creative Commons Attribution-ShareAlike 4.0 International. The SA — ShareAlike — is the contemplative equivalent of copyleft. Anyone who builds on the grammars must share their derivatives under the same terms. The freedom propagates. The platform code is open-source. The content created by the community is commons. What is proprietary is nothing.

Coordinating co-creation modulates coordinating collaboration. The community voting on features, contributing tools, creating together. The design principle from the structured disagreement research: not resolving conflicts but building the capacity to hold them.

The movement modulates proselytizing. This book. The argument — not for a specific metaphysics, but for a structural claim: humans need non-dogmatic, non-revealed symbolic systems to make meaning, and these systems should be commons, not commodities. The argument is made not through rhetoric but through the production of actually existing alternatives. The platform exists. The grammars are available. The community is forming. The proof is in the practice.

· · ·

Compassionate.

The alternative is the attention economy. Co-Star sending push notifications designed to generate engagement anxiety. Headspace charging seventy dollars a year for access to meditation — a practice that has been freely transmitted for twenty-five hundred years. Labyrinthos gamifying tarot into streaks and achievement badges.

A commons is compassionate because it makes meaning-making infrastructure available to everyone — not as a product to consume but as a practice to participate in. A ten-dollar tool feels very different to someone in Brazil than to someone in Silicon Valley. When access is not tied to purchasing power, people can focus on genuine contribution rather than financial transactions.

The architecture embodies this. No ads. No algorithm. No notifications. No tracking. Privacy by design — you can use every tool without creating an account. Optional AI — assistance is always opt-in, never required. Donation-sustained access — one hundred percent free, sustained by voluntary contribution. Everything exportable, everything deletable, everything yours.

The platform does not monetize attention. It does not gamify practice. It does not claim authority over your experience. It holds your question without answering it.

· · ·

Fred Rogers did not solve television. He showed what it looked like when the medium served the child. His design philosophy was not anti-technology. It was pro-ritual: slow pace, direct address, real-time activities, respect for the child's tempo, the deliberate inclusion of mistakes. He proved that a technological medium could serve the inner life. The question was never whether the medium was capable of care. It was whether anyone would design it that way.

Margaret McFarland, the developmental psychologist who collaborated with Rogers for thirty years, once told a visiting sculptor from Carnegie Mellon: "I don't want you to teach sculpting. All I want you to do is to love clay in front of the children." The children caught his enthusiasm. They did not need instruction. They needed someone who cared about the material in their presence.

This is the design philosophy. Not instruction. Not prediction. Not authority. Presence. The grammars are the clay. The platform is the workshop. The community is the teacher — not telling anyone what the cards mean, not predicting the future, not claiming expertise over anyone's experience. Just loving the material in front of the children.

· · ·

Kelty quotes Creative Commons with the phrase "If we succeed, we will disappear." The idea is that if the licenses work — if they become the default infrastructure through which culture is shared — then the organization that created them becomes invisible. The licenses are just there, like plumbing.

The same applies here. The goal is not to build a company. The goal is not even to build a platform. The goal is to build an infrastructure that becomes so fundamental it becomes invisible — the way Wikipedia became invisible, the way Linux became invisible, the way the protocols that run the internet became invisible. Not noticed, not branded, not owned. Just there. Available. Modifiable. Maintained by the community that uses it.

This is also what the grammars themselves do. The I Ching has been continuously modified for three thousand years — from oracle bones to the Ten Wings to Wang Bi's commentary to the Wilhelm-Baynes translation to whatever version you cast this morning. Tarot has been continuously modified since 1440 — from Visconti-Sforza to Pamela Colman Smith to Rachel Pollack to whoever designs the next deck tomorrow. Ifá has been continuously modified through every Babalawo who commits a new verse to memory and every diaspora community that adapts the practice to new circumstances.

The grammars are recursive publics. They always have been. They are communities of practitioners who build, maintain, and modify the very symbolic infrastructure through which they make meaning. What changes now is the speed and scale at which this can happen. AI makes it possible to create a new grammar — a new tarot deck, a new set of contemplative prompts, a new children's story — in hours rather than years. The platform makes it possible to share that grammar with everyone on earth, instantly, for free.

The hypothesis is that this acceleration is a gift, not a threat — but only if the infrastructure is held as commons. If it is held privately, the acceleration serves the attention economy. If it is held as commons, the acceleration serves the community.

· · ·

This is an experiment. It might work. It might not. But the hypothesis is clear, the evidence is substantial, and the need is urgent.

Grammars have survived millennia through compactness, openness, and the irreducible participation of practitioners. AI is here, and we need to build culture faster. The recursive public is the form that fits. The commons is the license that protects. The grammars are the source code.

Everything else is practice.
