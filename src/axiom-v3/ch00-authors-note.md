# Author's Note

---

I should tell you why I wrote this book before you read it, because the why is the only honest place to start.

Marshall Rosenberg, who created Nonviolent Communication, traced the origin of his life's work to a single question from childhood: his grandmother radiated something that drew people toward her, while others in the same neighborhood seemed to cast shadows wherever they went. What made the difference? That question — unanswered, unresolvable, but impossible to put down — organized the next fifty years of his work.

I have a version of that question. It is less generous than Rosenberg's and more tangled, and I have not resolved it.

The question that would not close was simpler and harder than Rosenberg's: *Is there a ground beneath human value, or is the whole thing a construction?* Not as philosophy. As a felt reality. Can I stand on something, or am I building the floor while I walk?

That question brought me, at forty, to a retreat led by Christopher Wallis — a scholar-practitioner of nondual Shaiva Tantra who translates a thousand-year-old contemplative tradition into language the analytical mind can hold. I did not go because I had found an answer. I went because the question had become unbearable and I had run out of ways to hold it alone.

What happened at the retreat was not a conversion. It was stranger. The practices worked — not as belief, but as something the body recognized even when the mind resisted. The images on the altar drew a comparison from my husband to the *Wild Wild Country* documentary, and he was not entirely wrong, and I could not entirely dismiss the comparison.

I asked Wallis directly: are the teachings just a construct? His answer was five words that this entire book circles: *It is, until you experience it.*

I have not fully experienced it. I may never. The doubt has not resolved. But something shifted, and the shift was not intellectual. The proof came on an ordinary evening: an AI system I had been building for months broke apart — months of architecture, collapsed in an hour — and I put my daughter to sleep with grace. My husband noticed, because he knows this is not my default state. The equanimity was not mine. Or rather, it was mine in a way I had not previously been able to access. Something was holding that I had not built. Whether it was the ground the tradition describes or a useful fiction my nervous system had adopted, I cannot tell you. Both descriptions pass the filter that this book will introduce. And the space between them is where this book lives.

I keep returning to something Wallis said about the temple: it is not the temple itself that raises the energy, but the open-hearted people who inhabit it. I need a container to open my heart. A community of open-hearted people is the best container I have found. But I am writing from the edge of that container — one foot in, one foot braced against the doorframe, the doubt still present, the body still settling.

This is not a book by someone who has arrived. It is a book by someone who cannot fully get rid of doubt and has chosen to write from inside it.
