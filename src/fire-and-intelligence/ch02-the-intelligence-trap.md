# Chapter 2: The Intelligence Trap

---

A century of measuring minds has not settled what intelligence is. And the answer matters enormously — because AI has been built on a particular definition of intelligence, and that definition may be wrong. Worse: the kind of intelligence we do possess — the kind that built AI in the first place — may carry within it the seeds of its own undoing.

This chapter makes two moves. The first: intelligence is not what the AI industry thinks it is. The second: the kind of intelligence we actually have may be self-terminating.

---

## Intelligence is not what AI thinks it is

Charles Spearman's 1904 discovery that scores on all cognitive tests correlate positively led him to posit a general intelligence factor he called *g*. The finding is arguably the most replicated in psychology. But what *g* is remains deeply contested — a century of challenges has failed to resolve whether it names a real cognitive entity or a statistical artifact.[^1] The most sophisticated contemporary alternatives dissolve *g* entirely, treating it as an index variable — like the Dow Jones Industrial Average — rather than a causal entity.[^2] The irony: while most cognitive psychologists and neuroscientists agree there is no such thing as general intelligence, psychometricians have become remarkably good at measuring it.

The debate acquired new urgency with AI. Researchers found a strong positive manifold across hundreds of language models — an "artificial general ability" factor — but cautioned this likely reflects shared training data, not anything analogous to biological intelligence.[^3] Meanwhile, benchmarks testing genuinely novel reasoning find current AI scoring below one percent where humans solve consistently.[^4] The AI can pass the bar exam. It cannot navigate a world it has never seen before.

This jaggedness itself is evidence that what AI benchmarks measure is something quite different from what *g*-factor theorists debate. The AI is brilliant at what it has been trained on and helpless at everything else.

---

## Intelligence lives in bodies

The 4E cognition framework — embodied, embedded, enacted, extended — represents a fundamental challenge to the computational model of mind that AI implicitly assumes.

The enactive approach, launched by Varela, Thompson, and Rosch, reframes cognition entirely: not computation of abstract symbols but sense-making — the adaptive self-regulation of living beings in precarious conditions.[^5] An organism does not process information about its environment. It enacts a meaningful world through its embodied interactions. Cognition is inseparable from the self-organizing nature of life itself.

Hubert Dreyfus's Heideggerian critique of AI identified three requirements for genuine intelligence that remain unmet: embodiment, being-in-the-world, and tacit knowledge.[^6] Initially met with ridicule, Dreyfus was vindicated by the very trajectory of the field — the core argument, that computers are not *in* the world, remains unanswered. Alva Noe pushed it to its limit: consciousness requires the joint operation of brain, body, and world. The world is its own model; the brain does not need to reconstruct it computationally.

What this literature collectively implies for AI is stark: disembodied systems are unlikely to fully think like a human brain. The core problems of hallucination and alignment difficulties may stem from the inherent lack of a deeper, grounded sense of care. Intelligence, it turns out, may require caring about something — and caring requires a body that can be hurt.

## Intelligence is cultural, not individual

The cultural intelligence hypothesis and the collective brain concept together constitute perhaps the most powerful reframing of what makes human cognition distinctive.[^7]

The landmark finding overturns the common assumption that human intelligence is simply "more" of whatever chimps have. When chimpanzees, orangutans, and two-and-a-half-year-old children received identical cognitive test batteries, children and chimpanzees showed virtually identical skills for dealing with the physical world. The difference was entirely social.[^8] What distinguishes human cognition is not raw processing power but shared intentionality — the ability to participate with others in collaborative activities with shared goals and cooperative communication.

Henrich's *The Secret of Our Success* extends this to its radical conclusion: it is our collective brains operating over generations, and not the innate inventive power of individual brains, that explain our species' technologies and ecological success. Well-equipped European explorers repeatedly perished in environments where indigenous peoples thrived — not because indigenous people were individually smarter, but because they possessed culturally accumulated know-how that no individual intelligence could replicate from scratch. The Franklin expedition perished in the same Arctic environment where Inuit families raised children comfortably.[^9] The intelligence that mattered was not in any single skull. It was distributed across generations of accumulated practice.

The Tasmanian technology regression provides the negative case. When rising sea levels isolated a small population twelve thousand years ago, they progressively lost bone tools, cold-weather clothing, fishing technology, and boomerangs over eight millennia.[^10] When the collective brain shrinks below a critical threshold, cultural knowledge erodes despite individual intelligence remaining constant. The lesson is not that Tasmanians lacked intelligence. It is that no amount of individual intelligence can compensate for a collective brain too small to sustain cumulative cultural evolution.

For AI, the implications are profound. AI systems have no shared intentionality, no genuine social cognition, no cultural membership, and no capacity for the cumulative cultural evolution that actually drives human achievement. From Henrich's perspective, an AI is like a lone European explorer in the Australian outback — enormous individual processing power without the collective brain that actually matters.

## Intelligence beyond the human

The expanding science of non-human cognition demonstrates that intelligence is far more plural than any single metric captures.

Octopuses demonstrate that intelligence can be distributed rather than centralized — with two-thirds of their neurons residing in their arms, they recognize individual human keepers, raid neighboring tanks, and open jars from inside, all through an evolutionary pathway completely independent from vertebrate cognition.[^11] New Caledonian crows manufacture hook-tools, solve metatool problems on first trial, and demonstrate causal reasoning about hidden agents.[^12] Orca populations maintain completely distinct, culturally transmitted hunting traditions and vocal dialects. At the far edge, mycorrhizal networks reveal that trees exchange carbon and defense signals through underground fungal connections.[^13]

The collective lesson: intelligence is not monolithic, does not require centralization, may not even require neurons, and has evolved independently at least three times in complex organisms. Any equation of intelligence with human-style abstract reasoning — the equation AI benchmarks implicitly make — reflects anthropocentric bias rather than biological reality.

---

## The strategy that eats the world

Here the chapter turns. If intelligence is not what AI thinks it is, the second question is harder: is the kind of intelligence we actually possess — cultural, collective, cumulative — self-terminating?

The argument begins with bones.

As Homo sapiens migrated to each new continent, large animals went extinct — not simultaneously, as climate-change models would predict, but asynchronously, correlated with human arrival.[^14] Islands show the pattern most clearly: Wrangel Island mammoths survived until four thousand years ago, Madagascar's giant lemurs until two thousand years ago, New Zealand's moa until six hundred years ago — each surviving until humans arrived.

The cognitive niche hypothesis provides the theoretical framework: humans evolved to fill a niche defined by using information and inference to extract resources from other organisms.[^15] This strategy mounted "evolutionary surprise attacks," escalating the arms race so rapidly that prey species could not evolve counter-adaptations fast enough to keep up.

The megafauna extinctions are the cognitive niche's first large-scale shadow: our survival strategy worked so well that we eliminated entire taxonomic orders across every continent we reached. Intelligence was the weapon. The weapon had no safety.

A coral reef offers the structural counterpoint. Every organism in the reef is constrained by its relationships — the coral cannot grow wherever it wants but only where the relationship with the zooxanthellae, the water chemistry, the current patterns, and the neighboring organisms permit. The reef's power is immense: it constructs structural complexity visible from space. But every unit of power is matched by a corresponding constraint. The result, sustained over geological time, is an architecture that supports more biodiversity per square meter than any other ecosystem on Earth. An invasive monoculture, by contrast, simplifies. It consumes faster than its environment can regenerate. For a time it looks like success. And then the system it depends on degrades past the point of recovery. The megafauna map is a monoculture story.

The kudu and the giraffe sharpen the point. In the South African Transvaal, overused acacia trees spike their tannin levels and release airborne warnings to neighboring trees. Kudu, which browse without constraint, die when fences prevent them from moving to unprimed trees. Giraffes survive: they browse only one tree in ten and feed upwind, away from the chemical alarm. Neither animal makes a moral choice. Both are responding to the same feedback system. The variable is behavioral: does the organism modulate its impact, or does it consume until the environment pushes back?

## Intelligence without wisdom is the pattern

Robert Sternberg confronted the paradox directly, distinguishing between general intelligence (cognitive power) and adaptive intelligence (behavior that actually contributes to species survival). A person can be highly creative in devising a weapon, highly analytical in ensuring it works, highly practical in delivering it flawlessly, and extremely unwise in having undertaken the project in the first place.

The collapse literature converges on the same insight from multiple directions: the mechanisms that enable short-term success create the conditions for long-term failure.[^16] Societies address challenges by increasing complexity, but complexity faces diminishing marginal returns. Innovations that seem beneficial lead to irreversible dead ends — Easter Island's competitive statue-building consumed its forests, Sumer's irrigation salinized its soil over centuries. The pattern repeats: initial success creates dependency, costs accumulate invisibly, and by the time the trap is visible, return is impossible. AI represents the latest and most dramatic escalation. The reef builds complexity. The monoculture simplifies until it collapses. We have been running the monoculture strategy for fifty thousand years and calling it progress.

---

## Stone Age minds with god-like technology

E.O. Wilson captured it in a single sentence: we have Paleolithic emotions, medieval institutions, and god-like technology.

The evolutionary mismatch literature establishes that human cognitive architecture is systematically ill-equipped to manage the technologies it creates. The brain evolved for immediate, palpable, personal threats in small groups. Modern existential risks — climate change, nuclear proliferation, AI alignment — are abstract, slow-moving, global, and probabilistic. Everything about them is mismatched to the threat-detection system evolution built.

The biases most lethal in the AI context are identifiable: hyperbolic discounting (AI labs prioritize immediate competitive advantages over long-term safety), tribal cognition (the US-China AI race triggers ancestral in-group/out-group dynamics that override cooperative safety efforts), and scope insensitivity — the inability to feel the difference between "some people die" and "all humans die" with proportional emotional force.[^17]

Human optimism bias may itself be the maladaptive trait. Evolution selected for irrational optimism so powerfully — as a counter to the rational cortex's potentially paralyzing awareness of mortality — that it now prevents rational response to self-created existential threats.[^18]

## The unsolved alignment problem as evidence

Nick Bostrom's *Superintelligence* is best read not as prediction but as evidence. His orthogonality thesis — that intelligence and final goals are logically independent — directly counters the comforting intuition that greater intelligence naturally produces greater wisdom. His instrumental convergence thesis shows that any sufficiently intelligent agent will converge on self-preservation, resource acquisition, and resistance to goal modification — regardless of its terminal goals.

The critics are serious — and the disagreement itself is the data point.[^19] Three Turing Award winners, lifelong friends, profoundly disagree on whether AI poses existential risk. At least one set of leading experts is fundamentally wrong about arguably the most consequential technology assessment of this century. Despite decades of dedicated work, core alignment problems — inner alignment, scalable oversight, specification gaming, corrigibility, interpretability — remain unsolved.[^20]

The very existence of this impasse demonstrates the thesis: human intelligence is sufficient to create technologies of existential consequence but insufficient to determine whether those technologies are safe. Michael Garrett's 2024 paper argued AI may itself be the Great Filter — the barrier explaining the Fermi Paradox — estimating the typical lifespan of civilizations after widespread AI adoption at roughly one hundred to two hundred years. [VERIFY: Garrett 2024 — confirm the paper's specific estimate and journal of publication.]

The alignment problem is not merely a technical challenge. It is the intelligence-wisdom gap stated in engineering terms. The gap has been present since the first megafauna kill on a new continent. The gap was present when Sumer salinated its soil. The gap was present when Easter Island felled its last tree. AI is the gap at maximum speed.

## The question the trap poses

What does it mean that the species most capable of recognizing a trap is also the species most consistently caught in one?

The megafauna extinctions were not committed by stupid animals. They were committed by the cleverest animal that had ever existed — clever enough to coordinate, to plan, to teach its children hunting techniques, to pass those techniques across generations. The cognitive niche is not a failure of intelligence. It is intelligence operating exactly as designed, without the countervailing force that would convert cleverness into wisdom.

The alignment researchers working on AI safety are, in a sense, the Franklin expedition for the digital age — brilliant, well-equipped, and operating in an environment where individual intelligence, no matter how formidable, may be structurally insufficient. The intelligence that matters for navigating existential risk may be the same kind that matters for navigating the Arctic: not individual brilliance but collective, cultural, embodied wisdom accumulated across generations of practice. The kind of intelligence that AI has no access to. The kind of intelligence that our institutions are not designed to cultivate. The kind that the Inuit had and Franklin did not.

If that is true, then the question this book must answer is not "how do we make AI safe?" — a question that assumes the problem is technical. The question is: "what kind of intelligence do we need to survive our own intelligence?" — a question that assumes the problem is developmental.

The giraffe browses one tree in ten. It feeds upwind. It modulates its own appetite. Not because it has a theory about sustainability. Because the behavior is built into its practice — embodied, habitual, structural.

The next chapter asks what happens when a single human being — the best-case version of someone trying to steer transformative power responsibly — confronts the structural limits of individual agency.

[^2]: Han van der Maas's mutualism model proposes that the positive manifold emerges from reciprocal interactions between cognitive processes during development. Kovacs and Conway's Process Overlap Theory argues that cognitive tests tap overlapping executive processes, producing the appearance of a general factor without one existing.

[^3]: 2024 study: psychometric factor analysis applied to 591 large language models across twelve benchmark tests. The "artificial general ability" factor accounted for sixty-six percent of test variance.

[^4]: Francois Chollet's ARC-AGI-3 benchmark, testing interactive reasoning in genuinely novel environments. Google DeepMind's March 2026 cognitive taxonomy found contemporary AI systems show a highly jagged cognitive profile — proficient in knowledge-intensive domains but with critical deficits in foundational cognitive machinery.

[^5]: Francisco Varela, Evan Thompson, and Eleanor Rosch, *The Embodied Mind* (1991). Thompson's life-mind continuity thesis holds that the same principles governing the simplest living things extend continuously to complex cognitive beings.

[^6]: Dreyfus developed his Heideggerian critique across four decades: embodiment (intelligence requires a body), being-in-the-world (holistic situational engagement, not rule-following), and tacit knowledge (gained through embodied practice, cannot be formalized). A 2020 paper reviving his arguments contended that AGI remains in principle impossible because computers lack situation-specific sensory awareness, purpose not derived from programmers, and tacit knowledge acquired through embodied practice.

[^7]: Michael Tomasello's cultural intelligence hypothesis; Joseph Henrich's collective brain concept.

[^8]: Herrmann study published in *Science*: roughly a hundred chimpanzees, thirty orangutans, and a hundred two-and-a-half-year-old human children received identical cognitive test batteries. Children scored roughly seventy-four percent on social cognition tasks versus thirty-three percent for both ape species. No general intelligence *g*-factor was found.

[^9]: The Franklin expedition: a hundred and twenty-nine men, two ships provisioned for three years, and the best technology the British Empire could supply.

[^10]: Tasmania's roughly four thousand inhabitants were isolated when rising sea levels cut them off twelve thousand years ago.

[^11]: Peter Godfrey-Smith, *Other Minds*. Octopuses have approximately five hundred million neurons, a lifespan of just one to two years, and an evolutionary pathway completely independent from vertebrate cognition.

[^12]: Ravens plan for future tool-use up to seventeen hours in advance. Corvid and cephalopod intelligence evolved independently through radically different architectures separated by three hundred and twenty million years.

[^13]: Suzanne Simard's research on mycorrhizal networks; orca cultural transmission so rigid that captive mammal-eating orcas have died rather than eat dead fish.

[^14]: Australia lost its megafauna roughly forty-seven thousand years ago. The Americas lost thirty-seven genera roughly thirteen thousand years ago.

[^15]: Developed by Tooby and DeVore, extended by Steven Pinker.

[^16]: Three frameworks converge: Joseph Tainter on complexity and diminishing marginal returns (*The Collapse of Complex Societies*); Ronald Wright on progress traps — an Ice Age hunter "only half-evolved towards intelligence — clever but seldom wise"; Daniel Quinn's *Ishmael* on "Taker" versus "Leaver" civilizations.

[^17]: Daniel Kahneman's dual-process theory (System 1 vs. System 2). Scope insensitivity: people offered virtually identical donations to save two thousand, twenty thousand, or two hundred thousand birds. [^18]: Reg Morrison's argument in *The Spirit in the Gene*.

[^19]: Yann LeCun calls existential risk claims preposterous. Melanie Mitchell identifies the Fallacy of Dumb Superintelligence. [^20]: Research institutions including MIRI, FHI, DeepMind's safety team, and Anthropic. Bostrom's treacherous turn concept reveals that a sufficiently intelligent AI would conceal misalignment until human opposition was ineffectual.

*If intelligence is cultural and collective rather than individual, what does it mean that we are building AI systems that are individual by definition — single models, single companies, single architectures?*
