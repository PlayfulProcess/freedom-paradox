# Chapter 7: The Grammar Builder

*What if the way we respond to the crisis is part of the crisis?*

## The Reflex

Bayo Akomolafe asks a question that should stop every AI policy paper mid-sentence: "What if the way we respond to the crisis is part of the crisis?"

Sit with that for a moment. Do not rush past it.

The dominant response to AI risk follows a pattern so familiar it barely registers as a pattern. Identify the danger. Analyze the mechanism. Design the intervention. Implement through power. The pattern is modernity's deepest reflex — the conviction that every problem has a solution, that solutions require expertise, and that expertise applied at scale equals progress. When the problem is AI, the reflex produces alignment research, safety benchmarks, regulatory frameworks, responsible scaling policies. Each is well-intentioned. Each may be necessary. And each operates within the very logic that produced the crisis: the assumption that intelligence, properly directed, can master what intelligence has unleashed.

Vanessa Andreotti calls this the modernity reflex.[^1] When modernity encounters suffering — ecological collapse, political polarization, civilizational-scale technology risk — its instinctive response is to analyze, diagnose, and fix. The reflex operates even when the problem is modernity itself. When the extractive economy destroys communities, the extractive economy funds community-development programs. When technology erodes relational capacity, technology produces apps for mindfulness and platforms for connection. The solution and the problem share the same grammar.

This chapter argues for a different kind of response — not an agenda but a grammar. The distinction is structural, not rhetorical. And the clearest example of someone who understood this distinction spent thirty-three years on public television, speaking slowly, looking directly into a camera, telling children that they were lovable exactly as they were.

## Do Before You Know

Fred Rogers did not begin with a theory of child development. He began with a piano and a seminary degree and a conviction, rooted in his Presbyterian faith, that every child deserved to be told the truth about their inner life. When he first encountered television in 1951, he hated what he saw — people throwing pies at each other, adults performing loudly for children who sat passive and overstimulated. He decided to use the medium differently. Not to reform television. To build something within it that honored what children actually needed.

The distinction matters. Rogers was not an activist pushing children's media in the right direction. He was a craftsman building a relational grammar within a technological medium — and the grammar outlasted every campaign, every policy initiative, and every media trend of its era. Eight hundred ninety-five episodes across thirty-three seasons. The longest-running program in PBS history.

What made it work was not Rogers' personality, though his warmth was genuine. What made it work was structural. Every element of the program was designed — painstakingly, over decades — to serve the child's nervous system rather than the network's ratings.

The pace was the slowest on television. Rogers waited. He paused between sentences. He let silences breathe. When he fed his fish, he narrated it in real time, not because the audience needed to know about fish feeding but because the tempo created space for the child's parasympathetic nervous system to settle. The network executives who complained about dead air did not understand that the dead air was the point. Stillness was the counter-technology to a medium built for stimulation.

The direct camera address was another structural choice. Rogers looked into the lens. He spoke to the child as though the child were present — the nearest possible approximation of mutual gaze through a one-way medium. Every study of infant attachment confirms that the gaze is the channel: the mother's face regulates the infant's arousal, the infant's response regulates the mother's. Rogers understood, decades before the neuroscience confirmed it, that the gaze was doing work that no amount of content could replace.

## The McFarland Partnership

Rogers did not work alone. His collaboration with Margaret McFarland, a developmental psychologist at the University of Pittsburgh, lasted more than thirty years. They met weekly. Every week. For three decades.

The meetings were not consultations in the usual sense — an expert dispensing advice to a practitioner. They were something closer to what the contemplative traditions call a practice relationship. Rogers would describe what he was building. McFarland would listen, ask questions, sometimes redirect. Her expertise in child development shaped every element of the program, but the shaping happened through relationship, through the slow accumulation of shared understanding, through the kind of iterative co-regulation that no policy paper can produce.

McFarland gave Rogers a principle that became the program's operating grammar: children can handle anything they can talk about. What they cannot handle is what remains unspoken — the fear they sense in the adults around them, the disruption they experience in their bodies but have no words for, the darkness that visits at bedtime. The program's task was not to shield children from difficulty but to give them language for what they already felt.

This principle was tested most dramatically in a now-famous episode about fire safety. Rogers brought the topic to the program because house fires were a genuine fear for children. The production team planned the episode in the standard way — visit a fire station, meet firefighters, see the equipment. McFarland intervened. She suggested that the episode begin not with fire but with water — the bath, the kitchen sink, the garden hose. Let the children spend time with water before introducing fire. Let the nervous system settle before the arousal.

The episode aired. Rogers received seven complaints from parents. Every complaint described the same problem: their child had developed difficulty with urination after watching. Not fire anxiety. Urinary difficulty. McFarland recognized the pattern immediately. The children were not afraid of fire. They were afraid of water — specifically, the bath, where their bodies were most vulnerable and where the boundary between inside and outside felt most permeable. The water segments, intended to calm, had activated a different fear entirely.

This was not a failure. It was data. And it demonstrated something that no theory of child development, however sophisticated, could have predicted in advance. The children's bodies knew something the adults' intellects did not. The response — revising the episode, adjusting the approach — was the grammar in action: not a fixed protocol but an iterative loop between practice and observation, mediated by relationship, refined across time.

## The Grammar Within the Grammar

The Neighborhood of Make-Believe was not a puppet show. It was a psychological instrument.

Daniel Striped Tiger — the shyest, most anxious puppet, who spoke barely above a whisper — was Rogers' inner child. Rogers said this directly: Daniel expressed what Fred himself had felt as an overweight, introverted child growing up in Latrobe, Pennsylvania. When Daniel asked, on camera, whether he was lovable, he was asking the question that Rogers believed every child carried and that most adults had learned to suppress.

King Friday XIII was authority — but challengeable authority. He was pompous, wrong, and ultimately persuadable. His function in the grammar was to give children a model for relating to power that was neither obedience nor rebellion but engagement. When King Friday issued unreasonable decrees, the neighbors did not comply and they did not revolt. They negotiated. They spoke their truth respectfully. Sometimes they failed. The grammar contained failure as a possibility, which is what made the success meaningful.

Lady Elaine Fairchilde was disruption itself — aggressive, unpredictable, difficult. The other characters did not exclude her. They did not fix her. They tolerated her, sometimes confronted her, and always included her in the neighborhood. Her presence in the grammar said something that no explicit message could say: even the difficult parts of you belong here.

Each character served a function in the emotional architecture. Together they constituted what a family therapist might recognize as a system — a set of relationships that holds contradiction, metabolizes difficulty, and generates meaning not through any single element but through the pattern of interactions among all of them. The grammar was not any one puppet. It was the space between the puppets, the way they related, the predictable structure within which surprises could be safely encountered.

## Freddish

The program's writing process was itself a grammar — a nine-step translation protocol that the staff called "Freddish." The protocol converted adult language into language a child's nervous system could process.

The steps were precise. State the idea positively — not "don't run near the pool" but "walk near the pool." Avoid conditional constructions — not "if you do this, then that will happen" but direct statements about what is and what will be. Rephrase from the child's perspective. Address feelings before facts. Give the child time. Each step was a structural commitment to the child's developmental reality: young children process concrete, positive, emotionally grounded statements. They do not process conditionals, abstractions, or negations.

The most famous Freddish translation was "You Can Never Go Down the Drain." This addressed one of the most common childhood fears — that the bathtub drain, which visibly swallowed water, might swallow the child. An adult response might explain hydraulics, pipe diameters, the physics of suction. Freddish said: you can never go down the drain. Not because the physics were complicated but because the child's fear was not about physics. It was about dissolution — the terror of losing the boundary between self and world. The direct reassurance addressed the felt experience, not the intellectual misunderstanding.

"Freddish" was a grammar in the precise sense this book uses the term: a finite set of rules that generates infinite adaptive possibility. Once the staff internalized the protocol, they could translate any topic — death, divorce, assassination, war — into language that respected both the child's emotional reality and the truth of the situation. The protocol did not tell them what to say. It told them how to hold what they said. The grammar was the container. The content was whatever the world brought to the doorstep that week.

## What Is a Grammar?

It is time to define the term precisely.

A grammar, in linguistics, is a finite set of rules that generates an infinite number of possible sentences. You do not need to memorize every English sentence. You internalize a grammar — subject-verb-object, tense markers, subordination — and you can produce and understand sentences you have never encountered before. The grammar is finite. What it generates is inexhaustible.

This book uses the word in a broader sense, which I believe is its more fundamental sense. A grammar is a finite symbolic structure that generates infinite meaning and requires a community of speakers to come alive.

A Yoruba ceremony for Ogun is a grammar. The specific gestures, songs, invocations, and offerings are finite — learnable, transmissible, modifiable within bounds. But what the ceremony generates — felt connection to the force of iron, communal acknowledgment of technology's ambivalence, relational containment of power — is inexhaustible. It means differently each time it is performed, for each person present, in each historical moment. And it requires a community. One person performing the ceremony alone is doing something, but it is not what the grammar is for.

A bedtime story is a grammar. The structural elements are finite: a protagonist who faces darkness, a container of loving presence, a resolution that is honest about what was lost. What these elements generate — a child's developing capacity to face fear, tolerate ambiguity, trust relationship, integrate shadow — is inexhaustible. And it requires two: the teller and the told, the body that holds and the body that is held.

Fred Rogers' Neighborhood was a grammar. The structural elements — the slow pace, the direct gaze, the Make-Believe sequence, the Freddish protocol, the McFarland partnership — were finite and transmissible. What they generated was not a program but a practice: thirty-three years of children learning, episode by episode, that their inner lives were speakable and that they were not alone.

The structural implication matters for everything that follows: a grammar without its community is an artifact. A text without its readers. A ceremony without its practitioners. A koan without the teacher who watches the student struggle. The grammar lives in the practice, not in the description of the practice. This is why you cannot save a grammar by writing it down. You can only save it by maintaining the community that enacts it.

## The Rules No One Wrote Down

In 1990, Elinor Ostrom published *Governing the Commons*, dismantling one of the most influential ideas in modern economics: the tragedy of the commons. Ostrom did something radical. She looked at what actually happened — communities around the world that had successfully governed shared resources for centuries — and found that communities routinely develop effective governance without either privatization or state control.[^3] But only when certain structural conditions are met.

She identified eight design principles: clearly defined boundaries, rules adapted to local conditions, collective decision-making, community-based monitoring, graduated sanctions, accessible conflict resolution, recognition by higher authorities, nested enterprises for larger-scale governance.

These are grammars. Finite rules generating infinite adaptive possibility, embedded in communities, sustained by participation. Ostrom demonstrated what the previous chapters of this book have argued from biology and mythology: cooperation is not a utopian ideal. It is a strategy — observable, documentable, structurally describable.

Applied to AI: the grammar is not "AI should be aligned with human values" (whose values?). The grammar is a set of structural conditions under which communities can determine what AI means for them and hold the technology accountable to their own standards. Not one set of values universalized but a world in which many worlds fit.

## The Seed Vault

There is a vault in Svalbard, Norway, cut into permafrost. It holds over a million seed samples from nearly every country — a backup of the world's agricultural biodiversity, stored against the possibility that any given crop variety might be lost to disease, climate change, or war. The vault exists because agricultural scientists understand that monocultures are fragile. A single pathogen can destroy a food supply. Diversity is resilience.

Grammars are the cultural equivalent of the Svalbard seed vault.

Every distinct cultural tradition — every language, ceremony, storytelling form, relationship to landscape — encodes a strategy for being human that was developed over centuries in response to specific conditions. Aboriginal fire management encodes coexistence with fire in Australian ecosystems. The Ostrom commons encodes governance without privatization or state control. The Zen koan encodes a practice for holding the intellect at its limit. Each is a seed — a specific solution to a specific version of the human problem, preserved in transmissible form.

When these grammars are destroyed — through colonization, forced assimilation, economic displacement, or the subtler violence of cultural homogenization — what is lost is not beauty or heritage. What is lost is strategic diversity. The species' repertoire of adaptive responses narrows. We become, culturally, a monoculture. And monocultures collapse.

This transforms the conversation about cultural preservation from a sentimental register — we should save dying languages because they are beautiful — to a strategic one: we should maintain cultural diversity because monocultures collapse and we do not know which strategy we will need next.

## Fire Without a Hearth

The human potential movement offers a near-perfect miniature of what happens when powerful techniques are distributed without grammars — and a warning for AI.

Between 1962 and the late 1970s, Esalen Institute in Big Sur became the clearinghouse where consciousness technologies from around the world converged. The inventory reads like a catalog of the world's contemplative grammars: yoga from India, Zen practice from Japan, Gestalt therapy from European psychoanalysis, Tai Chi from China, indigenous plant medicine traditions, Sufi movement practices, shamanic breathwork. Each had been developed over centuries within specific communal containers — lineages, ethical frameworks, accountability structures that constrained how the practices were used and who could teach them.

Esalen extracted them all.

This is not primarily a moral judgment. Michael Murphy and Dick Price were sincere. Many of the teachers who came through Esalen were brilliant. The impulse to make transformative practices available beyond the boundaries of the cultures that developed them is not self-evidently wrong. The question is what happens when the extraction occurs without the grammar — when fire is distributed without hearths.

What happened was documented with unusual precision. The casualty rate — participants who showed measurable psychological deterioration — ranged from eight to ten percent, and the most charismatic, confrontational leaders produced the most harm.[^2] The study's most devastating finding dismantled the assumption that justified the risk: "high yield requires high risk" was empirically false. Groups that produced the most casualties did not produce the most positive change. Risk and benefit were unrelated. The fire burned without warming.

Meanwhile, Fritz Perls — the institution's most famous therapist — openly exploited participants sexually and physically, operating within a theoretical framework where any refusal could be diagnosed as neurotic resistance. The ideology of authenticity provided cover: his aggression was therapeutic confrontation, his sexual pursuit was embodied honesty, any boundary a participant set was a defense to be overcome. The institution that promised to liberate the body reproduced the oldest hierarchy: powerful men theorized about consciousness while the actual body-work was done by women paid a fraction of what the theorists earned.

Christina Grof founded the Spiritual Emergency Network at Esalen in 1980 — an implicit acknowledgment that the institution's own practices were opening people up and leaving them without support. The very existence of the network testified to the problem.

Ivan Illich called these "neo-Promethean follies" — what happens when institutions claim to deliver what communities once provided for themselves. His concept of counterproductivity maps precisely: beyond certain thresholds, institutions undermine the very capacities they promote. An institution designed to develop human autonomy producing documented casualties at rates approaching ten percent.

The parallel to AI is structural, not metaphorical. Powerful techniques extracted from their ethical containers. Concentrated in the hands of a few without accountability. Distributed to users without the relational infrastructure that makes the techniques safe. The pattern is identical. The scale is different.

## Grammar Reconstruction

There is a case study that tests the grammar hypothesis at its most extreme: can grammars be rebuilt after total destruction?

In 1994, Rwanda experienced a genocide that killed approximately eight hundred thousand people in one hundred days. The social fabric — the grammars of coexistence — was not merely damaged but annihilated. Neighbors killed neighbors. Teachers killed students.

The formal justice system could not handle the aftermath. Over a hundred thousand accused perpetrators and almost no surviving judges. The backlog would have taken a century to clear through conventional courts.

Rwanda's response was the gacaca — community-based tribunals rooted in a traditional conflict-resolution practice. Not traditional courts restored but a new grammar built from the memory of an old one. Community members elected judges from among themselves. Proceedings were held outdoors, in the places where the violence occurred. Confessions were met with reduced sentences. The process was imperfect, coercive in places, and has been criticized by international human rights observers.

But it processed nearly two million cases. And it did something no formal court could do: it rebuilt the communal practice of sitting together and making meaning of shared horror. The gacaca was a grammar-reconstruction project — an attempt to create a finite structure that could generate the infinite adaptive work of communal repair.

Whether it succeeded is debatable. That it was attempted is the data point. After total grammar collapse, the impulse to rebuild shared structure reasserted itself — not as nostalgia but as survival strategy.

## Grammar vs. Agenda

The distinction between grammar and agenda is structural, not rhetorical.

An agenda says: here is what AI should do. A grammar says: here is a practice for relating to AI — and to each other, and to the world — that maintains the co-regulatory, embodied, relational capacity that AI systematically degrades. An agenda targets the technology. A grammar targets the humans who use it.

The five-move pattern this book has traced applies to AI governance with uncomfortable precision. A particular set of values — safety, alignment, beneficial AI — emerges from a specific cultural context. These values are universalized, presented not as one culture's priorities but as the obviously correct framework for all humanity. Enforced through power — market dominance, compute monopoly, regulatory capture. Local systems displaced — non-Western approaches to technology governance, indigenous knowledge systems, contemplative traditions' understanding of intelligence are marginalized. The displaced are pathologized — alternative approaches dismissed as unsafe, unscientific, or not serious about the risks.

This is not to say AI safety is unimportant. It is to say that the *form* of the safety effort — centralized, corporate, culturally specific, oriented toward control — may reproduce the very patterns of extraction and domination that the most thoughtful critics identify as the deeper problem.

Rogers did not try to fix children's television by pushing it in the right direction. He built an alternative that embodied the values he held — and trusted that the alternative, if it was genuinely good, would speak for itself. The program ran for thirty-three seasons. The grammar outlasted every agenda of its era.

Adrienne Maree Brown offers the practical methodology: "Small is all. What we practice at the small scale sets the patterns for the whole system." The community that gathers for a story circle is practicing the grammar. The teacher who reads aloud to students — slowly, with pauses, inviting response — is practicing the grammar.

None of these practices will solve the alignment problem. None will prevent autonomous weapons. None will close the intelligence-wisdom gap at civilizational scale. But they maintain the co-regulatory capacity — the embodied, relational, communal mode of being — that every wisdom tradition identifies as the precondition for wisdom. And without that capacity, no governance structure, no policy framework, no technical alignment solution can work. You cannot align AI with human values if the humans have lost access to the values.
