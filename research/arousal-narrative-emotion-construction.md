# When the body cries for joy: arousal, narrative, and the construction of emotion

The body's experience of intense sadness and intense joy shares far more physiological overlap than intuition suggests — but the two states are not identical. The strongest available evidence, drawn from meta-analyses of **202 autonomic studies** (Siegel et al., 2018), Barrett's constructed emotion theory, Russell's circumplex model, Buddhist phenomenology of vedana, and somatic psychology, converges on a layered model: a relatively undifferentiated arousal substrate is shaped into discrete emotional categories through cognitive appraisal, narrative framing, and conceptual categorization. However, the claim requires an important caveat that every tradition in this analysis — constructionist psychology, Buddhist philosophy, and even somatic therapy — recognizes: **valence** (the pleasant/unpleasant dimension) appears to be biologically and phenomenologically foundational, not merely a narrative overlay. The defensible thesis is not that sadness and joy are "the same state with different stories" but that narrative and context do far more differentiating work than most people assume, operating on a shared arousal substrate that is constrained but not fully determined by valence.
