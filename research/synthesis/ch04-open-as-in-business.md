# Synthesis: Chapter 4 — Open as in Business

**Sources:** open-source-universe-deep-research.md (Parts I, III), bibliography

## Narrative Arc

This chapter covers the 1998 rebranding from "free software" to "open source" — a deliberate strategic move that gained the world and arguably lost the soul. The chapter should present both sides fairly (the equity analyst instinct) but make clear what was at stake: the movement chose pragmatism over principle, and that choice shaped everything that followed, including the AI question.

## Key Story Beats

### 1. The Netscape Earthquake (January 1998)
- Netscape announced it would release Navigator source code
- This was unprecedented — a major commercial company going open
- A group gathered in Palo Alto to discuss how to capitalize on the moment

### 2. The Palo Alto Strategy Session
- Attendees: Eric Raymond, Bruce Perens, Michael Tiemann, Jon "maddog" Hall, Larry Augustin, Sam Ockman
- Christine Peterson suggested "open source" — the term that changed everything
- The motivation was *explicitly strategic*: "free software" confused people ("free as in speech, not beer?") and alienated business
- "Open source" was marketing — emphasis on practical benefits over ideology

### 3. The OSI and the Summit
- February 1998: Raymond & Perens founded the Open Source Initiative (OSI)
- Torvalds endorsed the next day — critical legitimacy
- April 1998: Tim O'Reilly's "Open Source Summit" — Torvalds, Larry Wall (Perl), Behlendorf (Apache), van Rossum (Python)
- This was the moment the movement went mainstream

### 4. The Stallman Schism
- Stallman rejected the rebranding — consistently maintained "free software" carries essential ethical meaning
- "Open source" deliberately discards the moral dimension
- This split persists to this day
- [FOR THE BOOK: This is the original sin of the movement. By choosing pragmatism over ethics, open source gained corporate adoption but lost the moral vocabulary needed to confront AI.]

### 5. Raymond's "Cathedral and the Bazaar" as Business Case
- The essay (1997) became the intellectual foundation for the business case
- Argued that open, decentralized development *produces better software*
- This reframed the argument: not "open is right" but "open is effective"
- Directly influenced Netscape's decision

## Key Themes for This Chapter

1. **The pragmatism-ethics tradeoff** — What the movement gained (corporate adoption, global infrastructure) and what it lost (moral vocabulary, the ability to say "some things shouldn't be opened")
2. **Christine Peterson's contribution** — A woman coined the most consequential term in software history. This deserves recognition.
3. **The marketing genius** — "Open source" is one of the most successful rebrandings in the history of technology
4. **The Stallman question** — Was Stallman right? The AI era suggests maybe he was — without the ethical framework, "open source" becomes a pure business strategy, and business strategies serve whoever has the most power
5. **The "bazaar" ideology** — The belief that decentralized, open development is *always* better. This belief is now being tested by AI.
