## Title Direction

## Ch30 — "The Invisible World" / Ontological Status

**Author position**: Don't claim "the invisible exists" as ontological truth. Frame it more like Spinoza's matrix of believing or not believing in God — if the practice is good for somatizing the need for responsibility and appreciation, it's useful. "This too shall pass the Linehan test." This is at the individual level for people to decide, and the key is: **don't overidentify with it**.

## 2.1 — Drugification of Connection (Ch18)

Frame as **near enemies of the body** (Christopher Wallis's "near enemies of the truth" concept applied somatically). Sugar, drugs, frictionless digital connection — they solve short-term but are not adaptive long-term. Near enemies look like the real thing but lack the essential quality.

## Andreotti Critique — "Modernity" as Misdiagnosis

**Author position**: The problem with calling it "modernity" is that it implies before or after modernity there was/will be a solution. The real problem is **the human condition itself**. More aligned with Eckhart Tolle: we were animals, balanced with nature. We got intelligence. Now we need to surpass intelligence with wisdom/responsibility. This is not a historical era problem — it's a species-level developmental problem.

## 3.3 — Harris's Sufficiency-of-Reason Challenge

**Author position**: Harris would argue we need to at minimum agree on **open society values**, even if he thinks building a whole mythology risks religious dogmatism (which hasn't worked). His real objection isn't to telling good stories — it's to **identifying with the make-believe as truth**, treating the story's narrative as "my truth" rather than a useful frame.

**CRITICAL**: Verify this is compatible with the book's thesis. If not, the book may need structural changes. The author deeply respects Sam Harris and will not publish something incongruent with Harris's valid concerns.

**Resolution direction**: The book's position should be: stories are tools for somatizing responsibility, not ontological claims. The Linehan test applies. The danger Harris identifies — overidentification with narrative as literal truth — is real and the book should name it explicitly as the near enemy of good storytelling.

## 3.1 — Akomolafe's 69/31 Challenge

**Author position**: Has seen firsthand how this posture immobilizes people. Same root as "accept what is" — but **the will to change IS part of accepting what is**. Akomolafe proposes the "fugitive" as a kind of solution, which is his biggest incongruence — because it's not useful.

**Lean more into**: Christopher Wallis + couples therapy research rather than Akomolafe's mythopoetic-as-truth framework, which is becoming a new religion that Harris would rightly critique.

## 3.4 — Andreotti's "Not Yet Ready to Build"

**Author position**: How can she know we're not ready? She says "hospicing to give birth" — but eventually we NEED to give birth. Don't wait for the universe to solve problems you created or to fix your roof.

**Parallel**: Haidt wanted to write about polarization but wrote *The Anxious Generation* instead because Gen Z was urgent and therefore useful. Change IS happening — maybe the Metta trial was influenced by the book or similar work. Research this more.
