## 1. MacIntyre *After Virtue* Chapter 5 — Kant Critique

**Chapter title**: "Why the Enlightenment Project of Justifying Morality Had to Fail"

**Page numbers (3rd edition, Notre Dame Press 2007)**:
- pp. 43–46: The Kant critique — universalizability test, counterexamples, "parasitic" argument
- pp. 51–55: The three-part teleological scheme and how the Enlightenment destroyed it
- pp. 54–55: The "joint effect" passage on rejecting theology and Aristotelianism

**MacIntyre's specific Kant critique (3 prongs)**:

1. **The categorical imperative is formally empty.** It cannot discriminate between genuine moral rules and absurd ones. MacIntyre's counterexamples:
   - "Always eat mussels on Mondays in March" — passes the universalizability test
   - "Persecute all those who hold false religious beliefs" — universalizable without logical contradiction
   - "Keep all your promises throughout your entire life except one" — trivially passes
   - "Let everyone except me be treated as a means" — undermines Kant's second formulation

2. **Kant's ethics is "parasitic" on prior morality.** Kant smuggles in the moral content of the Christian/classical tradition (promise-keeping, justice, truthfulness) and then claims to have deduced it from pure practical reason. The categorical imperative only *appears* to work because Kant already knows which maxims he wants to validate.

3. **Kant shares the same fatal flaw as all Enlightenment moralists.** All agreed on moral content (from shared Christian heritage). Each tried a different justification strategy — Hume (passions), Diderot (desire/nature), Kant (pure reason), Kierkegaard (radical choice). Each failed because the three-part teleological scheme had been gutted.

**The three-element scheme** (Aristotle → medieval tradition):
1. Human-nature-as-it-happens-to-be (untutored, raw)
2. Human-nature-as-it-could-be-if-it-realized-its-telos (the goal)
3. Ethics — precepts enabling transition from (1) to (2)

The Enlightenment removed element (2). Ethics became unintelligible — moral rules without the purpose that gave them meaning. Result: emotivism. People still use moral vocabulary (rights, duty, justice) but these are now rhetorical tools for expressing preferences. MacIntyre compares this to Captain Cook's Polynesian taboo encounter — prohibitions surviving without the framework that gave them meaning.

**→ Target**: Ch24 (The Species That Got Fire), near the Enlightenment's Gift and Gap section.

## 2. Goldstein *The Mattering Instinct* — PUBLISHED Jan 13, 2026

**Status**: Already published. Not forthcoming.
- **Full title**: *The Mattering Instinct: How Our Deepest Longing Drives Us and Divides Us*
- **Publisher**: Liveright / W.W. Norton, 352 pages
- **ISBN**: 9781324096856

**Core thesis**: The will to survive evolves, in sufficiently conscious creatures, into the will to matter. This "mattering instinct" is biological in origin (evolved, creaturely) but Goldstein refuses to reduce it to mere illusion. She argues mattering bridges the is-ought gap — determining that things matter IS saying we ought to pursue them. Humans are *homo justificans* — beings driven to justify themselves in their own eyes.

**The Mattering Map — 4 archetypes**:
1. **Transcenders** — seek mattering through spiritual/cosmic connection
2. **Socializers** — seek mattering from relationships and community
3. **Heroic Strivers** — seek mattering from internal standards of excellence
4. **Competitors** — seek mattering by mattering *more than others*

**Key for the book**: The naturalist-friendly bridge. A strict naturalist stops at "mattering is evolved and real." The book goes further — inherent value as axiom, not just useful fiction. Goldstein's position occupies the exact middle ground the Ch26 passage needs.

**Depression reframed**: Clinical depression = the pathological conviction that you don't and will never matter.

**Key interviews with transcripts/summaries**:

**Previous work**: The concept first appeared in her 1983 debut novel *The Mind-Body Problem* (the mattering map as fictional device). Developed through *Plato at the Googleplex* (2014). She has described "mattering theory" as an alternative to utilitarianism.

## 6. Deligny Fugitive Camps — Primary Sources

**Who**: Fernand Deligny (1913-1996), French educator/filmmaker. Worked with autistic children outside all institutional frameworks.

**The Cévennes network (1967-1996)**: In 1967, a mute autistic boy named **Janmari** was entrusted to Deligny. He established informal living areas (*aires de séjour*) near Monoblet in the Cévennes mountains. Caregivers were called *présences proches* (close presences) — peasants and non-specialized workers, explicitly NOT psychiatrists or psychoanalysts. Deligny called these "non-violent guerrillas" and the whole project a *tentative* (attempt) — never a program, treatment, or institution.

The aim: construct a living environment based on the positivity of a world-outside-language, not compensate for alleged deficiencies. Janmari lived there 35 years until his death in 2002.

**Lignes d'erre (wander lines)**: Starting 1969, close presences traced autistic children's movement patterns through space. Multiple tracings layered over years — continuous works in progress. Revealed that movements were not random but followed patterns (landmarks, water sources, pathways). Made visible a spatial intelligence existing entirely outside language. Knowledge WITH autistic bodies, not knowledge OF autism.

**Primary sources in English**:
1. **"The Arachnean and Other Texts"** (2015, Univocal/U of Minnesota Press) — translated by Drew S. Burk and Catherine Porter. 16 essays from the late 1970s. The most significant English-language Deligny collection. ISBN: 978-1937561109
2. **"Cartes et lignes d'erre / Maps and Wander Lines"** (2013, Éditions L'Arachnéen) — bilingual French/English, 416 pages. Nearly 200 maps from 1969-1979. ISBN: 978-2954105901
3. **"Œuvres"** (2007, Éditions L'Arachnéen) — 1,856-page collected works (French only), edited by Sandra Alvarez de Toledo. The publication that revived Deligny from obscurity.
4. Films: *Le Moindre Geste* (1971, Cannes Critics' Week), *Ce Gamin, là* (1976, co-produced by Truffaut)

**Connection to Deleuze/Guattari**: Deligny stayed at La Borde clinic (1966-67) under Guattari's direction but left — found psychoanalysis incompatible with his commitment to life outside language. His *lignes d'erre* directly influenced Deleuze/Guattari's *lignes de fuite* (lines of flight). The network concept influenced the rhizome. But Deligny was a practitioner, not a philosopher — his maps were tools for living, not theoretical propositions.

**Recent exhibitions**: "In Praise of Asylum" (Barcelona, 2023-24), "Approaching Unreason" (Palais de Tokyo, Paris, 2024).

**→ Target**: Ch39 (The Grammar We Need)

## 7. Four Mountains Cree Elder Story + CARE Principles

**The Four Mountains teaching** comes from **Cree elder John Crier**, documented through **Dr. Cash Ahenakew** (Cree, Ahtahkakoop Cree Nation), Canada Research Chair in Indigenous Peoples' Well-Being at UBC.

**The Four Mountains = four stages of life**:
1. **Baby Mountain** — beginning of existence
2. **Warrior Mountain** — developing strength and direction
3. **Hunter/Provider Mountain** — learning to sustain others
4. **Elder Mountain** (behind the first three) — helping others with their own rites of passage; developing hindsight, insight, and foresight

Key: Each mountain has rites of passage. The land is your teacher. To reach Elder Mountain (near Baby Mountain), you must go around all three and revisit transition points. A greater force decides the length of time and the visions received.

**Sources**:
- https://decolonialfutures.net/portfolio/fourmountains/

## 8. Kevin Hall Metabolism Research — Paper Citations

**Who**: Kevin D. Hall, Ph.D., Senior Investigator at NIDDK (NIH), Bethesda, Maryland. Studies integrative physiology of metabolism, body composition, energy expenditure, food intake control. Published *Food Intelligence* (2025, with Julia Belluz).

**Key papers with full citations**:

### The Biggest Loser Metabolic Adaptation Study
Fothergill E, Guo J, Howard L, Kerns JC, Knuth ND, Brychta R, Chen KY, Skarulis MC, Walter M, Walter PJ, Hall KD. "Persistent metabolic adaptation 6 years after 'The Biggest Loser' competition." *Obesity (Silver Spring)*. 2016;24(8):1612-1619. DOI: 10.1002/oby.21538

**Findings**: 14 of 16 Season 8 contestants studied. At competition end: lost avg 58.3 kg, RMR dropped 610 kcal/day. Six years later: regained avg 41.0 kg, RMR now 704 kcal/day below baseline (worse than at show's end). Metabolic adaptation averaged -499 kcal/day — bodies burned ~500 fewer calories/day than expected for their size. Adaptation did NOT resolve with weight regain.

### Biggest Loser Reinterpretation
Hall KD. "Energy compensation and metabolic adaptation: 'The Biggest Loser' study reinterpreted." *Obesity (Silver Spring)*. 2022;30(1):11-13. DOI: 10.1002/oby.23308

**Findings**: Those who maintained greater weight loss also experienced greater concurrent slowing — suggesting adaptation may be proportional to ongoing weight-suppression effort rather than permanent "broken metabolism."

### Ultra-Processed Food Randomized Controlled Trial
Hall KD, Ayuketah A, Brychta R, et al. "Ultra-Processed Diets Cause Excess Calorie Intake and Weight Gain: An Inpatient Randomized Controlled Trial of Ad Libitum Food Intake." *Cell Metabolism*. 2019;30(1):67-77.e3. DOI: 10.1016/j.cmet.2019.05.008

**Findings**: People spontaneously ate ~500 extra kcal/day on ultra-processed vs unprocessed diets, even when matched for calories, macros, sugar, sodium, and fiber. A second trial found the gap was ~1,000 kcal/day at midpoint.

### Low-Fat vs. Low-Carb (Plant-Based vs. Keto) Trial
Hall KD, Guo J, Courville AB, et al. "Effect of a plant-based, low-fat diet versus an animal-based, ketogenic diet on ad libitum energy intake." *Nature Medicine*. 2021;27(2):344-353. DOI: 10.1038/s41591-020-01209-1

**Findings**: Plant-based low-fat → lower calorie intake. Animal-based keto → lower insulin/glucose. Predictions of the carbohydrate-insulin model were inconsistent with observations. Neither approach is metabolically superior in the way advocates claim.

### Mathematical Modeling
Hall KD. "Predicting metabolic adaptation, body weight change, and energy intake in humans." *Am J Physiol Endocrinol Metab*. 2010;298(3):E449-E466. DOI: 10.1152/ajpendo.00559.2009

**Main conclusions**:
- Metabolic adaptation is real and persistent but does not fully explain weight regain
- Ultra-processed foods drive overeating independent of macronutrient composition
- The food environment matters more than individual biology — changing environments produced 500-700 kcal/day differences, far larger than individual metabolic variation
- The obesity epidemic is driven by the food supply, not individual metabolic defects
