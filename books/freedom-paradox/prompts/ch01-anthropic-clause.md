# Research Session: The Anthropic Clause (Chapter 1)

## Context
This is research for Chapter 1 of "The Freedom Paradox," a book about open source confronting AI risk. This chapter opens the book with the February 2026 confrontation between Anthropic and the US Department of Defense.

## What I Need

### The Incident
- Full timeline of the Anthropic/DoD clash in February 2026
- Exact terms Anthropic refused to remove (mass domestic surveillance, fully autonomous weapons)
- Pete Hegseth's role and specific demands
- The "supply-chain risk" designation — what it means legally and practically
- Which US agencies were ordered to stop using Claude
- Anthropic's public response

### Dario Amodei's Position
- His January 2026 essay "The Adolescence of Technology" — the five categories of AI risk
- His 60 Minutes interview (November 2025): "I'm deeply uncomfortable with these decisions being made by a few companies"
- His Dwarkesh Podcast appearance — the admission about balancing safety and commercial pressure
- His NYT op-ed criticizing the 10-year AI regulation moratorium

### The Counterfactual
- If Claude's weights were open source (like Llama), could the DoD simply run it themselves?
- Technical feasibility of stripping safety guardrails from an open-weight model
- Examples of open models being fine-tuned to remove safety measures
- Dwarkesh Patel's specific argument about open source making ethics debates moot

### The Broader Context
- Trump administration's stance on AI regulation in 2025-2026
- Yann LeCun's criticism that Anthropic's safety warnings are designed to limit open-source models
- The tension between national security hawks (who want AI access) and safety advocates (who want constraints)
- Other cases where AI companies have been pressured by governments
