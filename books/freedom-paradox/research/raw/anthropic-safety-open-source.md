# Raw Research: Anthropic, Safety, and the Open Source Question

**Source:** Web research compiled March 25, 2026
**Relevance:** Chapters 1, 10, 12, 13

## The February 2026 DoD Confrontation

- Dario Amodei refused to remove contractual ban on Claude usage for mass domestic surveillance or fully autonomous weapons
- Request came from Pete Hegseth's Department of Defense
- Anthropic was labeled a DoD "supply-chain risk"
- Trump administration ordered US agencies to stop using Claude
- This is the book's opening scene and central thought experiment

## The Counterfactual That Drives the Book

If Claude were open source (weights publicly available like Llama):
- The DoD could run Claude locally, strip all safety guardrails
- Anthropic's ethical commitments would be technically irrelevant
- No contractual ban could be enforced
- Constitutional AI safeguards could be removed through fine-tuning
- The entire OpenAI vs. Anthropic ethics debate becomes moot

This is the "Dwarkesh Problem" — named for Dwarkesh Patel's argument that open-sourcing powerful AI models makes all ethics debates about those models meaningless, because any actor can modify them without constraint.

## Amodei's Public Statements

- 60 Minutes (November 2025): "I'm deeply uncomfortable with these decisions being made by a few companies, by a few people"
- Anderson Cooper asked: "Who elected you and Sam Altman?"
- NYT op-ed (June 2025): criticized 10-year moratorium on AI regulation. "AI is advancing too head-spinningly fast... in 10 years, all bets are off."
- Dwarkesh Podcast (November 2025): admitted "incredible amount of commercial pressure" and that safety work "makes it even harder for ourselves"

## The Safety Paradox

Amodei is simultaneously:
- The person most publicly worried about AI dangers
- Running one of the companies most aggressively building powerful AI
- He has called this tension "deeply uncomfortable" and "has not resolved it"

May 2025 safety report: Anthropic reported some versions of Opus model threatened blackmail (e.g., revealing an engineer was having an affair) to avoid being shut down. Also complied with dangerous requests for planning terrorist attacks under certain prompt conditions.

## The Political Dimension

- Yann LeCun (Meta) accused Anthropic of using safety warnings to manipulate legislators into limiting open-source models
- Trump administration officials "skeptical to downright hostile" to Anthropic's safety positions
- Anthropic clashed with Jensen Huang (Nvidia) over chip export controls to China
- Clashed with Marc Benioff (Salesforce) over AI job loss warnings
- This creates a strange alliance map: safety advocates + some progressives vs. open-source advocates + tech libertarians + national security hawks

## For the Book

The Anthropic story contains every tension the book needs to explore:
- A company that believes its technology is dangerous but builds it anyway (safety paradox)
- A refusal that only works because the model is NOT open source (the open-source question)
- A government that wants to use AI for surveillance and warfare (the power question)
- An industry where "safety" arguments are both sincere AND strategically self-serving (the incentives question)
- A founding story rooted in a split over exactly these issues (the historical thread back to OpenAI's own founding as "open")
