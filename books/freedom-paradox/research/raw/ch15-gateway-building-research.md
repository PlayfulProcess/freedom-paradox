## Ghost CMS

### Founding & Structure
- Founded by John O'Nolan, 2013
- Kickstarter campaign: £25K goal, raised £196,362 from 5,236 backers in 29-day campaign (funded in 11 hours)
- Notable backers: Seth Godin, Leo Babauta, Darren Rowse, Microsoft, Envato
- First public release (codename "Kerouac"): September 19, 2013
- Ghost Foundation: Singapore-based nonprofit
- MIT license — fully open source
- ~35 staff as of 2025

### Revenue & Customers
- $10.4M revenue in 2024, ~25% YOY growth
- 20.4K customers (2024 figure via Latka)
- Ghost Pro: ~18K active licensees
- 0% transaction fees — connects directly to creator's own Stripe account
- Compare: Substack takes 10% of earnings + Stripe processing fees
- Example: $60K ARR publication pays $348/year hosting on Ghost vs $6,000/year to Substack in platform fees

### ActivityPub & Federation
- Ghost 6.0 shipped August 2025 with ActivityPub support
- Publishers can connect with Mastodon, Threads, Flipboard, WordPress, WriteFreely
- Federation means Ghost publications become actors on the open social web
- Still labeled beta but functional
- Source: TechCrunch (Aug 5, 2025), We Distribute

### Key Structural Feature
- Nonprofit structure makes it structurally impossible to sell to a venture-backed acquirer or pivot to extractive model
- No investors to appease, no exit to pursue
- O'Nolan's February 2026 essay questions whether open-source licenses still matter in the AI age — but Ghost's structural protections (nonprofit + community) go beyond the license

## Wikipedia / Wikimedia Foundation

### Scale
- ~250,000 active volunteer editors
- Articles in 300+ languages
- English Wikipedia: 6.8M+ articles [VERIFY: current count]
- CC BY-SA license for content; MediaWiki software is GPL
- Top 10 most-visited website globally

### Wikimedia Foundation Budget 2025-2026
- Revenue goal: $207.5M (up ~6% YOY)
- Budget allocation: 47% infrastructure, 29% volunteer support, 24% effectiveness
- 417 grants dispersed in FY 24-25 totaling $18.2M to volunteers, affiliates, partners
- Challenge: continued decline in new and returning editors

### Governance
- Wikimedia Foundation is a 501(c)(3) nonprofit
- Community governance through consensus, barnstars, adminship
- Board of Trustees with community-elected seats
- No advertising, donation-funded
- Purest example of commons-based peer production (Benkler's term)

## Creative Commons

### Founding
- Founded 2001 by Lawrence Lessig (Stanford Law)
- First licenses released December 2002
- Inspired by free software licenses but for creative works
- Suite of 6 licenses: CC BY, CC BY-SA, CC BY-NC, CC BY-NC-SA, CC BY-ND, CC BY-NC-ND
- Plus CC0 (public domain dedication)

### Scale
- Over 2 billion works licensed with CC licenses as of 2021 (20th anniversary figure)
- Flickr: 415M+ CC-licensed photos
- YouTube: 49M+ CC-licensed works
- Wikimedia Commons: 37M+ works
- DeviantArt: 40M+ works

### 2025-2026 Developments
- Focus shifting to AI and data governance — CC-licensed works are in AI training sets
- 2026 focus: ensuring technological change strengthens the commons
- Ongoing debate about whether CC licenses adequately address AI training use

## Open Hardware

### RISC-V
- Open instruction set architecture (ISA), not a specific chip
- 1 billion cores shipped in 2024 alone
- 25% market penetration achieved late 2025
- Projected: 33.7% by 2031
- Major moves: Qualcomm acquired Ventana Micro ($2.4B), Meta acquired Rivos
- 2025: RISC-V laptops arrived (IEEE Spectrum)
- Used in automotive, data center, edge AI, security, space
- BSD license for the ISA specification

### Arduino
- Open-source electronics platform (hardware + software)
- Founded 2005 in Ivrea, Italy
- Millions of boards sold worldwide
- Now incorporating RISC-V chips
- CC BY-SA for hardware designs, LGPL for software

### Open Compute Project
- Founded by Facebook (Meta) 2011
- Open-source data center hardware designs
- Saved Facebook/Meta $1.2B+ in data center costs
- 400+ member companies (Meta, Google, Microsoft, Intel, NVIDIA, Goldman Sachs, etc.)
- 2025: Unveiled Open Rack Wide (ORW) specs for AI infrastructure
- OCP Ethernet for Scale-Up Networking (ESUN) initiative launched

## Platform Cooperativism

### Stocksy United
- Stock photography platform, collectively owned by ~1,000 photographers
- Founded by former iStockphoto owners (sold to Getty Images 2006, regretted it)
- 90% of profits distributed to artists
- $10.7M annual revenue (2016 figure) [VERIFY: more recent revenue]
- Based in Victoria, British Columbia

### Up & Go
- Worker-owned cleaning cooperative platform in New York City
- Workers accept jobs, set their own pay rates
- Full-time worker and partial owner of their coop
- Benefits included
- Alternative to gig economy platforms like Handy, TaskRabbit

### Other Examples
- CoopCycle: cooperative food delivery platform
- Drivers Cooperative: ride-hail cooperative in NYC
- Platform Cooperativism Consortium: founded 2014, academic/advocacy network

## Key Patterns Across All Projects

1. **Structural protection**: Nonprofit status (Ghost, Wikipedia, Wikimedia), copyleft/share-alike licenses (Wikipedia CC BY-SA, MediaWiki GPL), cooperative ownership (Stocksy)
2. **Community governance**: Wikipedia's consensus model, Ghost's nonprofit board, cooperative member voting
3. **The thing being opened is generative**: Publishing tools, knowledge, creative works, hardware designs — not weapons or surveillance tools
4. **Scale trade-off**: These projects work but at smaller scale than corporate counterparts. Ghost ($10.4M) vs Substack ($100M+ ARR). Stocksy ($10.7M) vs Getty Images ($1B+). Wikipedia is the exception — the only commons project that achieved true planetary scale.
5. **Sustainability challenge**: Wikipedia faces editor decline. The Chosen relies on <5% paying viewers. Patreon creators see declining numbers. The commons requires ongoing commitment.
