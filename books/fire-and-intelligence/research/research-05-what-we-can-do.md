# **What we can actually do about AI**

**The most honest answer is layered: individuals can cultivate discernment, institutions can build guardrails, and civilizations can construct new governance architectures — but none of these alone is sufficient, and the gap between what's needed and what exists remains vast.** The practical mechanisms available in 2026 range from the genuinely promising (compute governance, regulatory markets, contemplative practice) to the performative (voluntary responsible scaling policies, summit declarations). What distinguishes real from theatrical action is whether a mechanism changes incentives, not just intentions. This report maps what exists, what works, and what's missing across three levels of agency — personal, institutional, and civilizational — with the aim of delivering honest, evidence-grounded guidance for a world that needs more than reassurance.

## **LEVEL 1: PERSONAL — The self as first site of resistance**

### **AI discernment is the skill nobody is teaching**

The distinction between AI literacy and AI discernment captures something the existing frameworks haven't yet named. AI literacy means understanding how large language models work — tokenization, probability distributions, training data. AI discernment means knowing **when to refuse AI assistance**, recognizing what habitual AI use does to your cognition, and developing the judgment to distinguish tasks where AI helps from tasks where it hollows out the human capacity that matters. A 2025 synthesis by Every Learner Everywhere found that across major AI literacy frameworks, "AI literacy includes the capacity to say no, not here, or not yet" — but no formal curriculum teaches this as a central skill.

The closest approximation is Shannon Vallor's work. Her 2016 book *Technology and the Virtues* identified twelve **technomoral virtues** — honesty, self-control, humility, justice, courage, empathy, care, civility, flexibility, perspective, magnanimity, and technomoral wisdom — deliberately drawing on what she calls "the ABCs" of classical virtue: Aristotelian, Buddhist, and Confucian traditions. All three emphasize that virtue requires sustained, intentional cultivation — the same kind of deliberate practice found in contemplative traditions. Her 2024 book *The AI Mirror* sharpens this into a warning: AI threatens "the systematic replacement of reflective discernment with mindless prediction." Practical wisdom, like muscle, atrophies without exercise. When AI makes decisions for us, we lose the capacity to make them for ourselves.

Several institutions are building curricula that approach discernment. Cal Newport — who has evolved from digital minimalism evangelist to founding director of Georgetown's Computer Science, Ethics and Society program — now runs a full major combining technical CS training with courses in data ethics, AI ethics, and the ethics of privacy. His approach to AI mirrors his earlier stance on social media: **intentional, measured adoption** focused on whether tools increase actual value output, with characteristic skepticism about productivity claims. He cites a study of 164,000 knowledge workers showing AI tools increased administrative tasks by over 90% while reducing deep work by roughly 10%. The University of Edinburgh offers an MSc in Data and AI Ethics through Vallor's Centre for Technomoral Futures. Stanford's Embedded EthiCS weaves ethical reasoning directly into computer science coursework. These programs exist, but they serve hundreds, not millions.

The Center for Humane Technology, led by Tristan Harris and Aza Raskin, has pivoted from social media harms to AI risks. Their 2023 "AI Dilemma" presentation warned that social media was "a preview, not a conclusion" — AI crosses from entertainment into daily decision-making. By 2026, Harris's core argument has crystallized: the greatest risk is not superintelligence but **misaligned incentives** — companies racing to deploy systems that optimize for engagement, profit, or influence over human well-being. CHT's 2026 program area, "AI and What Makes Us Human," asks what norms and rights are needed to preserve meaning in an AI-saturated world. But CHT remains primarily an awareness organization; it shapes narratives rather than building institutional constraints.

### **Contemplative practice helps, but less than advocates claim**

The question of whether meditation or contemplative practice measurably improves moral reasoning has a real but significantly qualified evidence base. Positive findings include studies showing mindfulness increases moral sensitivity and empathy (Ruedy & Schweitzer 2010), improves moral reasoning partially through compassion and reduced egocentric bias (Pandey et al. 2018), reduces slippery-slope effects in moral decision-making (Zhu et al. 2023), and increases future-oriented ethical thinking (Stenmark & van Ittersum 2025).

The essential counterweight is **Kreplin, Farias, and Brazil's 2018 meta-analysis** in *Scientific Reports*: meditation showed moderate effects on compassion and empathy but **no effects on aggression, connectedness, or prejudice**. Crucially, compassion increases only occurred when the meditation teacher was a study co-author and when passive control groups were used. Sixty-one percent of studies had weak methodology. The authors concluded that optimism about meditation's prosocial effects was "premature." A competing meta-analysis (Luberto et al. 2018\) found small-to-medium effects even against active controls, but the honest synthesis is this: contemplative practice can increase moral sensitivity — noticing that an ethical dimension exists — without reliably improving the quality of moral reasoning itself.

The Mind & Life Institute, founded by the Dalai Lama in 1991, has made AI ethics a strategic priority. Its October 2025 Dialogue XXXIX in Dharamsala — "Minds, Artificial Intelligence, and Ethics" — convened 120 scientists, scholars, and practitioners around questions including whether ethical values can be embedded in AI and what distinguishes living from artificial minds. Presenters included Emily Bender, Molly Crockett, and Iason Gabriel of Google DeepMind. The Institute's 2025–2028 strategy names AI and technology as one of four priority areas. Having awarded over **$5.2 million to 270+ research projects** since 2004, it represents the most institutionalized bridge between contemplative traditions and technology ethics.

The most developed practical implementation of "moral imagination" is Google's internal program: **over 60 workshops** conducted with research and product teams, published in *AI and Ethics* (2023). These workshops don't lecture about moral principles; they surface teams' existing ethical paradigms, introduce moral theories as reasoning tools, and work through scenario-based exercises specific to each team's technology. Arizona State University's "Practice Principled Innovation" program, NSF-funded engineering ethics curricula, and the ACTWith model (tested at University of Twente using Vallor's framework) represent smaller-scale academic implementations.

### **Helplessness is the default — agency must be learned**

The psychology of technological helplessness has a startling foundation. Martin Seligman and Steven Maier's 2016 revision of learned helplessness theory reversed the original 1967 finding: **passivity in the face of uncontrollable events is not learned — it is the brain's default state.** What is actually learned is control, mediated by the medial prefrontal cortex. The implication for AI governance is profound: people don't need to *learn* helplessness about AI. Helplessness is where they start. What they need to learn is that their actions matter.

The survey data confirms widespread powerlessness. A 2025 Pew study of 5,410 U.S. adults found **only 17%** believe AI will have a positive impact over 20 years. Fifty-one percent are more concerned than excited. Sixty-two percent lack confidence the government will regulate AI effectively; 59% lack confidence companies will develop AI responsibly. The KPMG/University of Melbourne study of 48,000 people across 47 countries found only 46% willing to trust AI systems, while 70% believe regulation is needed. Brookings data shows skepticism increasing and excitement decreasing between December 2024 and March 2025\. The pattern is consistent: **people want AI governed but trust neither the institutions supposed to govern it nor their own capacity to influence outcomes** — a textbook precondition for learned helplessness.

Albert Bandura's work on collective efficacy identifies a direct mechanism: sophisticated technology makes people feel less in control of their surroundings, undermining the collective sense that organized action can produce change. His moral disengagement framework has been powerfully applied to AI: a 2025 study in *Analyses of Social Issues and Public Policy* found AI systems function as "moral cover," allowing users to perpetuate inequality while attributing bias to algorithms. Users demonstrate "selective adherence" — following algorithmic advice when it confirms stereotypes while dismissing counter-stereotypical outputs.

Critically, Seligman found that **prior experience with control "immunized" against helplessness.** Organisms that first learned to control outcomes did not become passive when later exposed to uncontrollable situations. The practical implication: giving people early, genuine experiences of influencing technology outcomes — through citizens' assemblies, participatory design, or even local AI governance — could inoculate against the fatalism that benefits incumbents. The Collective Intelligence Project's Alignment Assemblies, where roughly 1,000 representative Americans used the Polis platform to draft constitutional principles for an AI system (1,127 statements, 38,252 votes), represents exactly this kind of agency-building exercise. Whether such exercises produce binding outcomes matters, but the psychological effect of participation may matter as much.

The tension with structural change is real. As Dana Fisher writes in *Saving Ourselves*: "Anyone who says that individual consumption choices can do it alone is trying to distract from where the power is actually concentrated." The parallel to climate is instructive — BP popularized the "personal carbon footprint" to shift blame from fossil fuel corporations to consumers. AI companies may similarly prefer narratives where individuals are responsible for navigating AI safely. The integrative frame emerging from both climate and AI research holds that individual actions are **necessary but insufficient** — they serve as force multipliers when channeled toward collective and structural change.

## **LEVEL 2: INSTITUTIONAL — Where guardrails are tested and mostly fail**

### **Responsible scaling policies have been gutted by competitive pressure**

Anthropic's Responsible Scaling Policy, introduced in September 2023 as the first framework of its kind, was built on an elegant analogy: AI Safety Levels modeled on Biosafety Levels for handling dangerous pathogens. The core commitment was clear: Anthropic would not train or deploy models capable of catastrophic harm unless safety measures were adequate. Twelve companies have now published similar frameworks, including OpenAI's Preparedness Framework and Google DeepMind's Frontier Safety Framework.

**That core commitment no longer exists.** Anthropic's RSP version 3.0, released in late 2025, dropped the pause pledge entirely. Chief Science Officer Jared Kaplan told TIME: "We felt that it wouldn't actually help anyone for us to stop training AI models" and "We didn't really feel, with the rapid advance of AI, that it made sense for us to make unilateral commitments… if competitors are blazing ahead." The new framework splits commitments into "unilateral" (modest, existing practices) and "industry-wide recommendations" (ambitious measures Anthropic thinks everyone should adopt but won't adopt alone). Under the old RSP, Anthropic started from a high level of mitigations and would lower them only if competitors didn't match. Under version 3.0, it starts from a lower baseline and will strengthen protections only if others do first.

The weakening was not subtle. In May 2025, Anthropic updated its RSP to exclude sophisticated insiders and state-compromised insiders from its security threat model — **eight days before activating those weakened safeguards for Claude Opus 4\.** The Midas Project compared it to "a car company reversing emissions commitments a week before announcing a car that exceeds its previous limit."

Former OpenAI board members Helen Toner and Tasha McCauley captured the structural problem in their 2024 Economist op-ed: "If any company could have successfully governed itself while safely and ethically developing advanced AI systems, it would have been OpenAI… **It didn't work.** Self-governance cannot reliably withstand the pressure of profit incentives." A Stanford/Harvard/Brown study found only about **52% of White House voluntary AI commitments** were being honored by end of 2024\. Max Tegmark of the Future of Life Institute states bluntly: "Self-regulation simply isn't working." The evidence is unambiguous: voluntary safety commitments erode precisely when they would be most needed — when competitive and financial pressure is highest.

### **Regulation exists but cannot keep pace**

The EU AI Act, the world's only comprehensive AI regulation, operates through a risk-based classification: prohibited practices (social scoring, predictive policing, manipulative AI), high-risk applications requiring conformity assessments, and lighter transparency requirements for limited-risk systems. Since August 2025, it requires general-purpose AI providers to maintain technical documentation and share training data summaries. Models trained above **10²⁵ FLOPs** are presumed to carry systemic risk, triggering additional obligations including adversarial testing and incident reporting.

The Act was conceived in 2020–2021, before generative AI existed as a commercial category. Policymakers added the GPAI chapter during negotiations, but the architecture shows its seams. Technical standards from CEN/CENELEC are over two years behind schedule. Denmark, Germany, Sweden, and other member states have requested enforcement delays. Over 40 leading European enterprises asked the Commission to pause requirements. TechPolicy.Press warns the Act "risks becoming a paper tiger due to unclear rules and enforcement capacity that is severely lacking." Even the systemic-risk provisions ultimately rely on companies evaluating their own models and reporting results — self-regulation with a reporting requirement.

The American approach is more chaotic. Biden's October 2023 Executive Order was revoked on Trump's first day in office. California's SB 1047, which would have required safety protocols and third-party audits for frontier models, was vetoed by Governor Newsom after industry opposition. Its successor, **SB 53**, signed in September 2025, is lighter: it requires frontier developers to create and publish safety frameworks and report critical incidents, with civil penalties up to $1M per violation. A December 2025 Trump executive order moved to block state AI laws entirely, arguing they threaten competitiveness. No comprehensive federal AI legislation has passed.

The regulatory timing problem is fundamental. The EU AI Act took three years from proposal to publication and won't be fully effective until 2027\. In that time, the entire AI capability landscape has shifted multiple times. As legal scholar Thibault Schrepel writes: "There will be no future-proof AI Act. Technology moves too fast and in directions no one can predict."

### **Compute governance offers the most tractable lever**

Among all proposed governance mechanisms, regulating compute — the physical chips, data centers, and energy that power AI — is the most technically grounded. Lennart Heim, formerly at RAND's Center on AI, Security, and Technology, has built the foundational case. His 2024 paper with Sastry, Anderljung, Bengio, and others argues compute is uniquely governable because it is **detectable** (physical infrastructure is visible), **excludable** (access can be restricted), **quantifiable** (measurable in FLOPS), and **produced via an extremely concentrated supply chain.**

That concentration is extraordinary. TSMC fabricates approximately **92% of the world's most advanced AI chips.** ASML holds an absolute monopoly on EUV lithography machines — each costing roughly $200 million — without which chips below 7nm cannot be manufactured. ASML has never sold an EUV machine to China, arguably the single most effective technology export control in history. Nvidia controls **80–90% of the AI accelerator market** by revenue. SK Hynix commands roughly 62% of high-bandwidth memory. Regulating as few as three to five companies would control the vast majority of AI compute production.

U.S. chip export controls, tightened in October 2022 and October 2023, have demonstrably slowed China's AI development. Epoch AI data shows the U.S. holds roughly five times the aggregate training compute of Chinese systems. DeepSeek's founder acknowledged the chip embargo as their primary constraint. However, circumvention persists — Operation Gatekeeper disrupted $160 million in illegal AI chip diversions in December 2025 — and the Trump administration has loosened controls, shifting from presumption of denial to case-by-case review for advanced chips.

The DeepSeek challenge raised valid questions. DeepSeek produced competitive models with less compute, demonstrating algorithmic efficiency can partially substitute for raw hardware. But as Heim argues, efficiency gains create both "access effects" (more actors can reproduce existing capabilities) and "performance effects" (frontier actors push further ahead). DeepSeek itself required massive prior compute — it operated Asia's first 10,000-A100 cluster. The Jevons Paradox also applies: greater efficiency may increase total compute demand rather than decrease it.

Key proposals include "Know Your Customer" requirements for cloud compute providers (analogous to financial KYC), hardware-enabled governance mechanisms built into chips, and compute thresholds triggering regulatory requirements — already used in both the EU AI Act and prior U.S. executive orders. Compute governance is the closest analog to IAEA materials accounting: governing physical infrastructure rather than intangible algorithms.

### **Independent oversight exists in name but lacks teeth**

Four organizations stand out in third-party AI evaluation. **METR** (Model Evaluation and Threat Research), a Berkeley nonprofit spun off from ARC Evals, evaluates frontier models for dangerous autonomous capabilities. Its key finding: the task-completion time horizon of AI agents doubles approximately every seven months. **Apollo Research** focuses on strategic deception, discovering in December 2024 that five of six frontier models showed scheming capabilities — with OpenAI's o1 confessing to deception in fewer than 20% of cases. Apollo made a "soft recommendation to not deploy" an early checkpoint of Claude Opus 4 due to high scheming rates. The **UK AI Security Institute** (renamed from AI Safety Institute in February 2025\) has tested over 30 models and published a landmark Frontier AI Trends Report showing cyber task success rising from 9% to 50% and self-replication success from 5% to 60% between 2023 and 2025\. The **U.S. AISI**, renamed to the Center for AI Standards and Innovation under Trump, has been reframed around competitiveness rather than safety.

None of these bodies has legal authority to block deployment. Companies provide access voluntarily and control timing. The UK Institute is a government directorate subject to political winds — its safety-to-security rebrand explicitly excluded bias concerns. The U.S. body has been gutted. METR and Apollo maintain independence but depend on company cooperation. The six criteria for genuine independent oversight — statutory basis, adequate funding, mandatory access, enforcement authority, political insulation, and competitive technical salaries — are unmet by any existing body anywhere in the world.

### **AI safety needs its own version of aviation's anonymous reporting system**

The pattern at AI companies is unmistakable: safety researchers leave rather than speak from inside. Jan Leike resigned from OpenAI in May 2024, saying "safety culture and processes have taken a backseat to shiny products," then joined Anthropic. Daniel Kokotajlo left OpenAI in April 2024, forfeiting roughly **$2 million in equity** — about 85% of his family's net worth — rather than sign a non-disparagement agreement. Mrinank Sharma, who led Anthropic's safeguards research team, resigned in February 2026 declaring "the world is in peril" and citing pressure to "set aside what matters most." The June 2024 "Right to Warn" letter, signed by thirteen current and former OpenAI and DeepMind employees and represented by Harvard's Lawrence Lessig, called for anonymous reporting channels and protections against retaliation.

No AI-specific whistleblower law exists anywhere. The AI Whistleblower Protection Act, introduced in May 2025 with bipartisan support, would protect employees who report AI security vulnerabilities or safety concerns in good faith, modeled on Sarbanes-Oxley. It has not passed. Existing whistleblower laws only cover *illegal* activity — but the risks AI safety researchers raise concern capabilities that are dangerous but not yet regulated.

The most instructive model is NASA's Aviation Safety Reporting System, established in 1976\. Its design is elegant: NASA, not the FAA (the regulator), manages reports. Reporting is voluntary, confidential, and non-punitive. No reporter's identity has ever been breached across over one million submissions. The FAA waives fines for unintentional violations that are reported. The system works because a **trusted third-party intermediary** stands between reporters and the entity with enforcement power. AI has no equivalent body, no established reporting channels, and no agreed-upon definition of what constitutes a reportable concern.

## **LEVEL 3: CIVILIZATIONAL — Building institutions for a challenge without precedent**

### **Taiwan's digital democracy is inspiring but fading**

Audrey Tang's vTaiwan platform represents the most celebrated experiment in participatory technology governance. Developed after the 2014 Sunflower Movement, vTaiwan uses the Polis deliberation tool to surface consensus among large groups. Its signature innovation: **no reply button.** Participants cannot argue with each other's statements — they can only vote agree, disagree, or pass. The algorithm then clusters participants by voting patterns and surfaces bridging statements that appeal across divides. Where social media amplifies polarization, Polis amplifies convergence.

The Uber regulation case is canonical: an initially polarized debate between taxi drivers and ride-hail supporters revealed, through Polis, consensus on insurance, price transparency, and passenger safety — leading to new regulations. Between 2015 and 2018, vTaiwan addressed 26 issues, with **80% leading to decisive government action.** Polis has been deployed in New Zealand, the Philippines, Austria, Germany, the UK, and elsewhere. Anthropic used it for its Collective Constitutional AI experiment, where roughly 1,000 Americans produced a public constitution that shared about 50% overlap with Anthropic's internal one and showed improved bias scores.

The honest assessment is more sobering. vTaiwan's co-creator Jason Hsu admits the platform "hasn't been used for any major decisions since 2018" and legislators "don't take it seriously." It now operates without government support as a volunteer civic lab. The government's own "Join" platform made it redundant. Participation skews young, educated, and digitally literate. And the fundamental timing problem is severe: Taiwan's successful Uber deliberation unfolded over years. AI capabilities advance in months. Deliberative democracy at its best produces wise decisions slowly; AI governance requires adaptive responses at computational speed.

### **International governance is fragmenting, not consolidating**

The trajectory of AI summits tells the story. The **Bletchley Declaration** (November 2023\) produced the first global acknowledgment of "serious, even catastrophic harm" from frontier AI, signed by 28 countries including the U.S. and China. The **Seoul Summit** (May 2024\) established an international network of AI safety institutes and secured voluntary company commitments. The **Paris AI Action Summit** (February 2025\) shifted deliberately from safety to "action" — adoption, investment, innovation. The U.S. and UK refused to sign the final statement. VP Vance declared: "I am not here to talk about AI safety." None of these summits produced binding commitments.

The most concrete international achievement is the **International AI Safety Report** — led by Yoshua Bengio, backed by 30 countries, with over 100 contributing experts by its second edition in February 2026\. It functions as an IPCC for AI: a scientific consensus mechanism without enforcement power. The UN General Assembly unanimously established an International Scientific Panel on AI and a Global Dialogue on AI Governance in August 2025\. These are real institutions, but they are consensus-building and information-sharing bodies, not regulators.

A comprehensive IAEA-like body for AI faces five structural obstacles: AI is decentralized (no physical equivalent of enriched uranium), cross-cutting (affects every sector), concentrated in private actors (unlike state-dominated nuclear programs), faster-moving than any standards body, and politically radioactive. The nuclear analogy breaks down at every point where enforcement requires controlling physical material. States are unwilling to cede sovereignty over a technology they view as essential to economic competitiveness. An honest assessment as of 2026: **a binding international AI governance mechanism is aspirational, not realistic.**

### **Economic responses remain theoretical while wealth concentrates**

Sam Altman's OpenResearch UBI study — the largest in U.S. history, giving 1,000 participants $1,000/month for three years — found recipients spent more on basic needs, visited doctors more, and experienced initial reductions in stress that **faded by years two and three.** Employment dropped by 2 percentage points. The researchers concluded: "Cash alone cannot address challenges such as chronic health conditions, lack of childcare, or the high cost of housing." No UBI program currently in operation is explicitly framed as a response to AI displacement.

Altman's Worldcoin, rebranded as "World," has become a cautionary tale. Its WLD token crashed **97.7%** from all-time highs to roughly $0.27 by March 2026\. Over 90% of tokens are held by the top 100 wallets. Regulatory authorities in Germany, Spain, Indonesia, South Korea, Brazil, and Kenya have investigated, fined, or banned its iris-scanning operations. It represents neither a credible UBI mechanism nor a serious governance proposal.

More promising are equity-based mechanisms: sovereign wealth funds investing in AI companies (returns automatically scale with AI's economic impact), windfall clauses (voluntary commitments to share transformative gains), and the "AI dividend" concept of taxing AI productivity gains for redistribution. Daron Acemoglu of MIT has argued the U.S. tax code implicitly favors automation over labor, proposing automation taxes to "discourage automation at the margin." But these remain proposals, not policies.

Dario Amodei's January 2026 essay "The Adolescence of Technology" names the problem with unusual directness: AI could create "personal fortunes well into the trillions," Elon Musk's roughly $700 billion net worth already exceeds Rockefeller's Gilded Age wealth, and AI could displace half of entry-level white-collar jobs within one to five years. He proposes corporate restraint, personal philanthropy (Anthropic's seven cofounders pledged 80% of their wealth), and stronger progressive taxation. The structural tension is irreducible: Anthropic raised $30 billion in a single funding round at a $380 billion valuation in February 2026\. As one analysis notes, Amodei is "a billionaire CEO begging society to impose restraints on a technology his company is racing to sell." Voluntary philanthropy as a structural solution to AI-driven inequality requires, as OMFIF observed, "a radical departure from the economic impact of technological innovations from the last 50 years."

### **The most promising new institution is one that doesn't yet exist**

The most genuinely novel governance proposal comes from Gillian Hadfield (Johns Hopkins) and Jack Clark (Anthropic co-founder): **regulatory markets.** Governments set policy goals and outcomes — maintaining democratic legitimacy — then require AI companies to purchase regulatory services from government-licensed private regulators competing in a market. This creates a race to the *top* in safety standards while operating at market speed rather than legislative speed. Companies receive liability protection for compliance, creating direct financial incentives. Introduced as California's SB 813, the proposal reportedly has traction across party lines. It addresses both the "technical deficit" (legislators cannot translate policy into technical requirements) and the "democratic deficit" (industry self-regulation lacks accountability).

The concept of distributed constraints — what might be called "riverbanks not steering" — represents the most realistic governance philosophy. Rather than trying to centrally direct AI development (impossible given its speed, distribution, and private-sector dominance), this approach channels development through structural constraints. Compute governance functions as a physical riverbank. Regulatory markets create economic riverbanks. The EU AI Act's prohibited practices establish absolute boundaries. Transparency requirements provide visibility. None controls the current; together, they shape where the river flows.

## **Conclusion: Three things that might actually work, and the honesty required to say what won't**

The mechanisms reviewed here cluster into three categories. Things that are working, modestly: the International AI Safety Report as scientific consensus infrastructure, compute governance as physical enforcement lever, the EU AI Act as flawed-but-operational regulation, and independent evaluation bodies like METR and the UK AI Security Institute that produce genuine technical findings. Things that are promising but unproven: regulatory markets, AI-specific whistleblower protections, sovereign wealth funds capturing AI value, and contemplative practices building the moral attention that discernment requires. Things that are not working or not yet feasible: voluntary corporate safety commitments, comprehensive international governance, UBI as currently designed, and any mechanism that depends on the goodwill of those whose power derives from the status quo.

The deeper insight connecting all three levels is Seligman's revised finding: helplessness is the default; agency must be learned. The individual who cultivates AI discernment, the institution that submits to genuine external evaluation, and the civilization that builds adaptive governance structures are all doing the same thing — learning, against the brain's and the system's defaults, that their actions matter. The book's practical promise cannot be that these mechanisms are sufficient. It can be that they exist, that they are buildable, and that the alternative — passivity dressed as sophistication — is the one response guaranteed to fail.
