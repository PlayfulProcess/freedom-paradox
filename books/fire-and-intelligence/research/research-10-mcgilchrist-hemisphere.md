## **I. McGilchrist's "Resist the Machine Apocalypse" (*First Things*, March 2024\)**

### **Origin and Context**

The essay originated as the opening speech at the 2022 World Summit AI in Amsterdam and was published in *First Things* (March 2024). It represents McGilchrist's most sustained application of the hemisphere hypothesis to the specific problem of artificial intelligence. The essay functions simultaneously as an introduction to his broader framework and as a warning about where AI sits within the civilizational trajectory he mapped in *The Master and His Emissary* (2009) and expanded in *The Matter with Things* (2021).

### **The Core Argument: AI as Left-Hemisphere Externalization**

McGilchrist's central claim is explicit: **"AI—artificial information-processing, by the way, not artificial intelligence—could in many ways be seen as replicating the functions of the left hemisphere at frightening speed across the entire globe."** He insists on the terminological correction—"artificial *information-processing*"—as itself a philosophical point. What we call AI has no intelligence in any meaningful sense; it processes symbols.

He draws the parallel precisely: the left hemisphere "manipulates tokens or symbols for aspects of experience," while "the right hemisphere is in touch with experience itself, with the body and deeper emotions, with context and the vast realm of the implicit." AI, like the left hemisphere, operates without a sense of "the bigger picture, of other values, or of the way in which context—or even scale and extent—changes everything."

This is not merely a metaphor. McGilchrist is arguing that AI *is* what the left hemisphere does, scaled globally and computationally. The technology instantiates a particular mode of attention—narrow-beam, decontextualized, manipulative—and projects it across the entire planet at unprecedented speed.

### **The Two Hemispheric Visions (As Applied to AI)**

McGilchrist characterizes the two hemispheric orientations as follows:

**Left hemisphere** (= AI's mode): Using narrow-beam attention to one detail after another, it sees what is "familiar, certain, static, explicit, abstract, decontextualized, disembodied, categorized, general in nature, and reduced to its parts. All is predictable and controlled. This is an inanimate universe—and a bureaucrat's dream." It perceives everything as *re-presentation*—presenting things *again* when they are "no longer present, but dead and gone."

**Right hemisphere** (= what AI cannot replicate): Bringing "broad, open, sustained, vigilant attention," it sees "what is fresh, unique, never fully known, never finally certain, but full of potential." It understands "humor, poetry, art, narrative, music, the sacred, indeed everything we love" and grasps that "nothing is ever static and unchanging, that everything is flowing and interconnected."

The critical argument for your book: **both modes are vital for survival, but catastrophe follows when only one is used.** McGilchrist's civilizational thesis—that ancient Greece and Rome each began with left-right harmony and collapsed into left-hemisphere dominance—is now playing out for the third time, with AI as the ultimate accelerant.

### **The Colonization of Reality**

A key passage for your argument about grammars and fire: McGilchrist describes a feedback loop in which the left hemisphere's model of reality gets externalized (through technology, bureaucracy, urban life) so thoroughly that when the right hemisphere "checks back with experience" to test the left hemisphere's theory, it "finds that the left hemisphere has already colonized reality—at least for those of us who lead a modern Western urban life. It finds a perfect simulacrum of the world according to the left hemisphere."

This is the self-reinforcing loop: the left hemisphere's map replaces the territory, and then the territory-checking function finds only the map. The five things that used to break this loop are, he says, "fading away": the natural world; coherent shared culture; the lived body; great art; and the sacred.

### **"Machine Guns in the Hands of Toddlers"**

The exact quote and its context: **"AI exists to make things happen, to give us control; but its doing so is good only if we make progress in wisdom as fast as we make progress in technical know-how. Otherwise, our 'progress' is like putting machine guns in the hands of toddlers."**

This appears in the essay's pivotal turn from diagnosis to prescription. It is McGilchrist's version of your Prometheus argument—power acquired without the responsibility structures to wield it. He frames this as the asymmetry between the left hemisphere's single goal ("power") and what imagination and wisdom require. The word "toddlers" is specifically chosen: not evil agents but *developmentally unready* ones. This maps directly onto your thesis that humanity got fire before it had the grammars to hold it.

### **The Gardener vs. the Manipulator**

McGilchrist's prescription is paradoxical: **"To succeed at AI, whose purpose is to give us control, we must relinquish control."** He invokes the gardener who "cannot create a plant or make it grow" but can only "permit and encourage the plant to do what it does." The gardener metaphor is explicitly anti-left-hemisphere: it requires working *with* nature rather than against it, trusting spontaneity, openness to risk, and intuition.

He also argues that excessive monitoring and control can "never get more than mediocrity"—an implicit critique of the metrics-driven culture that AI both exemplifies and enforces.

### **The Schizophrenia Parallel**

A striking passage links the AI worldview to psychopathology: people with schizophrenia (who exhibit "left-hemisphere overdrive and right-hemisphere hypofunction") "see a world of bits and pieces" and "often imagine that people have become inanimate, machine-like, or zombies." McGilchrist argues that the belief machines could become sentient is "the obverse of the view that we, sentient beings, are really just machines." He quotes R.D. Laing's patient who saw his wife as a machine—"a predictable, determined response"—and notes this mentality is "not unlike the way we may begin to see each other" as AI processes become the standard for behavior.

### **What Makes Life Worth Living**

The essay closes with McGilchrist's concept of **resonance**: "the encounter with other living beings, with the natural world, and with the greatest products of the human soul." Resonance requires what AI cannot provide—"freedom, spontaneity, life." It "is not possible where there is no freedom, no spontaneity, no life."

## **II. B. Alan Wallace's "Contemplative Science" Proposal**

### **What It Is**

Wallace coined the term "contemplative science" in his 1986 undergraduate honors thesis at Amherst College, *Words of Emptiness: A Centrist View of Science and Reality*, where he defined it as "the phenomenological study of physical and cognitive events." The term was first set in print in that thesis, submitted to his faculty readers Arthur Zajonc and Robert Thurman.

Wallace's proposal, fully developed in *Contemplative Science: Where Buddhism and Neuroscience Converge* (Columbia University Press, 2007), is built on a radical claim: **meditative practices are not merely objects to be studied by science; they are themselves instruments of observation and experimentation**, analogous to telescopes or particle accelerators. As the Center for Contemplative Research puts it: "imagine if astronomy merely comprised the study of telescopes—never relying on these powerful instruments to make verifiable observations about celestial objects."

Today, "contemplative science" is often reduced to the neuroscientific study of meditation (measuring brain states of meditators). Wallace argues this misses the point entirely. His version has two defining attributes:

1. **Contemplatives are regarded as colleagues, not simply study subjects**, with respect to their scientific collaborators.  
2. **Both ancient and modern methods are used** to make intersubjectively verifiable observations about the nature of mind and reality.

### **The Methodological Architecture**

Wallace identifies a "taboo of subjectivity" in Western science—the insistence that only third-person, publicly observable data count as empirical. He argues this leaves a "gaping blind spot at the center of modernity: our understanding of consciousness." Just as scientists refine physical instruments to make better observations, contemplatives refine the instrument of attention itself through practices like **samatha** (meditative quiescence).

Samatha, the cornerstone practice, involves systematic training of attention through stages that progressively stabilize and clarify awareness. Wallace uses the telescope metaphor extensively: just as a space telescope orbits beyond the distortions of Earth's atmosphere, samatha meditation provides "a platform for exploring the deep space of the mind" by training awareness beyond the distortions of ordinary cognitive noise. The practice develops two key faculties:

* **Mindfulness** (*smṛti*): sustained, non-forgetful attention to the meditative object  
* **Introspection** (*samprajanya*): metacognitive monitoring that detects excitation or laxity—the "quality control" of the contemplative process

Wallace argues that once samatha is achieved (a state of flawless concentration sustainable for extended periods, with the five hindrances dormant), the meditator can make reliable first-person observations about consciousness that are replicable across practitioners, just as scientific observations are replicable across laboratories.

### **The Four Imbalances Contemplative Science Addresses**

Wallace identifies four categories of imbalance that contemplative science can address, which standard science ignores or mishandles:

* **Conative** (intention and desire)  
* **Attentional**  
* **Cognitive**  
* **Affective**

These are not merely psychological problems to be treated but epistemological distortions that compromise the reliability of any first-person observation. Contemplative training is the corrective.

### **The Institutional Vision**

Wallace founded the Santa Barbara Institute for Consciousness Studies (2003) and later the Center for Contemplative Research in Tuscany, Italy—conceived as a community where contemplatives and scientists work together to "integrate first-person meditative experience with third-person methods of science." He has also advocated "contemplative observatories"—institutional settings analogous to astronomical observatories but dedicated to systematic observation of mental phenomena.

He cites William James: an education that improved the individual's ability to maintain "sustained, voluntary attention" would be "the education par excellence."

### **Application to AI Evaluation**

Wallace's framework implies that evaluating AI's impact on human consciousness requires instruments that science currently lacks. If the most important effects of AI are on attention, intention, and the quality of subjective experience, then third-person metrics alone (brain scans, behavioral data) will miss what matters most. Contemplative science offers:

* **First-person methodologies** for detecting subtle shifts in attentional quality that neuroscience cannot yet measure  
* **A trained observer paradigm**: just as you wouldn't ask an untrained person to interpret telescope data, you shouldn't rely on untrained introspection to evaluate AI's effects on consciousness  
* **A framework for distinguishing genuine from simulated understanding**: the contemplative tradition has millennia of practice in distinguishing authentic insight from conceptual fabrication—exactly the distinction AI collapses

## **III. Peter Hershock's "Freedom-of-Attention" in the AI Context**

### **The Core Framework**

Peter D. Hershock is Director of the Asian Studies Development Program and Coordinator of the Humane AI Initiative at the East-West Center in Honolulu. His *Buddhism and Intelligent Technology: Toward a More Humane Future* (Bloomsbury, 2021\) is one of only two book-length studies of AI ethics from a Buddhist perspective. The book's chapter structure reveals its architecture: from "Buddhism: A Philosophical Repertoire" through "Total Attention Capture and Control: A Future to Avoid" to "Humane Becoming: Cultivating Responsive Virtuosity."

### **The Alignment Predicament (Not Just the Alignment Problem)**

Hershock distinguishes the technical **"alignment problem"** (ensuring AI delivers what designers and users want) from the deeper **"alignment predicament"**: conflicting human values being recursively amplified by algorithmic systems that are increasingly adept at predicting and shaping both individual and societal wants and needs.

His key metaphor: **"AI is holding up a wish-fulfilling mirror to humanity. What we see looking into it is not reassuring."** (Note the convergence with Vallor's mirror metaphor, arrived at independently from Buddhist rather than Western philosophical premises.)

### **Freedom-of-Attention as Buddhist Foundation**

Hershock's central concept draws on the *Dhammapada*'s opening: "All things are preceded by mind, led by mind, made by mind." He argues that our lives unfold as "a karmic function of what matters most to us—the patterns of our values and intentions, our likes and dislikes, our habits of thought and feeling."

**Freedom-of-attention** is the capacity to *choose* what we attend to, rather than having attention captured and directed by external forces. He defines it as the first step toward "liberating relational dynamics"—or, as he puts it in conversation, "freedom from compelled/compulsive presence."

Without freedom-of-attention, there can be no **freedom-of-intention**. And without freedom-of-intention, "the line between choice and compulsion dissolves." This is the existential risk AI poses that most Western ethicists miss: not that AI will become conscious, but that it will systematically erode the human capacity for *consciousness* in the Buddhist sense—aware, intentional, free presence.

### **AI as "Karmic Intermediary"**

Hershock's most original contribution: as AI systems become adept at anticipating, interpreting, and enacting our intentions, they perform as **"karmic intermediaries"**—doing so on platforms that "inequitably valorize competition, convenience, choice, and control" with "reward structures that foster addictive engagement and the consolidation of what amount to digital *saṃskāra* or habit formations."

The term *saṃskāra* (habitual mental formations/impressions) is key: AI isn't just serving desires; it's *forming habits of mind*, shaping the very structures through which consciousness encounters the world. This makes the attention economy not merely an economic phenomenon but a **"consciousness hacking karmic accelerator."**

### **Epistemic and Ontic Powers**

Hershock distinguishes between AI's **epistemic powers** (unprecedented ability to predict human behaviors, beliefs, and desires) and its **ontic powers** (unprecedented ability to *produce* patterns of thought, speech, emotion, and action). This distinction is crucial: AI doesn't just *know* us better; it *makes* us into particular kinds of beings. These are "powers to sculpt who consumers and citizens become, not through acts of *coercion*, but through algorithmically reinforced *craving* and the curation of choice- and decision-making environments."

### **Diversity vs. Variety**

Hershock makes a distinction relevant to your colonial-reflex argument: **variety** is a "numeric measure of multiplicity—something that can be both seen at a glance and mandated." **Diversity** is "a relational index of the degree to which differences are the basis of mutual contribution to sustainably shared flourishing." A zoo has variety; an ecosystem has diversity. AI platforms typically increase variety while decreasing diversity—more choices, less genuine difference.

### **Prescription: Four Principles for Humane AI**

Hershock proposes that ethically sound AI development should be based on:

1. **Supporting intelligent human practices** rather than supplementing and eventually supplanting them with smart services  
2. **Fostering concentration** rather than distraction  
3. **Promoting freedom-of-attention and freedom-of-intention**  
4. **Realizing environments conducive to the effortful cultivation** of appreciative and contributory virtuosity

## **IV. Shannon Vallor's Technomoral Virtues**

### **The Framework**

Shannon Vallor (Baillie Gifford Professor in the Ethics of Data and AI, University of Edinburgh; Director, Centre for Technomoral Futures) developed her framework in *Technology and the Virtues: A Philosophical Guide to a Future Worth Wanting* (Oxford University Press, 2016\) and extended it in *The AI Mirror: How to Reclaim Our Humanity in an Age of Machine Thinking* (Oxford, 2024).

Her foundational argument: in a period of "acute technosocial opacity"—where technology reshapes societies faster than we can understand the implications—neither deontological rules (too rigid) nor consequentialist calculation (impossible given unpredictability) can guide us. Only **virtue ethics**, which cultivates character capable of responding to the unprecedented, is adequate.

Vallor's distinctive move: she draws not from one tradition but from **three classical virtue ethics traditions simultaneously—Aristotelian, Confucian, and Buddhist** (her "ABCs"). She seeks their structural commonalities as a foundation for a "global technomoral virtue ethic" appropriate to our interconnected technological world.

### **The Twelve Technomoral Virtues**

Vallor presents these as provisional starting points, not dogma:

1. **Honesty** — "An exemplary respect for truth, along with practical expertise to express that respect appropriately in technosocial contexts." In an era of deepfakes and algorithmic amplification of engaging-but-false content, this virtue requires active cultivation, not passive habit.

2. **Self-control** (temperance, discipline, moderation) — "An exemplary ability in technosocial contexts to choose, and ideally to desire for their own sake, those goods and experiences that most contribute to contemporary and future human flourishing." This directly addresses the attention economy's engineered addictiveness.

3. **Humility** (modesty, reverence, wonder) — "A recognition of the real limits of our technological knowledge and ability; reverence and wonder at the universe's retained power to surprise and confound us; and renunciation of the blind faith that new technologies inevitably lead to human mastery and control of our environment." This is the anti-Promethean virtue.

4. **Justice** (responsibility, fairness, reciprocity) — "A reliable disposition to seek a fair and equitable distribution of the benefits and risks of emerging technologies" and "a characteristic concern for how emerging technologies impact the basic rights, dignity, or welfare of individuals and groups."

5. **Courage** (hope, perseverance, fortitude) — "A reliable disposition toward intelligent fear and hope with respect to the moral and material dangers and opportunities presented by emerging technologies." Note: *intelligent* fear—not techno-panic, but discerning assessment.

6. **Empathy** (compassion, benevolence) — "Cultivated openness to being morally moved to caring action by the emotions of other members of our technosocial world." Explicitly distinguished from "virtue signaling."

7. **Care** (generosity, love, service) — "A skillful, attentive, responsible, and emotionally responsive disposition to personally meet the needs of those with whom we share our technosocial environment."

8. **Civility** (respect, tolerance, engagement) — "A sincere disposition to live well with one's fellow citizens of a globally networked information society: to collectively and wisely deliberate about matters of local, national, and global policy."

9. **Flexibility** (patience, forbearance, equanimity) — "A reliable and skillful disposition to modulate action, belief, and feeling as called for by novel, unpredictable, frustrating, or unstable technosocial conditions."

10. **Perspective** (discernment, attention, understanding) — "A reliable disposition to attend to, discern, and understand moral phenomena as meaningful parts of a moral whole." This is closest to McGilchrist's right-hemisphere holistic attention.

11. **Magnanimity** (equanimity, courage, ambition) — A form of moral leadership that refuses petty ego-driven disputes in favor of "great-souledness"—Aristotle's *megalopsychia* adapted for the technological age.

12. **Technomoral Wisdom** — The integrative meta-virtue: moral living through the cultivation and integration of all eleven preceding virtues plus the seven components of moral habituation. This is the capstone—not a separate skill but the emergent capacity that arises from practiced virtue.

### **The Process of Cultivation**

Vallor identifies seven elements of moral self-cultivation that are common across the Aristotelian, Confucian, and Buddhist traditions:

* **Moral habituation** — Gradual transition from uncultivated to morally habituated state, guided by exemplars  
* **Relational understanding** — Ethical decisions require attentiveness to specific relationships and roles  
* **Reflective self-examination** — Regular examination of behavior against aspirations  
* **Intentional self-direction** — Taking ownership of one's moral development as a personal aspiration  
* **Perceptual attention to moral salience** — Noticing the morally relevant features of situations (Vallor notes this is "undertheorized in classical virtue ethics")  
* **Prudential judgment** (*phronesis*) — Choosing well among available means in specific situations  
* **Appropriate extension of moral concern** — The most challenging: extending concern "to the right beings, at the right time, to the right degree, and in the right manner." She cites Mahayana Buddhist *tonglen* (exchanging self and others) meditation as one practice for developing this.

### ***The AI Mirror*** **(2024): The Updated Argument**

Vallor's more recent book argues that AI technologies function as "immense mirrors of human intelligence"—reflecting our "errors, biases, and failures of wisdom." The Narcissus myth structures the book: we risk falling in love with our own reflection in AI, mistaking its projections for truth.

Key concepts from the newer work:

* **Autofabrication**: Humans are "creatures of autofabrication"—future-oriented beings who "choose to make ourselves and remake ourselves, again and again." AI's backward-facing architecture (extrapolating from past data) undermines this essentially human capacity.  
* **The bootstrapping problem**: To develop the technomoral wisdom needed to design AI well, we need the very capacities that poorly designed AI is currently eroding.  
* **Moral debt**: Society accumulates "moral debt" when it defers ethical reckoning—and AI is being used as a "Band-Aid or sort of easy technical fix for big social problems" in ways that compound this debt.  
* **AI lacks *phronesis***: Aristotelian practical wisdom—the ability to adapt ethical principles to complex, evolving circumstances—cannot be computed. AI "can only optimize within historically defined parameters."

## **V. Synthesis: Convergences for Your Chapter**

### **The Prometheus Pattern**

All four thinkers converge on a version of your core thesis: humanity has acquired power that outstrips its wisdom-structures.

* **McGilchrist**: "progress is like putting machine guns in the hands of toddlers"  
* **Hershock**: the "alignment predicament"—value conflicts recursively amplified before we can resolve them  
* **Wallace**: the "gaping blind spot at the center of modernity"—we've built civilization-transforming technology without understanding the instrument (consciousness) through which we engage it  
* **Vallor**: the "bootstrapping problem"—we need technomoral wisdom to design AI wisely, but poorly designed AI erodes the capacity for that very wisdom

### **Attention as the Contested Ground**

The single concept that most powerfully links hemisphere theory to contemplative traditions is **attention**:

* **McGilchrist**: the hemispheres represent two fundamentally different *modes of attention*—narrow-beam/manipulative vs. broad/receptive. AI replicates the first globally while the second atrophies.  
* **Wallace**: samatha practice is literally the systematic refinement of attention as a scientific instrument. Contemplative science proposes that trained attention reveals features of consciousness that untrained attention (and third-person methods) cannot detect.  
* **Hershock**: "freedom-of-attention" is the foundational capacity without which freedom-of-intention is impossible. The attention economy systematically degrades this capacity, making it a threat not just to privacy or autonomy but to the Buddhist project of liberation itself.  
* **Vallor**: "perceptual attention to moral salience" is the least theorized but most essential component of moral development. She calls it "undertheorized in classical virtue ethics"—a gap the contemplative traditions can fill.

### **The Left-Hemisphere/AI Isomorphism**

McGilchrist's specific claims about left-hemisphere cognition map onto AI's actual architecture with striking precision:

| Left Hemisphere Feature | AI Equivalent |
| ----- | ----- |
| Manipulates tokens/symbols for aspects of experience | Operates on data representations, not experience |
| No sense of the bigger picture | Optimizes within defined parameters without contextual judgment |
| Decontextualized | Training data stripped of lived context |
| Static categories | Pattern-matching against historical data |
| Re-presentation (presenting what is dead and gone) | Backward-facing: reflects past data, never "where we might venture for the first time" (Vallor) |
| Vacuously cheerful in the face of disaster | Confident outputs even on topics where uncertainty is appropriate |
| Lies and makes stuff up when it doesn't know | Hallucination in LLMs |
| Dunning-Kruger: doesn't know what it doesn't know | No model of its own ignorance |

### **What First-Person Methods Would Add to AI Evaluation**

If Wallace's contemplative science were applied to AI evaluation:

1. **Attention quality metrics**: Trained contemplatives could assess whether interaction with AI degrades or supports the specific qualities of attention (stability, vividness, openness) that constitute healthy consciousness—qualities invisible to behavioral metrics.

2. **Saṃskāra detection**: Hershock's concept of "digital saṃskāra" could be investigated through first-person methods—do AI interactions create subtle habit-formations in consciousness that persist and compound?

3. **Context-sensitivity testing**: McGilchrist's argument that the right hemisphere detects context while the left (and AI) strips it away suggests that contemplative practices designed to enhance contextual awareness could serve as a diagnostic: does AI interaction diminish the practitioner's felt sense of context?

4. **The "resonance" test**: McGilchrist's concept of resonance—the encounter with the living, uncontrollable other—could become a criterion: does this technology support or degrade the human capacity for genuine relational encounter?

### **The Colonial Reflex Connection**

These frameworks intersect with your colonial-reflex argument:

* Hershock's distinction between **diversity and variety** directly maps onto colonial extraction: colonialism reduces diversity (relational, mutual contribution) to variety (categorical, manageable difference). AI platforms do the same digitally.  
* McGilchrist's "colonization of reality" by the left hemisphere parallels literal colonization: the map replaces the territory, the abstract system overrides the living context.  
* Vallor's critique of AI as backward-facing mirrors connects to your separability thesis: AI reproduces the past's hierarchies and exclusions, encoding colonial patterns into computational infrastructure.
