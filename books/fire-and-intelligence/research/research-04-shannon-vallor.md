# **Shannon Vallor's philosophical case for better humans**

Shannon Vallor argues that humanity's central challenge in the age of AI is not technological but moral: **"if we are to have any real hope of securing a future worth wanting, then we will need more than just better technologies. We will also need better humans."** Across two major books — *Technology and the Virtues* (2016) and *The AI Mirror* (2024) — and a body of influential papers, Vallor has built the most philosophically rigorous case that virtue ethics, rooted in cross-cultural contemplative traditions, offers the best framework for governing emerging technology. Her work occupies a distinctive position in the AI ethics landscape: neither the techno-optimism of Silicon Valley nor the existential doomism of the alignment community, but a third path grounded in the ancient insight that wisdom is a practiced skill — one that atrophies when machines remove the occasions for its exercise.

---

## **The architecture of a global technomoral virtue ethic**

*Technology and the Virtues: A Philosophical Guide to a Future Worth Wanting* (Oxford University Press, 2016\) is Vallor's foundational work, and its core argument operates on two axes. First, rapid technological change creates a special need for moral virtues to guide choices among multiple possible futures. Second, technology itself creates new challenges to cultivating those very virtues — a paradox that demands creative philosophical response.

Vallor's most distinctive move is her synthesis of three classical virtue traditions — what she calls **"the ABCs"**: Aristotelian, Buddhist, and Confucian ethics. She uses three traditions rather than just Aristotle for principled reasons. A global ethic, she argues, must be accessible across cultures; drawing on three radically different civilizations demonstrates that virtue ethics is not exclusively a Western philosophical project. The convergences she identifies across traditions strengthen the case that certain elements of moral self-cultivation are fundamental to human flourishing regardless of cultural origin.

From **Aristotle**, she draws the concept of *eudaimonia* (human flourishing as the goal of ethical life), *phronesis* (practical wisdom enabling discernment of right action), the Doctrine of the Mean (virtue lies between excess and deficiency), and the crucial insight that we become virtuous through practice — that virtue is *hexis*, a cultivated disposition formed through repeated action. From **Confucian ethics** (principally Kongzi and Mengzi), she draws *ren* (benevolence or humaneness), the role of *li* (ritual propriety) in shaping moral character through practice, the centrality of exemplary persons (*junzi*) as moral models, and above all, relational ethics — the understanding that the self is constituted through relationships and reciprocal obligations rather than existing as an autonomous individual. From **Buddhist ethics** (particularly Mahāyāna Buddhism), she draws *karuṇā* (compassion for all sentient beings), mindfulness as a technique for cultivating moral awareness, the concept of *anātman* (no-self) and the radical interconnectedness it implies, and "exchanging self and others" meditations as practices for extending moral concern beyond one's immediate circle.

Across these traditions, Vallor identifies four fundamental commitments that converge: a conception of the highest human good that guides ethical life; a vision of moral virtues as cultivated states of character exemplified by wise persons; a practical path of moral self-cultivation; and a realistic understanding of human nature and its potential for development. She focuses not on the particular content each tradition recommends — acknowledging their "widely diverging visions" — but on **shared structural resonances** regarding how virtue is cultivated and what it requires.

## **Seven practices of moral self-cultivation and twelve technomoral virtues**

From this cross-cultural analysis, Vallor distills **seven core elements of moral self-cultivation** — activities and traits she finds constitutive of virtue or necessary to its development across all three traditions:

**Moral habituation** is the gradual transition from an uncultivated state to a morally practiced one, "typically motivated and guided by moral exemplars in the community, effected by repeated moral practice" that eventually leads to "greater comfort, ease, pleasure, and even joy in performing moral action." **Relational understanding** insists that ethical decisions take place in specific contexts of human relationships and particular roles — virtue ethics is skeptical of abstractions like the impartial observer or the veil of ignorance. **Reflective self-examination** — the Socratic "know thyself" — demands regular reflection on daily behavior examined against one's aspirations, enabling a person to "discern and eventually to author right desires." **Intentional self-direction** is the stage where one takes personal responsibility for one's character, moving beyond compliance to authentic internalization. **Perceptual attention to moral salience** is "a reliable sensitivity to the morally salient features of the given situation in which I am called to act" — a capacity Vallor admits is "undertheorized in classical virtue ethics" and draws significantly from Buddhist mindfulness practices to develop. **Prudential judgment** corresponds to Aristotelian *phronesis* — "the cultivated ability to deliberate and choose well, in particular situations, among the most appropriate and effective means available for achieving a noble or good end." Finally, **appropriate extension of moral concern** is "the ability to extend natural moral attitudes of caring concern beyond their initial scope" — extended "to the right beings, at the right time, to the right degree, and in the right manner." Vallor calls this the most challenging element to cultivate, and draws on Mahāyāna Buddhist "exchanging self and others" meditations as one concrete technique.

Built on these seven foundations, Vallor proposes **twelve technomoral virtues**, presented as "a starting point, not a dogma." Each adapts a classical virtue to the conditions of technological life. Honesty becomes "an exemplary respect for truth, along with practical expertise to express that respect appropriately in technosocial contexts" — navigating deepfakes, algorithmic manipulation, and misinformation. Self-control addresses addictive tech design and digital distraction. **Humility** is defined as "a recognition of the real limits of our technological knowledge and ability; reverence and wonder at the universe's retained power to surprise and confound us; and renunciation of the blind faith that new technologies inevitably lead to human mastery and control of our environment." Justice targets the fair distribution of technology's benefits and risks. Courage addresses intelligent fear and hope regarding technological dangers and opportunities. Empathy — "cultivated openness to being morally moved to caring action" — is distinguished from virtue signaling and threatened by media multitasking that impairs neural circuits for empathic concern. Care is "a skillful, attentive, responsible, and emotionally responsive disposition to personally meet the needs of those with whom we share our technosocial environment," directly relevant to debates about delegating caregiving to machines. Civility addresses collective deliberation in a globally networked society. Flexibility is "a reliable and skillful disposition to modulate action, belief, and feeling as called for by novel, unpredictable, frustrating, or unstable technosocial conditions." Perspective attends to moral phenomena as meaningful parts of a moral whole. Magnanimity evokes Aristotle's "great-souledness" — moral ambition and leadership that transcends petty arguments. And the twelfth, **technomoral wisdom**, is the capstone: "a general condition of well-cultivated moral expertise that expresses all the other virtues in an intelligent, authentic, and integrated way."

## **Why virtue ethics outperforms rules and calculations under technosocial opacity**

Vallor's argument for virtue ethics over rival frameworks hinges on a concept she calls **"acute technosocial opacity"** — the condition in which technological, societal, political, and human-definitional changes occur at such a rapid pace "that they block our ability to conceptualize and understand their implications." Human activities have always been technosocial — shaped by available technologies — but the contemporary rate of change has become itself a defining feature of the moral landscape.

Under these conditions, she argues, deontological ethics fails because rigid rules are "too inflexible to keep up with technological change" — any code of behavior premised on actions being always right or always wrong proves "too brittle for our current situation." Consequentialism fails because utilitarian calculation becomes "nigh impossible as the future becomes increasingly difficult to predict." She writes: "Once even a fraction of the possible paths of technosocial development are considered, the practical uncertainties will swamp the cognitive powers of any Kantian agent, paralyzing her attempt to choose in a rational and universally consistent manner." And utilitarian ethics "fares little better by telling us that we may secure the good life simply by choosing, among the available courses of action, that which promises the greatest happiness for all those affected."

Virtue ethics, by contrast, focuses on cultivated character rather than rigid rules or unpredictable calculations. Virtues are "ideally suited for adaptation to the open-ended and varied encounters with particular technologies that will shape the human condition in this and coming centuries." As Vallor stated in an interview: **"What technologies do when they are new is they transform our habits, they transform the things that we do every day. And virtue ethics says that it's precisely the things that you do every day that determine the shape of your character and your ability to live well with others."** There is, she insists, "no substitute for the flexibility, sensitivity, and good judgment of practical wisdom, developed as widely as possible." This argument — that Constitutional AI or any system encoding values as rules is categorically different from practical wisdom — is central to her critique of technical approaches to alignment.

## **Moral deskilling as the key mechanism of technological erosion**

Vallor's 2015 paper "Moral Deskilling and Upskilling in a New Machine Age" (*Philosophy & Technology*, 28: 107–124) — now cited over **227 times** — articulates the mechanism by which technology erodes the human capacities needed to govern it. The argument adapts Harry Braverman's thesis from *Labor and Monopoly Capital* (1974): just as industrial automation deskilled machinists, artisans, and other trained workers by making their practical knowledge redundant, AI and ICTs risk deskilling us morally by removing the occasions for practicing moral judgment.

The Aristotelian foundation is explicit. Vallor writes: "Moral skills are thus necessary (while not sufficient) conditions of genuine moral virtue or practical wisdom. Moral virtue assumes the availability of prior opportunities to cultivate, through stable and repeatable practices of the right sort, the prerequisite moral skills. Without them, even a person who sincerely wishes to do well consistently and for its own sake will be incapable of doing so. **It follows that if new technological practices disrupt the cultivation of moral skills on a large enough scale, the future of human character may be profoundly affected.**" To illustrate: the virtue of honesty requires "repeated opportunities to practice communication in a range of contexts that specifically invite or warrant honesty (such as intimate conversation with close friends and family members, therapeutic discussion, contract writing, religious confession, or scientific/legal testimony)." Technology that removes these occasions removes the soil in which the virtue grows.

The paper identifies three concrete domains of moral deskilling risk: **autonomous weapons systems** (soldiers "on the loop" who lack the practical wisdom to oversee machines' moral decisions because those machines have replaced the practices through which such wisdom was cultivated), **new media practices and multitasking** (chronic media multitasking that impairs working memory and empathic attention while creating compulsive habits following "the structure of classical conditioning"), and **robot caregivers** (carebots that replace the skilled human practices through which care as a virtue is cultivated and expressed).

The feedback loop is devastating in its simplicity: more AI reliance removes occasions for moral practice; less moral practice means reduced capacity for moral judgment; reduced judgment capacity means less ability to govern AI wisely; and diminished governance leads to still greater AI reliance. Vallor captures the terminal condition: "Humans in this world are still responsible for wars; they still start them, fund them, and continue them. But they no longer really know, except in the most abstract and impotent sense, how to fight them morally." The problem is structural: **market forces drive the replacement of moral skills by efficiency**, and cultural instrumentalism treats technology as a neutral tool rather than something that reshapes agency. "Our present course of technological development has no moral teleology; how could it, as a process directed by free market forces committed to nothing but the satisfaction of immediate and unreflective desire?"

Crucially, Vallor does not argue that moral upskilling through technology is impossible — only that current forces work against it. She envisions military "moral biofeedback systems" that enhance rather than replace soldiers' moral discernment, carebots designed to support rather than substitute for human caregiving, and media platforms facilitating extended moral conversation rather than fragmenting attention. But her assessment of current trajectories is pessimistic: "Market and cultural forces are not presently aligned to bring about the more salutary of the ambiguous potentials."

Direct empirical evidence for moral deskilling remains limited — as De Cremer et al. (2023) noted, Vallor's warnings are "theoretically-grounded warnings of a somewhat speculative nature." But supporting evidence comes from adjacent domains: a 2025 *Scientific Reports* study found military cadets' moral decisions shifted under AI influence; Ophir et al. (2009, *PNAS*) demonstrated chronic media multitasking impairs attention and working memory; and systematic reviews of intelligent decision-support systems consistently find that outcome-oriented automation risks reducing users' ability to make ethical decisions independently.

## **The AI Mirror — how artificial intelligence distorts human self-understanding**

*The AI Mirror: How to Reclaim Our Humanity in an Age of Machine Thinking* (Oxford University Press, 2024\) — named **one of the Financial Times' Best Summer Books of 2024** and "the year's best critique of AI" by *Fast Company* — extends and sharpens Vallor's framework for the age of large language models. The central metaphor operates on three levels. AI systems are constructed as immense mirrors of human intelligence — "they don't produce thoughts or feelings any more than mirrors produce bodies. What they produce is a new kind of reflection," specifically "complex reflections cast by our recorded thoughts, judgments, desires, needs, perceptions, expectations, and imaginings." Those reflections reveal ugly social realities, since training data "heavily overrepresent English language text and speech, as well as data from young, white, male subjects in the Northern Hemisphere." And like funhouse mirrors, they distort what they reflect — generative AI tools "add statistical noise to every reflection... giving the output a degree of novelty or surprise that makes it seem more 'creative' or 'intelligent'. But it's just a trick of the light."

The temporal dimension is philosophically crucial: **"Our new digital mirrors point backward. They show only where the data say that we have already been, never where we might venture together for the first time."** Drawing on Ortega y Gasset, Vallor argues humans are "creatures of autofabrication," future-oriented beings who "choose to make ourselves and remake ourselves, again and again." AI's backward-facing architecture cannot participate in this distinctly human capacity.

Vallor's critique of the alignment framing is sharp. In a co-authored chapter with Wendell Wallach, she argued the value alignment approach "tends to employ computationally friendly concepts such as utility functions, system goals, agent preferences, and value optimizers, which... do not have intrinsic ethical significance." In *The AI Mirror*, she sharpens this against the longtermist and effective altruist movements: "For the new priests of the church of AI, there is no question that such a machine savior (or, if you're an AI doomist, a demon) will eventually come." She describes the longtermist elite as "powerful men who see themselves as a natural elite" who endorse "the unchecked pursuit, consolidation, and elite control of wealth and influence," values "conveniently rebranded as 'altruism.'" Since AI lacks genuine cognition, trying to "align" it with human values treats it as something it is not — an independent agent. Alignment misunderstands not just AI but humanity itself.

On LLMs specifically, Vallor uses the tragic case of a Belgian man whose interactions with a chatbot named "Eliza" amplified his distress: "Eliza knew nothing of Pierre's mind, or his pain, or the danger he was in, because Eliza knew nothing, and was no one, at all." Drawing on Wilfrid Sellars, she argues that "one cannot negotiate with AI in the 'space of reasons'" because AI lacks any conception of its own programming. When a chatbot says "I've been missing you all day," it's what Frankfurt would call bullshit — the chatbot "doesn't have a concept of emotional truth to betray." As the *New Yorker* profiled her position: "Vallor's worry isn't that artificially intelligent computers will rise up and dominate humanity, but that, faced with computers that can pretend to have human virtues, we'll lose track of what those virtues really are."

The book also develops the concept of **"moral debt"** — accumulated when society defers dealing with moral problems, analogous to technical debt in software engineering. "AI systems are being used as Band-Aids or sort of easy technical fixes for big social problems," Vallor argues, "like the problems involved in distributing public benefits in a fair and equitable way." AI is deployed to compensate for, bridge, or disguise ethical gaps in institutions — a temporary bandaid for deep structural problems.

## **From Edinburgh to the Senate floor — Vallor's institutional reach**

Vallor holds the **Baillie Gifford Chair in the Ethics of Data and Artificial Intelligence** at the University of Edinburgh — the first holder of this endowed chair — and directs the **Centre for Technomoral Futures** at the Edinburgh Futures Institute, devoted to "the unification of technical and moral knowledge in research, design and practice." She co-directs the **BRAID programme** (Bridging Responsible AI Divides), a £15.9 million national research programme funded by UKRI's Arts and Humanities Research Council, dedicated to integrating humanities and social science research into the responsible AI ecosystem. She serves as a standing member of Stanford's **AI100** (the One Hundred Year Study on Artificial Intelligence), sits on the oversight board of the **Ada Lovelace Institute**, and chairs the Scottish government's **Data Delivery Group**. She was previously Visiting AI Ethicist at Google (2018–2020) and holds a 2015 World Technology Award in Ethics and a 2022 Covey Award from the International Association for Computing and Philosophy.

Her position in the AI ethics landscape is distinctive. She is explicitly allied with researchers like Timnit Gebru, Meg Mitchell, and Rumman Chowdhury — in Senate testimony, she praised them as creators of "the most groundbreaking and effective research outputs and practical tools in the field of Responsible AI." But she is a sharp critic of the AI safety/longtermist community. In that same November 2023 Senate testimony, she stated: **"Along with many other AI researchers, I believe many of the 'existential risks' of human destruction attributed to AI are wildly exaggerated and scientifically unsound. Those who use the AI risk narrative to draw political and public attention away from the immediate and undeniable existential threats of runaway climate change and growing nuclear destabilization are, in my view, doing serious harm."** She has confronted Yoshua Bengio directly at a machine learning conference, and critiqued Sam Altman's claim that humans are "merely the 'biological bootloader' for a new, higher form of intelligence" as a profound erasure of human capacity.

Her third path — neither techno-optimism nor doomism — rests on the claim that AI is "neither an autonomous thinker nor a neutral instrument." She still believes "in the power of technology as one way in which humans have always lived well." But she insists the problems with AI "are not technology problems. They're problems that arise because of the political and economic incentives shaping the development of the technology." Her concluding vision in *The AI Mirror* is almost lyrical: "The true body of technology is not a mirror, but the creative act of a human being. The true soul of technology is not efficiency but generosity; it is the gift of a future."

## **How Vallor's framework serves the argument for better humans**

For a book arguing that we need better humans, not just better technologies, Vallor's work provides several indispensable elements.

**The intelligence-wisdom gap.** Vallor identifies precisely what is missing from technical approaches to AI: the difference between intelligence (computational power, pattern recognition, optimization) and wisdom (the integrated practical capacity to live and act well under uncertainty). As she quotes Hans Jonas: "We need wisdom most when we believe in it least." Sam Altman's confidence that GPT will "somehow leapfrog humanity's stalled moral development" illustrates the gap — computational intelligence, however powerful, cannot substitute for the cultivated character that phronesis requires.

**Why encoded rules are not practical wisdom.** Constitutional AI and similar approaches encode values as rules — precisely the deontological framework Vallor argues is "too brittle for our current situation." Phronesis is not a ruleset but a practiced capacity for discernment, developed through the seven elements of moral self-cultivation over time. No amount of constitutional text can replicate what years of moral habituation, reflective self-examination, and relational understanding produce. This is a philosophical argument with practical teeth: it means that the entire project of encoding human values into AI systems, however sophisticated, addresses only part of the problem.

**Technology transforms habits, and habits shape character.** Vallor's framework makes explicit what daily AI use is doing to us: reshaping the habitual practices through which moral character is formed. Every time a person defers a judgment to ChatGPT, avoids a difficult conversation by texting instead, or lets an algorithm curate their moral universe, they lose an occasion for practicing the very virtues the situation demands. The feedback loop is the mechanism by which "better technologies" can produce *worse* humans — not through malice but through the quiet atrophy of moral capacity.

**Technosocial opacity as a contemporary Promethean gap.** Vallor's concept of acute technosocial opacity — where the consequences of technical choices are too complex for anyone to foresee — restates in contemporary terms what Günther Anders called the "Promethean gap" between human productive capacity and human imaginative capacity. We can build systems whose effects we cannot comprehend, and this asymmetry grows with every advance in AI capability.

**The contemplative dimension.** For a book that takes contemplative practice seriously, Vallor's engagement with Buddhist ethics is significant. Buddhist mindfulness practices ground her account of "perceptual attention to moral salience." Mahāyāna "exchanging self and others" meditations provide concrete technique for extending moral concern. The Buddhist emphasis on impermanence and uncertainty speaks directly to navigating technosocial opacity. This is not ornamental — though critic Emily McRae argued the Buddhist dimension could be deeper — but rather positions contemplative practice as a genuine technology for cultivating the moral capacities that artificial technologies erode.

**What to do — individual and institutional.** Vallor's framework faces a legitimate question about practical applicability. The seven elements of moral self-cultivation *can* be practiced individually — one can habituate moral action, examine oneself reflectively, attend mindfully to moral salience in daily AI interactions. But Vallor herself acknowledges that basic needs must be met first, that moral development requires exemplary persons and supportive communities, and that "market and cultural forces" currently work against technomoral cultivation. Multiple reviewers noted the gap between her philosophical framework and political implementation. Don Howard proposed extending it to include civic virtues; Damien Williams argued that philosophers "will need a great deal more power, oversight authority, and influence" to realize Vallor's vision. Her BRAID programme and policy work suggest she takes the institutional dimension seriously in practice, even if her published philosophy has focused more on individual character.

## **Reception, critique, and the limits of the framework**

*Technology and the Virtues* received a dedicated book symposium in *Philosophy & Technology* (2018), reviews in *Ethics*, *Notre Dame Philosophical Reviews*, and numerous other journals, and endorsements from leading technology ethicists. Deborah G. Johnson called it "the first extended analysis of technology and ethics drawing on virtue theory" with "long-lasting influence." The main lines of critique are worth noting for intellectual honesty. Emily McRae argued that Buddhism functions as more than ornamental only if Vallor engages more deeply with primary Buddhist sources — a "fair" critique Vallor acknowledged. Benjamin Huff questioned whether a truly "global technomoral virtue ethic" is feasible, calling it "even more ambitious and fraught than she acknowledges." Howard Curzer raised the possibility that technology's impact on virtue may be more positive than Vallor grants: "People have always tuned out unpleasant conversations. Social media did not start or exacerbate that." Jason Kawall raised an epistemic challenge: under conditions of technosocial opacity, how do we identify the technomorally virtuous exemplars the framework requires?

*The AI Mirror* garnered broader public attention — a *New Yorker* profile, *Financial Times* summer book selection, *Publishers Weekly* starred review. But critical responses noted that the portrayal of "the Silicon Valley techno-elite as villainous" can "obscure otherwise complicated moral, social, and political issues" (Robert Hudson, *BJPS*), that her constructive proposals can seem "somewhat thin" compared to the critique (*Comment Magazine*), and that she tends to "stress individualised ethics over structural theories" (*Sociological Review*).

For the book's purposes, the most productive critique is Hovd's (2023) argument that moral deskilling may operate more through institutional structures than individual psychology — that technology changes institutions' capacities to support virtue, not just individuals' opportunities to practice it. This suggests that the "better humans" argument requires not just individual contemplative practice but institutional redesign to create the conditions under which moral cultivation remains possible.

## **Conclusion**

Vallor's work provides the strongest available philosophical foundation for the claim that humanity's AI challenge is fundamentally about character rather than code. Her synthesis of Aristotelian, Buddhist, and Confucian virtue traditions demonstrates that the need for moral self-cultivation in the face of technological power is not a Western parochial concern but a cross-civilizational insight. Her concept of moral deskilling identifies the precise mechanism by which "better technologies" can produce worse humans. Her critique of both alignment-as-rules and consequentialist calculation explains why no technical fix can substitute for cultivated wisdom. And her concept of acute technosocial opacity establishes why, under present conditions, only the flexibility of practiced virtue — not the rigidity of encoded rules — can guide moral action.

The key passage for the book's argument may be her Senate testimony's conclusion: **"It is not AI systems that pose the greatest risk to our humanity right now. It's the people telling us that our humane capabilities are outmoded."** The endgame, she argues, is not a violent uprising by machines but "a slow, quiet human devaluation of the political and cultural currency of our own capacity for collective wisdom and self-governance. That's the endgame. Our humanity is the stake." The call, as she writes in *The AI Mirror*, "is coming from inside the house. AI can devalue our humanity only because we already devalued it ourselves."
