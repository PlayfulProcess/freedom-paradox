# **Six voices who saw the machine age coming**

These six thinkers — Ivan Illich, Ursula Franklin, Simone Weil, Donna Haraway, Norbert Wiener, and the collective moral responsibility theorists — share a single devastating insight: **technology reshapes the humans who use it, not just the world around them**. Each, from a distinct disciplinary vantage, arrived at the book's core thesis decades before large language models existed. Illich showed how tools cross a threshold from serving to enslaving. Franklin demonstrated how prescriptive technologies breed compliance cultures. Weil identified attention as the moral faculty most endangered by mechanization. Haraway offered a stance between techno-salvation and doom. Wiener, the founder of cybernetics itself, warned his own creation would be turned against human dignity. And the collective responsibility literature reveals that our moral frameworks for assigning blame cannot keep pace with the systems we build. Together, they form an intellectual arsenal for arguing that the AI challenge is fundamentally a human development challenge — that the Promethean gap can only be closed from the human side.

## **I. Ivan Illich and the two watersheds of technology**

### **Tools that cross the line from servant to master**

Ivan Illich (1926–2002), the Vienna-born priest and radical critic who founded the Centro Intercultural de Documentación in Cuernavaca, Mexico, published *Tools for Conviviality* in 1973 as his most general theoretical statement. Where *Deschooling Society* (1971) attacked education and *Medical Nemesis* (1975) attacked medicine, *Tools for Conviviality* attacked the entire structure of industrial society's relationship to its instruments.

His central concept is **conviviality**, defined with precision that belies its warm connotations: "I choose the term 'conviviality' to designate the opposite of industrial productivity. I intend it to mean autonomous and creative intercourse among persons, and the intercourse of persons with their environment; and this in contrast with the conditioned response of persons to the demands made upon them by others, and by a man-made environment." He traces the word not to its modern sense of jolly togetherness but to Thomas Aquinas, for whom it meant joyful personal relatedness — friendship as a structural condition, not a sentiment.

Convivial tools meet specific criteria: they "give each person who uses them the greatest opportunity to enrich the environment with the fruits of his or her vision." They can be used "by anybody, as often or as seldom as desired, for the accomplishment of a purpose chosen by the user." The alphabet, the printing press, the public library, the telephone, the Mexican market — all qualify. Crucially, the distinction "is independent of the level of technology." A nuclear power plant is anti-convivial; a sophisticated but user-repairable bicycle is convivial.

### **The two watersheds and radical monopoly**

Illich's most powerful analytical tool is his concept of **two watersheds** in any technology's lifecycle. Using medicine as his case study, he identifies the first watershed around 1913, when medical science began reliably improving outcomes — anesthesia, antiseptics, antibiotics. The second watershed arrives around the 1950s, when medicine's harms begin to rival its benefits through iatrogenesis, professionalization, and the delegitimization of non-medical healing. The institution crosses from serving people to imposing its own logic on them.

The passage deserves full quotation: "There are two ranges in the growth of tools: the range within which machines are used to extend human capability and the range in which they are used to contract, eliminate, or replace human functions. In the first, man as an individual can exercise authority on his own behalf and therefore assume responsibility. In the second, the machine takes over — first reducing the range of choice and motivation in both the operator and the client, and second imposing its own logic and demand on both."

His companion concept, **radical monopoly**, describes what happens after the second watershed. Radical monopoly is not one brand dominating a market — it is one *type of product* restructuring society so that alternatives become structurally impossible. Cars do not merely compete with walking; they reshape cities so that walking becomes impractical. "Radical monopoly exists where a major tool rules out natural competence." The devastating observation: "Monopoly is hard to get rid of when it has frozen not only the shape of the physical world but also the range of behavior and of imagination. Radical monopoly is generally discovered only when it is too late."

### **AI as the ultimate anti-convivial tool**

Modern scholars have applied Illich's framework directly to artificial intelligence, and the fit is disturbingly precise. Andreas Beinsteiner's 2020 analysis in *Open Cultural Studies* identifies AI's core problem in Illich's terms: it is fundamentally opaque to users, creating what Beinsteiner calls "potentially non-recoverable proletarianization." Unlike earlier anti-convivial tools, AI involves a new kind of opacity — not merely trade secrecy or technical complexity, but the inherent inexplicability of machine learning processes that even their creators cannot fully trace.

AI violates every criterion of conviviality. It is **opaque** (users cannot understand or modify it), **centralized** (controlled by a handful of corporations), **capital-intensive** (training frontier models costs billions, making democratic ownership impossible), and it creates **radical monopoly** (once embedded in hiring, healthcare, education, and criminal justice, non-AI alternatives vanish). It represents the second watershed applied to knowledge and judgment themselves — not just physical labor but cognitive and creative capacity crossing from augmentation to replacement.

Illich's warning about imagination deserves particular emphasis for a book about the AI era: "Our imaginations have been industrially deformed to conceive only what can be molded into an engineered system of social habits that fit the logic of large-scale production." When AI becomes the default cognitive tool, the very capacity to imagine alternatives atrophies.

### **Connections and limitations**

Illich connects powerfully to the Promethean gap concept (originated by Günther Anders): both diagnose humanity's tools outgrowing its moral capacity. Where Anders focuses on the existential-emotional gap, Illich focuses on institutional consequences. He connects to Vallor through their shared rejection of technological neutrality — both insist that tools shape character. And his radical monopoly concept speaks directly to AI governance: the window for setting limits closes once the monopoly is established.

The honest limitations are real. Illich can romanticize pre-industrial life — his examples of Mexican funerals and Thai canals can seem nostalgic rather than analytical. The standard of "conviviality" is arguably too vague for policy work. His extreme positions on abolishing schooling and professional medicine remain controversial. Most critically, he offers no clear path for recovering conviviality once radical monopoly is established — the very situation we face with AI. Notably, when *Tools for Conviviality* was reissued in Germany nearly thirty years after publication, **Illich himself acknowledged that the computer had changed the terrain of his arguments**, making the distinction between autonomous and heteronomous action increasingly blurred.

## **II. Ursula Franklin's prescriptive technologies and the culture of compliance**

### **A metallurgist reads the structure of modern work**

Ursula Franklin (1921–2016), the German-born Canadian physicist who survived a Nazi labor camp and pioneered the field of archaeometry at the University of Toronto, delivered the 1989 CBC Massey Lectures under the title *The Real World of Technology*. Her intellectual authority derived from an unusual combination: rigorous physical science, direct experience of totalitarian systems, lifelong Quaker pacifism, and feminist analysis. Her Holocaust experience gave her a visceral understanding of what happens when people are systematically trained to comply. "There were so many people who perished. I didn't," she said. "The basic question is: How does one conduct a responsible life that really has been given by an accident of history?"

Franklin's foundational move is redefining technology itself. Technology, she insists, is not "the sum of the artifacts, of the wheels and gears, of the rails and electronic transmitters." It is a **practice** consisting of "organization, procedures, symbols, new words, equations, and, most of all, a mindset." Or, in her most compressed formulation: technology is simply **"the way we do things around here."** This redefinition matters enormously for AI discourse, which fixates on the device (the model, the algorithm) while ignoring the organizational practices, power relations, and habits of compliance that surround it.

### **Holistic versus prescriptive: a distinction that illuminates everything**

Franklin's central framework emerged from her archaeological study of ancient Chinese bronze casting. Shang Dynasty ritual vessels (circa 1200 BC) required a tightly organized division of labor — and studying them as a metallurgist, "the extraordinary social meaning of prescriptive technologies dawned on me."

**Holistic technologies** involve specialization by product. "Artisans, be they potters, weavers, metal-smiths, or cooks, control the process of their own work from beginning to end. Their hands and minds make situational decisions as the work proceeds." The artisan draws on experience, applies it to unique situations, and retains control over the creative process.

**Prescriptive technologies** involve specialization by process. "Each step is carried out by a separate worker, or group of workers, who need to be familiar only with the skills of performing that one step." Control migrates from worker to manager. The critical condensation: "In political terms, prescriptive technologies are designs for compliance."

The implications cascade. Prescriptive technologies don't just organize labor differently — they produce a different kind of person. "Today's real world of technology is characterized by the dominance of prescriptive technologies... We live in a culture of compliance, that we are ever more conditioned to accept orthodoxy as normal, and to accept that there is only one way of doing 'it.'" Worse: **"The acculturation to compliance and conformity has, in turn, accelerated the use of prescriptive technologies in administrative, government, and social services. The same development has diminished resistance to the programming of people."**

### **The production model invading growth domains**

Franklin distinguishes between growth models (organic, unpredictable, requiring discovery of conditions) and production models (mechanical, controllable, predictable). Education is inherently a growth process; so is healthcare, democratic governance, and community building. Yet the production model colonizes them all: "The unchallenged prevalence of the production model in the mindset and political discourse of our time, and the model's misapplication to blatantly inappropriate situations, seems to me an indication of just how far technology as practice has modified our culture."

Her feminist analysis reveals that prescriptive technologies reproduce "male patterns of hierarchy, authoritarianism, competition and exclusion," while women's traditional work — household management, childcare, coping with crisis — represents a different paradigm: holistic, adaptive, aimed at "minimizing disaster" rather than "maximizing gain."

### **AI as prescriptive technology perfected**

Franklin's framework is almost uncannily prescient for AI-driven workplaces. Algorithmic management in Amazon warehouses, Uber's gig economy, and platform labor replicate the prescriptive pattern exactly: tasks broken into algorithmically prescribed steps, workers monitored in real-time, control separated from execution, deviation from the algorithm punished. As Meredith Whittaker (president of Signal and co-founder of the AI Now Institute, directly influenced by Franklin) has argued, prescriptive technologies "create a world in which it's normal to do what we're told, and to do so without the ability to control and shape the process or the outcome."

Franklin's final recorded statement to Whittaker, in a December 2015 Skype call, cuts to the heart of the book's argument: **"There is no technology for justice. There is only justice."**

Her seven-criteria checklist for evaluating technologies asks whether a technology promotes justice, restores reciprocity, favors people over machines, minimizes disaster rather than maximizes gain, and favors the reversible over the irreversible. Her closing principle: "The viability of technology, like democracy, depends in the end on the practice of justice and on the enforcement of limits to power."

### **Connections and limitations**

Franklin connects to Vallor through their shared insight that technology transforms habits and habits transform character — prescriptive technologies produce compliance, virtue ethics demands deliberate cultivation of judgment. Her concept of compliance culture directly describes the evolutionary trap: populations trained to follow algorithmic instructions become less capable of democratic self-governance, which accelerates adoption of more prescriptive systems. Her production/growth distinction maps onto the Promethean gap: production models give us power without wisdom.

A Marxist critique from Sam Popowich argues Franklin is fundamentally a liberal positivist who explains *that* technology has changed society but cannot explain *why* — she cannot account for the structural logic of capitalism driving technological innovation. The holistic/prescriptive binary may be too clean (most technologies, like word processors, involve both elements). She may romanticize craft production. And her analysis, developed primarily from industrial technologies, requires extension for information technologies where users are also producers. But these limitations do not diminish her central insight: the social mortgage of prescriptive technologies is a culture of compliance that corrodes democratic capacity.

## **III. Simone Weil and the moral faculty AI most endangers**

### **Attention as the foundation of ethical life**

Simone Weil (1909–1943), the French philosopher, mystic, and factory worker who died at thirty-four, produced a body of work whose relevance to the AI age is almost eerie. Her central concept — **attention** as the highest moral faculty — speaks directly to what algorithmic systems capture, fragment, and commodify.

Weil's famous claim, written in a letter to the poet Joë Bousquet on April 13, 1942, is often quoted out of context: **"Attention is the rarest and purest form of generosity."** But its full meaning requires understanding what Weil means by attention — which is radically different from ordinary usage.

In her essay "Reflections on the Right Use of School Studies with a View to the Love of God" (written circa 1942, published in *Waiting for God*), Weil argues that "the development of the faculty of attention forms the real object and almost the sole interest of studies." The purpose of studying geometry or Latin prose is not mastery of the subject but cultivation of a capacity. And this capacity is not willful concentration — "Most often attention is confused with a kind of muscular effort" involving contracted brows and stiffened muscles. True attention is the opposite: "Attention consists of suspending our thought, leaving it detached, empty and ready to be penetrated by the object." It is **receptive, not grasping**; emptying, not filling.

The connection between academic attention and moral attention is Weil's most radical move. A student who learns genuine attention through geometry can one day "make us better able to give someone in affliction exactly the help required to save him, at the supreme moment of his need." Moral attention means being able to say to a suffering person: "What are you going through?" — and mean it. "The capacity to give one's attention to a sufferer is a very rare and difficult thing; it is almost a miracle; it is a miracle."

### **Decreation, affliction, and the factory**

Weil's concept of **decreation** deepens the understanding of what attention requires. "Decreation: to make something created pass into the uncreated. Destruction: to make something created pass into nothingness. A blameworthy substitute for decreation." Where destruction annihilates, decreation is a voluntary withdrawal of ego — making space for reality to appear. "We should renounce being something. That is our only good." In attention, "one renounces one's ego in order to receive the world without the interference of one's limited and consumptive perspective."

Her year working in Parisian factories (1934–35) — at Alsthom, Carnaud, and Renault — provided the experiential foundation. What she discovered was not the physical suffering she expected but something worse: **humiliation that produced not rebellion but docility**. "A modern factory reaches perhaps almost the limit of horror. Everybody in it is constantly harassed and kept on edge by the interference of extraneous wills while the soul is left in cold and desolate misery. What man needs is silence and warmth; what he is given is an icy pandemonium." A co-worker told her that after a year, "one no longer suffers, although one remains in a sort of stupor" — which Weil recognized as "the lowest stage of degradation." She wrote that the experience branded her forever: "There I received for ever the mark of a slave."

The factory demanded a degraded form of attention (constant alertness to machines and orders) while destroying the capacity for genuine attention (reflection, moral perception, creative thought). This is precisely the dynamic of modern algorithmic platforms.

### **The attention economy as moral catastrophe**

Multiple scholars have now connected Weil's framework to AI and the attention economy. Kathryn Lawson, writing in 2025, coins the term **"ersatz attention"** to describe what algorithms produce: "social media algorithms don't just distract us but commit a deeper form of violence, reshaping our minds and stripping away genuine human connection. Each scroll surrenders our thinking to machines designed solely to keep us engaged, replacing what Weil saw as true attention — essential for ethics and truth — with a hollow substitute."

April Owens, in the *Hedgehog Review* (2025), connects the dots directly: "Abetted by algorithms designed to hijack our deep psychological craving for novel stimuli, our smartphones, those digital slot machines in our pockets, deliver us an unending stream of amusement. In doing so, they rescue us from boredom by flooding the mental space that could be used to cultivate attention."

The parallel to Weil's factory is structural, not metaphorical. The factory demanded surface attention while destroying deep attention. Algorithmic platforms do the same — they demand constant engagement metrics while systematically preventing the patient, ego-dissolving, morally generative attention Weil describes. The attention economy does not merely distract; it corrodes the very faculty that Weil identifies as the foundation of all moral and spiritual life.

### **Weil to Murdoch to Vallor: a lineage of attention**

Weil's concept flows directly to Iris Murdoch, who acknowledged the debt explicitly: "I have used the word 'attention', which I borrow from Simone Weil, to express the idea of a just and loving gaze directed upon an individual reality. I believe this to be the characteristic and proper mark of the active moral agent." Murdoch secularized and stabilized the concept — preserving its ethical core while moderating Weil's more radical mystical claims.

The lineage continues to Shannon Vallor, whose *Technology and the Virtues* identifies "perceptual attention to moral salience" as one of seven core elements of moral self-cultivation. While Vallor does not cite Weil directly, the resonance is deep: attention as a cultivated moral capacity necessary for navigating what Vallor calls "acute technosocial opacity." The argument that AI endangers attention is simultaneously an argument about the erosion of moral development — the very thesis of the book.

### **Limitations**

Weil's extreme asceticism and self-denial raise legitimate concerns. Her refusal to eat adequately during her final illness, her sometimes masochistic spirituality, and her concept of decreation — which Robert Zaretsky says questions "not just how I have lived my life but why I should have ever bothered to live in the first place" — may make her a difficult exemplar. Her relationship with Judaism remains deeply contested; she wrote harshly about the Hebrew Bible during the Holocaust itself, leading some scholars to charge anti-Semitism. Whether her theological framework translates to secular contexts is debatable, though Murdoch's successful secularization demonstrates it can. And her concept may be too individualistic — focused on personal moral transformation rather than structural change. But as one piece of a larger argument about the human capacities AI threatens, her voice is irreplaceable.

## **IV. Donna Haraway's third path between salvation and doom**

### **Refusing to choose between techno-utopia and apocalypse**

Donna Haraway (born 1944), professor emerita at UC Santa Cruz's History of Consciousness Department, offers what may be the most urgently needed intellectual contribution to AI discourse: a sophisticated refusal of the binary that dominates it. In *Staying with the Trouble: Making Kin in the Chthulucene* (2016), she rejects both "comic faith in technofixes" — the belief that "technology will somehow come to the rescue of its naughty but very clever children" — and the equally destructive conviction that "the game is over, it's too late, there's no sense trying to make anything any better."

Against both, she proposes staying with the trouble: "In urgent times, many of us are tempted to address trouble in terms of making an imagined future safe, of stopping something from happening that looms in the future, of clearing away the present and the past in order to make futures for coming generations. Staying with the trouble does not require such a relationship to times called the future. In fact, staying with the trouble requires learning to be truly present, not as a vanishing pivot between awful or edenic pasts and apocalyptic or salvific futures, but as mortal critters entwined in myriad unfinished configurations of places, times, matters, meanings."

This maps directly onto the AI discourse landscape. Dario Amodei's "Machines of Loving Grace" essay envisions AI solving virtually all human problems within a decade — classic technofix. X-risk doomers predict civilizational collapse. Both are **future-oriented escapes from the present**, and both short-circuit the difficult work of developing human capacities to live well with powerful technologies. Haraway's alternative demands engagement with the messy, partial, ongoing present.

### **Response-ability, string figures, and the Chthulucene**

Haraway's concept of **response-ability** — deliberately hyphenated — emphasizes the ability to respond as a cultivated capacity, not just responsibility as obligation. "We are all responsible to and for shaping conditions for multispecies flourishing in the face of terrible histories... but we are not all response-able in the same ways." Response-ability is developed in relation to specific others, not as abstract principle. It is relational, situated, embodied — and it cannot be outsourced to algorithms or compliance frameworks.

Her use of **string figures** (cat's cradle) as a methodological metaphor models collaborative thinking against linear progress narratives. Patterns are passed back and forth between hands; the game requires receiving from another and adding something new. "It matters what stories tell stories, what concepts think concepts... what ties tie ties." The SF abbreviation deliberately encompasses "science fiction, speculative fabulation, speculative feminism, science fact, string figures, so far." The multiple meanings refuse the kind of totalizing clarity that AI discourse craves.

The **Chthulucene** — from Greek roots *khthôn* (earth) and *kainos* (now/new), pointedly not from Lovecraft — is Haraway's alternative to both Anthropocene (too centered on Man as singular agent) and Capitalocene (too focused on capital). It emphasizes tentacular, multispecies entanglement: "Nobody lives everywhere; everybody lives somewhere. Nothing is connected to everything; everything is connected to something." This reframes AI not as a universal force but as always situated, always partial, always entangled with specific communities and power structures.

### **From cyborg to companion species to compost**

Understanding Haraway's trajectory illuminates her contribution. The *Cyborg Manifesto* (1985) — "By the late twentieth century, our time, a mythic time, we are all chimeras, theorized and fabricated hybrids of machine and organism; in short, we are cyborgs" — celebrated boundary-crossing and hybridity against purity politics. It remains brilliant: "The boundary between science fiction and social reality is an optical illusion." But Haraway moved beyond the cyborg to companion species (emphasizing fleshy, bodily encounters) and then to the Chthulucene (full multispecies entanglement). Each figure broadens the scope and deepens the demand for relational engagement over abstract theorizing.

Her critique of the Anthropocene narrative is devastating: "The story of Species Man as the agent of the Anthropocene is an almost laughable rerun of the great phallic humanizing and modernizing Adventure, where man, made in the image of a vanished god, takes on superpowers in his secular-sacred ascent, only to end in tragic detumescence, once again." The AI salvation narrative is precisely this Adventure recycled.

### **Connections and limitations**

Haraway's response-ability and Vallor's technomoral virtues are deeply complementary — both reject rule-based approaches as insufficient, both emphasize cultivated capacities over fixed rules, both insist that character matters more than compliance. The key difference: Vallor works within recognizable philosophical traditions (Aristotle, Confucius, Buddhism); Haraway works from feminist science and technology studies and biology. Haraway is more radical in decentering the human.

Her string figures metaphor models polycentric, adaptive governance for AI — collaborative, ongoing, partial, embodied — rather than either laissez-faire or command-and-control. Her insistence on staying present counters the Promethean temptation to leap ahead to imagined futures.

The limitations are significant. Haraway's prose is notoriously dense and allusive, limiting accessibility. Whether "staying with the trouble" provides enough guidance for concrete action remains genuinely unclear — one reviewer compared it to "wishing for a great conversation and drinks while a meteor streaks towards Earth." Her "Make Kin Not Babies" slogan has drawn sharp feminist criticism for echoing neo-imperialist population control discourse. The Chthulucene concept may be too vague for analytical work, and its sonic resemblance to Lovecraft's Cthulhu — despite different etymology — is unfortunate given Lovecraft's virulent racism. For a book arguing we need better humans to deal with AI, Haraway's framework may be too abstract for the concrete policy domain. But as philosophical reorientation — a way of *thinking about* AI that escapes the salvation/doom trap — it is unmatched.

## **V. Norbert Wiener saw it all coming in 1950**

### **The father of cybernetics who refused his own creation**

Norbert Wiener (1894–1964), the MIT mathematician who coined the term "cybernetics" and essentially founded the science of control and communication, occupies a unique position in the intellectual history of AI: **the creator who became the prophet warning against his creation**. His child-prodigy biography (B.S. at 14, Ph.D. at 18\) gave him the technical authority; his humanistic philosophy gave him the moral clarity.

*The Human Use of Human Beings: Cybernetics and Society* (1950, revised 1954\) translates cybernetic theory into social and ethical argument. Its central demand is blunt: machines must serve human purposes, not replace human judgment. "Any use of a human being in which less is demanded of him and less is attributed to him than his full status is a degradation and a waste." And the corollary, which speaks directly to algorithmic management: "When human atoms are knit into an organization in which they are used, not in their full right as responsible human beings, but as cogs and levers and rods, it matters little that their raw material is flesh and blood. What is used as an element in a machine, is in fact an element in the machine."

Wiener saw automation as economic slave labor: "The automatic machine is the precise economic equivalent of slave labor. Any labor which competes with slave labor must accept the economic consequences of slave labor." His rebuttal to techno-optimism remains definitive: "The world of the future will be an even more demanding struggle against the limitations of our intelligence, not a comfortable hammock in which we can lie down to be waited upon by our robot slaves."

### **The machine à gouverner and the refusal**

Wiener was so struck by Dominican theologian Dominique Dubarle's 1948 *Le Monde* review of *Cybernetics* — which envisioned a "State apparatus covering all systems of political decisions," a machine that would render the State "the best-informed player at each particular level" and offer every citizen the choice of "either immediate ruin, or planned cooperation" — that he reproduced it across three pages of *The Human Use of Human Beings*. Dubarle called this a "prodigious Leviathan" creating "a world worse than hell for every clear mind." Wiener left the French term *machine à gouverner* untranslated, preserving its weight. The concept maps precisely onto contemporary algorithmic governance: predictive policing, automated welfare systems, algorithmic sentencing.

His ethical stance went beyond theory. In January 1947, he published **"A Scientist Rebels"** in the *Atlantic Monthly*, refusing a Boeing researcher's request for his wartime missile-guidance research: "The policy of the government itself during and after the war, say in the bombing of Hiroshima and Nagasaki, has made it clear that to provide scientific information is not a necessarily innocent act, and may entail the gravest consequences." He wrote to UAW President Walter Reuther warning about automation's impact on workers. He left computer science because military agencies funded it. The cost was real — the FBI opened a dossier on him, his career suffered, his reputation was damaged.

### **Prescience that borders on prophecy**

By 2026, Wiener's warnings read less like prediction than description:

* He predicted **automation would displace skilled knowledge work**, not just manual labor. The current displacement of writers, coders, analysts, and artists fulfills this precisely.  
* He warned that **absent policy intervention, automation gains would concentrate** among owners and managers. The billionaire concentration in AI confirms this.  
* He warned about **machines outpacing human control**: "By the very slowness of our human actions, our effective control of our machines may be nullified. By the time we are able to react to information conveyed by our senses and stop the car we are driving, it may already have run head on into a wall." This predicted the 2010 Flash Crash and the general problem of algorithmic systems operating beyond the speed of human oversight.  
* He warned that **machines transcend their designers' intentions**: "It is my thesis that machines can and do transcend some of the limitations of their designers... although they are theoretically subject to human criticism, such criticism may be ineffective until a time long after it is relevant." This is the alignment problem in embryo.  
* He identified the **"know-how" versus "know-what" gap**: "the modern man, and especially the modern American, however much 'know-how' he may have, has very little 'know-what.'" This is the Promethean gap stated in pragmatic terms.

His rejected 1949 *New York Times* op-ed contains perhaps his most prophetic line: **"The machines will do what we ask them to do and not what we ought to ask them to do."** This remains the single most concise statement of the alignment problem.

### **The contrast with Silicon Valley**

Wiener's humanistic framework stands in near-total opposition to Silicon Valley culture. Where he asked "know-what" before "know-how," Silicon Valley systematically prioritizes capability over purpose. Where he wrote to union leaders about displaced workers, Silicon Valley celebrates "disruption." Where he refused military collaboration, AI labs compete for defense contracts. Where he warned that "the simple faith in progress is not a conviction belonging to strength, but one belonging to acquiescence and hence to weakness," Amodei's "Machines of Loving Grace" envisions AI solving virtually all human problems within a decade.

Wiener would likely view Amodei's essay as a sophisticated version of his "comfortable hammock" delusion. His alternative: "We can be humble and live a good life with the aid of the machines, or we can be arrogant and die."

### **Connections and limitations**

Scholars have identified Wiener's ethical framework as deeply Aristotelian — emphasizing human flourishing through community participation, character formation over rule-following, practical wisdom alongside technical knowledge. This connects him directly to Vallor's technomoral virtues project. His statement that "we have modified our environment so radically that we must now modify ourselves in order to exist in this new environment" is essentially a description of the Promethean gap and an anticipation of the book's thesis.

The deepest irony is also the deepest criticism: **cybernetic principles — feedback, control, information processing — became the conceptual foundation for precisely the surveillance, algorithmic governance, and automated decision-making Wiener warned against**. The very framework of treating social systems as information-processing systems can be used to justify reducing humans to data points. Wiener acknowledged this tension, writing that cybernetics "belonged to the age, and the most any of us can do by suppression is to put the development of the subject into the hands of the most irresponsible and most venal of our engineers." He could not control what he had helped create — a fact that itself illustrates the Promethean gap.

His proposed solutions were also vague — educate the public, confine research to humane purposes, maintain communication integrity — amounting to moral exhortation without institutional mechanisms. And modern AI (deep learning, language models) goes far beyond the cybernetic feedback systems he envisioned. But his ethical framework — human dignity, meaningful control, communication integrity, scientists' responsibility — remains applicable regardless of the specific technology.

## **VI. Who answers when the algorithm harms?**

### **The collective responsibility literature confronts AI**

The question of who is morally responsible when AI causes harm exposes the inadequacy of frameworks designed for a world where individual humans made identifiable decisions. Six distinct bodies of thought converge on this problem, each illuminating a different facet.

**Philip Pettit and Christian List's** *Group Agency* (2011) establishes that corporate entities can be genuine agents — not metaphorically but functionally, possessing representational states (beliefs), motivational states (desires), and the capacity to act on them. Their key insight is the **discursive dilemma**: when a group votes on logically interconnected propositions, collective judgments can be logically inconsistent even when every individual member's judgments are consistent. A majority may endorse Premise A, a majority may endorse Premise B, yet a majority may reject the conclusion A∧B. This reveals that group agency is genuinely irreducible to individual members — and therefore that groups can bear moral responsibility that does not reduce to the sum of individual responsibilities. For AI, this supports holding companies like OpenAI, Google, and Meta responsible as corporate agents for decisions that no individual within them specifically authorized.

**Virginia Held**, in her landmark 1970 paper "Can a Random Collection of Individuals be Morally Responsible?", pushed further by asking whether even *unorganized* groups bear moral responsibility. Her scenario: three pedestrians encounter a man trapped under rubble with a bleeding leg. They fail to organize themselves, and he dies. Held argues the group was "morally responsible for failing to transform itself into an organized group capable of taking action rather than inaction." The distributed AI ecosystem — data collectors, algorithm designers, trainers, deployers, users — resembles Held's random collection. None has a unified decision-making structure, yet collectively they produce discriminatory outcomes that no individual intended. Held's framework says they bear collective responsibility to *organize themselves* into governance structures.

### **The problem of many hands grows new fingers**

**Dennis Thompson** coined the concept of the **"problem of many hands"** in 1980 for political accountability: "Because many different officials contribute in many ways to decisions and policies of government, it is difficult even in principle to identify who is morally responsible for political outcomes." **Helen Nissenbaum** applied this to computing in her crucial 1996 paper "Accountability in a Computerized Society," identifying four barriers: many hands (multiple contributors obscure individual responsibility), bugs (treating errors as inevitable), blaming the computer (attributing agency to machines), and software ownership without liability. Her warning: "Unless we pursue means for reversing this erosion of accountability, there will be significant numbers of harms and risks for which no one is answerable and about which nothing is done."

AI intensifies every one of Nissenbaum's barriers. A 2025 analysis proposes the "many-agents-many-levels-many-interactions" (M3) framework, arguing that large language model deployment "exacerbates the long-standing problem of 'many hands'" across multiple types of agents at multiple organizational levels. Andreas Matthias's landmark 2004 paper, "The Responsibility Gap," identified the core issue: learning machines make outcomes that their creators cannot predict, creating a gap that "cannot be bridged by traditional concepts of responsibility ascription." Shannon Vallor and colleagues have more recently identified a "vulnerability gap" — autonomous systems lack "the moral and emotional experiences needed to be vulnerable to responsibility practices," and the humans within the system "are often themselves cut off from the vulnerability needed" to bear responsibility.

### **Structural injustice and the banality of algorithmic evil**

**Iris Marion Young's** posthumous *Responsibility for Justice* (2011) provides the framework most suited to algorithmic bias. Structural injustice, she argues, "occurs as a consequence of many individuals and institutions acting to pursue their particular goals and interests, for the most part within the limits of accepted rules and norms." It cannot be traced to individual wrongdoing. Her **social connection model** of responsibility is forward-looking and shared: all agents who contribute to structural processes producing injustice have responsibility to remedy them. This directly illuminates how AI training data encodes existing inequalities, deployment reproduces them, and no individual is "at fault" in the liability sense — yet everyone connected to the supply chain shares political responsibility.

**Hannah Arendt's** concept of the banality of evil, from *Eichmann in Jerusalem* (1963), gains new force in the algorithmic age. Eichmann's crime was not malice but **thoughtlessness** — "something by no means identical with stupidity." He "merely, to put the matter colloquially, never realized what he was doing." Daniel McQuillan has drawn the connection directly: algorithmic governance enables a new form of Eichmann's thoughtlessness. The caseworker who denies benefits because "the algorithm said so," the judge who follows a sentencing recommendation without questioning it, the HR manager who rejects candidates based on automated screening — all replicate the structure of following orders without engaging moral judgment. Arendt's warning from *The Human Condition* is precise: we risk becoming **"thoughtless creatures at the mercy of every gadget which is technically possible."**

As Paula Boddington has argued: "As long as AI systems are designed to follow codes of ethics or particular normative ethical theories chosen by us and programmed in them, they are Eichmanns destined to commit evil." Intelligence alone is insufficient for ethical decision-making — what is needed is the *thinking activity* that Arendt identified as the antidote to evil: internal dialogue, the capacity to examine one's actions from the perspective of others.

### **The adequacy question**

Whether existing moral responsibility frameworks can handle AI remains genuinely contested. Deflationists like Alexander Tigard argue there is "no techno-responsibility gap" — the impression of gaps "only occurs if we gloss over the complexity of the moral situation." The most sophisticated position (Santoni de Sio and Mecacci) holds that existing frameworks need significant supplementation — through concepts like meaningful human control, forward-looking responsibility, and distributed accountability — but are not obsolete.

The deepest tension in the literature maps onto the book's central question: **do we need better individuals, better collectives, or better structures?** Arendt's emphasis on personal thinking and judgment pulls toward individual moral development. Young's structural framework pulls toward institutional redesign. Held and Pettit pull toward collective agency and organizational responsibility. The most defensible answer — and the one the literature collectively supports — is all three, in complementary relation. Individual virtue without structural reform is impotent; structural reform without individual moral capacity is empty. As Arendt herself warned: "When all are guilty, no one is; confessions of collective guilt are the best possible safeguard against the discovery of culprits."

## **The convergence these six voices demand**

These thinkers did not collaborate and would not agree on much. Illich the anarchist priest, Franklin the Quaker metallurgist, Weil the self-starving mystic, Haraway the postmodern feminist, Wiener the difficult mathematician, and the analytic philosophers of collective responsibility inhabit different intellectual worlds. Yet they converge on a single structural insight: **the deepest danger of powerful technology is not what it does to the world but what it does to the people who use it**. Illich's second watershed degrades human autonomy. Franklin's prescriptive technologies produce compliance. Weil's degraded attention destroys moral perception. Haraway's technofix fantasies prevent genuine engagement. Wiener's automation replaces human judgment. And the collective responsibility literature reveals that our very capacity to attribute moral agency dissolves as systems grow more complex.

Each thinker also points toward the remedy — and it is never technological. Illich calls for convivial tools that restore autonomy. Franklin demands holistic practices and limits to power. Weil prescribes the cultivation of genuine attention. Haraway insists on staying present and developing response-ability. Wiener demands that "know-what" precede "know-how." The responsibility theorists call for organized collective agency and, above all, for the preservation of thinking — Arendt's internal dialogue that is the last defense against the banality of algorithmic evil.

For the book's argument, these six voices provide something no amount of policy analysis can: **historical and philosophical depth demonstrating that the human development deficit is not a new problem created by AI but an old problem AI makes catastrophic**. The Promethean gap has been widening since the industrial revolution. AI does not create the gap — it makes it unsurvivable. The response cannot be merely to govern AI better, though that matters. It must also be to develop the human capacities — attention, judgment, response-ability, practical wisdom, collective moral agency — that these thinkers spent their lives articulating. The question is not whether we can build better machines. It is whether we can build better humans fast enough to survive the machines we have already built.
