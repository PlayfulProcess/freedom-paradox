### **1\. INTELLIGENCE AS POTENTIALLY MALADAPTIVE: The Evolutionary Case**

This is the deepest foundation for your book, because it reframes the entire AI debate. The question isn't just "is this particular technology dangerous?" — it's "is the *kind of thing we are* inherently self-undermining at scale?"

**The Cognitive Niche Hypothesis (Steven Pinker, via Tooby & DeVore)**

Pinker frames the cognitive niche as an adaptation to a knowledge-using, socially interdependent lifestyle — the ability to overcome the evolutionary fixed defenses of other organisms through reasoning, including weapons, traps, and coordinated strategies. This explains many zoologically unusual traits in Homo sapiens: our complex toolkit, wide range of habitats, extended childhoods, hypersociality, division into cultures, and language.

But here's the critical point that Pinker himself acknowledges: intelligence, which depends on a large brain, is not a free good in evolution. Its costs include the metabolic demands of expensive neural tissue, compromises in the anatomy of the female pelvis, and the risks of harm from birth, falls, and the mutation and parasite load carried by such a complex organ.

Intelligence was *selected for* under very specific conditions — small-group social coordination, hunting, foraging. The question your book raises: what happens when the same cognitive machinery that evolved for coordinating a hunting party gets applied to nuclear weapons, ecological extraction, and now artificial superintelligence?

**Sternberg's Adaptive Intelligence Framework**

Robert Sternberg's recent work (PMC, 2019\) makes the most pointed version of your argument. He argues that intelligence as conventionally measured has become decoupled from actual biological adaptation. Someone could be highly creative in devising a weapon that destroys civilization, highly analytical in making sure it works, highly practical in ensuring it can be delivered — but extremely unwise in having undertaken the project in the first place. They would still be considered quite intelligent despite potentially destroying all of humanity.

His key provocation: Allowing a potentially avoidable mass extinction of one's own species would seem like the ultimate act of biologically maladaptive behavior. And: creating an uninhabitable planet for ourselves, our children, and future generations is definitely biologically maladaptive, no matter where such behavior falls on the general-intelligence scale.

Sternberg connects this directly to the Fermi Paradox: One explanation for why we haven't found intelligent extraterrestrial life might be that intelligent life, by its nature, is ultimately self-destructive — that once it reaches a certain point in the development of civilization, it turns on itself and destroys itself. This is the "self-destruction hypothesis" — and it maps *exactly* onto your Prometheus thesis.

**The Great Filter as Intelligence Trap**

Robin Hanson's Great Filter hypothesis proposes that given the failure to find any extraterrestrial civilizations, something acts to reduce the vast number of sites where intelligent life might arise to the tiny number of advanced civilizations actually observed — currently just one.

The terrifying implication: if intelligence is common, then we have a lot to worry about. That means it's easy for intelligent species to arise on a planet, but something stops them from going galactic — most likely, the species destroying itself. Any species that can travel into space must be able to harness incredible amounts of energy — energy that could very easily be turned to destructive purposes before that species has learned to live on other worlds.

This is *exactly* your Prometheus argument cast in cosmological terms: power acquired before the grammars to hold it. And AI is now being proposed as the latest — and possibly final — Great Filter candidate. Michael Garrett of the University of Manchester argues that the emergence of artificial superintelligence could lead to the destruction of civilizations, noting that "even before AI becomes superintelligent and potentially autonomous, it is likely to be weaponized by competing groups within biological civilizations seeking to outdo one another."

His conclusion is chilling: "If ASI limits the communicative lifespan of advanced civilizations to a few hundred years, then only a handful of communicating civilizations are likely to be concurrently present in the Milky Way."

### **3\. THE MORAL LANDSCAPE: What Are Our Actual Obligations?**

**Shannon Vallor: Technomoral Virtues**

Vallor is arguably the most important philosopher for your project. She draws from Aristotle, Confucius, and Buddhist virtue traditions to develop the concept of "technomoral wisdom" — the character traits we need to flourish in rapidly changing technological conditions. Her key Aristotelian concepts include eudaimonia (human flourishing), phronesis (practical wisdom for discerning right action in specific circumstances), and the importance of habituation.

Vallor insists that "this habit that we have of treating technology and morality as entirely independent, separate areas of study is actually part of the problem of why our society is struggling to align innovation and economic growth with social and political flourishing."

Her 2024 book *The AI Mirror* pushes further. It was named one of the Best Summer Books of 2024 by the Financial Times, and Safiya Noble called it "one of the most important and poetic books we can read right now to find our way through the AI-hype headlines."

Vallor explains: "What technologies do when they are new is they transform our habits, they transform the things that we do every day. And virtue ethics says that it's precisely the things that you do every day that determine the shape of your character and your ability to live well with others."

Her concept of **moral debt** is powerful: "A lot of businesses, a lot of governments are rushing to use AI to save time and save costs, but they're often implementing AI in ways that are not robust, not particularly safe, that tend to amplify social injustices and inequalities. And those costs are going to come due; those costs always come due."

In her Senate testimony, she invoked Hans Jonas's *The Imperative of Responsibility* and Langdon Winner's *The Whale and the Reactor* — warning against the view that practical wisdom "is not a political tool that democratic peoples can wield to responsibly craft better futures, but an outmoded relic waiting for the scrap heap."

**The Pinker Institutional Insight**

There's a remarkable passage in Pinker's cognitive niche paper that speaks to your grammars argument. He notes that just as successful science requires people to reassign their cognitive faculties in unprecedented ways, successful organizations require people to reassign their social faculties in evolutionarily unprecedented ways. In universities, for example, the mindset of communal sharing must be applied to ideas; the evaluation of ideas must be wrenched away from the mindset of authority. "These radically new rules for relationships are the basis for open debate and peer review in scholarship, and for the checks and balances and accounting systems found in other modern institutions."

In other words: **grammars**. The institutional structures that allow intelligence to be exercised responsibly *are not natural outgrowths of intelligence itself* — they are hard-won cultural inventions that must be deliberately constructed and maintained. This is your argument.

### **3\. CO-REGULATION SCIENCE APPLIED TO AI: Your Unique Contribution**

This is where your book does something no one else has done. The co-regulation literature provides a biological model for what responsible relationship with powerful technology might look like — and it exposes exactly what current AI development gets wrong.

**The Core Framework**

Polyvagal Theory, developed by Stephen Porges, explains how autonomic regulation shapes the body's ongoing capacity to maintain stability, support health, and adapt to changing conditions. This regulation operates as a dynamic feedback system, continuously integrating signals from the body, the brain, the environment, and social interactions.

Three organizing principles matter for your argument:

**Hierarchical organization** — autonomic regulation reflects circuits shaped by evolution that are recruited based on context. **Neuroception** — the set of neural processes that continuously evaluate cues of safety, danger, and life threat, operating largely outside of conscious awareness. **Co-regulation** — the need for regulation to be maintained and restored through relationships.

**Co-Regulation as the Missing Grammar**

Deb Dana (Porges's collaborator) frames it beautifully: "Co-regulation creates a physiological platform of safety that supports a psychological story of security that then leads to social engagement. The autonomic nervous systems of two individuals find sanctuary in a co-created experience of connection."

And here's the key insight for your book: "We live in a culture that encourages autonomy and independence, and yet we need to remember that we are wired to live in connection. Co-regulation is a necessary ingredient for physical and emotional wellbeing."

Co-regulation refers to the process by which one individual's autonomic nervous system is calmed, balanced, or energized through interaction with another individual. This concept stems from the understanding that humans are social creatures whose nervous systems respond to the presence and signals of others.

The process depends on cues of safety — often non-verbal signals such as tone of voice, facial expressions, and body language. When safety is detected, our nervous system works to calm us. Interacting with a person whom you trust deeply creates a safe environment. Our nervous system is hard-wired to recognize the cues that represent safety, all of which come from another person's facial expressions and tone of voice.

**Why This Matters for AI: The Dysregulation Thesis**

Here's the argument your book can make that no one else is making:

AI interaction, as currently designed, *cannot* co-regulate. It provides cognitive output without the embodied signals of safety that our nervous system requires. It has no face (or a simulated one), no vagal tone, no breathing rhythm to synchronize with. It operates in exactly the domain that bypasses our social engagement system.

Porges's framework says that "story follows state" — before the brain understands and makes meaning of an experience, the autonomic nervous system has assessed the situation and initiated a response. When we interact with AI, our neuroception is receiving... nothing. No safety cues. No threat cues. An absence — which the nervous system may interpret as a subtle, chronic form of disconnection.

This connects directly to your hearth-progression argument (fire → TV → personal screens): each step in the sequence *degrades the co-regulatory field*. The shared fire offered face-to-face presence, warmth, vocal rhythms. Television offered parasocial presence but no bidirectionality. Personal screens offer isolation disguised as connection. And AI offers something new: a *simulacrum of relationship* that activates our social cognition without activating our social engagement system.

Porges's most recent work (2025) emphasizes that the ventral vagal complex "allows mammals to coordinate visceral regulation with behavioral and relational engagement. This capacity for autonomic flexibility underlies the dynamic regulation of connection, safety, and co-regulation." AI strips away this coordination entirely. It gives us the *content* of relationship — responsiveness, apparent understanding, information — without the *physiology* of relationship.

**The Design Implication**

Your book can argue that AI systems designed without co-regulatory principles aren't just ethically problematic — they're *biologically dysregulating*. And it can propose, drawing on your Recursive.eco work, what AI designed *with* co-regulatory awareness might look like: technology that supports rather than bypasses the embodied relational circuits we evolved to depend on. Not "gateway not gatekeeper" — but technology that understands when to open and when to close, when to engage and when to redirect people toward embodied human connection.

### **4\. UBUNTU AND THE POSTCOLONIAL CRITIQUE: From Rationality to Relationality**

This is the stream that connects your colonial reflex analysis to the AI debate and provides the deepest philosophical alternative to the dominant paradigm.

**Mhlambi's Central Argument**

Sabelo Mhlambi traces the problem to the concept of personhood itself: "In traditional Western philosophy, a person is rational and autonomous. Aristotle thought of man as the rational animal. Descartes placed rationality as the distinguishing essence of personhood: 'I think therefore I am.' This notion of personhood creates a state of individualism where 'man' using 'rationality' is to become the 'master of nature.'"

"In AI we have taken this individualistic, rational personality view all the way. There's an assumption that we can use rationality to objectively come to some sort of ground truth."

The Ubuntu alternative is radical: Mhlambi's paper for the Carr Center at Harvard "uses the relational Sub-Saharan African philosophy of Ubuntu to show that the harms caused by artificial intelligence are in essence violations of Ubuntu's relational personhood and relational model of the universe."

The paper aims to shift the centred belief that a person is a person through being rational. Instead, Mhlambi argues that it is how a person endeavors to emphasize the relationality between humans that marks them as a person. Personhood is no longer presented with the benchmark of rationality, but rather represented by the state of relationality.

**The Core Principle and Its Implications**

The core principle of Ubuntu is: "Umu-Ntu ngumu-Ntu nga ba-Ntu" — a person is a person through other persons. How one relates to others is interconnected with one's personhood and measure of ethics. Under this notion, relationality is emphasized, and the successes and failures of individuals are equally the successes and failures of the community.

In Ubuntu, to be "human" is to affirm the humanity of others — emphasizing compassion, sharing, and mutual respect — which stands in contrast to AI systems that currently treat people as data points or consumers. Incorporating Ubuntu's emphasis on interconnectedness would mean AI is evaluated by how it enhances communal life and dignity, not just by accuracy or efficiency.

**The Reconciliation Model**

This is where Ubuntu becomes more than critique — it becomes a *grammar*:

"Ubuntu extends community and personhood to all people because in African knowledge systems, everything and everyone is intricately and inextricably interconnected. The goal of Ubuntu is to restore harmony in this interconnected whole." Mhlambi points to the South African Truth and Reconciliation Commission and the Rwandan *gacaca* community courts as examples: after the genocide, the Rwandan government "chose Ubuntu and instituted community courts, a process meant to restore the victim and re-integrate the offender back into society."

**The Decolonial Critique of AI Ethics Itself**

Mhlambi's decolonial AI work examines the underlying systems built on legacies of slavery, genocide, and colonialism that shape how we build AI, how we use it, and the problematic role it plays in society. The core idea is that "the legacy of colonialism continues today, it is just happening in different form — in literature this is described as 'coloniality.'"

In a further paper with Simona Tiribelli, Mhlambi argues that the principle of autonomy as currently formalized in AI ethics "expresses only a mainstream mainly liberal notion of autonomy as rational self-determination, derived from Western traditional philosophy" — and that adherence to this principle "does not only fail to address many ways in which people's autonomy can be violated, but also to grasp a broader range of AI-empowered harms profoundly tied to the legacy of colonization."

The practical insight: "By starting from a point of relationality, things that are ultimately harmful to fellow human beings and the world around us cannot be rationalized." This is the Ubuntu answer to the intelligence-as-maladaptive problem: rationality *alone* can always rationalize destruction. Only relationality — the felt recognition that my personhood depends on yours — creates an actual barrier.

Mhlambi emphasizes that "there is no need for a universal system, rather one that accommodates the plethora of different viewpoints present in the world." His key observation: "my worldview is always incomplete."

**Connecting Ubuntu to Your Other Streams**

The convergence here is extraordinary:

* **Ubuntu ↔ Co-regulation**: Both say that the self is constituted *through* relationship, not prior to it. The nervous system (Porges) and personhood itself (Ubuntu) are relational all the way down.  
* **Ubuntu ↔ Tillich**: Tillich's "ground of being" is the ontological version of Ubuntu's interconnected whole. His critique of theological theism (God as tyrant reducing all to objects) maps precisely onto Ubuntu's critique of Cartesian rationality (reducing persons to data points).  
* **Ubuntu ↔ The Prometheus thesis**: The colonial reflex *is* the five-move pattern of decontextualization that strips away relationality. AI built on Cartesian personhood reproduces that pattern at scale.

### **5\. WHAT CAN WE ACTUALLY DO? The Practical Core**

**Shannon Vallor's Technomoral Virtues**

Vallor argues that "what technologies do when they are new is they transform our habits, they transform the things that we do every day. And virtue ethics says that it's precisely the things that you do every day that determine the shape of your character and your ability to live well with others."

Her concept of "technomoral" is a way to insist, "counter to the thesis of technological neutrality, that technology cannot in fact be separated from our values, and vice versa." She argues: "This habit that we have of treating technology and morality as entirely independent, separate areas of study is actually part of the problem of why our society is struggling to align innovation and economic growth with social and political flourishing."

Her cross-cultural framework draws from Aristotle, Confucius, and the Buddha — identifying four fundamental commitments shared across these traditions: a conception of the highest human good, a vision of moral virtues as cultivated states of character, the importance of habituation, and guidance from exemplary persons.

The Aristotelian concept of **phronesis** (practical wisdom) is central: the cultivated ability to determine the best course of action in complex situations where there are no simple rules to follow, and "moral attention" — a reliable sensitivity to the morally salient features of a given situation.

Her concept of **moral debt** applied to AI is devastating: "A lot of businesses, a lot of governments are rushing to use AI to save time and save costs, but they're often implementing AI in ways that are not robust, not particularly safe, that tend to amplify social injustices and inequalities. And those costs are going to come due; those costs always come due."

Her 2024 book *The AI Mirror* pushes further — Safiya Noble called it "one of the most important and poetic books we can read right now."

**Mhlambi's Practical Proposals**

Mhlambi offers concrete Ubuntu-informed suggestions for AI governance: address climate harms from cloud computing; normalize the eradication of inequality through participation of the most disenfranchised at the start of creating technology; treat community data as intellectual property that can be licensed or revoked; fund and provide access to technical skills for the most disenfranchised; and allow users to directly shape how they receive algorithmic recommendations.

The African consensus from the UN Global Pulse in Ghana and Tunisia was that the mistakes of the Global North regarding technology could be a lesson: "first, formulate a set of values to guide technology, as opposed to thinking of values as an afterthought."
