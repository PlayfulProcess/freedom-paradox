# Chapter 7: Not Pushing an Agenda

---

The dominant response to AI risk is to push AI in the "right" direction. This chapter argues that pushing may itself be the problem.

---

## The colonial reflex applied to AI

The five-move pattern: A particular set of values — safety, alignment, beneficial AI — emerges from a specific cultural context. These values are universalized — presented not as one culture's priorities but as the obviously correct framework. Enforced through market dominance and regulatory capture. Local systems displaced — non-Western approaches to technology governance marginalized. The displaced are pathologized — alternative approaches dismissed as "unsafe" or "unscientific."

This is not to say AI safety is unimportant. It is to say that the *form* of the safety effort — centralized, corporate, culturally specific, oriented toward control — may reproduce the very patterns of extraction and domination that the critics identify as the deeper problem.

Bayo Akomolafe: "What if the way we respond to the crisis is part of the crisis?"

---

## The Fred Rogers alternative

Fred Rogers did not push an agenda. He did not lobby Congress for better children's television. He did not write policy papers about child development. He did not create a movement.

He built a grammar.

His program's pace — the slowest on television — was a structural commitment to the child's nervous system over the network's ratings. His direct camera address created the nearest approximation of mutual gaze through a one-way medium. Every design choice served the child's development, not the platform's engagement.

Rogers did not try to fix children's television by pushing it in the right direction. He built an alternative that embodied the values he held — and trusted that the alternative, if it was genuinely good, would speak for itself. Thirty-three seasons. Eight hundred ninety-five episodes.

The grammar approach differs from the agenda approach in structure, not just intent. An agenda says: here is what AI should do. A grammar says: here is a practice for relating to AI that maintains the co-regulatory, embodied, relational capacity that AI systematically degrades. An agenda targets the technology. A grammar targets the humans who use it.

## Ostrom's grammar

Elinor Ostrom's eight design principles for commons governance are a grammar — not a policy. They do not specify what the rules should be. They specify the structural conditions under which communities can determine their own rules.

Applied to AI: **Clear boundaries** — those affected participate in governing it. **Rules adapted to local conditions** — what works in Silicon Valley may not work in Lagos. **Participatory decision-making** — not advisory, binding. **Graduated sanctions** — the same gradient every culture used for fire, for initiatory knowledge, for stories graduated by age. **Accessible conflict resolution** — harm redress that doesn't require a lawyer. **Self-governance rights** — communities determine their own relationship to AI. **Nested enterprises** — the family's grammar, the school's grammar, the nation's framework, international coordination.

The grammar does not specify the rules. It specifies the conditions under which communities determine their own rules and hold themselves accountable.

## Fire without a hearth: the Esalen precedent

Between 1962 and the late 1970s, Esalen Institute became the clearinghouse where powerful consciousness techniques were extracted from their cultural containers and distributed without the ethical structures that had governed them for centuries.

Zen meditation severed from ethics and community. Yoga postures extracted from the eight limbs. Vajrayana practices that require years of foundation offered as weekend workshops. Each tradition had elaborate initiatory frameworks. Each was stripped in transmission.

The Stanford encounter group study documented what happened: a casualty rate approaching ten percent, with the most charismatic leaders producing the most harm. The study's most devastating finding: "high yield requires high risk" was empirically false. Groups that produced the most casualties did not produce the most positive change. Risk and benefit were unrelated.[^1]

Christina Grof founded the Spiritual Emergency Network at Esalen in 1980 — an implicit acknowledgment that the institution's own practices were opening people up and leaving them without support. The very existence of the network testified to the problem: Esalen had been distributing fire without building hearths.

AI is the neo-Promethean folly at civilizational scale. The pattern is identical: powerful techniques extracted from their containers, concentrated without accountability, distributed without relational infrastructure. The grammar approach says: build the hearth before you light the fire. The agenda approach says: the fire is too important to wait for hearths.

Esalen chose the agenda approach. The casualties were documented.

## Fire given as invitation

If AI is fire stolen — Prometheus in Silicon Valley — then the natural response is control. Regulate the theft. Build surveillance and containment. Every agenda-driven approach operates within this frame.

If AI is fire given — an initiatory ordeal — then the response is not control but cultivation. Not "what rules do we impose?" but "what capacity must we develop to hold what has been given?"

The distinction is not between doing nothing and doing something. It is between two fundamentally different postures toward transformative power. The agenda posture says: we know what should happen, and we must make it happen. The initiatory posture says: something is happening to us that we do not yet fully understand, and our task is to develop the capacity to respond with wisdom rather than react with fear.

The fire is given. The invitation is encoded in the ordeal. And the response it calls for is not a program but a practice.
