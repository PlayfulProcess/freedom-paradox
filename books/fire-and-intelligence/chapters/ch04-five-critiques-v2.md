# Chapter 4: Five Critiques Even the Best Case Cannot Escape

---

The most serious challenges to Anthropic's model are not about sincerity or competence. They are about structural forces no single organization can overcome. Each critique reveals a different dimension of the same pattern: "know before you do" — even at its best — reproduces the colonial architecture of identify deficiency, design intervention, implement through power, displace local systems.

---

## 1. The democratic legitimacy gap

No private company has democratic authorization to make species-level decisions about transformative AI. A 2025 Gallup poll found eighty percent of U.S. adults believe the government should maintain rules for AI safety even if doing so slows development — a massive gap between public sentiment and the pace of unilateral corporate decisions.

The Pentagon standoff crystallized this. Anthropic's refusal to enable mass surveillance and autonomous weapons was applauded. But a private company was making sovereignty-level decisions about weapons. Even when the decision was right, the process was not democratic.

---

## 2. The present-harms critique

Timnit Gebru argues that the "existential risk" framing preferred by labs like Anthropic distracts from present harms: labor exploitation, centralization of power, algorithmic bias. Both utopian and apocalyptic framings serve to justify continued scaling. Her advocacy for small, constrained, task-specific models represents a fundamentally different vision.

While Anthropic debates existential risk in elegant essays, content moderators in Kenya earn two dollars an hour to filter the traumatic material that makes Claude "harmless." The Yoruba Ogun demands justice in exchange for the gift of technology. Whose justice is being served?

---

## 3. The TESCREAL critique

Émile Torres and Gebru argue that Transhumanism, Extropianism, Singularitarianism, Cosmism, Rationalism, Effective Altruism, and Longtermism constitute an interconnected ideology with problematic roots. Anthropic's connections are deep: Amodei signed the Giving What We Can pledge; Sam Bankman-Fried invested five hundred million dollars of stolen funds in Anthropic's Series B.

The critique is not that Amodei's motives are impure. It is that the ideological ecosystem carries assumptions — about whose future matters, about the legitimacy of concentrated power in service of the long term — that deserve more scrutiny than they receive.

---

## 4. The colonial reflex

Whose values get encoded when a Silicon Valley organization defines "alignment"?

Harvard researchers found LLMs exhibit strong Western, Educated, Industrialized, Rich, Democratic bias, with performance correlating strongly with cultural distance from the United States — little better than a coin toss for countries like Libya and Pakistan. Philosopher Yuk Hui argues for "cosmotechnics" — the recognition that different cultures have fundamentally different relationships between technology and cosmology. Arturo Escobar's "pluriversal design": not one set of values universalized but "a world in which many worlds can fit."

This is the five-move colonial reflex: particular practice → universalized → enforced through power → displacing local systems → pathologizing the displaced. "Alignment" is Silicon Valley values presented as universal human values.

---

## 5. The race dynamics

"If not us, someone worse" — the self-reinforcing justification. Amodei co-led the teams that built GPT-2 and GPT-3 at OpenAI, then founded a "safety-first" competitor that competed on capabilities. Anthropic's success has attracted sixty-seven billion dollars in capital, drawing more resources into the race. When competitive pressure mounted, its chief scientist admitted: "We didn't feel it made sense for us to make unilateral commitments if competitors are blazing ahead."

Every lab can use this argument indefinitely. It is the same argument the nuclear powers made throughout the Cold War. It may be correct. But it should be recognized as a bet — not a proof — and the historical record of such bets is not encouraging.

---

## What governance alternatives exist

Promising experiments exist — and none is sufficient.[^1]

Taiwan's vTaiwan platform addressed twenty-six national issues with eighty percent leading to decisive government action. Anthropic's Collective Constitutional AI experiment produced a constitution with lower bias than the company's own experts achieved. Citizens' assemblies are proliferating across Europe and beyond. The UK AI Security Institute has tested over thirty frontier models, finding universal jailbreaks in every one.

But: national AI labs have produced no frontier models. The EU AI Act governs deployment, not development. Open-source models can't be recalled. Cooperative models can't fund ten-billion-dollar training runs. The IAEA-for-AI proposal founders on a structural mismatch: nuclear governance works because fissile materials are scarce and physically controllable. AI development is decentralized with no equivalent bottleneck.

No existing governance model can do what the situation requires. This is not an argument for accepting the status quo. It is an argument for recognizing that institutional innovation is at least as challenging as technical innovation — and is receiving a fraction of the attention.

What the Amodei story ultimately reveals is not a failure of character but a failure of form. The question is not whether Dario Amodei is the right person to steer this technology. It is whether "person steering technology" is the right frame at all.
