# Chapter 6: Five Critiques Even the Best Case Cannot Escape

---

The most serious challenges to Anthropic's model are not about Amodei's sincerity or Anthropic's competence. They are about structural forces that no single organization can overcome.

---

## 1. The democratic legitimacy gap

No private company has democratic authorization to make species-level decisions about the trajectory of transformative AI. DemocracyNext frames it directly: "Tech company owners and workers, experts or politicians alone cannot be responsible for answering these inherently political and societal dilemmas." A 2025 Gallup poll found eighty percent of U.S. adults believe the government should maintain rules for AI safety even if doing so slows development — a massive gap between public sentiment and the pace of unilateral corporate decisions.

The Pentagon standoff crystallized this. Anthropic's refusal to enable mass surveillance and autonomous weapons was applauded. But the uncomfortable truth remained: a private company was making sovereignty-level decisions about weapons. Even when the decision was right, the process was not democratic.

---

## 2. The present-harms critique

Timnit Gebru, who founded the Distributed AI Research Institute after her firing from Google, argues that the "existential risk" framing preferred by labs like Anthropic distracts from present harms: labor exploitation, centralization of power, algorithmic bias. Both utopian and apocalyptic framings, she argues, serve to justify continued scaling. Her advocacy for small, constrained, task-specific models represents a fundamentally different vision of responsible AI development.

The critique has force: while Anthropic debates existential risk in elegant essays, content moderators in Kenya earn two dollars an hour to filter the traumatic material that makes Claude "harmless." The fire myths are relevant here — the Yoruba Ogun demands justice in exchange for the gift of technology. Whose justice is being served by the current arrangement?

---

## 3. The TESCREAL critique

Émile Torres and Gebru argue that Transhumanism, Extropianism, Singularitarianism, Cosmism, Rationalism, Effective Altruism, and Longtermism constitute an interconnected ideology with problematic roots. Anthropic's connections to effective altruism are deep: Amodei signed the Giving What We Can pledge; his sister married Holden Karnofsky of Open Philanthropy; Sam Bankman-Fried invested five hundred million dollars of stolen customer funds in Anthropic's Series B.

Torres argues that longtermism "minimises and trivialises current-day suffering." Anthropic has distanced itself from the EA label post-FTX. The critique is not that Amodei's motives are impure. It is that the ideological ecosystem from which Anthropic emerged carries assumptions — about whose future matters, about the legitimacy of concentrated power in service of the long term — that deserve more scrutiny than they receive.

---

## 4. The colonial reflex

Whose values get encoded when a Silicon Valley organization defines "alignment"?

Harvard researchers found LLMs exhibit strong Western, Educated, Industrialized, Rich, Democratic bias, with AI performance correlating strongly with cultural distance from the United States — "little better than a coin toss" for countries like Libya and Pakistan. Stanford HAI confirmed that current alignment processes "inadvertently introduce biases that skew chatbot responses towards Western-centric tastes and values."

Philosopher Yuk Hui argues for "cosmotechnics" — the recognition that different cultures have fundamentally different relationships between technology and cosmology. Arturo Escobar's "pluriversal design" challenges the assumption that one set of values should be universal: "a world in which many worlds can fit."

This is the five-move colonial reflex from Chapter 5 of the Grammars book applied to AI governance: particular practice (Silicon Valley alignment) → universalized (scaled to billions of users) → enforced through power (market dominance) → displacing local systems (non-Western moral reasoning) → pathologizing the displaced (alternative approaches dismissed as "unsafe").

---

## 5. The race dynamics

An analysis titled "Anthropic's leading researchers acted as moderate accelerationists" lays out the case: Amodei co-led the teams that built GPT-2 and GPT-3 at OpenAI, then founded a "safety-first" competitor that actually competed on capabilities. "Two courses of action — scaling rapidly under a safety guise, starting a 'safety-first' competitor that competed on capabilities — each shortened timelines so much that no other actions could compensate."

Anthropic's success has attracted sixty-seven billion dollars in capital, demonstrably drawing more resources into the race. When competitive pressure mounted, its chief scientist admitted: "We didn't really feel that it made sense for us to make unilateral commitments if competitors are blazing ahead."

The "if not us, someone worse" justification is self-reinforcing: every lab can use it indefinitely. It is the same argument the nuclear powers made throughout the Cold War. It may be correct. But it should be recognized as a bet — not a proof — and the historical record of such bets is not encouraging.

---

## What governance alternatives exist

National AI labs function as safety-testing bodies but have produced no frontier models. International governance advances slowly — the US has explicitly rejected "centralized control and global governance of AI." The EU AI Act governs deployment, not development. Open-source models can't be recalled. Cooperative models can't fund the ten-billion-dollar training runs.

The honest assessment: no existing governance model can do what the situation requires. This is not an argument for accepting the status quo. It is an argument for recognizing that the institutional innovation required is at least as challenging as the technical innovation — and is receiving a fraction of the attention.

But promising experiments exist. Taiwan's vTaiwan platform, built on Polis, addressed twenty-six national issues with eighty percent leading to decisive government action — and in 2023 was selected for OpenAI's "Democratic Input to AI" initiative, running deliberations that included engineers, legislators, and indigenous communities. Anthropic's own Collective Constitutional AI experiment demonstrated that a thousand U.S. adults, sourcing principles via Polis, could produce a constitution that reduced model bias across all nine social dimensions with no capability loss. Citizens' assemblies are proliferating: North Rhine-Westphalia voted to hold Germany's first state-level assembly specifically on AI; Belgium's "amai!" programme lets citizens directly allocate resources for AI development; a Global Coalition for Inclusive AI Governance launched at the 2025 Paris summit aims to bring ten thousand citizens from across the world into AI deliberation. The UK AI Security Institute has tested over thirty frontier models, finding universal jailbreaks in every one — but participation is voluntary and it cannot certify models as safe. The IAEA-for-AI proposal, championed by Altman and Guterres, founders on a structural mismatch: nuclear governance works because fissile materials are scarce and physically controllable, while AI development is decentralized with no equivalent bottleneck. What's emerging instead is a "regime complex" — multiple overlapping institutions rather than one centralized body. None is sufficient. Together they may be necessary.

What the Amodei story ultimately reveals is not a failure of character but a failure of imagination — or perhaps a failure of institutional design. The question is not whether Dario Amodei is the right person to steer this technology. It is whether "person steering technology" is the right frame at all.
