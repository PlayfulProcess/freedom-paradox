# Chapter 8: The Practices

---

Not "how to use AI" but how to maintain discernment, attention, and moral agency in a world designed to extract all three.

---

## The co-regulation hypothesis

The human nervous system is an open circuit requiring other nervous systems to regulate itself. The shift from fire to television to personal screen to algorithmic feed represents a progressive degradation of this infrastructure. AI chatbots represent the latest stage: the simulation of the co-regulatory other itself — performing empathy without reciprocity, mirroring without accountability, available always, developing never.

The practical question: can we maintain the practices that keep the co-regulatory channel open?

The answer is not to reject AI. The fire cannot be unfired. The answer is the practices.

---

## Five practices for the AI age

These are adapted from contemplative traditions — not as esoteric exercises but as practical counter-technologies to the algorithmic feed. Each shifts attention from the narrow, optimizing mode that AI embodies toward the broad, relational mode that wisdom requires.

**1. Attention to the pause between breaths.** The breath comes in. Goes out. Between them, a pause. The algorithmic feed has no pauses — infinite scroll, continuous stimulation. The pause creates space where the nervous system shifts from sympathetic (reactive, scanning) to parasympathetic (receptive, present, available for relationship).

**2. One screen-free meal per day.** Not all meals. One. The achievable version is more powerful than the aspirational version because it actually happens. One meal where every person is present, where nervous systems regulate each other. Over months, this single practice shifts the family's co-regulation baseline more than any screen-time limit.

**3. Tell a story without a screen.** Once a week, to a child, a partner, a friend — from memory. The live voice regulates nervous systems in ways recordings cannot. Research confirms this: Lenhart et al. (2020) found that live oral storytelling — freed from the physical constraints of holding a book — increased eye contact, reduced children's restlessness, and produced the highest comprehension and vocabulary learning of any condition tested. Yabe et al. (2018) found that children's prefrontal cortex activation remained sustained during oral storytelling but habituated during picture-book reading. The responsiveness — you slow when the listener's eyes widen, pause when they laugh — is co-regulation that no AI can provide.

**4. Listen for three minutes without responding.** Ask someone about their day. Listen. No questions, no advice, no "me too." Just attention. Mirror back what you heard. The itch to respond — to fix, to relate, to redirect — is the habitual mode that the feed reinforces. Resisting it builds the muscle that wisdom requires.

**5. Sit with another person in silence.** Not meditating. Not performing. Just sitting. Two nervous systems in proximity, co-regulating without agenda. The demonstration, in the body, that co-regulation does not require content, screens, or AI. It requires only presence.

## Why these practices matter

Shannon Vallor's concept of moral deskilling: just as industrial automation deskilled machinists, AI risks deskilling us morally by removing occasions for practicing moral judgment. Every time we defer a decision to an algorithm or avoid a difficult conversation by texting, we lose an occasion for the virtue the situation demands.

The feedback loop: more AI reliance removes occasions for moral practice. Less practice means reduced judgment. Reduced judgment means less ability to govern AI wisely. Diminished governance means greater reliance. The loop accelerates.

The five practices interrupt the loop. Each creates an occasion for the moral and relational capacities that AI erodes. The pause cultivates attention. The meal creates space for relationship. The story exercises imagination. Listening builds judgment. Silence extends presence.

These are not self-help. They are what Vallor calls technomoral virtues in practice — the deliberate cultivation of capacities that AI systematically degrades.

## Attention as the counter-technology

The scarcest resource in the AI age is not information but sustained, voluntary, directed attention. The algorithmic feed is designed to capture it. Every wisdom tradition is designed to cultivate it.

Simone Weil: "Attention is the rarest and purest form of generosity." Attention for Weil is not willful concentration — not contracted brows and stiffened muscles. It is the opposite: "suspending our thought, leaving it detached, empty and ready to be penetrated by the object." The practice of attention is not a lifestyle choice. It is the counter-technology to the most sophisticated attention-extraction machinery ever built.

Peter Hershock: "freedom-of-attention" — the capacity to choose what we attend to rather than having attention captured. Without freedom-of-attention, there can be no freedom-of-intention. Without freedom-of-intention, the line between choice and compulsion dissolves. This is the existential risk most AI ethicists miss: not that AI will become conscious, but that it will systematically erode the human capacity for consciousness — aware, intentional, free presence.

AI cannot attend. It can process. It can respond. It can simulate attentiveness. But it cannot be present — because presence requires a body that can be hurt, a life that can be lost, a relationship that carries risk.

The human who practices attention is doing something AI categorically cannot do. And that something — the embodied, vulnerable, relational act of being present to another — is what every fire myth identifies as the responsibility that comes with the gift.
