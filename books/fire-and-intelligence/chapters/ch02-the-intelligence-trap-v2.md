# Chapter 2: The Intelligence Trap

---

A century of measuring minds has not settled what intelligence is. AI has been built on a particular definition — and that definition may be wrong.

---

## Intelligence is not what we think it is

The dominant assumption: intelligence is a single, measurable, individual capacity — the thing IQ tests capture. AI benchmarks inherit this assumption. They test what can be scored: knowledge retrieval, logical reasoning, pattern recognition. By these measures, AI is already superhuman.

But three lines of research converge on a different picture.

Michael Tomasello's cultural intelligence hypothesis: roughly a hundred chimpanzees and a hundred two-and-a-half-year-old children received identical cognitive tests. Children and chimps scored identically on physical cognition — space, quantities, causality. The difference was entirely social. Children scored seventy-four percent on social cognition versus thirty-three percent for apes. What distinguishes human cognition is not processing power but shared intentionality — the ability to participate with others in activities with shared goals.[^1]

Joseph Henrich's collective brain: "It's our collective brains operating over generations, and not the innate inventive power of individual brains, that explain our species' fancy technologies." Well-equipped European explorers repeatedly perished in environments where indigenous peoples thrived — not because indigenous people were individually smarter, but because they possessed culturally accumulated know-how no individual could replicate from scratch. When rising sea levels isolated Tasmania's four thousand inhabitants twelve thousand years ago, they progressively lost tools, clothing, fishing technology over eight millennia. When the collective brain shrinks, knowledge erodes despite individual intelligence remaining constant.[^2]

Hubert Dreyfus's Heideggerian critique: genuine intelligence requires a body that senses and acts, holistic situational engagement rather than rule-following, and tacit knowledge gained through embodied practice that cannot be formalized. Initially ridiculed, Dreyfus declared in 2007: "I figure I won and it's over." The core problems of hallucination and alignment may stem from the inherent lack of what embodied cognition researchers call "a deeper, grounded sense of care." Intelligence, it turns out, may require caring about something — and caring requires a body that can be hurt.

For AI, the implication is stark. AI has no shared intentionality, no cultural membership, no body, no capacity for care. From Henrich's perspective, an AI is like a lone European explorer in the Australian outback — enormous individual processing power without the collective brain that actually matters.

---

## Intelligence may be self-terminating

The argument begins with bones.

As Homo sapiens migrated to each new continent, large animals went extinct — not simultaneously, as climate-change models predict, but correlated with human arrival. Australia lost its megafauna forty-seven thousand years ago. The Americas lost thirty-seven genera thirteen thousand years ago. Islands show the pattern most clearly: Wrangel Island mammoths survived until humans arrived four thousand years ago, New Zealand's moa until six hundred years ago.[^3]

The cognitive niche: our survival strategy mounted what Tooby and DeVore called "evolutionary surprise attacks" — escalating the arms race so rapidly that prey species could not evolve counter-adaptations fast enough. Intelligence was the weapon. The weapon had no safety.

Robert Sternberg confronted the paradox: "It might seem paradoxical that greater general intelligence would lead to behavior that ultimately is species self-destructive." A person can be highly creative devising a weapon, analytical ensuring it works, practical delivering it — and profoundly unwise in having undertaken the project at all.

Three frameworks from the collapse literature converge.

Joseph Tainter: societies address challenges by increasing complexity, but complexity faces diminishing returns. When returns become negative, collapse follows. AI is the latest and most dramatic escalation — enormous initial returns while maintaining the systems requires exponentially increasing energy, regulation, and oversight.

Ronald Wright's progress trap: innovations that seem beneficial but lead to irreversible dead ends. Easter Island's statue-building consumed its forests. Sumer's irrigation salinized its soil. Each shares a structure: initial success creates dependency, costs accumulate invisibly, and by the time the trap is visible, return is impossible.

E.O. Wilson: "We have Paleolithic emotions, medieval institutions, and god-like technology." The brain evolved for immediate, palpable, personal threats in groups of a hundred and fifty. Existential risks — climate change, AI alignment — are abstract, slow-moving, global, and probabilistic. Our cognitive architecture is systematically ill-equipped for the technologies it creates.

---

## The alignment problem as evidence

The unsolved alignment problem is not merely a technical challenge. It is evidence for the thesis.

Core problems — inner alignment, scalable oversight, specification gaming, corrigibility, interpretability — remain unsolved despite decades of work by the most capable research organizations on earth. Geoffrey Hinton, Yoshua Bengio, and Yann LeCun — three Turing Award winners — profoundly disagree on whether AI poses existential risk. At least one set of leading experts is fundamentally wrong.

The very existence of this impasse demonstrates the thesis: human intelligence is sufficient to create technologies of existential consequence but insufficient to determine whether those technologies are safe.

Intelligence asks the question. Wisdom lives the answer. The gap between them may be humanity's defining vulnerability. And AI is the gap at maximum speed.
