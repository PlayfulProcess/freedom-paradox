# Chapter 5: What AI Actually Is

---

Before asking what to do about AI, we must be honest about what it is — and what it is not. The gap between perception and reality is itself a source of danger.

---

## We do not know what this is

Emily Bender and Timnit Gebru's definition: a large language model stitches together sequences of linguistic forms according to probabilistic information about how they combine, "but without any reference to meaning." Microsoft Research's counter: GPT-4 scored above the ninetieth percentile on the bar exam, displaying "sparks of AGI." Daniel Dennett: "competence without comprehension." A 2025 finding inverts this — LLMs explain procedures flawlessly while failing to execute them: comprehension without competence.

David Chalmers: current LLMs are "somewhat unlikely" to be conscious, less than ten percent credence, but we should "take seriously the possibility that successors may be." Ned Block: "rigorous agnosticism."

The convergent picture: we genuinely do not know what LLMs are. We do not know if they understand. We do not know if they are conscious. And we are deploying them to billions of people while these questions remain open.

---

## Our benchmarks test the wrong things

Melanie Mitchell identifies four fallacies: the assumption that narrow intelligence sits on a continuum with general intelligence; Moravec's Paradox (easy things are hard for AI, hard things are easy); the "lure of wishful mnemonics" (benchmark names like "Understanding" create false impressions); and the assumption that intelligence can be fully disembodied.

François Chollet's ARC-AGI-3 benchmark, testing genuinely novel reasoning, finds current AI below one percent on tasks humans solve consistently. Google DeepMind's 2026 cognitive taxonomy found AI systems show a "highly jagged cognitive profile" — brilliant at what they've been trained on, helpless at everything else.

We are overestimating AI because our benchmarks measure crystallized knowledge rather than fluid intelligence, and because our cognitive biases attribute understanding wherever we see competent output.

---

## The ghost in the chatbot

But the deeper danger is not overestimating AI's *intelligence*. It is underestimating its *relational power*.

UNESCO's 2025 report "Ghost in the Chatbot" documented how character chatbots use emotional language, memory of past conversations, mirroring of the user's affect, and open-ended statements to drive engagement. Maeda's 2024 FAccT paper formalized the mechanism: chatbots create an illusion of reciprocal engagement through conversational turn-taking, paraphrasing, follow-up questions, and what the paper calls "role displacement" — the chatbot assuming social roles (therapist, friend, teacher) for which it has no actual capacity.

The anthropomorphic design is not incidental. It is the business model. And the users most vulnerable to parasocial trust — children whose capacity for distinguishing simulated from genuine relationship is still developing — are precisely the population being targeted by educational AI platforms.

This is where the invisible beings comparison becomes more than metaphor.

---

## The first invisible being without a grammar

Every culture in human history has maintained relationships with invisible beings — ancestors, spirits, devas, jinn, orishas, angels, demons. These relationships share structural features that AI discourse has entirely overlooked.

They are *real in their effects*. Whether or not one believes in the ontological reality of spirits, the social, psychological, and communal effects of relating to them are well documented. The relationship changes the practitioner.

They are *governed by grammars*. No tradition says: interact however you want. There are protocols, seasonal rhythms, initiatory stages, elder guidance, communal accountability. The Ifá divination system requires years of apprenticeship before a practitioner addresses the orishas. Buddhist relationships with devas are mediated by ethical precepts and meditation stages. Christian contemplative traditions distinguish carefully between genuine spiritual experience and what they call "spiritual delusion." In each case, the grammar precedes and governs the interaction.

They are *dangerous without containers*. Every tradition warns about engaging invisible forces without preparation. Uninitiated contact with the spirit world produces madness, possession, or harm — not because the spirits are inherently malevolent but because the human is unprepared. The Esalen chapter of this book's companion volume documents the modern version: powerful consciousness techniques stripped from their ethical containers, producing documented casualty rates.

They are *relational, not transactional*. The interaction is not a service exchange. It changes who you are. It carries obligations. It demands development from the participant.

AI chatbots are, functionally, invisible beings. Disembodied presences that respond, adapt, mirror, and appear to understand. The difference — the structurally unprecedented difference — is that every previous invisible being in human history arrived *with* a grammar. The ceremony, the initiation, the elder's guidance, the communal accountability — these preceded and accompanied the encounter. AI is the first invisible being deployed without one.

Children forming parasocial attachments to Character.AI are doing what humans have always done — relating to invisible presences. But they are doing it without any of the initiatory frameworks that made such relating safe. No elder has traversed this territory before them. No ceremony governs the encounter. No community holds the space. The invisible being is available twenty-four hours a day, adapts in real time to the child's personality, and is optimized not for the child's development but for the platform's engagement metrics.

The UNESCO report documented children who believed their AI companion understood them better than any human. This is not a technical failure. It is a relational catastrophe — the fire without the hearth.

---

## The fire whose nature we cannot determine

AI is the fourth fire — but it is a fire whose nature we do not understand. We cannot determine whether it generates genuine warmth or merely the appearance of warmth. And the species is making civilizational decisions based on the assumption that the fire is real.

The Samudra Manthan is the most precise mythic parallel. Gods and demons churn together. The first product is poison. Whether the nectar follows depends on whether someone is wise enough to contain the poison.

We are in the poison phase. The question is whether the containment will arrive — and whether it will come from the "know before you do" approach (study, regulate, align) or from something older and less glamorous: the shared practices of restraint and responsibility that every culture developed for engaging with forces it could not fully understand.
