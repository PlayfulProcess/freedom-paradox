# Chapter 2: Intelligence

---

A century of measuring minds has not settled what intelligence is. And the answer matters enormously — because AI has been built on a particular definition of intelligence, and that definition may be wrong.

---

## The g-factor and its discontents

Charles Spearman's 1904 discovery that scores on all cognitive tests correlate positively led him to posit a general intelligence factor he called g. The finding is "arguably the most replicated result in all psychology." The statistical g-factor accounts for forty to fifty percent of variance in any diverse cognitive test battery, predicts academic achievement, job performance, and health outcomes. The Cattell-Horn-Carroll model, placing g at the apex with sixteen broad abilities beneath it, is the dominant framework in applied psychometrics.

But what g *is* remains deeply contested. Stephen Jay Gould accused psychometricians of reification — treating a mathematical abstraction as a concrete thing in the brain. Howard Gardner's multiple intelligences theory proposed eight distinct intelligences; it became enormously influential in education but struggled for empirical validation. Robert Sternberg's triarchic theory faced a devastating critique showing its "six key empirical claims rest primarily on the illusion of evidence."

The most sophisticated contemporary alternatives come from two directions. Han van der Maas's mutualism model proposes that the positive manifold emerges from reciprocal interactions between cognitive processes during development — g as an index variable like the Dow Jones Industrial Average, not a causal entity. Kovacs and Conway's Process Overlap Theory argues that cognitive tests tap overlapping executive processes, producing the appearance of a general factor without one existing. Their summary: "While most cognitive psychologists and neuroscientists today agree that there is no such thing as general intelligence, psychometricians have become remarkably good at measuring it."

The debate has new urgency with AI. In 2024, researchers applied psychometric factor analysis to 591 large language models across twelve benchmark tests and found a very strong positive manifold — an "artificial general ability" factor accounting for sixty-six percent of test variance. But the authors cautioned this likely reflects shared training data, not anything analogous to biological intelligence. François Chollet's ARC-AGI-3 benchmark, testing interactive reasoning in genuinely novel environments, found current AI models scoring below one percent on tasks humans solve consistently. The AI can pass the bar exam. It cannot navigate a world it has never seen before.

Google DeepMind's March 2026 cognitive taxonomy found contemporary AI systems show a "highly jagged cognitive profile" — proficient in knowledge-intensive domains but with "critical deficits in foundational cognitive machinery." The AI is brilliant at what it has been trained on and helpless at everything else. This jaggedness itself is evidence that what AI benchmarks measure is something quite different from what g-factor theorists debate.

## Intelligence lives in bodies

The 4E cognition framework — embodied, embedded, enacted, extended — represents a fundamental challenge to the computational model of mind that AI implicitly assumes.

Francisco Varela, Evan Thompson, and Eleanor Rosch launched the enactive approach: cognition is not computation of abstract symbols but sense-making — the adaptive self-regulation of living beings in precarious conditions. An organism doesn't process information about its environment. It enacts a meaningful world through its embodied interactions. Thompson's "life-mind continuity thesis" holds that the same principles governing the simplest living things extend continuously to complex cognitive beings. Cognition is inseparable from the self-organizing nature of life itself.

Hubert Dreyfus's Heideggerian critique of AI, developed across four decades, identified three requirements for genuine intelligence that remain unmet: embodiment (intelligence requires a body that senses and acts), being-in-the-world (intelligence requires holistic situational engagement, not rule-following), and tacit knowledge (much human knowledge is gained through embodied practice and cannot be formalized). Initially met with ridicule, Dreyfus declared in 2007: "I figure I won and it's over." A 2020 paper reviving his arguments contended that AGI remains "in principle impossible" because "computers are not in the world." Current AI systems lack situation-specific sensory awareness, purpose not derived from programmers, and tacit knowledge acquired through embodied practice.

Alva Noë pushed the argument to its limit: "Consciousness is not something the brain achieves on its own. Consciousness requires the joint operation of brain, body, and world." The world is its own model; the brain does not need to reconstruct it computationally.

What this literature collectively implies for AI is stark: disembodied systems are "unlikely to advance to the point where they can fully think like a human brain." The core problems of hallucination and alignment difficulties may stem from "the inherent lack of a deeper, grounded sense of care." Intelligence, it turns out, may require caring about something — and caring requires a body that can be hurt.

## Intelligence is cultural, not individual

Michael Tomasello's cultural intelligence hypothesis and Joseph Henrich's collective brain concept together constitute perhaps the most powerful reframing of what makes human cognition distinctive.

In the landmark Herrmann study published in *Science*, roughly a hundred chimpanzees, thirty orangutans, and a hundred two-and-a-half-year-old human children received identical cognitive test batteries. Children and chimpanzees showed virtually identical skills for dealing with the physical world — space, quantities, causality. The difference was entirely social: children scored roughly seventy-four percent on social cognition tasks versus thirty-three percent for both ape species. No general intelligence g-factor was found. What distinguishes human cognition is not raw processing power but shared intentionality — the ability to participate with others in collaborative activities with shared goals and cooperative communication.

Henrich's *The Secret of Our Success* extends this to its radical conclusion: "It's our collective brains operating over generations, and not the innate inventive power or creative abilities of individual brains, that explain our species' fancy technologies and massive ecological success." Well-equipped European explorers repeatedly perished in environments where indigenous peoples thrived — not because indigenous people were individually smarter, but because they possessed culturally accumulated know-how that no individual intelligence could replicate from scratch.

The Tasmanian technology regression provides the negative case. When rising sea levels isolated Tasmania's roughly four thousand inhabitants twelve thousand years ago, they progressively lost bone tools, cold-weather clothing, fishing technology, and boomerangs over eight millennia. When the collective brain shrinks below a critical threshold, cultural knowledge erodes despite individual intelligence remaining constant.

For AI, the implications are profound. AI systems have no shared intentionality, no genuine social cognition, no cultural membership, and no capacity for the cumulative cultural evolution that actually drives human achievement. From Henrich's perspective, an AI is like a lone European explorer in the Australian outback — enormous individual processing power without the collective brain that actually matters.

## Intelligence beyond the human

The expanding science of non-human cognition demonstrates that intelligence is far more plural than any single metric captures.

Peter Godfrey-Smith describes octopuses as "an independent experiment in the evolution of large brains and complex behavior." With approximately five hundred million neurons, two-thirds of which reside in their eight arms rather than their central brain, octopuses demonstrate that intelligence can be distributed rather than centralized. They recognize individual human keepers, raid neighboring tanks, open jars from inside — all with a lifespan of just one to two years and through an evolutionary pathway completely independent from vertebrate cognition.

New Caledonian crows manufacture hook-tools from pandanus leaves, solve metatool problems on first trial, and demonstrate causal reasoning about hidden agents. Ravens plan for future tool-use up to seventeen hours in advance. Intelligence evolved independently through radically different architectures separated by three hundred and twenty million years.

Orca populations maintain completely distinct, culturally transmitted hunting traditions and vocal dialects — so rigidly that captive mammal-eating orcas have died rather than eat dead fish. Sperm whale multilevel societies emerge from cultural transmission of acoustic repertoires.

At the far edge, Suzanne Simard's research on mycorrhizal networks revealed that trees exchange carbon and defense signals through underground fungal connections. Monica Gagliano's studies showed plants retaining "memories" for twenty-eight days and learning faster under energetically costly conditions.

The collective lesson: intelligence is not monolithic, does not require centralization, may not even require neurons, and has evolved independently at least three times in complex organisms. Any equation of intelligence with human-style abstract reasoning — the equation AI benchmarks implicitly make — reflects anthropocentric bias rather than biological reality.
