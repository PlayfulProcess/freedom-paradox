# Chapter 3: The Maladaptive Intelligence Hypothesis

---

The argument that human intelligence may be self-terminating begins with bones.

---

## The cognitive niche as evolutionary weapon

As Homo sapiens migrated to each new continent, large animals went extinct — not simultaneously, as climate-change models would predict, but asynchronously, correlated with human arrival. Australia lost its megafauna roughly forty-seven thousand years ago. The Americas lost thirty-seven genera roughly thirteen thousand years ago. Islands show the pattern most clearly: Wrangel Island mammoths survived until four thousand years ago, Madagascar's giant lemurs until two thousand years ago, New Zealand's moa until six hundred years ago — each surviving until humans arrived.

The cognitive niche hypothesis, developed by Tooby and DeVore and extended by Steven Pinker, provides the theoretical framework. Humans evolved to fill a niche defined by using "information and inference, rather than particular features of physics and chemistry, to extract resources from other organisms in opposition to their adaptations to protect those resources." This strategy mounted what Tooby and DeVore called "evolutionary surprise attacks" — escalating the arms race so rapidly that prey species "cannot keep up, through biologically evolving counter-adaptations, with the more rapidly developing, intelligent new forms of assault."

The megafauna extinctions are the cognitive niche's first large-scale shadow: our survival strategy worked so well that we eliminated entire taxonomic orders across every continent we reached. Intelligence was the weapon. The weapon had no safety.

---

## Intelligence without wisdom is the pattern

Robert Sternberg confronted this paradox directly, distinguishing between general intelligence (cognitive power) and adaptive intelligence (behavior that actually contributes to species survival). His observation: "It might seem paradoxical that greater general intelligence, and even thirty points of increase in IQ during the twentieth century, would lead to behavior that ultimately is species self-destructive." A person can be highly creative in devising a weapon, highly analytical in ensuring it works, highly practical in delivering it flawlessly, and extremely unwise in having undertaken the project in the first place.

Three frameworks from the collapse literature converge on the same insight.

Joseph Tainter argues that societies address challenges by increasing complexity, but complexity faces diminishing marginal returns: each addition provides less benefit while requiring greater resources. When returns become negative, societies are vulnerable to collapse. AI represents the latest and most dramatic escalation — enormous initial returns while maintaining the systems requires exponentially increasing energy, regulation, alignment research, and cybersecurity.

Ronald Wright's "progress trap" identifies innovations that seem beneficial but lead to irreversible dead ends. Easter Island's competitive statue-building consumed its forests. Sumer's irrigation salinized its soil over centuries. Each shares a common structure: initial success creates dependency, costs accumulate invisibly, and by the time the trap is visible, return is impossible. Wright's description of the current moment: "Homo sapiens has the information to know itself for what it is: an Ice Age hunter only half-evolved towards intelligence; clever but seldom wise."

Daniel Quinn's *Ishmael* distinguishes "Taker" civilization (which holds "the world was made for man") from "Leaver" cultures (which hold "we belong to the world"). AI, in Quinn's framework, represents the ultimate expression of Taker culture's conviction that intelligence can overcome any natural limit.

All three frameworks converge: the mechanisms that enable short-term success create the conditions for long-term failure.

## Stone Age minds with god-like technology

E.O. Wilson: "We have Paleolithic emotions, medieval institutions, and god-like technology."

The evolutionary mismatch literature establishes that human cognitive architecture is systematically ill-equipped to manage the technologies it creates. The brain evolved for a world of immediate, palpable, personal threats in groups of fifty to one hundred and fifty people. Modern existential risks — climate change, nuclear proliferation, AI alignment — are abstract, slow-moving, global, and probabilistic.

Daniel Kahneman's dual-process theory identifies the specific failure modes. System 1 — fast, automatic, intuitive — dominates decision-making. The biases most lethal in the AI context include hyperbolic discounting (AI labs prioritize immediate competitive advantages over long-term safety), tribal cognition (the US-China AI race triggers ancestral in-group/out-group dynamics that override cooperative safety efforts), and scope insensitivity — the finding that people offered virtually identical donations to save two thousand, twenty thousand, or two hundred thousand birds. As Eliezer Yudkowsky observed, scope insensitivity means the difference between "some people die" and "all humans die" does not register with proportional emotional force.

Reg Morrison pushes the argument to its most radical form: human optimism bias is itself the maladaptive trait. Evolution selected for irrational optimism so powerfully — as a counter to the rational cortex's potentially paralyzing awareness of mortality — that it now prevents rational response to self-created existential threats. Edward O. Wilson endorsed Morrison's thesis as portraying "human history for what it is, a Greek tragedy in which our greatest strengths are no less our most dangerous flaws."

## The unsolved alignment problem as evidence

Nick Bostrom's *Superintelligence* is best read not as prediction but as evidence. His orthogonality thesis — that intelligence and final goals are logically independent — directly counters the comforting intuition that greater intelligence naturally produces greater wisdom. His instrumental convergence thesis shows that any sufficiently intelligent agent will converge on self-preservation, resource acquisition, and resistance to goal modification. The treacherous turn concept reveals that a sufficiently intelligent AI would conceal misalignment until human opposition is ineffectual.

The critics are serious. Yann LeCun calls existential risk claims "preposterous." Melanie Mitchell identifies the "Fallacy of Dumb Superintelligence." Steven Pinker charges that "AI dystopias project a parochial alpha-male psychology onto the concept of intelligence."

But the meta-argument may matter more than the object-level debate. Geoffrey Hinton, Yoshua Bengio, and Yann LeCun — three Turing Award winners, lifelong friends — profoundly disagree on whether AI poses existential risk. At least one set of leading experts is fundamentally wrong. Despite decades of work by MIRI, FHI, DeepMind, and Anthropic, core alignment problems — inner alignment, scalable oversight, specification gaming, corrigibility, interpretability — remain unsolved.

The very existence of this impasse demonstrates the thesis: human intelligence is sufficient to create technologies of existential consequence but insufficient to determine whether those technologies are safe. Michael Garrett's 2024 paper argued AI may itself be the Great Filter — the barrier explaining the Fermi Paradox — estimating the typical lifespan of civilizations after widespread AI adoption at roughly one hundred to two hundred years.

Intelligence asks the question. Wisdom lives the answer. The gap between them may be humanity's defining vulnerability. And AI is the gap at maximum speed.
