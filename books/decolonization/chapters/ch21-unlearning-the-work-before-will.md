# Unlearning: The Work Before Will

*Mind*

## The Pattern

Andreotti describes 'the work before will' — the clearing that has to happen before you can act differently. You cannot simply decide to think decolonially. The colonial operating system is too deep, too integrated into your cognition, your desires, your aesthetics, your reflexes. Unlearning is not replacing colonial thoughts with decolonial ones. It's developing the capacity to notice the colonial furniture of your mind without immediately redecorating.

## What It Costs

Unlearning is uncomfortable. It means sitting with the recognition that your education, your taste, your moral frameworks, your very sense of self may be colonial products. It means grief — for the knowledge you were denied, the relationships severed, the ways of being foreclosed before you were born. Many people prefer the comfort of colonial certainty to the vertigo of unlearning.

## What Persists

The capacity to unlearn persists because it is not a colonial capacity — it's a human one. Every contemplative tradition knows it: Zen's beginner's mind, the apophatic tradition in Christianity, the via negativa. Andreotti's pedagogy of discomfort. The practice of sitting with not-knowing, which is not ignorance but a deeper kind of intelligence. The grandmother who says 'you don't need to understand everything. You need to be present to everything.'

## The Invitation

What if the point is not to replace your colonial education with a decolonial one — not to find the right answer — but to develop the stamina to hold the question? To sit with not-knowing as practice rather than failure? This is the work before will: making space for something you can't yet imagine.
