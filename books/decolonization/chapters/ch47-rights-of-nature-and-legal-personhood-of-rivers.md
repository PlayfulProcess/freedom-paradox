# Rights of Nature and Legal Personhood of Rivers

*Planet*

## The Pattern

In colonial law, nature is property. It has no standing in court, no rights, no voice. You can sue someone for damaging your property (including 'your' land), but the land itself cannot sue. This legal framework makes ecological destruction not just possible but rational — the legal system literally cannot see harm to nature as harm. Rights of Nature reverses this: what if rivers, forests, and ecosystems were legal persons with rights that could be defended in court?

## What It Costs

Centuries of ecological destruction enabled by nature's legal invisibility. Rivers poisoned with impunity. Forests clearcut legally. Species driven to extinction within the law. Environmental law, even at its strongest, protects nature instrumentally (for human use) rather than intrinsically (for its own sake). The legal framework inherited from colonialism treats the earth as a thing, and things have no rights.

## What Persists

Ecuador's 2008 constitution — the first to enshrine Rights of Nature. Bolivia's Law of Mother Earth. New Zealand granting legal personhood to the Whanganui River (Te Awa Tupua) in 2017 — recognizing the Māori understanding that the river is an ancestor, not a resource. India's courts recognizing the Ganges and Yamuna as legal persons. Colombia granting rights to the Atrato River. The legal revolution is small but growing: nature as subject, not object.

## The Invitation

If the river near your home had legal standing, what would it say in court? What harm would it name? What repair would it demand? The fact that this feels like a thought experiment rather than a legal reality tells you everything about the colonial legal imagination.
