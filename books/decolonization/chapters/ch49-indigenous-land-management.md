# Indigenous Land Management

*Planet*

## The Pattern

The colonial myth of wilderness — pristine, untouched nature — erases tens of thousands of years of indigenous land management. The Amazon 'rainforest' was partly cultivated by indigenous peoples over millennia. Australian Aboriginal fire management shaped the entire continent's ecology. The Great Plains' grasslands were maintained by indigenous burning and bison management. 'Wilderness' is a colonial concept that requires the removal of indigenous people to exist — which is exactly what happened in the creation of national parks.

## What It Costs

Yosemite was created by removing the Ahwahneechee people. Yellowstone by removing the Crow, Shoshone, and Bannock. This pattern — conservation through dispossession — continues globally. 'Fortress conservation' has displaced an estimated 14 million indigenous people worldwide. The land then degrades because the people who knew how to tend it are gone. Forest fires intensify because indigenous burning was suppressed. Ecosystems collapse because management was mistaken for wilderness.

## What Persists

Cultural burning returning to California, Australia, and the Pacific Northwest. Indigenous-led conservation — indigenous territories account for 80% of the world's remaining biodiversity. IPBES (the biodiversity equivalent of the IPCC) recognizing indigenous knowledge as essential. Co-management agreements that return stewardship to indigenous peoples. The growing scientific recognition that the 'pristine wilderness' never existed — that what colonists found was not untouched nature but the product of sophisticated, millennia-old land management.

## The Invitation

The land you live on was managed by someone before you. Their management made it what it was when your ancestors arrived. What did they know about this land that you don't? What would it mean to learn from them — not extractively, but in relationship?
