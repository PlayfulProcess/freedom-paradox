# Intergenerational Memory and Transmission

*Collective*

## The Pattern

Colonial modernity tells you that you are an individual — a self-contained unit beginning at birth and ending at death. Indigenous and traditional cultures know otherwise: you are a node in a web of relationships that extends forward and backward through time. Intergenerational trauma is now recognized by Western psychology (epigenetics, ACE studies), but intergenerational memory — the positive transmission of knowledge, identity, resilience, and belonging — is less discussed. Colonialism disrupted both: it inflicted trauma across generations while severing the transmission of memory that could heal it.

## What It Costs

Communities carrying trauma without the stories, ceremonies, and practices that would metabolize it. The boarding school survivor who cannot teach their children the language that was beaten out of them. The descendant of enslaved people who carries stress responses in their biology but not the ancestor practices that would address them. The settler descendant who inherits privilege without history — unable to account for what their ancestors did because the family narrative is silence.

## What Persists

Story. Song. Recipe. Craft. Naming practices. The grandmother who tells you where you come from. The uncle who teaches you the old way of doing things. Oral history projects that record elder knowledge before it's lost. Intergenerational healing circles. The growing field of historical trauma research that validates what indigenous communities have always known: what happened to your grandparents lives in your body. And: what your grandparents knew can still reach you.

## The Invitation

What did your grandparents carry that they couldn't speak about? What did they carry that they could? Both of these transmissions live in you. Intergenerational memory is not nostalgia — it's the infrastructure of belonging.
