# Religious Conversion as Colonial Tool

*Spirit*

## The Pattern

The mission preceded the fort. Missionaries arrived before soldiers because spiritual conquest was more efficient than military conquest. Convert the soul, and the body follows. Colonial Christianity offered salvation and literacy in exchange for language, ceremony, cosmology, and identity. The conversion was never just religious — it was civilizational. To accept the Christian God was to accept Christian time (linear), Christian space (sacred/profane), Christian hierarchy (God/man/nature), and Christian economics (stewardship as dominion).

## What It Costs

Cosmologies destroyed. Ceremony criminalized. Sacred objects burned as idols. Children taken to mission schools. Syncretism — the secret survival strategy of encoding indigenous practice within Christian forms — is both testament to resilience and evidence of the violence that made it necessary. The Virgen de Guadalupe carries Tonantzin. The orixás wear Catholic saints' faces. Survival required disguise.

## What Persists

Indigenous Christianity that has transformed the tradition from within. Liberation theology born in Latin America. Black church traditions that made Christianity a vehicle for liberation rather than submission. Syncretic traditions — Candomblé, Santería, Vodou — that maintained African cosmologies within Christian structures. The ongoing reclamation of pre-Christian indigenous practices. The recognition that Christianity itself contains resources for its own decolonization.

## The Invitation

If you are Christian: how does your faith relate to the faith of those who lived on this land before Christianity arrived? If you are post-Christian: what spiritual practices did your ancestors have before Christianity? These questions are not attacks on faith. They are invitations to the fullness of its history.
