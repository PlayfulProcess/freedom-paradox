# Monoculture vs. Biodiversity

*Planet*

## The Pattern

Colonial agriculture is monoculture: one crop, one field, maximum yield, chemical inputs, export orientation. It replaced polyculture — diverse crops growing in relationship, adapted to local ecology, sustaining local communities. The Green Revolution of the 1960s-70s intensified this pattern globally: high-yield varieties, irrigation, fertilizers, pesticides — all dependent on industrial inputs that created dependency on global markets. Monoculture is not just an agricultural practice. It's a metaphor for colonialism itself: one way, one crop, one knowledge, one future.

## What It Costs

75% of crop genetic diversity lost in the 20th century. Soil depletion from single-crop farming. Pesticide poisoning — 385 million cases annually. Farmer suicides in India linked to debt from purchased seeds and inputs. The destruction of local food systems that were diverse, resilient, and sustainable. Pollinator collapse. The vulnerability of monocultures to disease — the Irish Potato Famine as paradigm: a single variety, a single pathogen, a million dead.

## What Persists

Seed savers. Agrobiodiversity hotspots maintained by indigenous and smallholder farmers. The Svalbard Global Seed Vault — though its existence admits the crisis. Agroecology — the scientific and political movement to farm with ecology rather than against it. Permaculture. Forest gardens. The Slow Food movement. Navdanya, Vandana Shiva's seed-saving network. The stubborn, radical practice of growing diverse food in a world that demands monoculture.

## The Invitation

How many varieties of apple existed a century ago? Over 15,000 in the US alone. How many are in your supermarket? Five or six. That loss — applied to every crop, every ecosystem, every culture — is the cost of monoculture. What diversity are you cultivating, in your garden and in your life?
