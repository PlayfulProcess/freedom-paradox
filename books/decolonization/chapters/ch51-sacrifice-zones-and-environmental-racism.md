# Sacrifice Zones and Environmental Racism

*Planet*

## The Pattern

A sacrifice zone is a place deemed expendable — where pollution, extraction, and waste are concentrated because the people living there are considered less valuable than the resources beneath them or the waste dumped on them. Cancer Alley in Louisiana. The Navajo Nation's uranium mines. The Niger Delta's oil fields. Chevron's toxic legacy in Ecuador. Sacrifice zones are never wealthy, never white, never politically powerful. Environmental racism is not a side effect of colonialism — it is colonialism's spatial logic made visible.

## What It Costs

Communities with cancer rates orders of magnitude above national averages. Children with asthma from living near refineries. Water tables poisoned by fracking. Navajo communities still dealing with uranium contamination from Cold War mining — the US government knew the risks and mined anyway because the people were indigenous. The EPA's own research confirms that communities of color bear disproportionate pollution burdens. The sacrifice zone is colonialism's ongoing cartography: who is worth protecting and who is worth sacrificing.

## What Persists

Environmental justice movements born in Warren County, North Carolina (1982), when a Black community fought a toxic waste dump. Robert Bullard's foundational research documenting environmental racism. Frontline communities organizing — from the Ogoni Nine to Cancer Alley residents to indigenous resistance to pipelines. The insistence that no community is a sacrifice zone. The demand that those who pollute be held accountable, and that the communities most affected lead the response.

## The Invitation

Where is the nearest sacrifice zone to where you live? How far is it? The distance between your home and the nearest sacrifice zone is a measure of your privilege. Environmental justice is not about recycling. It's about who breathes poison so that others can breathe clean air.
