# Dance as Resistance and Remembering

*Body*

## The Pattern

Colonial powers understood intuitively what they claimed to deny: that dance carries culture, memory, prayer, and resistance in the body. The Sun Dance was banned. Capoeira was criminalized. Hula was suppressed. African dance was forbidden on slave ships and plantations — not because it was entertainment, but because it was communion, communication, and the maintenance of identity that slavery sought to destroy.

## What It Costs

When you ban a people's dance, you sever their body from their cosmology. The Sun Dance connected Lakota people to Wakan Tanka through physical sacrifice and communal prayer. Its prohibition (1883-1978 in the US) was explicitly about breaking spiritual resistance. Ghost Dance practitioners were massacred at Wounded Knee. The message was clear: your body's way of praying is illegal.

## What Persists

Capoeira survived disguised as play. Hula was revived and is now central to Hawaiian sovereignty movements. Powwow dance carries prayer and identity across North America. Afro-Brazilian sacred dance — candomblé, umbanda — persisted through syncretism and secrecy. Hip-hop emerged from colonized bodies in colonized urban spaces and became global. The body remembers what was forbidden.

## The Invitation

How do you move when no one is watching? What dances live in your lineage? The embodied grammars in this library — Indian classical dance, ballet, modern dance, anthropological dance — each carries a story of power, resistance, and the body's refusal to forget.
