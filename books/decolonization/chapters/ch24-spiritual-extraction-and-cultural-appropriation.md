# Spiritual Extraction and Cultural Appropriation

*Spirit*

## The Pattern

First colonialism takes the land. Then it takes the labor. Then — when the colonized culture becomes exotic rather than threatening — it takes the spirituality. Yoga without caste politics. Ayahuasca without Shipibo elders. Sweat lodges run by white entrepreneurs. Mindfulness without Buddhism. The pattern extracts the practice from its context, strips it of obligation, repackages it as personal growth, and sells it back as commodity.

## What It Costs

Sacred practices reduced to wellness products. Ceremony without accountability, lineage, or reciprocity. The spiritual teacher who downloads a tradition in a weekend workshop and teaches it on Monday. Indigenous communities watching their most sacred practices performed incorrectly, out of context, for profit, by people who would not recognize them on the street. The deeper cost: appropriation teaches that all knowledge is available to all people at all times — denying that some knowledge requires relationship, time, initiation, and permission.

## What Persists

Indigenous communities setting boundaries: 'This ceremony is not for sale.' The Lakota Declaration of War against exploiters of Lakota spirituality (1993). Communities distinguishing between sharing and theft, between cultural exchange and cultural extraction. The growing conversation about spiritual accountability. The understanding that respect means accepting limits — that some doors are not yours to open.

## The Invitation

If you practice yoga, meditation, or any tradition that originates outside your culture: what is your relationship with the source community? Not just intellectual acknowledgment but actual relationship? Are you a student or a consumer? The difference matters.
