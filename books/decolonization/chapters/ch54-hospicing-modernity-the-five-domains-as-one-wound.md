# Hospicing Modernity: The Five Domains as One Wound

*Synthesis*

## About

The five domains are not five separate problems. They are one pattern — colonial modernity — expressing itself through body, mind, spirit, collective, and planet. Vanessa Andreotti's metaphor of hospicing modernity names the integrated response: we are living in a system that is dying. Not because someone killed it but because its own contradictions are consuming it. The question is not how to fix it or how to replace it. The question is how to be present as it dies — without clinging, without triumphalism, without despair — and how to compost its remains into something that can feed what comes next.

## The Invitation

Decolonization is not a destination. It's not a checklist. It's not something you complete. It's an ongoing practice of noticing — noticing the colonial patterns in your body, your thinking, your prayer, your community, your relationship with the earth. And then: not rushing to fix what you notice, but sitting with it. Feeling the grief. Allowing the compost. Making space for what you can't yet imagine. The grandmothers knew this. Maybe that's what decolonization is: learning to listen to grandmothers again.

## What Hospicing Means

Hospicing is not fixing. It's accompanying. Sitting with someone — or something — as it dies. Modernity is dying: ecologically (it cannot sustain itself), socially (inequality is reaching breaking points), psychologically (mental health crises signal systemic failure), spiritually (the disenchantment of the world produces despair). The hospice worker doesn't try to cure the patient or hasten death. They provide presence, comfort, and honest companionship. This is what decolonization asks of us: not a triumphant replacement of one system with another, but the willingness to sit with complexity, grief, uncertainty, and not-knowing.

## Composting, Not Destroying

What dies feeds what grows. Compost is not trash — it's transformation. The invitation is not to burn modernity down (the master's tools) or to reform it (rearranging chairs on the Titanic) but to compost it — to transform what was harmful into nutrients for what comes next. This requires the grandmother's wisdom: patience, attention to cycles, trust that decomposition is part of creation, and the knowledge that you may not live to see what grows from the compost you tend.

## The Pattern Across Domains

In the body: the demand for productivity that breaks bodies. In the mind: the demand for one knowledge that kills others. In the spirit: the demand for one God (or no God) that severs relationship with the sacred. In the collective: the demand for one political form that eliminates alternatives. On the planet: the demand for infinite growth on a finite earth. One pattern. Five expressions. Each reinforcing the others. You cannot decolonize the mind while the body is colonized. You cannot decolonize the collective while the planet is treated as property.
