# Solidarity vs. Saviorism

*Collective*

## The Pattern

The savior arrives from above: educated, resourced, well-intentioned, and certain about what the community needs. The volunteer abroad. The nonprofit founder. The celebrity advocate. The tech entrepreneur solving Africa's problems. Saviorism reproduces colonial dynamics even when — especially when — it means well. The savior is the subject; the community is the object. The savior defines the problem; the community receives the solution. Power flows one way, and gratitude is the expected return.

## What It Costs

Communities disempowered by help. Local organizations undermined by international NGOs with bigger budgets and louder voices. Solutions that don't fit because they were designed elsewhere. Voluntourism that centers the volunteer's growth over the community's needs. The white savior industrial complex that Teju Cole named: 'The white savior supports brutal policies in the morning, founds charities in the afternoon, and receives awards in the evening.' The cost is not just inefficiency — it's the reinforcement of the colonial relationship in the language of love.

## What Persists

Solidarity — the practice of showing up alongside rather than arriving from above. Lilla Watson's formulation: 'If you have come here to help me, you are wasting your time. But if you have come because your liberation is bound up with mine, then let us work together.' Mutual aid networks led by affected communities. The decolonial turn in humanitarian practice. The growing insistence that communities are not problems to be solved but peoples to be accompanied.

## The Invitation

When you help, who benefits most — them or you? If your helping maintains your sense of yourself as good, generous, and needed, it may be saviorism wearing solidarity's clothes. Real solidarity is uncomfortable. It requires following rather than leading, listening rather than solving, and accepting that your role may be to get out of the way.
