# The Colonized Body at Work

*Body*

## The Pattern

Colonialism's economic engine was always bodily: enslaved labor, indentured servitude, coolie systems, maquiladoras, sweatshops, gig economy. The progression looks like freedom but the pattern remains — brown and Black bodies doing the most dangerous, least compensated, most invisible work. The global supply chain is a colonial anatomy: extraction in the Global South, consumption in the Global North, waste returned to the margins.

## What It Costs

Bodies broken in mines, fields, factories, warehouses. The triangle trade never ended — it just changed shape. Domestic workers excluded from labor protections. Agricultural workers exposed to pesticides. Garment workers in collapsing factories. Delivery drivers without bathrooms. The colonized body at work is still the body that makes comfort possible for others while being denied comfort itself.

## What Persists

Labor movements led by the most exploited workers — Dolores Huerta and the farmworkers, domestic workers organizing across borders, garment workers demanding accountability. Cooperative economics. Solidarity economy. Worker-owned enterprises. The insistence that labor is not a commodity and the body is not a tool. Mutual aid networks that predate and outlast union structures.

## The Invitation

Who made your clothes? Who picked your food? Who delivered your package? These are not rhetorical questions. They are relationships you're already in, whether or not you acknowledge them.
