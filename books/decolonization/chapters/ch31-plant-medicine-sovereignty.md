# Plant Medicine Sovereignty

*Spirit*

## The Pattern

The colonial state classifies peyote, ayahuasca, psilocybin, cannabis, coca — plants used ceremonially for centuries or millennia — as controlled substances. This criminalization serves multiple colonial functions: it disrupts indigenous ceremony, it pathologizes non-Western consciousness, it creates a drug war that disproportionately criminalizes communities of color, and it reserves the right to alter consciousness for pharmaceutical corporations (who sell antidepressants, anxiolytics, and stimulants at scale).

## What It Costs

Ceremonial use criminalized. Native American Church members arrested for peyote. Amazonian communities disrupted by ayahuasca tourism and law enforcement. Cannabis prohibition used as pretext for mass incarceration of Black communities. The drug war as a continuation of colonialism by other means — Nixon's domestic policy advisor John Ehrlichman admitted the war on drugs was designed to target 'the antiwar left and Black people.' Meanwhile, the psychedelic renaissance profits white entrepreneurs.

## What Persists

The Native American Church, which secured legal peyote use through decades of advocacy. Indigenous-led movements for plant medicine sovereignty. Decriminalization campaigns led by and centered on communities of color. The insistence that these are not 'drugs' but 'medicines' and 'sacraments' — and that the distinction matters. The growing recognition that criminalization was never about health and always about control.

## The Invitation

Who gets to decide which plants you can have a relationship with? Who profits from your inability to access certain states of consciousness, and who profits from selling you pharmaceutical alternatives? The politics of plant medicine are the politics of whose consciousness is legitimate.
