# Reconnecting with Non-Human Relations

*Spirit*

## The Pattern

Colonial ontology draws a hard line between human and non-human. Humans have souls, rights, language, culture. Animals, plants, rivers, mountains are resources, scenery, or at best, objects of conservation. This ontological apartheid is so deep that even well-meaning environmentalism often reproduces it — protecting 'nature' as something separate from human life rather than recognizing humans as participants in ecological community.

## What It Costs

The sixth mass extinction. Factory farming. Deforestation. Ocean acidification. The inability to grieve the loss of species because grief is reserved for persons, and animals are not persons. The loneliness of a species that has declared itself the only subject in a world of objects. Children who know brand logos but cannot name the birds outside their window. The rupture between human identity and ecological identity.

## What Persists

Indigenous cosmologies that never made the cut. The Lakota who addresses all beings as relatives. The Māori who recognize rivers as ancestors (the Whanganui River was granted legal personhood in 2017). The Aboriginal Australians whose law is inseparable from land. Animal communicators. Interspecies ethics. The growing field of plant intelligence research. The dog who teaches you about presence. The tree that teaches you about patience. Relationship persists wherever the colonial boundary fails.

## The Invitation

Go outside. Not to exercise, not to photograph, not to 'be in nature' (as if you were ever outside it). Just go outside and pay attention. What is alive around you? What is looking back? What would it mean to live as if you belonged to an ecological community rather than managed one?
