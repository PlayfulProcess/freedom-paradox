# Restorative vs. Punitive Justice

*Collective*

## The Pattern

Colonial justice is punitive: crime is an offense against the state, justice is punishment, the goal is deterrence. Indigenous justice systems across the world tended toward restoration: harm is a rupture in relationship, justice is repair, the goal is reintegration. The colonial prison — invented in its modern form in the 18th century — replaced banishment, restitution, mediation, and community accountability with cages. The US now imprisons more people than any nation in history.

## What It Costs

Mass incarceration as the continuation of slavery (the 13th Amendment explicitly allows involuntary servitude as punishment for crime). 2.3 million people in US prisons, disproportionately Black, Brown, indigenous, and poor. The destruction of communities through the removal of their members. Children growing up with incarcerated parents. The prison-industrial complex as an economic engine that profits from caging human beings. Recidivism rates that prove punishment doesn't work even by its own standards.

## What Persists

Restorative justice circles in schools, courts, and communities. South Africa's Truth and Reconciliation Commission. Navajo peacemaking courts. Rwanda's gacaca courts after the genocide. The growing abolition movement that asks not 'how do we reform prisons?' but 'what do we build instead?' Transformative justice practices in queer and activist communities. The ancient understanding that justice is not punishment but the repair of relationship.

## The Invitation

When someone harms you, what do you actually need? Punishment of the person who harmed you? Or acknowledgment, accountability, and the repair of what was broken? These are different needs. Colonial justice answers only the first. What would it look like to build systems that answer the second?
