# Decolonizing Research Methodologies

*Mind*

## The Pattern

Research is not a neutral act. Linda Tuhiwai Smith opens 'Decolonizing Methodologies' with: 'The word research is probably one of the dirtiest words in the indigenous world's vocabulary.' Research was the process by which indigenous peoples were measured, classified, photographed, sampled, theorized about, and made into objects of knowledge — all without consent, reciprocity, or accountability. The researcher took knowledge and left nothing.

## What It Costs

Communities studied to death — literally, in cases of medical experimentation, and figuratively, in the sense that being perpetually researched becomes its own form of extraction. Knowledge taken from communities, published in journals they can't access, used to advance careers in institutions that exclude them. The research relationship as a mirror of the colonial relationship: one side extracts, the other is extracted from.

## What Persists

Community-based participatory research. Indigenous research methodologies that begin with relationship and end with reciprocity. Kaupapa Māori research. The principle of OCAP (Ownership, Control, Access, Possession) for First Nations data in Canada. The growing insistence that communities have the right to own their own knowledge, approve research about themselves, and benefit from findings. Research as relationship rather than extraction.

## The Invitation

If you do research — academic, journalistic, artistic, technological — ask: whose knowledge am I using? What am I giving back? Who benefits from what I find? These questions don't make research impossible. They make it accountable.
