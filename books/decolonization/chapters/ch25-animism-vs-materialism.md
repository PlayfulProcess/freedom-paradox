# Animism vs. Materialism

*Spirit*

## The Pattern

The colonial division: subjects and objects. Humans have souls, agency, rights. Everything else — animals, plants, rivers, mountains, stones — is matter. Resource. Property. This ontological cleaving made colonialism possible. You cannot enslave a person you recognize as fully human. You cannot clearcut a forest you experience as a community of beings. Descartes' animal-as-machine and Bacon's nature-as-object-of-domination are not philosophy — they are permission slips.

## What It Costs

An ecological crisis that is, at its root, a spiritual crisis. When the river is a resource, you dam it. When the forest is timber, you cut it. When the animal is protein, you factory-farm it. Materialism didn't just disenchant the world — it made the world available for unlimited exploitation. The loneliness epidemic is partly this: living in a world drained of aliveness, surrounded by objects rather than relatives.

## What Persists

Animism never left. Most of the world's cultures maintain some form of relational ontology — the understanding that human beings exist within, not above, a community of living beings. Shintoism. Aboriginal Dreamtime. Andean Pachamama. West African Vodun. Even within Western culture, the stubborn intuition that the dog is not a machine, the tree is not lumber, the river is not a water delivery system. Science itself — through ecology, complexity theory, and quantum physics — keeps rediscovering interconnection.

## The Invitation

What if the world is alive? Not metaphorically. Not as a nice thought. But as an ontological reality that indigenous people have always known and Western science is beginning to confirm? What changes in how you live if you take this seriously?
