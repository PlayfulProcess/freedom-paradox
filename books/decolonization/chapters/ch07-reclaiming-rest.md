# Reclaiming Rest

*Body*

## The Pattern

Colonialism and capitalism share a logic: the body exists to produce. Rest is laziness. Sleep is wasted time. The 'Protestant work ethic' — rebranded from its religious origins into a supposedly universal virtue — pathologizes rest and sacralizes productivity. Grind culture is plantation logic with a MacBook. The colonized body was never allowed to rest; the post-colonial body has internalized the prohibition.

## What It Costs

Sleep deprivation as a racialized health crisis. Burnout as a badge of honor. The inability to rest without guilt. Communities of color bearing the highest burdens of overwork, shift work, and sleep disruption while being stereotyped as lazy. The erasure of sabbath, siesta, dreaming time, fallow seasons — all the ways cultures built rest into the structure of life before productivity became the only measure of worth.

## What Persists

The Nap Ministry, founded by Tricia Hersey, frames rest as resistance and reparation. Slow movements — slow food, slow medicine, slow scholarship. Indigenous time, which is not clock time. The Sabbath traditions that survive in multiple faiths. The growing recognition that burnout is not personal failure but systemic design. The radical act of doing nothing.

## The Invitation

Can you rest without producing? Without consuming? Without justifying the rest as making you more productive later? What if rest is not preparation for work but a form of life in itself?
