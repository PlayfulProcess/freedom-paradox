# Indigenous Governance and Radical Democracy

*Collective*

## The Pattern

Colonial governance presents itself as the invention of democracy. In reality, the Haudenosaunee Confederacy practiced participatory governance for centuries before European contact — and directly influenced the US Constitution (a debt rarely acknowledged). Indigenous governance systems worldwide developed sophisticated decision-making: consensus processes, council systems, matrilineal authority, seasonal rotation of leadership, governance embedded in ceremony rather than separated into a secular sphere.

## What It Costs

The erasure of indigenous political sophistication. The assumption that pre-colonial peoples had no governance — that colonialism brought 'order' to 'chaos.' The imposition of band council systems in Canada that replaced traditional governance with colonial structures. The destruction of matrilineal authority by patriarchal colonial systems. The loss of governance models that integrated ecological sustainability, spiritual accountability, and intergenerational thinking — models that Western democracy desperately needs.

## What Persists

The Haudenosaunee Confederacy still operates. The Zapatista juntas de buen gobierno (councils of good government) demonstrate indigenous-inspired direct democracy. The Kurdish experiment in Rojava draws on council-based governance. Indigenous women's leadership — which colonialism targeted specifically — reasserts itself from Idle No More to water protection movements. The recognition that the world's oldest continuous democracies are indigenous.

## The Invitation

What if democracy didn't begin in Athens? What if the most sophisticated governance experiments in human history were destroyed by the people who claimed to have invented governance? What governance wisdom exists in communities near you that political science doesn't recognize?
