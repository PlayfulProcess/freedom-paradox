# Grief as Spiritual Practice

*Spirit*

## The Pattern

Colonial modernity has no grammar for collective grief. Individual grief is medicalized (five stages, time-limited, therapist-managed). Collective grief — for genocide, for ecocide, for the ongoing losses of colonialism — has no sanctioned form at all. You are expected to 'move on,' to be 'resilient,' to 'not dwell.' Andreotti's central insight in Hospicing Modernity: modernity is dying, and we need to grieve it. Not celebrate its death or cling to its life, but hospice it — with presence, with honesty, with the willingness to sit in the room where something is ending.

## What It Costs

Unprocessed grief becomes depression, addiction, rage, numbness. Communities carrying intergenerational trauma without ceremonies adequate to hold it. The expectation that indigenous peoples should be 'over' colonialism. The expectation that Black communities should be 'over' slavery. The absence of national rituals of mourning for what was done — because mourning would require acknowledging what was done, and acknowledgment would require accountability.

## What Persists

Wailing traditions. Keening. The Irish wake. Jewish shiva. Aboriginal sorry business. The capacity for communal grief that colonial individualism tried to eliminate. The growing understanding that grief is not weakness but a form of love — love for what was lost, love for what might have been, love for what is dying. Joanna Macy's 'Work That Reconnects,' which centers grief for the world as the gateway to action. The emergence of grief rituals in activist communities.

## The Invitation

What are you grieving that you haven't been allowed to grieve? Not just personal losses — collective ones. The language your great-grandparents spoke. The forest that was here before. The world that should have been possible. Grief is not giving up. It's the body's way of acknowledging what matters.
