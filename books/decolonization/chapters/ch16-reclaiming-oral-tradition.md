# Reclaiming Oral Tradition

*Mind*

## The Pattern

The colonial hierarchy: written over oral, text over song, archive over memory, publication over performance. This hierarchy isn't about preservation — oral traditions maintained knowledge for tens of thousands of years before writing existed. It's about control. Written knowledge can be owned, copyrighted, stored in institutions, accessed by the literate (which is to say, the colonially educated). Oral knowledge belongs to communities, to relationships, to the living voice.

## What It Costs

The delegitimization of entire knowledge traditions. Aboriginal Australian oral tradition has maintained accurate geographical and ecological knowledge for over 10,000 years — longer than any written archive. But because it's oral, it's classified as 'myth' rather than 'science.' West African griots carry centuries of genealogy and history in memory — but because it's not written, it's 'folklore' rather than 'history.' The hierarchy of text over voice is a hierarchy of colonial over indigenous.

## What Persists

Griots still sing. Elders still teach. Story circles still carry law, medicine, history, and prayer. Podcasting and digital audio create new forms of oral culture. Hip-hop carries narrative tradition through rhythm and voice. Indigenous communities increasingly assert that their oral traditions constitute legal evidence of sovereignty and land relationship. The voice persists because it lives in bodies, not in buildings that can burn.

## The Invitation

What do you know by heart? Not memorized from a book but carried in your voice, passed to you by a voice? What stories were told to you that you now tell? Oral tradition is not the absence of writing. It's the presence of relationship.
