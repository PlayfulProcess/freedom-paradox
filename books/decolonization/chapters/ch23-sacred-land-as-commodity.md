# Sacred Land as Commodity

*Spirit*

## The Pattern

For colonial economics, land is property — a resource to be owned, developed, extracted from, and sold. For most indigenous cosmologies, land is relative — a living being with whom you have reciprocal obligations. These are not two perspectives on the same thing. They are two entirely different realities. The colonial conversion of sacred land into real estate is not a change of use — it's an ontological violence. You're not just taking someone's home. You're killing a relative.

## What It Costs

Mauna Kea, where telescopes stand on a Hawaiian sacred site. Standing Rock, where a pipeline crosses Lakota sacred water. Uluru, where tourists climbed a sacred being until 2019. Oak Flat, where copper mining threatens Apache sacred ground. The Black Hills, still legally Lakota territory by treaty. The pattern is consistent: colonial law recognizes property rights and religious freedom in the abstract while overriding both whenever extraction is profitable.

## What Persists

Land defenders. Water protectors. The legal recognition — slowly, inadequately — of sacred sites. Indigenous-led conservation that protects land not as wilderness (a colonial concept: land without people) but as homeland. The growing land back movement. The river people who still pray to the rivers even when the rivers are dammed. The mountain people who still greet the mountains even when the mountains are mined.

## The Invitation

Is there a place that is sacred to you? Not historically significant or aesthetically beautiful, but sacred — a place that would wound you to see destroyed? That feeling is not nostalgia. It's relationship. Now imagine your entire cosmology is built on that feeling, and the law calls it superstition.
