# Ancestor Practices and the Severed Lineage

*Spirit*

## The Pattern

Most human cultures maintain active relationship with ancestors — through altar, prayer, offering, festival, naming, dream. Colonialism severed these relationships in multiple ways: by physically displacing people from ancestral lands and burial sites, by declaring ancestor veneration demonic or primitive, by breaking family structures through slavery and boarding schools, by replacing ancestral identity with national identity. The colonized person is a person cut off from their dead.

## What It Costs

Rootlessness. The sense that you come from nowhere, that history began with your birth, that the dead are gone rather than present. For enslaved peoples, the severing was literal — names taken, languages forbidden, families separated, burial practices denied. For colonized indigenous peoples, the connection to ancestral land — where the bones are, where the stories live — was broken by removal, relocation, and reservation. For settlers, a different loss: the slow forgetting of the old country's practices, replaced by consumer identity.

## What Persists

Día de los Muertos. Qingming Festival. Obon. Samhain. Ancestor altars in Vodun, Candomblé, and Shinto traditions. The African American practice of pouring libation. DNA testing as secular ancestor search. The growing movement to build ancestor practices even — especially — when the lineage is broken. The understanding that you don't need to know their names to honor their existence.

## The Invitation

Who are your dead? Not famous dead — your dead. The ones whose choices, suffering, love, and survival put you here. What do you owe them? Not guilt — relationship. The dead are not past. They are the ground you stand on.
