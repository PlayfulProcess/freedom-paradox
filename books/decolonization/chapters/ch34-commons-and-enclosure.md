# Commons and Enclosure

*Collective*

## The Pattern

Before colonialism, most human communities managed shared resources — land, water, forest, fisheries, knowledge — through commons: collectively governed, rule-bound, sustainable. Enclosure — the privatization of commons — began in England and was exported globally through colonialism. Land that communities managed together became property that individuals owned. The commons didn't fail. It was enclosed — fenced, privatized, and sold. This is the original accumulation that made capitalism possible.

## What It Costs

Landlessness as a global condition. The creation of a class that must sell its labor because it no longer has access to land. The tragedy of the commons — Garrett Hardin's famous argument — is actually the tragedy of enclosure: commons worked for millennia; it's private property that produces overexploitation. Digital enclosure continues the pattern: knowledge, culture, and code that could be shared are fenced by copyright, patents, and paywalls.

## What Persists

Commons governance still functions — Elinor Ostrom won the Nobel Prize for demonstrating it. Community forests in Nepal. Irrigation commons in Spain and Bali. Open-source software. Creative Commons. Community land trusts. The ancient pattern of shared governance over shared resources refuses to die because it works. Indigenous land management — which is commons governance — is increasingly recognized as superior to both private ownership and state management for ecological outcomes.

## The Invitation

What do you share? Not as charity but as commons — resources you govern together with others? If the answer is 'not much,' that's not human nature. That's enclosure. What would it look like to rebuild commons in your life?
