# Reclaiming Indigenous Food Systems

*Body*

## The Pattern

Colonial agriculture replaced polyculture with monoculture, commons with enclosure, seed-saving with seed-buying, seasonal eating with global supply chains. The milpa — corn, beans, squash growing together in mutual support — was replaced by corn alone, standing in rows like soldiers. Food became commodity. Nutrition became science. Eating became consumption.

## What It Costs

Diabetes epidemics in indigenous communities fed commodity foods on reservations. Soil death from monoculture. Seed sovereignty lost to patent law. The knowledge of which plants grow together, which roots heal, which seasons call for which foods — this knowledge doesn't just vanish. It gets actively suppressed, ridiculed as primitive, replaced by nutritional science that keeps rediscovering what grandmothers always knew.

## What Persists

The Three Sisters still grow. Seed libraries and indigenous seed keepers maintain varieties that industrial agriculture abandoned. Food sovereignty movements from the Zapatistas to Standing Rock center food as political and spiritual practice. Urban indigenous gardens. Community kitchens. The return of bison to the plains. Ancestral grain revival — amaranth, teff, quinoa (though quinoa's global popularity created its own extractive dynamic).

## The Invitation

What did your great-grandparents eat? Not as nostalgia, but as inquiry. What food relationships were broken in your lineage? What would it mean to eat in relationship — to soil, to season, to the beings who become your body?
