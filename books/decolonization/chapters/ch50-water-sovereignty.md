# Water Sovereignty

*Planet*

## The Pattern

Water privatization is enclosure applied to the most essential commons. From Cochabamba to Detroit, from Flint to the Niger Delta, the pattern repeats: public water systems are defunded, privatized, or poisoned, and the communities affected are always the most colonized. The World Bank conditions loans on water privatization. Nestlé extracts billions of gallons from aquifers for pennies. Water becomes commodity in a world where 2 billion people lack safe drinking water.

## What It Costs

Flint, Michigan: majority-Black city poisoned by lead in the water for years while officials denied it. Cochabamba, Bolivia: water privatized until rain collection was illegal, provoking a popular uprising. Detroit: water shutoffs in a majority-Black city while corporations owed more and paid less. Standing Rock: the pipeline that oil companies wouldn't route through white communities was routed through Lakota water. The pattern is always the same: colonial and racial capitalism determines whose water is protected and whose is sacrificed.

## What Persists

The Cochabamba Water War (2000) that reversed privatization through popular uprising. The UN recognition of water as a human right (2010). Water protectors — Autumn Peltier, the movement at Standing Rock — who insist that water is not a commodity but a relative. 'Mni Wiconi' — Water is Life — as both Lakota prayer and political demand. Community water systems. Watershed councils. The ancient understanding that water belongs to no one and sustains everyone.

## The Invitation

Where does your water come from? Not the tap — the source. What watershed do you live in? Who else — human and non-human — depends on it? Water sovereignty begins with knowing your water, which begins with knowing your land.
