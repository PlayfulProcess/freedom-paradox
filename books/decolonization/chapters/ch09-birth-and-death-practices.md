# Birth and Death Practices

*Body*

## The Pattern

Colonization medicalized the two most sacred thresholds of human experience. Birth moved from home to hospital, from midwife to obstetrician, from community ritual to medical procedure. Death moved from the family home to the institution, from community vigil to professional management. Both transitions replaced feminine, communal, spiritual authority with masculine, institutional, scientific authority. The body entering and leaving this world was made into a patient.

## What It Costs

Maternal mortality rates for Black women in the US are three to four times higher than for white women — not because of biology but because of how colonial medicine treats Black bodies. Indigenous birthing practices disrupted by forced hospital births far from home communities. Death industrialized — embalming, caskets, funeral homes replacing home washing, community sitting, natural return to earth. Grief pathologized and time-limited.

## What Persists

Midwifery returning — indigenous midwives, doulas, birth workers centering cultural practice. Death doulas and home funeral movements. Green burial. The return to sitting with the dying. Indigenous communities reclaiming birth on the land. The understanding that how we enter and leave this world is not a medical question but a cosmological one.

## The Invitation

How do you want to be born? How do you want to die? These questions were answered for you before you could ask them. The answers came from a system that treats both thresholds as problems to be managed rather than mysteries to be accompanied.
