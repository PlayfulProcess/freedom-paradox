# Ceremony and Permission

*Spirit*

## The Pattern

Colonial spirituality is universally available — anyone can walk into a church, pick up a Bible, claim salvation. Indigenous ceremony is relationally mediated — you don't just attend, you are invited. You don't just learn, you are taught by someone authorized to teach. You don't just practice, you practice within a community that holds you accountable. The colonial impulse is to make all spiritual knowledge open-access. The indigenous response is that some knowledge requires preparation, relationship, and the right to receive it.

## What It Costs

When ceremony loses its gatekeepers, it loses its integrity. Ayahuasca drunk without a curandero. Sun Dance performed without Lakota elders. Pipe ceremony led by someone who bought a pipe online. The harm is not just cultural — it's spiritual (people entering powerful states without containers) and physical (ayahuasca tourism has produced injuries and deaths). The deeper loss: the understanding that not all knowledge is yours to have, and that limitation is not oppression but structure.

## What Persists

Communities that still hold ceremony with integrity. The elder who says 'you're not ready' and means it as a gift. The teacher who says 'this is not mine to give you' and models the difference between generosity and extraction. The growing conversation about spiritual accountability — not gatekeeping as exclusion, but gatekeeping as care.

## The Invitation

In a culture that treats access as a right, what does it mean to be told 'not yet' or 'not you' or 'not this way'? Can you receive a boundary as an invitation to deepen rather than a wall to climb?
