# Deep Ecology vs. Indigenous Ecology

*Planet*

## The Pattern

Deep ecology, founded by Arne Næss, calls for recognizing nature's intrinsic value beyond human use. This is an important corrective to purely instrumental environmentalism. But deep ecology often reinvents what indigenous peoples have practiced for millennia — without acknowledgment, relationship, or reciprocity. Worse, deep ecology's emphasis on wilderness and population reduction can reproduce colonial logics: 'too many people' has always meant 'too many of those people,' and wilderness preservation has always meant indigenous removal.

## What It Costs

The appropriation of indigenous ontology by Western environmentalism without credit or accountability. The 'population bomb' discourse that targets Global South reproduction while ignoring Global North consumption. The misanthropy that sees humans as the problem rather than colonial capitalism as the problem. The missed opportunity for genuine partnership between Western environmental movements and indigenous ecological knowledge holders.

## What Persists

Indigenous ecology — not as a category within environmentalism but as a distinct tradition with its own ontology, ethics, and practices. Robin Wall Kimmerer's 'Braiding Sweetgrass' bridging indigenous and scientific ecological knowledge. The Rights of Nature movement grounded in indigenous legal traditions. Political ecology that insists the environmental crisis is inseparable from colonial capitalism. The refusal to choose between human justice and ecological justice — because they are the same justice.

## The Invitation

When you feel love for the earth, where does that love come from? If it comes from a tradition that erased the people who practiced it first, it's worth asking: how do you love the earth without appropriating the love of those who were here first? The answer is relationship, not ideology.
