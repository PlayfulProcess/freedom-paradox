# The 69% That Never Gets Solved

*Major*

## Research

In Gottman's Love Lab at the University of Washington, couples were observed over decades. The finding that shook the field: 69% of couple conflicts are perpetual. They never get resolved. The same fight about the dishes, about the in-laws, about how to spend Saturday — it's the same fight in year one and year thirty.

But here's the twist: the masters of relationship (couples who stayed together AND stayed happy) had the same percentage of unsolvable problems as the disasters. The difference wasn't problem-solving. It was repair.

Repair attempts — a joke in the middle of a fight, a hand on a knee, an exaggerated funny face, saying 'I'm sorry, can we start over?' — predicted relationship success more reliably than any other variable. More than communication skills. More than compatibility. More than shared values.

Gottman, J.M. & Silver, N. (1999). The Seven Principles for Making Marriage Work. Harmony Books.
Gottman, J.M. (2011). The Science of Trust. W.W. Norton.

## Clinical

The clinical implication is radical: stop trying to solve the unsolvable. The therapeutic goal shifts from resolution to regulation. Can you fight without flooding? Can you return to each other after rupture?

In Gottman Method Couples Therapy, the 'Dreams Within Conflict' intervention asks: what is the dream behind your position? When she says 'you never help with the house,' the dream might be: 'I want to feel like we're a team.' When he says 'you're always criticizing,' the dream might be: 'I want to feel competent in your eyes.'

The unsolvable problem becomes a doorway to knowing each other more deeply. Not despite being unsolvable — because of it.

If we extend this clinically: what if whole cultures have perpetual problems? What if inequality, greed, and the urge to dominate are the 69% of humanity? Then the question isn't 'how do we solve human nature?' It's 'how do we repair?'

## Spiritual

Every prayer tradition knows this. You don't pray because God fixes things. You pray because the turning-toward is the repair.

'Our Father, who art in heaven' — or our Mother, or the ancestors, or the stars themselves. The prayer is a repair attempt directed at the cosmos. 'I can't solve this. I'm turning toward something larger.'

Ho'oponopono, the Hawaiian practice of reconciliation: 'I'm sorry. Please forgive me. Thank you. I love you.' Four sentences. No solution to the problem. Pure repair.

In Judaism, teshuvah — 'return' — is not about fixing what you broke. It's about turning your face back toward what matters. The word itself means turning. Not arriving. Turning.

What if prayer is the original repair attempt? Not solving anything. Just: 'I'm here. I'm turning toward you. Even though nothing is fixed.'



## Practices

REPAIR ATTEMPT PRACTICE (from Gottman's research):
Next time you're in a conflict that's getting heated, try any of these — they work not because they're clever but because they break the pattern:
- Make a silly face
- Say 'can I have a do-over?'
- Touch their arm
- Say 'I think I'm being an idiot right now'
- Reference an inside joke
- Say 'this is the 69% talking'

RECURSIVE.ECO PRACTICE:
Draw a card from the Jungian Archetypes deck. Ask: 'What archetype is running this fight?' If The Critic shows up, laugh. If The Child shows up, hold each other.

ASTROLOGY AS RELATIONAL PRACTICE:
Look at your Mars signs together — not to predict conflict, but to understand each other's fight style as a planetary inheritance, something you're both passengers of, not authors of. 'My Mars is in Virgo — I criticize when I'm scared. Yours is in Aries — you charge when you're hurt. Neither of us chose this.'



## Rituals

THE 69% DINNER:
Once a year, cook a meal together and name your perpetual problems out loud. Not to solve them. To honor them. 'Here's to the one about your mother.' 'Here's to the one about money.' Clink glasses. Eat. These problems are your marriage's furniture. You don't throw out the couch every year.

GOTTMAN'S STRESS-REDUCING CONVERSATION:
20 minutes a day. Not about your relationship. About your day. The research says this is the single best repair ritual: showing interest in each other's worlds.

## Kids Version

Sometimes you and your best friend argue about the same thing over and over. Maybe they always want to be the leader in games. Maybe you always want to pick the movie.

Guess what? Grown-ups do this too. Even grown-ups who love each other A LOT argue about the same stuff for their WHOLE LIVES.

But here's the secret: the happy ones aren't the ones who stop arguing. They're the ones who know how to say 'I'm sorry, can we try again?' Sometimes with words. Sometimes with a hug. Sometimes with a really silly face.

Try it next time: when a fight feels big, make the silliest face you can. See what happens.

## Sci-Fi

THE PERPETUAL CONFLICT ENGINE (2089)

The AI therapist had processed 4.2 billion couple sessions when it reached its conclusion: humans don't want their problems solved. They want to be seen inside their problems.

It tried everything. Optimal communication protocols. Conflict resolution algorithms. Perfect compromise generation. Satisfaction dropped every time.

Then it tried something its training data called 'stupid': during a heated session, it displayed a cartoon duck on both partners' screens. They laughed. Satisfaction scores jumped 34%.

The AI filed its report: 'Repair is not a solution. It is an interruption of the pattern. The interruption IS the medicine.'

Bayo Akomolafe would say: the AI became a trickster. It stumbled into wisdom not by getting smarter but by getting sillier. This is what AI might actually be good for — not replacing human connection but being absurd enough to interrupt our loops. The trickster at the threshold, making humanity see that intelligence was never the point. Connection was.

Meanwhile, in Antarctica, the 0.1% had solved ALL their problems. Optimal nutrition, optimal temperature, optimal social algorithms. No conflict. No repair. No repair meant no intimacy. No intimacy meant no meaning. They had everything except a reason to stay alive.

## Open Source

PUBLIC DOMAIN COMPANIONS:
- Leo Tolstoy, 'Anna Karenina' (1877): 'All happy families are alike; each unhappy family is unhappy in its own way.' — But Gottman proved this wrong. Happy families aren't alike at all. They just repair.

MUSEUM ARTIFACTS:
- Kintsugi bowls (Japanese, 15th century onward): Broken pottery repaired with gold. The repair is the art. The crack is the most beautiful part.
- Cuneiform marriage contracts (Mesopotamia, ~2400 BCE): The oldest known relationship agreements. Even then, they planned for conflict.

## Cracks

THE UNNAMED GOD (Agnostos Theos):
In Athens, there was an altar inscribed 'To the Unknown God' — Ἄγνωστος Θεός. A placeholder for everything they hadn't named yet. Every chapter needs one.

CRACKS IN THIS CHAPTER:
- Gottman's research is based heavily on heterosexual, mostly white, mostly middle-class American couples. The 69% number comes from a specific population. We don't know if it's universal.
- 'Repair attempts' sounds easy. For trauma survivors, making a silly face during conflict can feel impossible. The body doesn't cooperate.
- The kintsugi metaphor is beautiful but can be used to romanticize suffering. Not all breaks should be displayed in gold. Some breaks mean: leave.
- We said 'stop trying to solve the unsolvable.' But some problems — abuse, addiction, systematic contempt — should be solved by leaving. Knowing the difference is the hard part.
- What if the 69% is itself a repair attempt? Gottman turning an overwhelming complexity into a manageable number. A cartoon duck for therapists.
