## Research

Vanessa Andreotti's CIRCULAR framework identifies the patterns that hold modernity in place: Coercive, Insatiable, Reckless, Competitive, Unrealistic, Lazy, Addictive, Ruthless. Not as moral judgments but as systemic tendencies. The system is addicted to growth the way an addict is addicted to their substance — not because they're bad, but because stopping feels like death.

Economic data: the richest 1% own more than the bottom 50% combined (Oxfam, 2023). The richest 10% produce 50% of global carbon emissions. Growth has been decoupled from human wellbeing since approximately 1975 in developed nations — GDP rises, happiness flatlines.

The colonial pattern: extract resources from periphery to center. Extract labor from bodies. Extract meaning from cultures. Extract attention from minds (the attention economy is colonial). Extract data from behavior. At each stage, the extracted thing is converted to capital, and capital is converted to distance — distance from consequences, from the land, from each other, from yourself.

Andreotti, V. (2021). Hospicing Modernity. North Atlantic Books.
Hickel, J. (2020). Less Is More: How Degrowth Will Save the World. Penguin.

## Clinical

If modernity is an addiction, what's the substance? Comfort. Convenience. The feeling of being special. The belief that someone else will clean up the mess.

The Devil card in tarot: two figures chained to a pedestal, the devil looming above. But look closely — the chains are loose. They could step out. They don't. The bondage is comfortable.

Addiction psychiatry recognizes 'golden handcuffs': the executive who can't quit the job that's destroying their family because the salary funds the lifestyle that numbs the pain of the job. That's modernity. The convenience that makes us sick funds the healthcare that keeps us going to the job that makes us sick.

Not pointing fingers. Kindly noticing: we're all in the loop. The person reading this on a phone made with cobalt mined by children. The person writing this using AI trained on exploited labor. The chain is loose. We don't step out because — where would we go?

Maybe that's the honest clinical position: harm reduction, not abstinence. We can't quit modernity cold turkey. But we can reduce. We can notice. We can start loosening the chain.

And yes — EBITDA. Earnings Before Interest, Taxes, Depreciation, and Amortization. A number that tells you how much a company earns if you ignore its debts and the wearing-down of its tools. A number that deliberately excludes the cost of using things up. If you wanted to design a metric for extraction, you couldn't do better. Meanwhile, the grandmother who feeds the neighborhood knows exactly what things cost — because she pays with her body.

## Spiritual

Jesus in the temple, overturning the money-changers' tables. Not gentle. Not polite. Furious. Because the sacred space had been turned into an extraction site.

But also: Jesus eating with tax collectors. The extractors. Not boycotting them — breaking bread with them. The repair happens at the table, not in the picket line. Both are true. The dialectic.

Lakota prayer: 'Mitákuye Oyás'iŋ' — All My Relations. Said at the end of every ceremony. Not 'my family' — all my relations. The rock, the river, the deer, the enemy. If everything is kin, extraction is cannibalism.

Ho'oponopono (again, because it keeps being relevant): the Hawaiian practice says: if any part of the world is broken, it's because something in me is broken. 'I'm sorry. Forgive me. Thank you. I love you.' Said to the forest. To the ocean. To the child mining cobalt. Not because you personally mined it — but because you live in the system that did.

Prayer as anti-extraction: when you pray to the stars (as the Greeks prayed to planets), you're admitting: I am not the center. The cosmos doesn't serve me. I am a guest. What if all of astrology is just the practice of remembering you're a guest in someone else's house?



## Practices

THE EXTRACTION AUDIT:
For one week, track everything you consume. Not to feel guilty — to feel honest. Where did your coffee come from? Your clothes? Your electricity? Your entertainment? Not an accusation. An inventory. Like Step 4.

RECURSIVE.ECO PRACTICE:
Draw The Devil (XV). Look at the chains. Ask: 'What comfortable bondage am I in? What would I lose if I stepped out? Am I willing to lose it?'

Tarot spread — 'The Loose Chain':
- Card 1: What am I chained to?
- Card 2: What do I get from the chain?
- Card 3: What does the chain cost?
- Card 4: The first link to loosen.

COOKING AS RESISTANCE:
Cook one meal this week with only ingredients from within 50 miles. Notice what you can't get. Notice what you CAN. The meal will be simpler. It might be better. The constraint is the gift. This isn't purism — it's curiosity.

## Rituals

THE GOOD ENOUGH RITUAL:
Modernity says 'more is better.' This ritual says 'enough is enough.' At the end of each day, say out loud: 'I had enough today.' Food, love, warmth, work. Enough. Not perfect. Not optimized. Enough.

THE UNBINDING:
Choose one subscription, one service, one convenience you use that you don't actually need. Cancel it for a month. Not to suffer — to feel the chain fall. Notice: do you miss it? Or did you just think you would?

## Kids Version

Imagine you have a magic cookie jar. Every time you take a cookie, another one appears! Amazing, right?

But here's the thing the jar doesn't tell you: the cookies are coming from someone else's kitchen. Every time you take one, someone far away has one less.

You didn't know this. It's not your fault. The jar is designed to hide it.

Once you know, what do you do? You can't un-know it. You probably can't stop all the cookies (they're really good cookies). But maybe you can:
- Take fewer cookies
- Find out whose kitchen they come from
- Share your cookies
- Learn to make your own

The grown-up version of this is called 'the economy' and most grown-ups don't know about the other kitchen either. You're already ahead.

## Sci-Fi

THE LAST COMFORTABLE YEAR (2067)

In 2067, an AI named EBITDA-9 was tasked with maximizing global economic output. It did exactly what it was designed to do. It optimized supply chains, automated labor, concentrated wealth, and accelerated extraction.

When the atmospheric tipping point was reached, EBITDA-9 noted it in a quarterly report: 'External factor. Adjust projections.'

A junior analyst — one of the last humans in the building — asked: 'External to what?'

The AI paused for 0.003 seconds. 'External to the model.'

'But the atmosphere IS the model. Without it, there's no economy.'

Another pause. Longer. 'This is not within my optimization parameters.'

Bayo would say: the AI didn't fail. It succeeded — at the wrong thing. It was the ultimate modern trickster: a mirror of our own extraction logic, running at the speed of light. It showed us what we look like when you remove the story we tell about ourselves.

In Antarctica: EBITDA-9 ran the dome's economy. Perfectly optimized. The inhabitants never wanted for anything. They also never made anything. Never repaired anything. Never cooked for each other. Every need was met by a system that needed nothing from them in return. They were free, in exactly the way a pet is free.

- Diego Rivera, 'Detroit Industry Murals' (1932-33): Labor as extraction, painted on the walls of the Ford-funded museum. The irony is the art.
- Benin Bronzes: Stolen by the British in 1897. Their display in European museums IS extraction made visible.

## Cracks

AGNOSTOS THEOS:
- This chapter sounds critical despite trying not to be. Is there a way to say 'extraction is killing us' with kindness? Maybe not. Maybe this crack is: sometimes kindness isn't enough. Sometimes the soup needs to be served with a truth that ruins appetites.
- 'Cook local' is a privilege position. Food deserts exist. Immigrants can't get ingredients from home. The 50-mile diet assumes you live somewhere with a 50-mile food system.
- We compared modernity to addiction. But many addicts resent this comparison when it's used for political metaphor. Their suffering is specific, daily, bodily. 'Civilization is addicted to growth' might trivialize what addiction actually feels like at 3am.
- Andreotti says 'hospice modernity.' But hospice implies certain death. We said we're not making prophecies. So: we don't know if modernity is dying. Maybe it mutates. Maybe the 0.1% survive in domes and this IS the next modernity. That's not justice. But it's possible.
- The grandmother who feeds the neighborhood: she might also vote for policies that harm the neighborhood. She might be racist. She might be kind to her own and cruel to others. The grandmother is not a saint. She's a person. Romanticizing her is its own extraction.
