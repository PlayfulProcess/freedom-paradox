# Making Amends

*Major*

## Research

Steps 8 and 9 of Alcoholics Anonymous: 'Made a list of all persons we had harmed, and became willing to make amends to them all' and 'Made direct amends to such people wherever possible, except when to do so would injure them or others.' The 12-step model has been studied across 200+ randomized controlled trials. Cochrane reviews (2020) confirm AA/TSF is as effective as CBT for alcohol use disorder, and superior for continuous abstinence. But the amends steps are under-researched clinically — they're treated as spiritual, not scientific. Yet every recovering person will tell you: steps 8 and 9 are where the real work happens.

The mechanism isn't apology. It's inventory. You sit down and write the list. Every person. Every harm. The willingness comes before the action. You don't make amends because you're ready. You become ready by making the list.

Kurtz, E. (1979). Not-God: A History of Alcoholics Anonymous. Hazelden.

## Clinical

Addiction is a repair failure. Not a moral failure — a repair failure. The substance or behavior was originally adaptive: it repaired an intolerable feeling. Alcohol repaired social anxiety. Opioids repaired trauma pain. Gambling repaired the flatness of a life drained of meaning.

The clinical frame that changed everything: addiction as attachment disorder (Flores, 2004). The substance IS the attachment figure. It's always available, never rejecting, perfectly reliable — until it kills you.

Linehan's DBT (next chapter) would say: the behavior is maladaptive but the need is valid. You needed repair. You reached for what was available.

Now scale it: if modernity is an addiction — to growth, extraction, consumption, productivity — then modernity is also a repair failure at civilizational scale. We needed connection. We reached for stuff. The stuff worked, briefly. Now it's killing the host.

The 12-step insight: you cannot recover alone. The myth of the self-made individual IS the addiction. Recovery requires admitting: I need the group. I need something larger. I need to make amends.

## Spiritual

Step 2: 'Came to believe that a Power greater than ourselves could restore us to sanity.' The genius of AA is the vagueness: 'a Power greater than ourselves.' Not God. Not Jesus. Not Allah. A power. Greater than ourselves.

Some people choose the group itself as their higher power. Some choose nature. Some choose the mystery. The Unknown God — Agnostos Theos — is a perfectly valid higher power.

Confession in Christianity. Vidui in Judaism (the Yom Kippur confession). Pratikramana in Jainism (annual ritual of asking forgiveness from all beings — including animals, insects, and plants). The universal human intuition: naming what you've done, out loud, to someone or Something, is repair.

Prayer in recovery: 'God, grant me the serenity to accept the things I cannot change, the courage to change the things I can, and the wisdom to know the difference.' The Serenity Prayer is Reinhold Niebuhr meets attachment theory. I can't do this alone. Help me discern what's mine to carry.



## Practices

THE AMENDS LIST (adapted from Step 8):
Take a piece of paper. Write every person you've harmed. Not just the big ones — the small ones too. The friend you ghosted. The partner you lied to. The child you yelled at. Yourself.

Don't do anything with the list yet. The practice is the willingness to see.

RECURSIVE.ECO PRACTICE:
Draw Judgement (XX) from the Rider-Waite-Smith deck. The angel calls. The dead rise from their coffins. Ask: 'What is rising in me that I've tried to keep buried? What amends am I being called to make?'

COMMUNITY PRACTICE:
A talking circle. Each person names one thing they want to repair — not confessing, not performing guilt. Just: 'I want to repair my relationship with ___.' The circle holds it. No advice. No absolution. Just witnessing.

COOKING AS AMENDS:
Make someone a meal you know they love. Don't explain why. Don't attach a card. Just: here. I made this for you. The food says what the mouth can't.

## Rituals

YOM KIPPUR ADAPTATION (for anyone, any tradition):
Once a year, take a full day. No screens. No productivity. Review the year. Who did you harm? Who harmed you? Write both lists. For the first list: reach out where you can. For the second: practice letting go where you can. This isn't Jewish — it's human. Every culture has a day of reckoning. Make one.

THE LIVING AMENDS:
Some harms can't be fixed with words. The parent who was absent can't un-absent themselves. The living amends is: change your behavior going forward. Show up now. Not because it erases the past but because the present is the only repair site that's still open.

## Kids Version

Have you ever broken something that belonged to someone else? Maybe a toy. Maybe a promise.

Remember that tight feeling in your tummy? That feeling has a name: it's called 'I need to make it right.'

Making it right doesn't always mean fixing the broken thing. Sometimes you can't fix it. Sometimes 'making it right' means:
- Saying 'I'm sorry, I broke it'
- Asking 'what can I do?'
- Not doing it again

The hardest part isn't saying sorry. The hardest part is writing the list of all the things you need to say sorry FOR. But here's a secret: once you write the list, the tummy-feeling gets a little softer. Not gone. Softer.

And here's another secret: everyone has a list. Even your parents. Even your teachers. Everyone.

## Sci-Fi

THE AMENDS ENGINE (2096)

They built an AI that could calculate optimal amends. Feed it the harm, the relationship history, the cultural context — it would generate the perfect apology, the ideal gesture, the exact restitution amount.

It worked flawlessly. And it meant nothing.

Because amends aren't information. They're presence. The stammering father standing in his adult daughter's doorway saying 'I wasn't there and I should have been' — the AI could have scripted it better. But the script isn't the point. The shaking voice is the point. The courage to stand in the doorway is the point.

Bayo would say: the AI is a trickster mirror. It shows us that the thing we thought was about words (the amends) was always about bodies. Trembling bodies in doorways. Hands holding cups of tea they made because their mouth couldn't form the apology.

In Antarctica: the dome-dwellers had an Amends Department. All interpersonal conflicts were resolved by algorithm within 48 hours. Optimal outcomes. Zero residual resentment. Also zero transformation. Because amends without risk isn't amends. It's customer service.

## Cracks

AGNOSTOS THEOS:
- AA's 'higher power' language excludes atheists despite the 'as you understand Him' clause. The masculine pronoun. The assumption that surrender requires something to surrender TO.
- The amends framework assumes the harmed person wants to hear from you. Sometimes the kindest amends is staying away. 'Except when to do so would injure them' — but who decides what injures?
- 12-step programs have been critiqued for requiring identification as 'addict/alcoholic' forever. Is permanent identity-as-wound really recovery?
- The analogy 'modernity as addiction' is powerful but might let individuals off the hook. 'We're all addicted to capitalism' can become an excuse for personal inaction.
- Step 1 says 'we were powerless.' But historically, the people most harmed by addiction are also the ones most harmed by actual powerlessness — poverty, racism, colonialism. The powerlessness isn't metaphorical for everyone.
