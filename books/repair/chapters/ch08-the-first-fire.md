# The First Fire

*Major*

## Research

Between 50,000 and 10,000 years ago, as Homo sapiens spread across every continent, megafauna collapsed. In Australia: 85% of large animals gone within millennia of human arrival. In the Americas: mammoths, ground sloths, saber-toothed cats — 75% of large species gone. The mechanism: fire. Humans controlled fire and used it to reshape landscapes at scale. Fire-stick farming, drive hunting, habitat clearing.

This isn't controversial anymore. The Quaternary Extinction Event is the first mass extinction caused by a single species. Not modernity. Not capitalism. Not colonialism. Us. Homo sapiens. With fire.

The Tower card: the lightning strike that topples the structure. We've been the lightning since we could strike flint.

The uncomfortable truth: extraction isn't a modern invention. It's a human tendency. Agriculture intensified it. Civilization industrialized it. Capitalism financialized it. But the impulse — take more than you need, burn what's in the way — is as old as our species.

This doesn't excuse modernity. It deepens the problem. We're not dealing with a 500-year mistake. We're dealing with a 50,000-year pattern.

## Clinical

In trauma therapy, there's a concept called 'the original wound' — the earliest injury that set the pattern. Everything after is repetition.

If the First Fire is humanity's original wound — the moment we discovered we could destroy at scale and did — then every subsequent extinction is a trauma reenactment. The Tower falls and falls and falls.

But here's the dialectic (Linehan would approve): the same fire that burned the megafauna also cooked our food, warmed our children, gathered our communities. Fire IS the dialectic. Destruction AND creation. You can't have the campfire without the wildfire. You can't have cooking without burning.

Modernity's clinical error: believing you can have the campfire without the wildfire. Believing technology can give you the benefits of fire without the destruction. Every technological advance is a new fire — and every new fire burns something we didn't plan to lose.

AI is the newest fire. It will warm us AND burn us. The question isn't 'how do we prevent the burning?' It's 'what are we willing to lose?' And 'are we honest about what we're already losing?'

## Spiritual

Prometheus stole fire from the gods and gave it to humans. His punishment: an eagle eating his liver every day, forever. The myth says: fire is a gift AND a crime. You can't separate the warmth from the theft.

In Hindu cosmology, Agni is the god of fire — but also the god of the hearth, of sacrifice, of the bridge between human and divine. Fire is the mediator. The thing that transforms offering into spirit. Fire doesn't destroy — it transmutes.

Aboriginal Australian fire practice (cultural burning) is the counter-narrative: fire used not to extract but to tend. Gentle, cool burns that regenerate soil, reduce catastrophic fire risk, and maintain biodiversity. 65,000 years of fire as relationship, not weapon. The longest continuous culture on Earth is also the one that learned to burn kindly.

Prayer with fire: light a candle. Watch it. The flame consumes the wax to produce light. That's the deal. You can't have illumination without consumption. But you can choose what you burn. A candle or a continent. The prayer is: help me burn what I choose, not what I grab.



## Practices

FIRE PRACTICE (literal):
Light a fire — candle, campfire, hearth. Sit with it. Watch what it does. It eats what it's given. It doesn't hoard. It doesn't accumulate. It transforms and releases. Ask yourself: what am I burning? Am I burning what I chose, or am I burning everything I touch?

RECURSIVE.ECO PRACTICE:
Draw The Tower (XVI). When it appears in a reading, the usual reaction is fear. This time, ask: 'What needs to fall? What structure in my life is already crumbling? Can I let it fall instead of propping it up?'

I CHING: Hexagram 30 — 離 (The Clinging, Fire). Fire clings to what it burns. It needs fuel. The I Ching says: 'Care of the cow brings good fortune.' Tend the fire. Don't worship it.

WALKING PRACTICE:
Walk through a place that has burned — a forest after fire, an empty lot, a neighborhood that changed. Notice what's growing in the ash. The first green shoots after fire are called 'pioneers.' They're the bravest plants on earth.



## Rituals

THE BURNING RITUAL:
Write down something that needs to end — a habit, a belief, a relationship with a thing (not a person). Burn the paper. Watch it go. This is ancient. This is universal. Fire transforms what you release.

THE REMEMBRANCE:
Once a year, learn the name of one species that went extinct because of human action. Just one. Say its name out loud. The woolly mammoth. The dodo. The Tasmanian tiger. The golden toad. They were here. We burned their world. Saying their name is the smallest possible amends.

## Kids Version

A very very VERY long time ago — way before your great-great-great-great-great (keep going for a LONG time) grandparents — people discovered fire.

Fire was amazing! It kept them warm. It cooked their food. It scared away the scary animals at night. Fire was like a superpower.

But sometimes they used fire too much. They burned big forests to hunt animals. They burned so much that some animals couldn't find homes anymore, and they disappeared forever. The big woolly elephants (mammoths!), the giant sloths, the huge bears. Gone.

The people didn't mean to. They were just cold and hungry and fire was SO useful.

This is important: the same thing that helps can also hurt if we use too much of it. Like medicine. Like screen time. Like even love (have you ever been hugged TOO tight?).

The trick isn't 'never use fire.' The trick is: use it carefully. Use it together. And when you burn something by accident — say sorry. Plant something new.

## Sci-Fi

THE PROMETHEOME (2104)

They called the AI network 'Prometheome' — because it was humanity's latest fire. And like every fire before it, it warmed them while it burned.

Prometheome could simulate entire ecosystems, model climate interventions, design post-carbon infrastructure. It could also automate every remaining human job, consolidate power in fewer and fewer hands, and make decisions faster than democratic processes could govern.

A historian in the rewilding zones noted: 'We've done this before. Fire. Agriculture. Steam. Oil. Nuclear. Digital. AI. Each one a Prometheus moment — power stolen from the gods, or from nature, or from the future. Each one a Tower. Each one both gift and curse.'

Bayo: 'The trickster doesn't bring you fire to warm you. The trickster brings you fire to see what you'll do with it. The fire is a question, not an answer.'

The Antarctic dome was powered entirely by Prometheome. When asked 'what are we losing?', the AI responded: 'This question is outside my optimization parameters.' The junior analyst from Chapter 7 had retired by then. No one was left to ask the follow-up.

The communities outside the dome used AI too — but they treated it like fire. Gathered around it. Tended it together. Let it warm them. Watched it carefully. And when it started burning something they loved, they let it go out. Then they sat in the dark and sang until morning.

## Open Source

PUBLIC DOMAIN:
- Mary Shelley, 'Frankenstein; or, The Modern Prometheus' (1818): The subtitle says it all. Victor creates fire (life) and can't control it. The creature — like megafauna, like the planet — asks: 'Did I request thee, Maker, from my clay to mould me Man?'
- Ovid, 'Metamorphoses' — The Prometheus story (8 CE): Fire stolen from heaven. The liver eaten daily. The price of power.
- The Epic of Gilgamesh (~2100 BCE): The first recorded story of a human trying to transcend natural limits. Gilgamesh wants immortality. He doesn't get it. He goes home and looks at the walls of his city. The original Tower moment.

MUSEUM:
- Aboriginal Australian rock art in Kakadu (40,000+ years): The longest continuous artistic tradition on Earth. Created by the people who learned to tend fire rather than wield it.
- Pompeii casts: Bodies preserved in ash. The Tower made literal. The volcano didn't hate them. It was just fire.

## Cracks

AGNOSTOS THEOS:
- The megafauna extinction narrative can slip into 'humans are inherently destructive' nihilism. If we've always burned, what's the point of trying? The crack: maybe the point isn't to stop burning but to learn from 65,000 years of Aboriginal fire practice. We CAN burn kindly. Some of us already did.
- Blaming Homo sapiens as a species hides the specificity of which humans, in which systems, caused which damage. The Industrial Revolution was not all of humanity. It was specific empires, specific capitalists, specific decisions.
- The Prometheus myth is Greek. Telling this story through Greek myth centers Western cosmology even in a chapter about deep time. What about Maui's fire-theft in Polynesian tradition? Coyote's fire-theft in Indigenous American traditions? The Greek myth isn't THE myth. It's A myth.
- We said 'AI is the newest fire.' But fires are physical. They warm and burn actual bodies. AI is more like a mirror — it reflects what we put in. Calling it fire might inflate its importance. Or deflate fire's.
- Maybe the first fire wasn't the wound. Maybe the wound was forgetting. Aboriginal Australians used fire for 65,000 years without mass extinction. The wound isn't fire — it's the forgetting of how to tend it.
