# The Book of Changes

*Major*

## Research

In 213 BCE, Emperor Qin Shi Huang ordered the Burning of the Books — 焚書坑儒. Confucian texts, histories, poetry, philosophy: burned. Scholars who resisted: buried alive. The goal was total epistemic control. One emperor, one truth, one future.

The I Ching survived. Not because the Emperor valued it — because his court classified it as a divination manual. A fortune-telling tool. Useful for predicting harvests and military outcomes. The deepest cosmological text in Chinese civilization was spared because power mistook it for a gadget.

This pattern repeats. Indigenous knowledge systems worldwide survived colonization by being misclassified. Yoruba theology survived as 'folklore.' Aboriginal songlines survived as 'primitive myth.' Andean agricultural science survived as 'peasant tradition.' The colonizer's blindspot — the inability to recognize wisdom in unfamiliar packaging — became, paradoxically, the survival mechanism.

The I Ching's actual content is radical. 易 (yì) means 'change.' Not change as event — change as the fundamental nature of reality. The 64 hexagrams aren't predictions. They're a map of all possible states of transformation. Each hexagram contains its own opposite. Each line can change. Nothing is fixed. Nothing is permanent. The only constant is transformation itself.

## Clinical

The clinical argument for the I Ching follows directly from Chapter 11's case for tarot — but goes further. If tarot is a cognitive reframe (a card you didn't choose forces a perspective shift), the I Ching is a cognitive ECOSYSTEM. You don't get one card. You get a hexagram with six lines, each of which may be changing, transforming your hexagram into another. You get a situation AND its trajectory.

This mirrors what Gottman found about perpetual problems: the problem doesn't change, but the relationship to the problem can. Hexagram 29 (坎, The Abysmal) doesn't say 'you're in danger.' It says: 'You're in the pit. If you are sincere, you have success in your heart. Flowing water doesn't accumulate — it moves through.' That's radical acceptance. That's Linehan. The pit IS the practice.

Gottman's repair attempt is a micro-intervention: a silly face, a touch. The I Ching is a macro-intervention: an entire cosmological frame that says your problem is not a problem but a phase in an infinite cycle. Both work because they interrupt rumination. Both say: the stuckness is an illusion. Everything is already moving.

The therapeutic mechanism: when you throw coins and receive Hexagram 53 (漸, Gradual Progress), and your question was 'will my marriage survive?' — the hexagram doesn't answer yes or no. It says: things develop gradually, like a tree on a mountain, exposed to wind. The wild goose gradually approaches the shore. This is not prediction. This is REPERCEPTION. You were asking a binary question. The hexagram refuses binaries. Now you're seeing your marriage as a wild goose approaching a shore — slowly, with many stops. That shift in metaphor IS the repair.

Pennebaker's expressive writing research (1997) showed that narrative reframing of emotional experiences improves immune function. The I Ching is a narrative reframing engine. It takes your formless anxiety and gives it a shape — and the shape is always a process, never a verdict.

## Spiritual

The lineages behind the I Ching were not 'Chinese philosophy' — that's a colonial category. They were traditions of people who paid very close attention to nature. Who watched rivers and mountains and seasons and animals and saw in them a language. The eight trigrams (八卦) are: Heaven, Earth, Water, Fire, Mountain, Lake, Thunder, Wind. Not symbols OF nature. Nature itself, speaking.

☰ Qián (Heaven/Creative): three unbroken lines. Pure yang. Not masculine — generative. The sky doing what skies do.
☷ Kūn (Earth/Receptive): three broken lines. Pure yin. Not feminine — responsive. The earth receiving rain and making things grow.

The tradition says Fu Xi (伏羲) — mythical first emperor — observed the patterns on a dragon-horse rising from the Yellow River and derived the eight trigrams. Whether or not this is historical, the epistemology is clear: knowledge comes from watching the more-than-human. Not from thinking. From attending.

This is the same epistemology that Aboriginal Australians practice in songlines: the land teaches. The land IS the text. The I Ching is a songline made of broken and unbroken lines — a portable landscape you carry in your pocket.

When the Confucian texts burned in 213 BCE, the I Ching survived because it was too 'primitive' to threaten power. That's the trickster move. The deepest wisdom looks like the simplest game. Three coins. Heads or tails. Six throws. A pattern emerges. But what emerges isn't prediction — it's relationship. You are in conversation with 3,000 years of people who sat by rivers and watched the reeds bend.

In Bayo Akomolafe's language: the I Ching is a crack. It looks like a wall (a prediction system, a fortune-telling manual, an ancient superstition). But it's actually a fissure through which an entirely different way of knowing — relational, ecological, processual, non-dual — slips into modernity. Every time someone throws coins and reads a hexagram, they are briefly participating in an epistemology that predates and outlasts the one we call Western science. Not against science. Older than the split that made 'science' a separate thing.



## Practices

CONTEMPLATIVE ILLUSTRATION PRACTICE:
The I Ching has 64 hexagrams. Each one is a landscape, a weather system, a relationship, a season. Some people find peace coloring in a coloring book. This is the same practice, but alive.

Open recursive.eco. Navigate to the I Ching grammar. Draw a hexagram at random. Read its text. Then: illustrate it. You can:
- Use the AI assistant in recursive.eco to generate an illustration
- Draw it yourself on paper and photograph it
- Go outside and photograph something that FEELS like the hexagram
- Search public domain image archives (Smithsonian Open Access, Met Museum CC0, Biodiversity Heritage Library, NASA Image Gallery) for an image that speaks to the hexagram's meaning
- Upload someone else's illustration and add your own interpretation

This is not decoration. This is contemplation. When you search for an image that embodies Hexagram 57 (巽, The Gentle/Wind), you spend twenty minutes THINKING about what gentleness looks like. That's the practice. The hexagram reads you while you illustrate it.

DAILY COIN PRACTICE:
Three coins. Six throws. One hexagram. Don't ask a question. Just throw. Read the hexagram. Sit with it for five minutes. Write three sentences. Not interpretation — conversation. 'Good morning, Mountain over Water. What are you doing here? What is accumulating in me that hasn't moved yet?'

CHANGING LINES PRACTICE:
When you get changing lines, you get TWO hexagrams — the present and the becoming. Read both. The space between them is the liminal zone — the threshold where you actually live. Most of life is not 'here' or 'there.' It's in the changing lines. In the becoming.

NATURE PHOTOGRAPHY AS I CHING:
Go outside with your phone. Take 6 photos — any 6. Assign each to a line position (bottom to top). Unbroken feel = yang line. Broken/scattered feel = yin line. Build your hexagram from the walk. Look it up. This is not divination. This is attention. The I Ching is teaching you to see patterns, which is teaching you to see.

## Rituals

THE SEASONAL HEXAGRAM:
At each solstice and equinox, throw a hexagram. Don't ask a question — let the season ask. Write the hexagram on a card. Pin it where you'll see it for three months. At the next turning, review: what happened? What changed? The practice isn't prediction. It's PAYING ATTENTION to time.

THE ANNUAL RETURN:
On your birthday (or New Year, or whatever marks your personal turning), throw a hexagram. Compare it to last year's. You're not tracking your fortune. You're tracking your attention. What does it mean that last year you got 'The Taming Power of the Small' and this year you got 'Breakthrough'? Maybe nothing. Maybe everything. The practice is in the asking.

THE COUPLE'S HEXAGRAM (with Chapter 1):
With your partner, throw a hexagram together — one person throws, the other records. Read the hexagram as a description of your relationship RIGHT NOW. Not prediction. Description. 'We got Hexagram 37, The Family. Interesting. Wind over Fire — the eldest daughter's warmth kept alive by the eldest son's gentleness.' Read it to each other. Let it reframe the fight you had yesterday. This is Gottman's repair attempt in cosmological form.

THE GRIEF HEXAGRAM:
When someone dies, or something ends, throw a hexagram. Not to comfort yourself. To give the grief a shape. Grief without shape floods. The hexagram is a container — temporary, permeable, but shaped. Hexagram 23 (剝, Splitting Apart): 'The dark lines are about to mount upward and overthrow the last firm, light line.' Sometimes the hexagram confirms: yes, it's falling apart. That confirmation IS the container.

## Kids Version

Imagine you have three magic coins. You toss them six times and they make a picture — like a little tower of lines.

Some lines are solid: ———
Some lines are broken: — — —

Each picture has a name, like 'Thunder Over the Lake' or 'Wind Over the Mountain.' There are 64 pictures total. People have been tossing these coins for THREE THOUSAND YEARS. Your great-great-great-(lots more greats)-grandparents might have tossed them too.

The pictures don't tell the future. They tell you a STORY about right now. Like when you look at clouds and see a dragon — the dragon isn't really there, but seeing it makes you think about dragons, and maybe that's exactly what you needed to think about.

Here's the really cool part: some lines are CHANGING. A solid line might be turning into a broken line. So your picture is actually TWO pictures — what things are like NOW and what they're BECOMING. Like how a caterpillar is also a butterfly. Like how winter is also spring, it just hasn't arrived yet.

TRY THIS: Go outside. Take 6 photos of anything. Look at each photo. Does it feel MORE like a solid line (strong, bright, moving) or a broken line (soft, dark, still)? Stack them up. You just made your own hexagram from the world around you! Look it up in the I Ching and see what it says.

Some very mean people burned almost ALL the books a long, long time ago. But these coins survived because the mean people thought they were just a silly game. Sometimes the silliest things are the wisest. Like your favorite stuffed animal. Grown-ups think it's just a toy. You know it's your best friend.

## Sci-Fi

THE SURVIVAL PROTOCOL (date unknown, recovered from a civilization whose name has been composted)

When the Consolidation began, the scholars knew they had perhaps one generation before every text would be absorbed into the Unified Knowledge Base — searchable, optimized, and stripped of everything that couldn't be indexed.

They had a problem: their most important text wasn't a text. It was a practice. A set of relationships between broken and unbroken. Between stillness and movement. Between what is and what is becoming. You couldn't summarize it. You couldn't index it. You could only DO it.

So they disguised it as a prediction engine. They told the Consolidators: 'This tool forecasts agricultural yields and military outcomes. Very useful. Very practical.' The Consolidators were delighted. They added it to the Unified Base under 'Decision Support Tools.'

For 3,000 years, people used the 'Decision Support Tool.' Some actually used it for decisions. But a few — always a few — noticed something: the tool didn't predict anything. It CHANGED the person using it. Each consultation was a conversation with a pattern so old it had become geological. The tool wasn't a mirror. It was a landscape. And the landscape was alive.

When the Consolidation finally collapsed under the weight of its own optimization (as all consolidations do — this is Hexagram 23, Splitting Apart), the scholars' text was the only knowledge that survived intact. Not because it was strong. Because it was so simple it couldn't be broken. Three coins. Six throws. A pattern. A conversation.

The last Consolidator, reviewing the ruins, filed his final report: 'The Decision Support Tool appears to have been the actual operating system. Everything else was applications.'

Bayo's footnote, recovered from compost: 'Every empire mistakes the operating system for an app. That's how the operating system survives.'

## Open Source

THE I CHING IS THE ORIGINAL OPEN SOURCE PROJECT:
- 3,000+ years old
- No single author (attributed to Fu Xi, King Wen, the Duke of Zhou, Confucius — but really: anonymous, collective, emergent)
- Forked countless times: the Mawangdui Silk Manuscript (168 BCE) has a different hexagram sequence than the received text. The Guicang (歸藏) started with Hexagram 2 (Earth) instead of Hexagram 1 (Heaven) — a fork that centered the receptive over the creative
- Richard Wilhelm's 1924 German translation (with Cary Baynes' 1950 English translation) is one fork. John Blofeld's is another. Thomas Cleary's is another. Each translator is a contributor.
- Leibniz (1703) forked it into binary mathematics. That fork became computers. You are reading this on a descendant of the I Ching.

PUBLIC DOMAIN ILLUSTRATION SOURCES FOR YOUR PRACTICE:
- Smithsonian Open Access: 4.4M+ images — search 'Chinese landscape,' 'mountain and water painting,' 'seasons'
- Metropolitan Museum CC0: 490K+ artworks — extraordinary Chinese art collection

- Library of Congress: maps, manuscripts — historical I Ching editions

THE RECURSIVE PRACTICE REPOSITORY:

## Cracks

AGNOSTOS THEOS:

THE APPROPRIATION CRACK: We're writing about the I Ching in English, in a JSON file, in a repository built on infrastructure (Git, GitHub, cloud computing) that is itself a product of the extraction economy. The I Ching survived the Burning of the Books only to be New-Aged, self-helped, and turned into an app. Every time we frame it as 'useful for therapy' or 'like a coloring book for contemplation,' we're doing what the Consolidators did — making it palatable by making it practical. The trickster survival mechanism (looking like a gadget so power leaves you alone) becomes a trap when the gadget framing sticks.

THE TRANSLATION CRACK: We don't read Classical Chinese. The I Ching we know is Richard Wilhelm's I Ching, filtered through his friendship with Lao Naixuan, filtered through Cary Baynes' English, filtered through Jung's foreword that reframed the whole text as synchronicity rather than cosmology. We're reading a translation of a translation of an interpretation of an oral tradition. The original is not available. It may never have been a single text.

THE BINARY CRACK: The I Ching uses yin and yang — broken and unbroken lines. We mapped this to binary (0 and 1). Leibniz saw it. But yin-yang is NOT binary. Binary is either/or. Yin-yang is both/and — each contains the seed of the other (the dots in the tàijítú). When we celebrate 'Leibniz discovered binary in the I Ching,' we're celebrating a misreading that was extraordinarily productive (computers!) but that also LOST the non-dual epistemology. The I Ching in its own tradition says: yin becomes yang becomes yin. In binary: 0 is not 1 and never will be. The fork that became computers left the cosmology behind.

THE ILLUSTRATION CRACK: We proposed illustrating the I Ching as contemplative practice. But illustration is inherently a fixing — you take something fluid (a hexagram that means something different to each person in each moment) and pin it to a single image. The Mountain over Water in YOUR life today is not the Mountain over Water in mine. The moment you illustrate it, you've stopped it from changing. And the I Ching IS change. Can you illustrate change without stopping it? Maybe that's why the original text uses no images — only lines. Broken and unbroken. The minimum viable illustration. Everything else is commentary.

THE COLONIAL SURVIVAL CRACK: We told the story of the I Ching surviving because colonizers thought it was fortune-telling. This frames survival as clever. But the people whose philosophical traditions were burned — they weren't being clever. They were being killed. The Burning of the Books included the live burial of 460 scholars. Survival by misclassification is not a strategy. It's a wound that happened to leave a scar in the shape of a gift.
