# What Survives the Turning

*Major*

## Research

In evolutionary biology, fitness isn't strength — it's fit. The organism that survives isn't the strongest or smartest but the one most adapted to its environment. Darwin never said 'survival of the fittest' in the way we use it. He meant: survival of the most fitting. The most relational. The most responsive.

Elinor Ostrom (Nobel Prize 2009) studied communities that managed shared resources for centuries without tragedy of the commons. What survived: clear boundaries, collective decision-making, graduated sanctions, conflict-resolution mechanisms. In other words: repair infrastructure. The communities that lasted had built-in repair.

The ones that collapsed? They'd optimized for extraction. Maximum yield. No investment in the capacity to return, restore, replenish. They grew fast and died fast.

Debt — in David Graeber's anthropological sense — is the original social bond. 'I owe you' is a way of saying 'we're connected.' Adaptive debt says: I received, and I will give back. Maladaptive debt says: I took, and you owe me. The first builds community. The second builds empire.

Ostrom, E. (1990). Governing the Commons. Cambridge University Press.
Graeber, D. (2011). Debt: The First 5,000 Years. Melville House.

## Clinical

In family systems therapy, an adaptive family can handle stress, conflict, and change because it has repair mechanisms: they can fight and reconnect, they can grieve and support, they can make mistakes and say sorry.

A maladaptive family looks functional but runs on rigidity or chaos: either everyone conforms (enmeshment) or no one connects (disengagement). Both are extraction: enmeshment extracts individuality, disengagement extracts connection.

Minuchin's structural family therapy maps onto civilizations: healthy systems have clear but permeable boundaries. Sick systems have either no boundaries (colonialism: everything is mine) or walls (nationalism: nothing gets in).

Addiction, revisited: addiction is maladaptive because it extracts from the future self to pay the present self. Every hit borrows energy, health, relationships — with compound interest. Civilization-as-addiction does the same: every quarter of GDP growth borrows from future generations, future ecosystems, future stability.

The clinical question: can a system acknowledge its maladaptive patterns without collapsing? In family therapy, sometimes yes. The family can restructure. Sometimes no — and the healthiest response is for members to differentiate and form new systems. Maybe that applies to civilizations too.

## Spiritual

The Wheel of Fortune in tarot: everything rises and falls. The sphinx at the top, the snake descending, the dog-headed figure ascending. No one stays on top. The only constant is the turning.

Every agricultural tradition knows this: the field must lie fallow. The soil must rest. Extraction without rest is death. Sabbath — the original anti-productivity practice. God rested on the seventh day, not because God was tired, but because rest is part of creation.

Indigenous time: many First Nations conceptualize time as cyclical, not linear. The Anishinaabe Seven Generations principle: every decision considers its impact seven generations forward. That's 175 years of future. Modern corporations plan in quarters — 90 days. The ratio of indigenous to corporate time horizon: 700:1.

Prayer as planetary relationship: the ancients prayed to Jupiter (expansion, luck) and Saturn (restriction, time). The Wheel of Fortune is Jupiter's card. What if luck isn't random but relational? What if 'fortune' is what happens when you're in right relationship with the cycles — planting when it's time to plant, resting when it's time to rest, letting go when it's time to let go?



## Practices

THE DEBT INVENTORY:
List three debts you carry — not financial (or not only). Relational, ecological, ancestral. Who do you owe? What did you inherit that you didn't earn? What are you extracting from the future to pay for today?

RECURSIVE.ECO PRACTICE:
Spin the Wheel of Fortune (X) from the Rider-Waite-Smith deck. Ask: 'What's rising in my life right now? What's falling? Can I let both happen?'

I CHING: Hexagram 24 — 復 (Return). Thunder in the earth. The turning point. After the darkest point, light returns — not because you earned it, but because that's what cycles do. The I Ching says: 'Return. Success. Going out and coming in without error.'

SEASONAL PRACTICE:
Live one month by the moon cycle. Plant something on the new moon. Tend it through the waxing. Harvest or celebrate at the full moon. Let go during the waning. Rest on the dark moon. This isn't metaphor. This is how most humans lived for most of history.

## Rituals

THE FALLOW RITUAL:
Choose one area of your life and let it lie fallow for one season. Stop producing in that area. Stop optimizing. Stop improving. Let the field rest. See what grows on its own.

THE SEVEN GENERATIONS LETTER:
Write a letter to your descendant seven generations from now (circa 2201). What do you want them to know about why you lived the way you lived? What do you wish you could change for them? This isn't fantasy — it's accountability stretched to its natural scale.

## Kids Version

Look at a tree in winter. It looks dead, right? No leaves. No flowers. Just bare branches.

But it's not dead. It's resting. Under the ground, the roots are drinking slowly. Inside the branches, tiny buds are forming. The tree knows something we forget: you can't grow all the time.

Some things last a really long time — like forests and coral reefs and grandmas' recipes. You know what they all have in common? They take turns. They share. They rest. They give back.

Some things look REALLY big and strong — like a company that makes ALL the toys or a person who wants ALL the cookies. But you know what? Things that take everything and share nothing always run out. Always.

The tree knows: a little bit, then rest. A little bit, then share. A little bit, then rest. That's how you last.

## Sci-Fi

THE TURNING ARCHIVE (2142)

Historians in the rewilding zones catalogued civilizations by their Turning Score: the ratio of repair infrastructure to extraction infrastructure. Roads vs. councils. Pipelines vs. festivals. Prisons vs. circles.

Every civilization with a Turning Score below 0.3 (heavy extraction, minimal repair) collapsed within 400 years. Every one. Sumeria, Rome, the British Empire, American hegemony. The pattern was so consistent it became a law: Ostrom's Limit.

Civilizations above 0.6 (high repair, lower extraction) lasted thousands of years but produced fewer artifacts. They didn't build pyramids. They sang songs. They told stories. They tended gardens. The archaeological record was thin because they didn't externalize their culture into objects — they lived it in bodies.

The AI historians noted the irony: the civilizations modern humans most admired were the ones closest to collapse. The ones that lasted were invisible to extraction-based evaluation.

In Antarctica: Turning Score 0.01. The dome was a miracle of extraction technology. Zero repair capacity. When the first seal in the atmospheric system cracked, they couldn't fix it — not because the technology failed, but because nobody remembered how to ask for help.

PUBLIC DOMAIN:

- Peter Kropotkin, 'Mutual Aid: A Factor of Evolution' (1902): The counter-narrative to social Darwinism. Cooperation, not competition, drives evolution.
- Henry David Thoreau, 'Walden' (1854): 'The mass of men lead lives of quiet desperation.' An early diagnosis of extraction culture.

MUSEUM:
- Göbekli Tepe (Turkey, 9600 BCE): Monumental architecture built by hunter-gatherers. Before agriculture, before cities — people gathered to build something sacred together. Cooperation preceded civilization.
- Potlatch regalia of Pacific Northwest nations: ceremony of giving away wealth. The opposite of accumulation. Status through generosity.

## Cracks

AGNOSTOS THEOS:
- The 'indigenous time vs. corporate time' comparison romanticizes indigenous societies as uniformly wise. Many indigenous cultures practiced warfare, slavery, and ecological destruction too. The Noble Savage is a colonial myth — and using indigenous wisdom as decoration for Western arguments is its own extraction.
- Ostrom's commons research is robust but her cases are mostly small-scale. Can commons governance work at the scale of a nation? A planet? We don't know.
- 'Debt as social bond' is Graeber's thesis, and it's compelling, but it also makes debt sound nicer than it is for the person being crushed by it right now.
- The fallow metaphor assumes you can afford to rest. Minimum-wage workers, single parents, people in survival mode — they can't 'let the field lie fallow.' The luxury of rest is unevenly distributed.
- Maybe the Wheel of Fortune isn't comforting. Maybe 'everything rises and falls' is terrifying if you're at the bottom and can't wait for the turn.
