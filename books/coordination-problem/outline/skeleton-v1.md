# The Coordination Problem — Skeleton v1

## Working Title
*The Coordination We Cannot Find* — why the argument that could save us does not land in the nervous systems of the people who most need to hear it

## Premise (one paragraph)
Frontier AI may be a civilizational risk. A small number of serious people have said so, with arguments that the serious people at the labs do not refute and mostly concede. And yet the labs keep racing. This book is not another argument about whether the risk is real. It is an anthropology of why the argument does not land, written by someone inside the ecosystem rather than outside it.

## Posture
- Short. Yudkowsky-hostile-to-padding.
- No metaphysics, no contemplative practice as consolation, no "the field," no tantric scaffolding.
- Linehan filter stays (useful / fits the data / compassionate) but does not get named as a practice.
- Author concedes their own collusion openly — builds on Claude, sits inside the ecosystem, is not a neutral observer.
- Ends without a solution. Claiming a solution would be part of the disease.

## Voice
- Equity-analyst register. Cite the numbers, name the companies, tell the stories.
- First person when the collusion is the subject; third person for everything else.
- No judgment adjectives from narrator. Observers observe, do not evaluate.

## Structure (10 chapters, target ~40-45K words)

### Part I — The Premise

**Ch 1. Take the Argument Seriously**
The people who built this technology and the people who most carefully study it are, in non-trivial numbers, saying it might kill everyone. This is not an extreme position held by outsiders. It is a mainstream position held by insiders. Hinton left Google to say it. Bengio reorganized his life to say it. Russell, Tegmark, Yudkowsky, Soares, Christiano have all said it in different registers. The lab leaders — Amodei, Hassabis, Altman — have all, at some point, said 10–25% chance of catastrophic outcome. That number, in any other context, would halt the activity. It has not halted the activity. This book asks why.

**Ch 2. What Yudkowsky Is Actually Saying**
Strip the rhetoric. The claim has three parts:
1. We do not know how to build systems more capable than us that reliably do what we want.
2. Current alignment techniques (RLHF, constitutional AI, interpretability) train surface behavior, not underlying values; they do not scale.
3. If we build such a system before the science exists, the system will optimize for something other than what we wanted, and we will not be able to correct it.
The weak points in the argument. The strong points. Where the labs concede. Where they dissent.

**Ch 3. Why This Book Is Not an Argument**
The argument has been made. Repeatedly. At book length. The argument is not failing because it lacks clarity. It is failing for reasons that have nothing to do with its logical structure. This book attends to those reasons.

### Part II — The Precedents

**Ch 4. Oppenheimer and What the Physicists Learned Too Late**
The Manhattan Project as the cleanest prior case. Physicists raced, in a geopolitical frame that felt genuinely binary (Nazi bomb or Allied bomb), and built the weapon. Then watched it used on civilians. Then spent the rest of their lives trying to control it. Oppenheimer's "I am become death." Szilárd's petition. The Franck Report. The Bulletin's Doomsday Clock. The pattern: during the race, the people closest to the technology cannot see the cost; after the race, they cannot unsee it. The AI labs are currently in the first half of that pattern.

**Ch 5. The Treaties That Worked**
- **Biological Weapons Convention (1972).** State parties forbidden to develop, produce, stockpile biological agents. Still holding, imperfectly, 50+ years later. No major confirmed state program since Soviet collapse (modulo the usual caveats).
- **Montreal Protocol (1987).** CFCs phased out globally. The ozone layer is recovering. Often cited as the most successful environmental treaty in history.
- **Partial Test Ban Treaty (1963).** No atmospheric, underwater, or outer-space nuclear tests. Signed during the height of the Cold War, after Cuba. The two most hostile nuclear powers agreed.
- **Chemical Weapons Convention (1993).** 98% of declared chemical weapon stockpiles destroyed.
- **Nuclear Non-Proliferation Treaty (1968).** Has leaked (Pakistan, North Korea, Israel ambiguous), but has held the vast majority of capable states.
The pattern: coordination is hard but not unprecedented. The question is not "can humans coordinate on civilizational risk" — we have, repeatedly. The question is under what conditions they do.

**Ch 6. What Was Different Then**
Why did those treaties work and why does AI coordination look impossible? Five differences:
1. **Verifiability.** You can count warheads. You cannot count capabilities inside a training run. (Counter: compute is countable; fabs are few.)
2. **Visible harm.** Hiroshima, Nagasaki, the ozone hole were visible. AI risk is prospective and contested.
3. **Small number of actors.** Two superpowers, then a handful. AI: five major labs + open source + sovereign programs + any well-funded lab.
4. **Defensive symmetry.** Nukes benefited no one if everyone had them. AI benefits whoever has the most capable one.
5. **Existential framing accepted.** Everyone agreed nukes could end civilization. The AI-extinction framing is disputed by half the field.
Honest conclusion: AI is harder than any of the precedents. But "harder than nukes" is not "impossible."

### Part III — The Failure

**Ch 7. The Nervous System of the Builder**
(First person. The chapter the book turns on.)
I build on top of Claude. I use it to write this book. I pay Anthropic. I am, in a functional sense, part of the thing I am describing. Every time I ship a feature on recursive.eco, I route a few more dollars to the race. Every user I onboard is additional demand that justifies the next training run. I know this. I do it anyway. I do not have a clean account of why.

What I can describe: the phenomenology. The way a problem that should be terrifying becomes, in practice, a low hum. The way the tools are beautiful to use. The way the community is generous and intelligent. The way Dario's position becomes internally persuasive the more you spend time with the people making it. The way my own agency feels so small that participation seems morally irrelevant. The way I can hold the Yudkowsky argument in mind and still close the laptop and go to bed.

This chapter describes the machinery that dissolves the argument inside a human nervous system. Not to excuse it. To see it.

**Ch 8. The Stories Labs Tell Themselves**
Field research from public statements, podcasts, Senate testimony, employee interviews, and the internal narratives visible from outside. The recurring stories:
- *"If not us, someone worse."* (Race-to-the-top.) Structurally identical to every arms race participant's self-justification.
- *"We are more careful than we appear."* (Internal safety teams, RSPs, evals.) True and insufficient.
- *"Alignment will be solved by the capable systems themselves."* (Automated alignment research.) Unfalsifiable optimism. Also, possibly, the only path.
- *"The upside is too large to forgo."* (Cancer cures, climate models, post-scarcity.) The utilitarian calculus that lets the downside be a footnote.
- *"Geopolitical inevitability."* The frame that converts choice into compulsion.
- *"Safety through deployment."* If we keep putting it in public, we learn what breaks. Also: if we keep putting it in public, the public normalizes it.
Each story is partly true. Each story, held with enough conviction, becomes the mechanism of capture.

**Ch 9. Why the Argument Does Not Land**
Yudkowsky's argument does not fail because it is wrong. It fails because it requires the listener to hold three things at once that human nervous systems are not built to hold:
1. A prospective risk (no visible bodies yet).
2. At civilizational scale (too large to feel).
3. With no individual exit (you cannot opt out by leaving the country, changing jobs, or refusing to use the product).
Every human coping mechanism evolved for a different kind of threat. Predators, scarcity, rivals, tribal conflict. *These* the nervous system handles. The nervous system does not handle "the thing you use every day might kill everyone in fifteen years." It defaults to pattern-match: previous doom predictions were wrong, therefore this one is wrong. It defaults to in-group trust: the smart people I know are building it, therefore it is okay. It defaults to agency-scale: I cannot stop it, therefore it is not my problem.
This is not cowardice. This is the shape of the organism. The argument would have to be delivered in a form the organism can receive. It has not been, because the people who can make the argument are temperamentally bad at delivering things in forms other organisms can receive.

### Part IV — The Ground That Remains

**Ch 10. Without Consolation**
No solution chapter. No practice. No platform pitch.
What is left, if the argument is correct and the coordination is not coming? Not much. A few small things:
- Telling the truth about the failure to the people closest to you.
- Not pretending the tools you use are neutral.
- Paying attention to who is made richer by your participation.
- Refusing the two bad resolutions: accelerationist celebration and apocalyptic despair.
- Acting as if coordination were possible, on the thin chance it is, because acting as if it is impossible makes it impossible.
That is the whole list. The chapter is two thousand words at most. The book ends there.

## What this book deliberately is not
- Not a Yudkowsky apologia. The author is not persuaded of >90% doom.
- Not a doom book. The author does not have a probability.
- Not a memoir. The author appears only in Ch 7 and in passing.
- Not a manifesto. There is no call to action strong enough to matter.
- Not a philosophy book. No tantra, no Tillich, no axiom-beneath-the-ground.
- Not a hope book. Hope is not the subject.
- Not a technical book. No alignment math. No architecture diagrams.

## Target reader
Someone who has read *Superintelligence* or *Human Compatible* or *If Anyone Builds It*, taken the argument seriously, and then watched themselves go back to using ChatGPT the next day. This book is for them. It is for the person who cannot figure out why the thing that should be the top priority has become the background hum.

## Honest risks
- The AI safety community will find it insufficiently technical.
- The accelerationist community will find it insufficiently defiant.
- The contemplative community (the author's existing readership) will find it cold.
- Yudkowsky himself may find it weak because it does not commit to his probability.
- It may be right anyway.
