# Govern the Equity, Not the Cash
*Dwarkesh Prize Q3 — v4 with IPO research integrated (Novo Nordisk specifics, Hershey precedent)*
*~1,030 words*

---

The OpenAI Foundation does not have $180 billion to deploy. It has a 26 percent stake in OpenAI Group PBC plus a 15-year warrant for additional shares. Liquidating the stake depresses the valuation it depends on. Waiting for dividends produces nothing — Microsoft's first-quarter FY2026 10-Q recognized $4.1 billion in equity-method losses from OpenAI in a single quarter; OpenAI is loss-making, has never paid a dividend, and funds its compute commitments through primary equity raises and debt-financed multi-year cloud contracts. The Q4 2026 IPO target turns this paper into priced equity but does not solve the lever-preservation problem — re-papering Class N through a Novo Holdings–style intermediate that holds unlisted supervoting shares is the established template; selling at IPO to diversify triggers the Hershey-2002 fact pattern, in which charitable holders attempting to sell controlling stakes face *parens patriae* AG intervention. Most plausible "deploy the money" answers founder on these facts before they engage what to deploy *for*.

The Foundation's actual lever is the equity itself. Through the Class N Common Stock issued at the October 2025 recapitalization, the Foundation has prior-written-approval rights over six categories of corporate action — any amendment to the public-benefit mission, any sale or material asset transfer, any issuance of new voting capital, any disproportionate change to the PBC's charter or bylaws — as enumerated in Paragraph 7 of OpenAI's Memorandum of Understanding with the California Attorney General. Through Paragraph 2 of the same MOU, the Foundation has the *sole power to appoint and remove* every director of OpenAI Group PBC. These are not abstract entitlements. They are operational tools — currently held by an institution whose 7-of-8 board overlap with the for-profit (8-of-9 at recap closing; reduced after Larry Summers' November 2025 resignation, which has not been refilled five months later) means the same humans hold and exercise both sides.

If I ran the Foundation, I would not spend a dollar deploying capital to external causes first. I would use the trial that began jury selection today as the forcing function for making the equity into a binding constraint on the company it owns.

**One. Make the Attorneys General's already-binding commitments operational.** The California AG MOU and the Delaware AG Statement of No Objection have already named the binding commitments. Paragraph 14 of the MOU requires a second NFP-only director within one year of recap — by October 2026. The DE Statement conditions non-objection on Safety & Security Committee authority to halt model releases "even where applicable risk thresholds would otherwise permit release." Six months past closing, neither commitment is visibly operational. The Foundation's first job is not to invent new structure. It is to make the structure that has already been promised in writing functional before the deadlines name the gap publicly.

**Two. Separate the boards.** No director on the Foundation should also sit on the OpenAI Group PBC board. The current overlap is the structural feature that makes the Foundation's stated governance role inoperative; the same humans cannot meaningfully discipline themselves. Re-seat the Foundation board within twelve months with directors whose only fiduciary duty is the mission. Bret Taylor, Foundation chair, cannot also chair the operating company.

**Three. Operationalize the Class N six-trigger framework.** Define a public, binding list of mission-relevant decisions that trigger Foundation review under each of the six §7 categories. Each Foundation review produces a public report. The §7 prior-approval rights become a discipline rather than a paper right.

**Four. Test the SSC release-halt authority as a live tool, not a contingent one.** The Pentagon contract OpenAI signed February 28, 2026 — followed by Robotics/Hardware lead Caitlin Kalinowski's March 7, 2026 resignation citing "surveillance of Americans without judicial oversight and lethal autonomy without human authorization" — was a moment when the SSC's MOU-granted release-halt authority could have been visible. It was not. The Foundation should establish that the authority is real by exercising it on the next equivalent decision and documenting the exercise publicly.

**Five. Define mission-drift triggers in advance.** Anthropic's Responsible Scaling Policy v3.0 removed v2.2's binding pause commitment. Jared Kaplan attributed the change to a "collective action problem." The Foundation defines mission-drift thresholds in writing, publicly, with binding consequences before post-hoc rationalization becomes available.

None of these moves require institutional invention. The Novo Nordisk Foundation owns 100 percent of Novo Holdings A/S, which holds unlisted A-shares (100 votes each) and Nasdaq-Copenhagen-listed B-shares (10 votes each) — 28 percent of capital, 77 percent of votes — with Foundation Articles permanently prohibiting divestment of A-shares "for as long as the Foundation exists." This is the live precedent for foundation control surviving public-company status at frontier scale, with the Danish Commercial Foundations Act, the Foundation Articles approved by the Danish Foundation Authority, and the Novo Nordisk Articles forming a triple-lock the OpenAI Foundation could replicate via a Delaware non-stock holding entity. The Carlsberg Foundation runs the same architecture with both share classes listed. The Tata Trusts have controlled the Tata operating companies through a separated-board firewall for more than a century — the Indian Supreme Court upheld the structure against shareholder oppression in *Tata Consultancy Services v. Cyrus Investments* in March 2021. The templates exist. The Foundation has chosen not to use them.

The same structural fact applies to the Anthropic cofounders' January 26, 2026 pledge of 80 percent of wealth — pledged equity in a non-dividending lab is pledged paper, not pledged capital, until the governance question that converts paper to leverage is solved.

The strongest counter is that the Microsoft–OpenAI definitive agreement of October 28, 2025 substantially diluted the Foundation's post-AGI lever — Microsoft now holds IP rights through 2032 (including post-AGI models), and the unilateral nonprofit-board AGI declaration was replaced by an undisclosed third-party expert panel. This is correct. The remaining lever is therefore pre-AGI operating discipline: the SSC halt authority, the appointment power, the §7 prior-approval rights. Use it.

The catalyst is the trial itself. Over the four weeks of liability phase in *Musk v. OpenAI*, internal Foundation governance records will enter the public domain — board minutes from the recapitalization planning, voting records on specific decisions, the §7 framework's drafting history, the Pentagon contract review or its absence. The hypothetical Foundation chair's best move is not to wait for the May verdict. It is to act visibly during liability phase so that whatever the verdict produces, the Foundation has already moved. Fill the Summers seat with an independent NFP-only director this month. Publish the retrospective §7 voting record before discovery does it for them. Direct the SSC to produce a public post-mortem on the Pentagon contract review. Senator Warren's January 28, 2026 letter to Sam Altman — demanding disclosure of "circumstances under which OpenAI would seek a taxpayer bailout" — is the political-economy frame; the Q4 2026 IPO road show will require these governance commitments be made publicly visible regardless. The window is open between now and remedies phase. The court is more likely to defer to a Foundation visibly operating its lever than to one that has been dormant. The Foundation has the lever it has. Use it now.
