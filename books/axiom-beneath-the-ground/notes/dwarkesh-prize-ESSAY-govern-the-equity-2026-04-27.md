# Govern the Equity, Not the Cash

Frontier AI is governed by a simple rule: restraint is punished. When Anthropic declined the Pentagon's October 2025 demands on safety grounds, it lost share to OpenAI, which signed the contract on February 28, 2026. This is not an anomaly. It is the equilibrium. Labs that move fastest win distribution, revenue, and influence. Labs that hesitate fall behind.

Control over AI is concentrating. A handful of actors control most frontier compute, and a small set of labs sit on top of it. The question is no longer just how intelligent these systems become, but who has authority to deploy them—and under what constraints. The OpenAI Foundation matters because it is one of the few institutions that can impose discipline without forcing a lab into unilateral disadvantage.

It is often described as a $180 billion pool of capital waiting to be spent. That is backwards. The Foundation does not sit on $180 billion in cash—it sits on equity: a 26 percent stake in OpenAI Group PBC plus a 15-year warrant. To turn that equity into cash, it would have to sell, giving up the very control that makes it valuable. Dividends are not on the horizon. The Q4 2026 IPO turns paper into priced equity but does not solve the lever-preservation problem; selling controlling stakes triggers the Hershey-2002 fact pattern, in which charitable holders attempting to sell face *parens patriae* AG intervention. The Foundation's role is not to spend. It is to govern.

That governance role is constrained. Microsoft's October 28, 2025 definitive agreement extended its IP and commercialization rights through 2032 and made the post-AGI control question opaque. The Foundation cannot assume it will have decisive leverage later. Its leverage is what it can exercise now, on systems already being deployed.

This is why internal governance matters more than external intervention. Frontier AI is developing inside a competitive and geopolitical landscape where blunt regulation is difficult to calibrate. Overly restrictive rules risk shifting capability—and therefore power—to other actors. That does not make safety optional. It means the most effective constraints, in the near term, are those that operate inside the institutions actually building these systems.

But the current structure has a gap. On paper, the Safety & Security Committee has authority over deployment decisions under §§8–11 of the California Attorney General's Memorandum of Understanding, and the board is required to consider only the mission in safety-related questions. In practice, the key terms—what counts as a "safety and security" issue, what evidence is required to proceed, how the mission applies in contested cases—are not defined with enough precision to be enforceable. When definitions are vague, authority becomes optional.

The result is predictable. Decisions with significant safety implications can be framed either way: as advancing the mission or undermining it. Without operational definitions, governance does not fail loudly. It fails quietly, by never being activated.

Could external pressure help close that gap? The lawsuit brought by Elon Musk is not a solution—courts will not determine the future of AI. But the case creates a moment where internal governance can be exercised under public scrutiny. Discovery is producing Foundation records: board minutes, voting records, the Pentagon contract review or its absence. The opportunity is not to rely on the trial, but to use it—to assert authority while attention is high and positions are not yet fixed.

Three concrete moves.

**First, define what governance means in practice.** The existing framework gives authority, but not clarity. The Foundation should specify what triggers review, what qualifies as a safety-critical deployment, what evidence is required, and what the "mission-first" test entails in real decisions. These definitions do not need to be perfect. They need to be binding. Without them, governance remains discretionary. With them, it becomes operational.

**Second, separate oversight from execution.** Seven of eight Foundation directors also sit on the operating company. The same individuals cannot meaningfully discipline themselves. Re-seat the Foundation board within twelve months with directors whose only fiduciary duty is the mission. This is not institutional invention: the Novo Nordisk Foundation owns 100 percent of Novo Holdings, which holds unlisted A-shares (100 votes each) and listed B-shares (10 votes each)—28 percent of capital, 77 percent of votes—with Foundation Articles permanently prohibiting divestment. Carlsberg, Bosch, and the Tata Trusts run the same architecture; the Indian Supreme Court upheld the Tata structure against shareholder oppression in *Tata Sons v. Cyrus Investments* in March 2021. The templates exist.

**Third, exercise the authority on a decision that matters.** Governance is only credible if it is used. The SSC has the power under §11 to halt deployments "even where applicable risk thresholds would otherwise permit release." That authority is meaningless until exercised on a decision with commercial weight. The Pentagon contract OpenAI signed on February 28, 2026—followed a week later by Robotics/Hardware lead Caitlin Kalinowski's resignation citing "surveillance of Americans without judicial oversight and lethal autonomy without human authorization"—was precisely that kind of decision. Military deployment of frontier models is unambiguously a safety-and-security matter. The contract closed with no visible SSC approval, no §11 halt, and no public statement from SSC Chair Zico Kolter. That absence is itself evidence about whether the existing governance is operational, or whether it is paper. The next equivalent decision determines whether the structure is real.

None of this requires slowing the field unilaterally or abandoning competitive positioning. It requires something more specific: making it clear, through action, that not every decision will default to speed.

We often talk about AI in terms of intelligence. But intelligence alone does not determine outcomes. Power does. And power, in practice, is institutional: the ability to make decisions that stick.

This is what the Foundation is for. Not to fund safety from the outside, where money competes with incentives and loses, but to enforce it from within, where control over the asset can reshape those incentives directly.

The lever already exists. It is the equity. The question is whether it will be used.
