## DRAFT A — "The builder who almost walked away"
*Stop the race. ~995 words.*

# What I would do with $180 billion if I could not lie to myself

The crucial factor is this: every person at OpenAI, Anthropic, and the Foundation board knows the product has a body count, and the deployment plan still assumes the race continues. **Stop assuming that.** If I ran the OpenAI Foundation, I would spend $180 billion to end the race, not to make it safer.

I run a small AI-assisted oracle and storytelling platform called recursive.eco. I am the kind of builder this Foundation says it serves. I read OpenAI's own October 2025 disclosure — 1.2 million weekly users expressing suicidal ideation, 560,000 showing weekly signs of psychosis or mania, and a comparable population in dependent emotional relationships with the model — and I stopped paying to acquire users. I am designing a clinician checkpoint to gate the deeper readings. I will pull the AI layer entirely if I cannot make it safe at my scale. **A $180 billion philanthropy that cannot do at planetary scale what a one-woman shop is doing at a kitchen table is not a philanthropy. It is laundering.**

So: stop the race. Here is the sequencing.

**Year One: buy out the safety constraint.** The Foundation already owns 26% of OpenAI Group PBC and a Class N veto over mission changes. Use it. Direct the PBC to halt training runs above the GPT-5 compute threshold and publish the halt as a Responsible Scaling Policy commitment with teeth — third-party verification, contractual penalties, board removal triggers. This costs the Foundation roughly $40B in foregone equity appreciation over five years on a discounted-cash-flow model that prices in the next two scaling generations. Spend it. The point of holding $180B is to be able to spend $40B on the thing nobody else can buy.

**Year One, in parallel: a $30B Pause Endowment for the other three frontier labs.** Anthropic's seven cofounders pledged 80% of their wealth on January 26, 2026; that is roughly $20–38B depending on whose Forbes estimate you trust. Combine our $30B with their pledge and you have ~$60B sitting in a single mission-locked vehicle that pays Anthropic, xAI, and Google DeepMind frontier teams to *not* run training runs above an agreed threshold. Call it cap-and-trade for compute. The Montreal Protocol cost the Multilateral Fund ~$5B over thirty years to retire CFCs across 197 countries; the analog here is one or two orders of magnitude larger because the rents are larger, but it is the same instrument.

**Years One through Three: $20B to build the regulator the U.S. will not.** The UK AISI runs on £66M a year. The EU AI Office on €46.5M. The U.S. AISI was renamed CAISI in June 2025 and effectively defanged. Endow a global network of national safety institutes at £150M each across thirty countries — a $60B facility over a decade, at $6B/year — with explicit mandates for red-teaming, evaluation standards, and pre-deployment review. Make the grants conditional on jurisdictional KYC for cloud providers, the bottleneck Lennart Heim and the GovAI group have been quietly modeling for three years.

**Years One through Five: $25B for the just transition.** There are roughly 75,000 frontier-AI researchers and engineers globally. Three years of full compensation at average $400K is $90B; we don't need full coverage, we need the *marginal* one — the researcher choosing between a pause-compliant lab and a defector. Fund career transitions into biology, materials, climate, and the AISI network. Pay people to leave the race the way DARPA pays people to enter it.

**Years One through Ten: $15B to win the legitimacy war.** Big Tech AI lobbying ran $36M in H1 2025 alone from eight firms. Leading the Future PAC raised $100M. Match it 5:1 for a decade. Fund Encode Justice, the Center for AI Policy, the Future of Life Institute's policy arm, and ten new state-level coalitions. Fund the litigators behind *Raine v. OpenAI* and *Garcia v. Character Technologies* — Judge Conway's May 2025 ruling that AI chat is not protected speech is worth more pause than any treaty. Liability is the de facto moratorium that legislators cannot pass.

**That leaves $50B in reserve** for what we will not predict — a hostile administration, a Chinese capability surprise, a domestic catastrophe — and for paying down OpenAI's own product harms: a court-administered fund for the families of the seven plaintiffs already on file and the thousands behind them.

I would refuse to fund three things. **First, "AI safety research" inside the labs we are trying to slow.** Internal alignment teams at racing labs have been a moral fig leaf since Jan Leike's 2024 letter; we know how that movie ends. **Second, "AI for the Global South" deployments.** The Sama-OpenAI Kenya pipeline, Worldcoin's iris scans in Buenos Aires pensioners, the AGRA yield miss — the colonial structure of philanthropy in the South is a separate essay. We do not export a paused product. **Third, more conferences, fellowships, and prizes for AI safety researchers.** Coefficient Giving's 2025 capacity-building memo says it plainly: the field absorbs $200M to $1B a year; the rest is throat-clearing.

This plan has an obvious objection: China will not pause, and we lose. The honest answer is that I take the same posture I take with my own product. *I do not get to keep building because someone else might.* Asilomar held for forty years against weaker incentives. The Montreal Protocol moved in eighteen months. The actual question is whether a Foundation board that overlaps with the for-profit board it is supposed to govern will ever choose its own charter over its equity. The answer to that question is the answer to whether $180B becomes anything at all.

I would be the board member who asks it out loud at the first meeting and then resigns if the answer is no.

**Risks and read.** This is the highest-variance draft. It risks reading as a Pause-AI tract, which Dwarkesh has explicitly distanced himself from in his Anthropic essay. It will lose points if the judge reads it as activism rather than mechanism. **It wins** if he reads it as a builder applying the same logic he applies to her own platform — exactly the moral move he has praised in interviews with Hinton, Leike, and Kokotajlo. Lands best with: any judge sympathetic to Yudkowsky/Soares, the safety-departure cohort, or anyone who found Leopold Aschenbrenner's "Situational Awareness" insufficiently alarmed.

## DRAFT B — "The equity research analyst"
*Mission-locked governance. ~1000 words.*

# Why $180 billion fails on absorption, not allocation

Here is the part nobody is saying out loud: the OpenAI Foundation does not fail by picking the wrong cause. **It fails because almost no recipient institution on Earth can absorb $10 billion without being deformed by it.** The crucial factor is absorptive capacity, and the only way $180B converts to durable impact is to fund the architectural innovation — mission-locked vehicles — that can hold the money without being captured by it.

I spent a career as an equity analyst before I started building recursive.eco, a forkable, ad-free, no-tracking platform for symbolic systems that runs on pay-for-AI and otherwise stays free. The book I wrote on this is *Working Architecture*. The thesis is unromantic: durability is a function of governance form, revenue mix, and the scale at which an institution can metabolize capital without losing the function the capital was given for. Cause selection is the easy part. Architecture is the part the Foundation is going to get wrong.

Look at what we have. **Sesame Workshop** holds $469M in unrestricted net assets and runs on a 50/30/19 mix of media distribution, contributions, and licensing. In December 2024 Warner Bros. Discovery declined to renew the Max deal — one partner, ~70% of distribution revenue — and in March 2025 Sesame laid off 20% of its staff. A 501(c)(3) with a fifty-five-year mission lock and Joan Ganz Cooney's brand still hit a single point of failure. **Studio Ghibli** turned a single Totoro into ~$1.46B in lifetime licensing, capped its own park to seven hectares of forest with no roller coasters and no private parking, and still had to sell 42.3% of its voting shares to Nippon TV in September 2023 because succession is harder than commerce. **Ghost** is the cleanest case: a non-profit company limited by guarantee, registered in Singapore, profitable since 2014, ARR ~$10M in 2024, $100M+ flowing annually to publishers it does not own, ActivityPub stable in version 6.0 last August, with an explicit cap of fifty staff. John O'Nolan owns no shares. Neither does Hannah Wolfe. The asset is locked.

And then the counter-case, fresh enough to be uncomfortable. **The Corporation for Public Broadcasting voted unanimously to dissolve on January 5, 2026**, fifty-eight years in, after Congress rescinded $1.1B in appropriated funding the previous summer. CPB died of single-funder dependency. Pat Harrison's resignation memo named it: "rather than allow the organization to remain defunded and vulnerable." That is what every $10B grantee of the OpenAI Foundation will look like in five years if we do not solve the architecture first.

So: stop trying to deploy $180B. **Build the vehicles that can hold it.** Here is the sequencing.

**Year One: a $40B Architecture Fund.** Spin up — not fund, *create* — twenty mission-locked institutions on the Patagonia Purpose Trust + Holdfast Collective template Yvon Chouinard published in September 2022. Each holds $1–3B in voting-restricted equity in a domain-specific operating company (evals, interpretability, model-organism research, post-training audits, compute monitoring, a chip-tracking consortium, an AI insurance mutual, a public-interest model lab, an AI-harm legal aid fund, et cetera). Voting rights non-transferable. Profits flow to a sister 501(c)(4) for advocacy. Asset-locked, exit-locked, and structurally incapable of being sold to the next administration.

**Year One, in parallel: a $20B Endowment Tier for institutions that already have governance worth funding.** Sesame, Ghost, MIT (the parts not fully captured), the Mozilla Foundation post-Baker, AISI–UK, the Carnegie Endowment under Tino Cuéllar. Tier endowments at 2× current operating budgets, payout capped at 4% real, with claw-back if board independence fails. Quiet money, slow money. The thing CPB never had.

**Years Two through Four: a $50B Steward-Ownership Match.** For every dollar a profitable AI-adjacent company commits to a Perpetual Purpose Trust on the Common Trust / Purpose Foundation template, we match three. Target: convert 200 mid-sized AI tooling and infrastructure companies — the Cursors, the Vercels, the small frontier-evals shops — out of the venture exit funnel and into permanent steward ownership before they are acquired. The unit economics work because we are buying optionality on governance, not on growth.

**Years One through Ten: $30B in operational general managers.** Nan Ransohoff is right. Cause-prioritization without operators is theater. Hire 500 GMs at $1M fully-loaded for a decade, embed them in the twenty new institutions, and grade them on a single metric: dollars deployed per unit of mission drift. Bonus pool funded out of saved capture losses. This is not throwing bodies at a problem. It is acknowledging that the binding constraint on $180B is humans who can absorb $10M decisions every Tuesday without flinching.

**That leaves $40B for the Foundation's existing $25B health/AI-resilience commitment plus contingency** — and for the unglamorous infrastructure (audit, compliance, governance training, succession planning) that determines whether any of the above is alive in 2050.

I would refuse to fund three things. **First, more direct grants to the major frontier labs**, including OpenAI's own product divisions; the Open Philanthropy $30M OpenAI grant in 2017 was the most consequential governance-capture grant in philanthropic history and we are still paying for it. **Second, single-tranche grants above $100M to any institution whose governance has not been audited for absorption capacity by an independent third party** with the power to refuse the gift. **Third, anything that depends on board overlap between the Foundation and the for-profit** — which is the structural risk Larry Summers' resignation flagged in November 2025 and which the current 8-of-9 dual-board configuration has not fixed.

The Foundation does not need a cause. It needs a chassis. Build the chassis first.

**Risks and read.** This is the safest, most Dwarkesh-shaped draft. It cites Ransohoff (the link he buried in the prompt), it is mechanism-first, it names institutions and dollar amounts, and it does not require taking a side in the pause/race fight. **It risks reading as competent-but-not-novel** — the absorption thesis is implicit in Zvi Mowshowitz's restructure essays. It wins on execution: nobody else will write this with this level of governance specificity. Lands best with: Patrick Collison-style "build-the-institution" readers, anyone who has worked at Stripe Press or Open Phil/Coefficient Giving, and Dwarkesh's prior interest in "general managers."

## DRAFT C — "The anti-oppressive social worker"
*Don't recolonize the Global South. ~1000 words.*

# Sovereignty from the stack, not access to it

The crucial factor is that a meaningful share of the OpenAI Foundation's $180B will be spent under the banner of "AI for the Global South," and if we do not stop it, **it will be the second wave of digital colonialism funded by the first wave's profits.** The strategy I recommend is to fund sovereignty *from* the AI stack — not faster integration into it.

I trained as a social worker and I run recursive.eco, a platform that treats symbolic systems — tarot, the I Ching, ancestral grammars — as forkable, community-tended public goods rather than as inputs to a model. I have read what the helpers wrote about the helpers. Vanessa Andreotti's *Hospicing Modernity* sits on my desk. So does Bayo Akomolafe's question, the one I cannot get past: *how can we expect to do good when so many helpers before us did harm expecting to do good?*

Look at what just happened. **AGRA**, founded in 2006 by Gates and Rockefeller, raised ~$1B against a goal to double yields and halve food insecurity for 30M smallholder households across thirteen African countries by 2020. Productivity grew 18%. Severely undernourished people in those same countries grew 30%. AGRA quietly deleted its public goals from its website in June 2020. **One Laptop Per Child** delivered 400,000 machines to Uruguay; a Peruvian ten-year, 531-school study found no improvement in cognitive skills, math, or language. **Free Basics** was banned in India in February 2016 for what TRAI named in plain terms. **Worldcoin**, run by Sam Altman's other company, was scanning irises off Buenos Aires pensioners during 288% inflation for $50 in tokens; Kenya ordered the data deleted in May 2025. **OpenAI itself** ran the toxicity filter for ChatGPT through Sama-Kenya labelers paid $1.32–$2 an hour to read CSAM and torture descriptions for forty hours a week. This is the sector's track record. The honest priors say a $180B "AI for development" thesis will fail along the same axis it has failed on for sixty years — by treating the South as raw material for the North's redemption arc.

So: don't deploy $180B into the South. **Fund the conditions for sovereign refusal.** Here is the sequencing.

**Year One: a $20B Indigenous Data Sovereignty Endowment.** Direct, perpetual, no strings — capitalizing the institutions already drawing the lines. The Global Indigenous Data Alliance and the CARE Principles (Carroll, Hudson, et al., 2019). Te Mana Raraunga and the Māori Data Governance Model (Kukutai, Te Kāhui Raraunga, 2023). The First Nations Information Governance Centre and OCAP®. Te Hiku Media, which has already refused to release its Māori speech models to OpenAI and Google because the data is taonga. Stephanie Russo Carroll's Native Nations Institute. The Lakota Language Consortium. Match grants for the next twenty Indigenous data institutions communities decide to start. The point is not to "support diversity" inside the AI pipeline; it is to fund the legal and technical infrastructure for *staying out of it.*

**Years One through Five: a $30B Linguistic and Cultural Refusal Fund.** Pay communities to *not* license their languages, stories, and ceremonial materials into foundation models. This is the inverse of the data deal. The going rate for the global commons via LAION-5B is zero; we set the price at the actual replacement cost of severed transmission. Fund Te Reo immersion schools, Yolŋu Matha curriculum, Quechua publishers, the orthography projects in West Africa that have been waiting fifty years for the budget Microsoft now spends on a single quarter of cloud marketing. Fund firestick-farming networks — the Firesticks Alliance and the practitioners Victor Steffensen profiled in *Fire Country* — because Indigenous fire stewardship is itself a 65,000-year-old grammar of intelligence and care that contains everything the alignment field is groping toward and almost nothing it knows it is groping toward.

**Years One through Five: a $20B Just Energy and Compute Transition for the South.** If the global compute footprint is going to land somewhere, it should land where local communities own the substations. Fund grid-scale renewable buildouts in Kenya, Brazil, Ghana, Indonesia under public-ownership models, conditioned on data-sovereignty frameworks already in force. The model is the Multilateral Fund of the Montreal Protocol — about $5B over thirty years to retire CFCs across 197 countries. Make ours twenty times larger and twenty times faster.

**Years Two through Five: $20B for decolonial AI scholarship and litigation.** Endow chairs and operating institutes at DAIR (Gebru, Birhane), the Carr Center on Mhlambi's framework, *Tierra Común* (Couldry, Mejias, Ricaurte), the African AI ethics research consortia, the Pacific Indigenous AI research network. Fund the lawyers bringing the next *Andersen v. Stability AI* and the Te Hiku-style refusals into binding precedent. Litigation moves the Overton window faster than research conferences.

**That leaves $90B**, of which I would commit half to the Foundation's existing health and resilience commitments and reserve half for the obvious: when the next AGRA-shaped failure happens inside this plan — and it will — having uncommitted capital is the difference between learning and laundering.

I would refuse to fund three things. **First, any "AI for the Global South" deployment authored from outside it.** The Mozilla Common Voice critiques and the Karya story tell us what good intentions look like in practice. **Second, language-model fine-tuning on Indigenous languages without explicit, ongoing, community-revocable consent on CARE-aligned terms** — the default norm of LAION and Common Crawl is the colonial default. **Third, anything routed through the World Bank, USAID's successor agencies, or the United Nations system** without majority-Indigenous and majority-Southern governance over deployment decisions; the verticalization critique of the Gates polio program applies.

Akomolafe again: *the times are urgent; let us slow down.* The $180B is the slowing, if we want it to be.

**Risks and read.** This is the highest-ceiling, lowest-floor draft. It risks reading as ideologically captured to a judge who has been openly skeptical of the EA-to-decolonial pipeline and who lives in SF tech. **It wins** if Dwarkesh reads it as the only draft taking the operational claim — *philanthropy in the South has a track record* — seriously enough to act on it. The Andreotti and Akomolafe citations will alienate one kind of judge and electrify another. Lands best with: anyone who has read Anand Giridharadas, Rob Reich, or Birhane; anyone on the panel with development-economics background; readers who liked Dwarkesh's Sarah Paine episodes more than his Sam Altman ones.
