# Anti-Oppressive AI Development?
*Dwarkesh Prize Q4 — submission for PlayfulProcess LLC*
*Draft v1 — 2026-04-28*
*~970 words*

---

The United States cannot decide whether AI is a national-security issue or a source of economic power, and the gap between those two answers is where the rest of the world has to plan its life. Treating AI as national security means restricting who builds it, where the chips go, and which capital can fund what — and that restriction blocks economic development, or limits it. Treating AI as economic power means letting capital flow from anywhere willing to pay, and that flow leaks the strategic information the national-security frame would protect. Washington has tried to do both. The G42 deal in 2024 was the first time it was forced to choose. *Financial Times*: G42 CEO Peng Xiao, after the Commerce Department review and the Microsoft $1.5 billion investment, said simply, "We cannot work with both sides." The chip chokepoint did the wall-enforcement that capital fungibility could not.

The U.S. has not yet finished making the choice. Until it does, the rest of the world is being asked to choose first.

If I were the President of Brazil, I would want to stay as far from the geopolitical race as possible. Not because Brazil has no preference — Brazil is, at this point, substantially Chinese-owned in land, energy, and increasing infrastructure, and China is and will remain a critical economic partner — but because Brazil has no power to change the geopolitical game and no interest in being conscripted into someone else's. The honest Brazilian platform is the one that already differentiates Brazil internationally: environment first, peace, and civilian-application AI on Brazilian terms. On the environmental commitment alone, China is currently more aligned with Brazil than the U.S. is. On peace, neither superpower is.

Three identities are available at the application layer, and a leader who refuses to choose becomes the third by default.

**The hawk** treats AI as national-security infrastructure. The state nationalizes the strategic stack and accepts forgone monetization as the price of sovereignty.

**The rentier** treats AI as a vehicle for capital formation — sovereign funds and private capital from anywhere finance application-layer ventures in exchange for strategic concessions. G42 and Humain are the empirical proof that hawk and rentier cannot coexist. Capital leaks. Chip licenses do not.

**The deployer** treats AI as a development tool. Bring applications to agriculture, education, health, public services. This is the kindest-sounding option. It may also be the worst.

The argument I want to make is the one nobody is making, which is that "saving the developing world by giving it the best possible tutor — which will be AI" is not obviously a moral good and may be the opposite. The case for it is one century old: every prior wave of educational technology, from Pressey's 1924 testing machine through Skinner, PLATO, the MOOC pivot, and One Laptop Per Child, was sold to Global South governments with the same Antagonism-Urgency rhetoric — *the existing system is failing the children, we cannot wait* — and every wave produced marginal-to-null measured learning gains while transferring procurement budgets to Northern vendors.

Khan Academy's Khanmigo is the current iteration. Adoption has scaled from 40,000 to 1.4 million users in two years on the Bloom two-sigma promise. The only published peer-reviewed RCT (Slijepcevic & Yaylali, *Journal of Teaching and Learning*, 2025) found no statistically significant learning gains over Google search or paper. Khan Academy's own efficacy page describes outcome studies as "underway." Their chief learning officer, Kristen DiCerbo, hedges that Khanmigo faces "the same challenge we have seen historically with educational technology." The most-cited learning gains come from a ~50-student selective microschool using a vendor-branded assessment with no control group. Paulo Blikstein and colleagues at Columbia Teachers College have documented the pattern — the AAA discourse (Antagonism, Antiquity, Alarmism) — across the full century.

Underneath the deployer path is a structural geometry that decolonial theorists have named, even if the AI-policy conversation has not. Vanessa Andreotti, in *Hospicing Modernity*, observes that "modern skills training" delivered to young people displaces them from intact communities and traditions into a dislocated economy that, increasingly, does not need their labor. The residential-schools logic — "kill the Indian, save the man" in Pratt's 1892 phrasing — was dressed in the same vocabulary of benevolent civilizational uplift that today dresses adaptive-learning platforms. The parallel is structural, not legal: the question is who designs, who receives, and what the receiver loses in the act of receiving. If the people designing the tutor have decided what counts as success, what counts as knowledge, and what language the conversation happens in, the tutor is teaching the receiver to be a worse version of someone else.

This is not an argument against AI. It is an argument against the assumption that being a deployer is the moral high ground.

So if I were the President of Brazil, the platform would look like this. Refuse to host hyperscaler datacenters as a primary economic strategy. The Microsoft $2.7 billion and AWS $1.8 billion already committed in São Paulo, plus the Scala $50 billion AI City announced for Rio Grande do Sul, dwarf the R$ 23 billion PBIA budget by an order of magnitude — the "sovereign cloud" rests on hyperscaler capacity. Lean into the application layer, where Maritaca's Sabiá family has shown that domain-specialized Brazilian models can match frontier on Brazilian benchmarks at 10× lower cost. Fund Portuguese-language LLMs, Brazilian healthcare and judicial-process applications, and Cerrado/Amazon environmental monitoring. Build institutions that constrain state AI capacity from drifting toward autocracy — corruption is engrained enough in Brazil that a part of me wants the system to collapse, but the wiser part knows that what replaces collapse is populist dictatorship, which is worse. So: institutional limits, environmental commitment, peace, and the candid acknowledgment that the deployer path is not virtue.

The trilemma does not have a fourth answer. The Bandung leaders in 1955 understood this. Non-alignment was not the absence of choice; it was the active construction of a third position that did not yet exist. Tito, Nehru, and Nasser said: "we will choose a third path, and we will make it exist by acting." The Brazilian, Indian, or Nigerian leader who treats AI as a domain where neutrality is possible has chosen the deployer path by default. The honest position is to choose with eyes open, knowing that all three available identities carry colonial geometry, and that the deployer path — the kindest-sounding one — may be the worst because it brings the harm directly to one's own population.
