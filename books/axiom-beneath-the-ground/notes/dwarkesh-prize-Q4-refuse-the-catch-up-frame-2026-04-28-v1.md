# Refuse the Catch-Up Frame
*Dwarkesh Prize Q4 — v1 draft*
*~1,000 words target*

---

The question asks how countries outside the AI production chain avoid being sidestepped by transformative AI. The framing assumes a race with a finish line and asks the laggards what to do. Both halves of that framing should be examined before any leader acts on the question.

Jeffrey Ding, in his September 2025 RAND paper *Running the Right AI Race*, distinguishes sprint dynamics — who builds the most capable frontier model — from diffusion dynamics — which economy absorbs and applies AI fastest across sectors. The historical record is unambiguous: industrial revolutions are won at the diffusion margin, not at the invention margin. Britain invented the steam engine; Germany and the United States overtook Britain by deploying it. The catch-up frame asks India and Nigeria to imitate the steam engine. The diffusion frame asks what they want to power.

This is not abstract. India's Krutrim-2 12B, released February 2026, is dominated by GPT-4o on most English benchmarks. AI4Bharat's IndicTrans2 — open-source — already outperforms GPT-4o on Indic-language translation, the application Indians actually need. The IndiaAI Mission's 38,000 subsidized GPUs cannot match Stargate's 5GW UAE buildout. India's ₹65–116 per GPU-hour subsidies cannot compete with a U.S.–Gulf compute alliance funded by a $1.4 trillion infrastructure commitment. The catch-up game is unwinnable on its own terms. But the catch-up game is also the wrong game.

If I led India or Nigeria today, I would refuse the catch-up frame and act on three structural moves, in order.

**First: form the regulatory bloc.** India, Nigeria, Brazil, Indonesia, South Africa, Kenya, and Egypt cover roughly three and a half billion people across four continents. Any frontier model deployed at scale in those markets requires regulatory permission. Coordinated, that permission is leverage. Uncoordinated, it is captured one bilateral negotiation at a time. The bloc should establish, by Q4 2026, a shared evaluation regime: any model deployed to more than ten million users in any member country must pass a bloc-administered safety and capability suite. The model is not banned. It is conditioned. The U.S.–EU AISI network shrank in 2025 when Paris and Washington refused the safety-to-action redefinition at the February summit; the gap is exactly where bloc evaluation authority can stand. The Bharat Mandapam summit of February 2026, with ninety-two countries and over $200B in commitments, named the opportunity. The bloc should claim it. Co-host the technical body across IIT Madras, Strathmore CIPIT, AIMS, and Tec de Monterrey, at five hundred million dollars over five years — less than the Foundation pledges that have already been announced and undisbursed.

**Second: redirect domestic AI spend from frontier imitation to application infrastructure.** Stop trying to build the next OpenAI. Build the rails that carry intelligence into agriculture, public health, the judicial backlog, and adaptive learning in languages the frontier labs do not prioritize. India Stack, the DPDP Act, and AIKosh are the substrate. The application layer is where Indian and Nigerian populations are large enough to constitute their own market. Krutrim cannot compete with GPT-4o for English chat. AI4Bharat is already winning at Indic translation because the data, the speakers, and the use cases are domestic. Awarri's N-ATLaS, built on Llama-3 8B for Yoruba, Hausa, Igbo, Ibibio, Pidgin, and Nigerian English, applies the same logic to Nigeria. Microsoft's $17.5B India investment, the AWS Africa expansion, the Stargate UAE buildout — these are infrastructure imports priced in the buyer's leverage. Domestic spend at the application layer keeps the leverage at home.

**Third: establish data-provenance and consent rules as a balance-of-payments instrument.** The single largest unmonetized export from the Global South to frontier labs is training data: scraped, captured, labeled at one dollar thirty-two cents per hour by Sama-Kenya workers, used to train models sold back to the populations whose labor produced them. Couldry and Mejias name this data colonialism; the bloc should treat it as such. By 2028, member states require traceable provenance for any training data sourced from bloc populations and a consent regime backed by a revenue royalty paid to a bloc-administered fund. The royalty is small per-token. At the scale of bloc populations, it is structural revenue. This is the only Global South lever that scales with the size of the AI economy itself, because the input it taxes is the input AI needs.

A move applies through all three: refuse the urgency. The race framing that demands India sign onto AISI miniaturization, accept Tier 2 status under the Biden Diffusion Rule, or align with U.S. foreign policy in exchange for H200 access is a framing that loads the dice. Aschenbrenner's June 2024 *Situational Awareness* projected $100B run-rate revenue at frontier labs by mid-2026; the actual figure is roughly half that. The race is not over. The race is also not what its strongest advocates predicted. The bloc has time to build differently if it does not accept the frame that says it does not.

There is a serious counter-argument: proliferation risk. If the bloc lowers the cost of capable models in member countries, biological and cyber misuse risks rise. This is the strongest version of the case for U.S. export-control gatekeeping, and it should not be dismissed. But proliferation risk is addressable at the chokepoint that matters — DNA synthesis, cyber-offensive capability evaluation — through targeted bloc-administered controls, not blanket compute denial. The Hendrycks–Schmidt–Wang nonproliferation argument applies to bioweapon uplift specifically. It does not justify general gatekeeping that costs three and a half billion people meaningful regulatory authority over the systems being deployed in their lives.

The deeper question Q4 asks is whether the AI production chain is the only chain worth being on. The answer, on the available evidence and the historical record of industrial diffusion, is no. The frontier is a story. The leverage is in the population, the regulatory authority, and the data — three resources the bloc countries already have. The work is to use them before the framing tells them they cannot.
