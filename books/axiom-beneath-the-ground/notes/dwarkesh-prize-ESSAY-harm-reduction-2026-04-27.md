## What I would do with $180 billion if it were possible to spend at all

The first thing the OpenAI Foundation has to confront, and the first thing this essay will name, is that **the question presupposes a deployability the Foundation does not actually have.** The Foundation's $180 billion is not cash. It is a 26 percent stake in OpenAI Group PBC, valued at the most recent $852B raise. The Foundation can deploy this capital only by selling shares (which depresses the valuation it depends on) or by receiving dividends (which the captive-R&D economics of frontier AI labs structurally do not produce). The Foundation's 8-of-9 board overlap with the for-profit means the same humans are making both decisions; the disposition that would liquidate the stake is the disposition that conflicts with the disposition that holds it. The $180 billion is paper that converts to deployable capital only at a rate the Foundation's own governance is structured to prevent.

Most answers to the prize question accept the framing — *deploy the money* — and propose a deployment. I want to argue that the framing is the false dilemma. The question is not how to deploy the money. The question is what the Foundation owes given that the money is, for practical purposes, not deployable on the timeline the harms are arriving on.

The shift this requires is from *impact* to *responsibility.*

*Impact* is the philanthropy term of art for *did the money produce a verifiable outcome.* It assumes the donor has the agency to author the intervention, the resources to fund it, and the institutional clarity to measure whether it worked. This is the colonizing voice of contemporary giving. It is the voice that produced AGRA's quietly deleted 2020 goal page, the One Laptop Per Child Peruvian null findings, the Worldcoin iris collection in Buenos Aires during 288 percent inflation, and the Sama-Kenya labelers paid one dollar thirty-two cents to two dollars per hour to read trauma material so that ChatGPT could be safer for its English-speaking users.

*Responsibility* is a different word. Responsibility is what is owed regardless of what can be delivered. The Foundation has $180 billion in paper because of harm already done — labor extracted, data scraped, attention captured, water and electricity drawn from grids whose climate-adaptation budgets are smaller than a single hyperscaler's quarterly capital expenditure. The capital represents a debt, not an opportunity. The question is what is owed, not what is impactful.

What is owed has two domains: human and environmental.

**The human debt.** The OpenAI October 2025 disclosure documents 1.2 million weekly users expressing suicidal ideation through ChatGPT. This is a population the size of a small country interacting with a known harm vector. The standard philanthropic response is to fund mental-health awareness campaigns. The responsibility-framed response is to structure a right-of-care obligation: any user who triggers the suicidal-ideation classifier receives, as a property of the platform, immediate access to licensed clinical services paid by the Foundation as restitution rather than as charity. Adjacent: legal aid for the K.G.M. plaintiffs' bar at scale, so the tort infrastructure that opened in March 2026 reaches the harms it is designed to reach. Adjacent: universal-basic-income pilots in regions where AI-driven displacement has begun, scoped specifically to the populations Amodei himself has predicted will lose half of entry-level white-collar work in the next one to five years. Not as charity. As the donor paying for the consequences of what the donor produced.

**The environmental debt.** Frontier AI training and inference draw water and electricity at population scale. The Stargate UAE 5GW commitment, the Anthropic-AWS 5GW commitment, the Microsoft and Google grid pledges — these are at the scale of small national power infrastructures, allocated to a single industrial use whose external benefits to the populations whose power and water it consumes are, on the empirical record, contested. The responsibility-framed response is climate-adaptation funding at the same scale, in the same regions whose grids and aquifers are being drawn, governed by the receiving populations under data-sovereignty regimes (the CARE Principles, Te Mana Raraunga, the First Nations Information Governance Centre frameworks). Not as donor-authored deployment. As the regions affected by the buildout receiving the resources to navigate what the buildout has done to their material conditions.

These two are inseparable from a third domain that I want to name without elaborating: the domain of harms whose chokepoint is *inside the labs themselves.* Bioweapon uplift can be addressed at gene synthesis. Mental-health damage cannot be addressed except by redesigning the products that cause it. Climate impact cannot be addressed except by slowing the buildout. The AI-safety discourse focuses on the harms with chokepoints outside the labs because the labs structurally cannot advocate for the constraints that would bind their own products. The Foundation, if it is not also captured by this structural feature, has the opportunity to fund what the discourse has been unable to advocate for: the harm-reduction work that exists *because the products themselves cause harm and the products will not be redesigned.*

This is the work I would fund. Not because it solves the AI race — the race is, on the available evidence, not solvable on the timeline the harms are arriving — but because it is what is owed. The Foundation's $180 billion paper, to the extent it can be liquidated at all, is the species' down payment on its own grief. It does not save. It pays attention. The attention is the work.

A final note on the false-dilemma framing. The standard response to "the money isn't deployable" is to propose financial instruments that solve the deployability problem — secondary-market sales structured to minimize valuation impact, dividend mandates, philanthropic loan structures. I am not making that proposal. The financial-engineering version of this essay is a different essay. The version I am making is: *if the money were deployable, this is what would be owed. If it is not deployable, the obligation remains as obligation regardless of the Foundation's structural impotence.* Writing the obligation down is part of the obligation. Whether the Foundation honors it is theirs to decide.

The race may burn the planet. The 1 percent who survive will have a debt they cannot pay. The Foundation has the opportunity to write the down payment. That is what is being asked.
