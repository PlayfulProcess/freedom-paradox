# Govern the Equity, Not the Cash
*Dwarkesh Prize Q3 — corrected v2 after fact-check pass*
*~1,000 words*

---

The OpenAI Foundation does not have $180 billion to deploy. It has a 26 percent stake in OpenAI Group PBC plus a 15-year warrant for additional shares. Liquidating the stake depresses the valuation it depends on. Waiting for dividends produces nothing — Microsoft's first-quarter FY2026 10-Q recognized $4.1 billion in equity-method losses from OpenAI in a single quarter; OpenAI is loss-making, has never paid a dividend, and funds its compute commitments through primary equity raises and debt-financed multi-year cloud contracts. Most plausible "deploy the money" answers founder on this fact before they engage what to deploy *for*.

The Foundation's actual lever is the equity itself. Through the Class N Common Stock issued at the October 2025 recapitalization, the Foundation has prior-written-approval rights over six categories of corporate action — any amendment to the public-benefit mission, any sale or material asset transfer, any issuance of new voting capital, any disproportionate change to the PBC's charter or bylaws — as enumerated in Paragraph 7 of OpenAI's Memorandum of Understanding with the California Attorney General. Through Paragraph 2 of the same MOU, the Foundation has the *sole power to appoint and remove* every director of OpenAI Group PBC. These are not abstract entitlements. They are operational tools — currently held by an institution whose 7-of-8 board overlap with the for-profit (8-of-9 at recap closing; reduced after Larry Summers' November 2025 resignation, which has not been refilled five months later) means the same humans hold and exercise both sides.

If I ran the Foundation, I would not spend a dollar deploying capital to external causes first. I would spend the next twelve months making the equity already held into a binding constraint on the company it owns.

**One. Make the Attorneys General's already-binding commitments operational.** The strongest version of this argument is not what the Foundation should newly commit to. The California AG MOU and the Delaware AG Statement of No Objection have already named the binding commitments. The MOU's Paragraph 14 requires a second NFP-only director within one year of recap — by October 2026. The DE Statement conditions non-objection on Safety & Security Committee authority to halt model releases "even where applicable risk thresholds would otherwise permit release." Six months past closing, neither commitment is visibly operational. The Foundation's first job is not to invent new structure. It is to make the structure that has already been promised in writing functional before the deadlines name the gap publicly.

**Two. Separate the boards.** No director on the Foundation should also sit on the OpenAI Group PBC board. The current overlap is the structural feature that makes the Foundation's stated governance role inoperative; the same humans cannot meaningfully discipline themselves. Re-seat the Foundation board within twelve months with directors whose only fiduciary duty is the mission. Bret Taylor, Foundation chair, cannot also chair the operating company. The Midas Project's October 2025 statement on the recap and Public Citizen's 2024–2025 letter series to the California and Delaware Attorneys General have both named this; the response is the reform.

**Three. Operationalize the Class N six-trigger framework.** Define a public, binding list of mission-relevant decisions that trigger Foundation review under each of the six §7 categories. Each Foundation review produces a public report. The §7 prior-approval rights become a discipline rather than a paper right.

**Four. Test the SSC release-halt authority as a live tool, not a contingent one.** The Pentagon contract OpenAI signed February 28, 2026 — followed by Robotics/Hardware lead Caitlin Kalinowski's March 7, 2026 resignation citing "surveillance of Americans without judicial oversight and lethal autonomy without human authorization" — was a moment when the SSC's MOU-granted release-halt authority could have been visible. It was not. The Foundation should establish that the authority is real by exercising it on the next equivalent decision and documenting the exercise publicly.

**Five. Define mission-drift triggers in advance.** Anthropic's Responsible Scaling Policy v3.0, effective February 24, 2026, removed v2.2's binding pause commitment, replacing it with a non-binding "Frontier Safety Roadmap." Jared Kaplan attributed the change to a "collective action problem." OpenAI's own equivalent commitments are softer than the AG documents already require. The Foundation defines mission-drift thresholds in writing, publicly, with binding consequences (board action, public report, vote-against on related items). Defining thresholds in advance is the move that prevents post-hoc rationalization.

None of these moves require institutional invention. The Mozilla Foundation has run the 501(c)(3)-parent-over-for-profit-operating-company structure for two decades. The Tata Trusts have controlled the Tata operating companies through a separated-board firewall for more than a century. The Bosch Stiftung holds 92 percent of Robert Bosch GmbH with explicit board separation. The Novo Nordisk Foundation governs a $600-billion-market-cap pharmaceutical operating company through a holding-company intermediary. The templates exist. The Foundation has chosen not to use them.

The same structural fact applies to the Anthropic cofounders' January 26, 2026 pledge of 80 percent of wealth — pledged equity in a non-dividending lab is pledged paper, not pledged capital, until the governance question that converts paper to leverage is solved. The frontier-lab philanthropy category, not just the OpenAI Foundation specifically, is captive to its own asset structure.

The strongest counter is that the Microsoft–OpenAI definitive agreement of October 28, 2025 substantially diluted the Foundation's post-AGI lever — Microsoft now holds IP rights to models and products through 2032 (including post-AGI models), and the unilateral nonprofit-board AGI declaration has been replaced by an undisclosed third-party expert panel. This is correct. It also sharpens rather than breaks the thesis. The remaining lever is therefore *pre-AGI* operating discipline: the SSC halt authority, the appointment power, the §7 prior-approval rights, the AG-mandated commitments. That is the lever still available. Use it.

Whatever the May 2026 verdict in *Musk v. OpenAI* — where jury selection began April 27, 2026 on two surviving claims (unjust enrichment and breach of charitable trust), with remedies-phase sought to include disgorgement to the nonprofit and unwinding of the for-profit conversion — the structure that emerges will require Foundation governance to be operational rather than nominal. The verdict cannot save governance the Foundation has not built. As Senator Elizabeth Warren named explicitly in her January 28, 2026 letter to Sam Altman demanding disclosure of "circumstances under which OpenAI would seek a taxpayer bailout," the political-economy stakes have already moved past the framing the Foundation has been comfortable inside.

The Foundation has the lever it has. The lever is the equity, and what the equity reaches is the root. I would start within twelve months.
