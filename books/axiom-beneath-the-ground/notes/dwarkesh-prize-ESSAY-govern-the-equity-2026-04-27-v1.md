## What I would do as the OpenAI Foundation, exactly, and when

The first thing the Foundation has to confront, and the first thing this essay will name, is that the question is malformed at the unit of analysis. The Foundation does not have $180 billion to deploy. It has a 26 percent stake in OpenAI Group PBC valued at the latest funding round, plus a Class N veto over mission changes. The cash interpretation of the prize question — *how should we spend the money* — assumes a deployability the Foundation does not have. The Foundation can liquidate the stake (which depresses the valuation it depends on, and dilutes the lever) or it can wait for dividends (which the captive-R&D economics of frontier labs structurally do not produce). Most plausible deployment plans, evaluated honestly, founder on this fact before they engage the question of what to deploy *for*.

The Foundation's actual lever is the equity itself. The 26 percent of OpenAI Group PBC, the Class N veto, the legal right to nominate directors, the remaining mission-lock provisions in the corporate charter. These are not abstract entitlements. They are operational tools, currently held by an institution whose 8-of-9 board overlap with the for-profit means the same humans hold and exercise both sides. **If I ran the Foundation, I would not spend a dollar deploying capital to external causes. I would spend the next eighteen months restructuring the Foundation's governance so that the equity it already holds becomes a binding constraint on the company it owns.**

Six concrete moves.

**One. Separate the boards.** No director on the Foundation should also sit on the OpenAI Group PBC board. The current overlap is the structural feature that makes the Foundation's stated governance role inoperative; the same humans cannot meaningfully discipline themselves. The Foundation's board should be re-seated within twelve months with directors whose only fiduciary duty is to the mission. Bret Taylor cannot chair both. Public Citizen, the Midas Project, and Larry Summers's November 2025 resignation memo have all named this problem; the response should be to fix it.

**Two. Operationalize the Class N veto.** The veto exists. It has not been visibly exercised. Define a public, binding list of mission-relevant decisions that trigger Foundation review — Pentagon contracts above a threshold, deployment expansions into surveillance domains, agentic-AI products shipped without crisis-detection, training-run thresholds above defined compute caps. Each Foundation review produces a public report explaining the decision. The veto becomes a discipline rather than a paper right.

**Three. Establish vote-against thresholds.** The Foundation's 26 percent is not a majority. It is a meaningful minority block on shareholder votes. The Foundation should publish a list of decisions it will vote against by default — anything that materially weakens the mission lock, any acquisition that subjects OpenAI to a parent without equivalent governance, any restructuring that converts the Foundation's stake into a non-voting interest. These commitments, made publicly, constrain the for-profit's deal flow.

**Four. Mandate transparency.** Foundation board votes are public. Foundation positions on OpenAI's strategic decisions are public. The Foundation publishes an annual governance report assessing OpenAI's compliance with the stated mission. The mechanism is the same one nonprofit boards use to discipline their own staff; the discipline becomes operative when the audit is public. Sam Altman's five-day November 2023 firing-and-reinstatement was, among other things, a failure of public process; the same scenario in 2027 should run differently because the Foundation has structured public accountability into the governance.

**Five. Whistleblower protection and resourcing.** OpenAI employees, Foundation staff, and contractors who raise mission-drift concerns should have legally protected, financially supported channels to do so. The Foundation funds an independent ombuds office staffed outside OpenAI's control, with subpoena-equivalent powers within the corporate context. Mrinank Sharma's resignation in 2025 was an event the Foundation could have absorbed and made consequential; under the current structure it was absorbed and made nothing. The next equivalent event should produce a Foundation review.

**Six. Define and disclose mission-drift triggers in advance.** What does it mean for OpenAI to fail at its mission? The Foundation answers this question in writing, publicly, with specific quantitative and qualitative thresholds. Failure at any threshold triggers an automatic Foundation review with binding consequences (board action, public report, vote-against on related items). Defining the thresholds in advance is the move that prevents post-hoc rationalization. Anthropic's RSP v3.0 dropped its unilateral pause commitment because it was easier to drop than to honor; the Foundation defines its triggers in a form that makes them harder to drop.

These six moves cost a fraction of $180 billion. The bulk of the equity remains as it is — held, voted, watched, used. The work of the Foundation, on this reading, is not philanthropy. It is governance. The unit of impact is not a deployed grant. It is a binding constraint on OpenAI's deployment decisions for as long as the Foundation holds the lever.

There is an obvious objection: the Foundation will not do this because the same humans who would have to enact the reforms benefit from the current structure. This is the strongest version of the captive-board critique and I want to answer it honestly. The Foundation's directors are not bad people. They are people whose financial and reputational incentives are aligned with the for-profit's success in a way that prevents them from exercising the discipline the governance documents say they should exercise. The reforms above are designed to remove the structural alignment, not to change the people. Re-seating the board is the move that makes the rest possible. The current directors retire to roles outside the Foundation; new directors take seats whose only fiduciary obligation is the mission. The reform is governance-engineering, not character-change.

The race may still be unwinnable. The substrate may still transform on a timeline shorter than any institutional response can address. The cyanobacterial frame from elsewhere in this book may still apply, in which case the Foundation's governance reforms become the witness work of an institution that did the discipline it could do, knowing what it could not do. None of that contradicts the answer. The Foundation has the lever it has. The lever is the equity, not the cash. The work is to use it.

That is what I would do, and I would start within twelve months.
