## What to do with $180 billion: fund the institutions whose form does not match OpenAI's

The OpenAI Foundation will deploy capital through the same machinery the parent company built. Bay Area, technical, scientific-managerial, peer-reviewed, RFP-driven, university-allied. That machinery produced the alignment gap. Funding more of it produces more of the gap, at higher resolution. The crucial factor is not *what to fund* but *what institutional form is missing* — and *which form can be financed into existence by capital this large*.

Five concrete allocations. Each has a documented theory of change. None requires inventing a category.

### 1. Subsidize the plaintiffs' bar (~$15B / 5 years)

In March 2026, a Los Angeles Superior Court jury returned the first social-media-addiction bellwether verdict — $6 million against Meta and YouTube — and the court ruled that Section 230 does not cover product-design claims. Engagement-design (variable-reward, infinite scroll, persistent personalization, push-style re-engagement) is now litigated harm. The federal MDL contains 2,300+ cases; future bellwethers are scheduled for summer 2026; TikTok and Snap have begun settling.

The plaintiffs' bar that won K.G.M. v. Meta does not need a new legal theory to file the same case against the AI companies. The mechanisms are identical. What constrains the legal layer is not theory but capital — discovery is expensive, expert witnesses cost money, MDL coordination requires staff. The Foundation can fund litigation finance for AI-harm cases the way the Schuette Fund funded environmental tort.

This is not philanthropy. It is buying the responsibility infrastructure that regulation has failed to build, at the leverage ratio of tort law (each verdict reshapes the design space of every defendant in the category).

### 2. Insurance market formation (~$25B / 5 years)

Aviation safety did not improve because the FAA wrote regulations. It improved because insurers refused to underwrite carriers whose maintenance records were poor. The premium curve drove the design.

There is no insurance market for AI-induced mental health harm, AI-decision failure, or AI-amplified misinformation liability. The Foundation can capitalize a reinsurer-of-last-resort plus three or four primary carriers willing to write these policies. Once products carry insurance, design changes — because actuaries demand it. The capital required is meaningful but bounded ($25B is roughly Berkshire's reinsurance float). The pricing signal then shapes every AI deployment in the addressable market.

### 3. Mission-locked institutional alternatives (~$30B endowments)

Sesame Workshop sustained its child-development mission for 55 years across leadership transitions, platform shifts, and the death of its most beloved performer. Studio Ghibli refused to monetize its characters and remained profitable. Ghost is a publishing platform legally structured so it cannot be acquired. The pattern: governance built into the corporate charter so future leadership cannot sell the mission.

The Foundation should endow ten or fifteen new AI-deployment organizations under Ghost-style charters — community-controlled, mission-locked, legally barred from acquisition or pivot. Not "fund existing nonprofits doing alignment work." That is the same institutional form. *Build different forms*, then fund them adequately to compete with venture-capitalized incumbents.

### 4. K.G.M.-style adversarial design audits (~$10B / 5 years)

There is no funded category for "independent labs whose job is to break AI products in the way penetration testers break software security." Currently, model evaluations happen inside the lab that built the model, with auditor access controlled by the lab. This is structurally analogous to letting Boeing self-certify the 737 MAX.

Fund five or ten independent adversarial labs at the scale of the major safety teams, with statutory access protocols to every model deployed at scale. The cost is modest. The output is the empirical record on which the legal and insurance layers can build.

### 5. Non-Anglo institutional design experiments (~$20B / 10 years)

Every governance experiment cited in current AI-safety discourse is American or British. The Foundation can fund AI governance research and institutional experimentation in jurisdictions whose legal traditions are *different in kind* — Brazilian (relational personhood in environmental law), Indian (constitutional commitments to plurality), Indonesian (adat / customary law co-existing with statute), Indigenous (treaty-based relational accountability). Not "diversity in alignment research." *Funding the work of designing AI institutions inside non-Western legal cultures, on their terms.*

The colonial reflex in AI governance is to ask Lagos and Jakarta to adopt Brussels' rules. The Foundation can fund the inverse experiment: pluriversal governance design, with the resources to make the experiments survive long enough to produce evidence.

### What this leaves out

This proposal explicitly does *not* fund: more alignment research at the existing labs, more think-tank policy papers, more academic AI-ethics centers, or more "AI safety summits." Those institutions are already adequately capitalized. They are not what is missing.

### Why the Foundation will not do this

The Foundation's leadership comes from inside the institutional culture this proposal critiques. The instinct will be to fund more of what is recognizable: more ARIA, more Apollo, more MIRI, more academic chairs, more conferences. Each is a respectable choice. None is the choice that converts $180B into responsibility infrastructure.

The harder question for the Foundation is whether it has the institutional courage to fund alternatives whose form is not legible to its board. If it does, the deployment looks roughly like the above. If it does not, the $180B will be allocated to the same institutional form that produced the gap.

The cost of getting this wrong is the cost of the gap continuing.
