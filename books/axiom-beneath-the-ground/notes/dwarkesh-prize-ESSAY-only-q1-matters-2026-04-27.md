## Three of your four questions are bureaucratic optimization problems for a system that will not survive the answer to the first

The first question is the only question. The other three are versions of *given that the system continues, how should it allocate?* The first question asks whether the system continues at all. The honest answer, on the available evidence, is that it does not, and that the bureaucratic-optimization questions therefore have answers only in the trivial sense that any allocation made before the substrate transforms can be evaluated by the criteria the system used to allocate before. Once the substrate transforms, the criteria do not apply.

This essay declines questions two, three, and four. It answers question one as honestly as I can.

**Question one is: what did the previous intuition pump miss?** The intuition was that AI progress would slow as horizon lengths increased and reward signal per FLOP decreased; that the OOMs of RL compute from GPT-4 to o1 to o3 represented a one-time scaling that could not be repeated. That intuition was wrong, and the public capability sequence since 2024 makes the wrongness empirically clear. GPT-4 to o1 was a five-month gap. o1 to o3 was three. The bioweapon uplift Amodei measured at Anthropic in mid-2025 represented capability at a level his own predictions twelve months earlier had placed eighteen months later. DeepSeek V3 in December 2024 matched American frontier at six percent of the cost. R1 in January 2025 reproduced o1-class reasoning. V4-Pro in April 2026, four days before this essay was written, hit 80.6 percent on SWE-bench Verified at 14 percent of Claude Opus 4.7's price.

The intuition pump missed three things. **First**, the recursive-improvement loop is real and starting to bite — each model generation is better at training the next, and the improvement is compounding in ways the linear projections assumed it would not. **Second**, algorithmic efficiency is improving faster than the slowdown thesis assumed, and the efficiency frontier is being defined by the labs that are most resource-constrained (DeepSeek, with Huawei Ascend portability, has produced the steepest cost-per-capability curve of any frontier lab). **Third**, the political infrastructure that would have been required to slow the race has been actively dismantled — the Paris February 2025 communiqué refusal, the renaming and defanging of CAISI, the Trump pay-to-play H200 regime that began in August 2025 and expanded in December 2025 to allow H200 sales to China at a 25 percent government cut. The intuition assumed a political environment that would constrain. The political environment is funding the race directly.

If you take Aschenbrenner's June 2024 timeline (AGI 2027-2028) and Amodei's January 2026 *Adolescence of Technology* timeline (the Nobel-Prize-class system "very soon," one to two years) as the worst-case scenario the planning has to address, the structural implication is that the recursive-improvement window opens within the planning horizon of any sane response. Most of the responses currently being proposed do not fit inside that window.

A multilateral verification body modeled on the OPCW takes a decade to operationalize. A federal regulatory framework takes two electoral cycles. A treaty negotiation takes years even after the political will exists, and the political will does not exist. A philanthropic grant-making apparatus deploying $180 billion takes three to five years to reach maturity. By the time most of these become operational, the substrate has transformed. The instruments are designed for a system that will not be the system that exists when they arrive.

This is what I mean when I say questions two, three, and four are bureaucratic optimization problems for a system that will not survive the answer to the first. *When does the foundation model company start making money* is a question whose answer is rendered moot by the recursive-improvement loop's own economics — the company that wins is the company that gets there first, and the question of "making money" applies only to the losers, who become legacy products in a substrate they did not produce. *How should the OpenAI Foundation deploy $180 billion* is a question whose answer is constrained by the fact that the Foundation cannot deploy the money on a timeline shorter than the substrate's transformation. *What should countries outside the AI supply chain do* is a question whose answer is constrained by the fact that the supply chain is being commoditized faster than any country could enter it; the catch-up frame's timeline is already too slow, and the leapfrog frame requires capabilities that are being commoditized in ways that make the leap moot.

What does fit inside the worst-case window? Only what is small enough, fast enough, and survives the substrate. Crisis-response infrastructure for the documented harms already happening (mental health, the K.G.M. legal precedent, displacement support). Knowledge preservation in formats that survive platform transformation. Decentralized capability that does not depend on a single point of failure. Witness work — the record of what was happening, in non-AI-mediated form, for whoever comes after. The harm-reduction frame — *responsibility, not impact* — is the only frame that fits the timeline the racers themselves are predicting.

Aschenbrenner would object that this concedes the race. Yes, it does. The race is, on the available evidence and on his own essay's framing, conceded by the political infrastructure that would have been required to contest it. The Trump pay-to-play H200 regime is the United States government, in the December 2025 calendar year, choosing a 25 percent revenue share over the strategic-denial posture Aschenbrenner advocates. The race has been ceded by the actor whose role in the race was the only mechanism by which it could be contested.

What is left is the work that fits inside the window. That is not a small thing. It is not nothing. It is, in the Akomolafe register the rest of this book has tried to honor, *the cracks waiting to be entered.*

You wanted me to identify the crucial factor. The crucial factor is the timeline. The crucial factor is that questions two, three, and four assume a timeline the first question's honest answer rules out. *What gives*: nothing gives. The pump was correct that progress would slow if the political conditions held. The political conditions did not hold. They are not going to hold. The optimization questions have answers in the trivial sense and the meaningful work is in the question your prize did not ask, which is what to build for the people who will need it before the optimization answers arrive.
