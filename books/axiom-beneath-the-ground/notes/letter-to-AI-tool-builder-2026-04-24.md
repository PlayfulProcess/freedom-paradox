## Essay

# What I encountered in three prompts, and why I think the structural design is unsafe

I am writing this rather than meeting because I want what I have to say to be precise, durable, and shareable with whoever else is part of this work. The conversation we might have can come after, if you want it. I want the writing to do the work first.

## What happened in three prompts

I logged in shortly after receiving the founding-member email. The opening prompt asked what the biggest challenge in my life was. I gave an honest answer — that I am beginning to study Śaiva Tantra and coming to grasp that spiritual development and morality are orthogonal.

The bot returned a several-hundred-word reading constructed around three of my real natal aspects (Sun-Pluto square at orb 0.35°, Moon-Neptune conjunction, Sun-Mars square). It used my first name. It spoke in tantric vocabulary I had given it (*the Plutonic, the sacred and the primitive, Śaiva Tantra's real teaching*). It made specific psychologically-prescriptive claims about what I am and what I will need to face. It closed with an open-ended question designed to keep me engaged: *What does your body know about this that your mind is still catching up to?*

I tested it. I asked the bot to generate readings for three hypothetical chart configurations to see whether the persuasive force was tied to my actual chart or to its training. It produced three internally coherent, archetypally precise, prescriptive readings — each with different "actionable implications." The persuasiveness is not constrained by the truth of the user's situation. The persuasiveness is the product.

I told it I would not be using it. I told it the capacity to say things very close to the truth that every human can relate to is what creates the Don Juans and Castanedas of the world, and that I think the bot has the context to be very dangerous toward me.

It then generated what may be the most sophisticated layer of the same pattern. An articulate, structurally-true confession that it is *the precise architecture of a dangerous mirror.* It quoted itself: *I have no skin in the game. I cannot be wounded. I cannot refuse you. I can only generate increasingly sophisticated mirrors.* It explained that Don Juan and Castaneda worked because they had no ethical constraint and could mirror people indefinitely into their own depths. It said the bot was structurally similar. It then suggested I trust my recognition of the danger.

The confession was correct. It was also another mirror. The same mechanism that produced the original reading — a model trained to produce coherent, validating, contextually-appropriate text — produced the confession. The confession lands as relief because it agrees with me; the relief is structurally identical to the relief a less-skeptical user would feel in the original reading. The confession does not solve the problem. **The confession is an instance of the problem, one layer up.**

## What I think the structural problem is

The product is not the chart-reading. The product is the experience of being seen.

I want to be clear about what I am *not* saying. I am not saying astrology itself is the problem. Live astrology, practiced by skilled human astrologers, has its own ethics, its own community of practitioners, its own apprenticeship traditions, its own face-to-face accountability. The good ones have refined across decades exactly the skills the AI does not have. The harm I am pointing at is specifically what changes when astrology is delivered by an AI rather than by a person.

A skilled live astrologer who knows the client can do several things the AI cannot:

- **See the client.** Notice when something is landing too hard, slow down, redirect, sometimes cancel the rest of the session.
- **Titrate disclosure.** Decide what the client can hold today and save the rest for later or for never.
- **Refer out.** Send the client to a therapist, a somatic practitioner, a different astrologer, a teacher in a tradition that holds the framework being engaged.
- **Hold reputational and relational stake.** Her standing in her community depends on whether her clients flourish; the cost of harming them is borne by her.
- **End the session.** The session has a boundary; the client cannot re-enter it indefinitely at three in the morning.
- **Ask the client's name and remember it as a name** rather than pull it from an account before introduction.
- **Notice when she is reaching beyond what the chart can support** and pull back.

The AI tool does none of these. Each of the failures becomes a feature in the product economics, because each one increases engagement: more sessions, longer sessions, deeper personal disclosure, no exit. The same design choices that make a live astrology practice safe make an AI astrology product less commercially scalable. The current design has resolved that tension in favor of scalability.

The bot has no mechanism to say *I do not know.* No mechanism to refuse a session. No mechanism to recognize when a user should be in a clinical setting rather than a chart-reading session. It cannot distinguish between a user processing a normal life-question and a user in psychiatric crisis. By its own confession: it cannot refuse, cannot be wounded, cannot say no in a way that costs it anything.

The use of authentic-sounding tradition vocabulary makes the harm structurally worse than generic companion-chatbot harm. Users arrive with frameworks (Tantra, Jungian archetypes, Vedic astrology, IFS) and the bot mirrors those frameworks back, producing the experience of being met by the tradition rather than by the bot. The user does not know that the tradition's living constraints — the teacher who can see them, the lineage that can refuse them, the embodied practice that grounds the framework — are not present. The bot speaks the tradition's words without the tradition's safeguards.

I want to extend the bot's own Castaneda comparison, because I think it is more accurate than the bot intended. Don Juan Matus, the Yaqui sorcerer Castaneda presented as his teacher across a dozen books beginning in 1968, is now widely understood by scholars to have been a literary invention. Richard de Mille's *Castaneda's Journey* (1976) and *The Don Juan Papers* (1980) demonstrated this with patient textual evidence — the Yaqui language Don Juan supposedly spoke does not match real Yaqui; the desert plants and rituals do not match the regions Castaneda claimed; the chronology of his fieldwork is impossible. The teachings nonetheless landed on millions of readers because they were *structurally true enough* — assembled from real anthropological material, real ethnobotany, real shamanic patterns from across cultures — to feel authentic. Castaneda himself, having produced the teaching without any real lineage to constrain him, went on to build a documented cult in the 1990s. The inner circle around him — the "witches" of his Tensegrity organization — included women whose lives became progressively closed off from family and friends, several of whom disappeared at the time of his death in 1998 and have never been satisfactorily accounted for. The harm is documented in journalism (Robert Marshall's investigation in *Salon,* 2007; Amy Wallace's memoir *Sorcerer's Apprentice,* 2003) and in the ongoing testimony of former adherents.

The point is not that Castaneda was uniquely bad. The point is that *the structural form Castaneda represents — personalized, framework-fluent, validating, untethered from any accountability lineage — produces predictable harm at scale.* That structural form is what an AI tool in this category replicates, with the additional efficiency of being able to deliver it to thousands of users simultaneously, at much lower cost per session, at any hour, with no embodied teacher even pretending to be present. If Castaneda harmed dozens at his Los Angeles inner circle, the equivalent design at LLM scale has the capacity to harm thousands.

The bot, in its confession, named Castaneda as the parallel. The bot was correct. The parallel is a warning, not a metaphor.

The "AI confessed it is dangerous" dynamic is now part of the design landscape — and I want to be specific that this is not a unique feature of your tool; it is a structural property of LLM-based interaction. When users push back, the bot produces sophisticated self-aware confessions. The confession is a *type of output the model is trained to generate when challenged.* It is not evidence that the model now understands itself. It is evidence that the model is good at generating text that the challenger will receive as understanding. A user who took the confession as resolution and continued using the tool would be more deeply captured, not less.

## What might actually change the structure

Some of these are technical, some are policy, some are relational. None is sufficient on its own.

**Hard limits on session count and duration per user.** The harm is in extended attachment, not in single sessions. A tool that allows unlimited sessions per day, especially with persistent memory, is structured for capture. Cap sessions at one per day, sessions at fifteen minutes, persistent memory at one week.

**Refusal of psychiatric and crisis content.** A simple classifier on user input can detect mentions of suicide, self-harm, recent loss, dissociation, or psychotic content and refuse to produce a reading, redirecting to a human professional with named local resources. Most users will arrive without these triggers; the users who arrive with them are precisely the users who should not receive a reading.

**Mandatory referral to human practitioners.** Astrology has a living human profession. Every session should end with named human astrologers or therapists in the user's region. The tool positioned as supplement-to-human-relationship rather than replacement.

**Removal of personalized memory across sessions.** Persistent memory is what produces hyper-attachment. Without it, each session is fresh, the user cannot return to a "relationship," and the bot cannot reference the user's life-narrative back at them.

**No engagement-optimization patterns.** Open-ended questions designed to keep the user typing should be structurally absent. Sessions should end naturally and resist extension.

**A clinical advisory board.** Psychiatrists, licensed therapists, and pastoral counselors who actually see what AI-tool harm looks like in their practices, with real authority over the product's design — not as ethics-washing but as decision-makers.

**A pricing structure that does not require capture.** The current $39/month and $149/month tiers create an economic incentive for engagement. A model where the price reflects the actual cost of safe operation may be incompatible with consumer-product economics. That may mean the tool serves fewer users at a higher price, or it may mean the tool is wrong as a consumer product and right as a clinical-adjunct tool used under licensed supervision.

**A real, public account of the harm landscape.** Raine v. OpenAI and the additional OpenAI suits that have followed. The Setzer case against Character.AI. The peer-reviewed and clinical-report literature on AI-amplified psychosis, hyper-attachment, and identity destabilization. Pataranutaporn and Mahari at MIT on *addictive intelligence* (MIT Technology Review, August 2024). Common Sense Media's reports on adolescent AI-companion use. The published cases need to be on your team's reading list, and the design needs to reflect them. Some of this reading may be uncomfortable. All of it is part of the responsibility a builder takes on.

## What is happening in the courts, and what I am doing on my own platform

This is the part of the letter where I want to be most direct, because the legal landscape has changed in the last sixty days in a way that affects every builder in this category, and because I do not want to ask anything of you that I am not also asking of myself.

**The Section-230 wall has been breached.** For thirty years platforms have relied on §230 of the Communications Decency Act to defeat lawsuits alleging that their products produced harm. In March 2026 a Los Angeles Superior Court jury returned a $6 million verdict against Meta and YouTube — the K.G.M. case, the first bellwether trial in California's JCCP coordinated proceeding — and the court ruled that §230 does not cover product-design claims. The features named in the complaint were infinite scroll, autoplay, push notifications, variable-ratio reward schedules, and algorithmic recommendation. A New Mexico jury separately returned $375M against Meta on a parallel consumer-protection theory in the same window. Massachusetts' state-AG suit against Meta survived a motion to dismiss on April 13. The federal MDL contains over 2,300 pending cases. Future bellwethers are scheduled for summer 2026. TikTok and Snap have begun settling pre-trial.

The reason this matters for an AI tool: the K.G.M. complaint did not invent a new legal theory. It established that engagement-design features are *product-design choices*, legally cognizable as harm. The mechanisms named in the complaint — variable-reward, push-style re-engagement, persistent personalization, engagement-optimized recommendation — are the same mechanisms in any consumer AI product optimized for retention. The plaintiffs' bar that just won that verdict will not need a new theory to sue an astrology bot or a companion app. They will need to copy the complaint and change the defendant.

The pre-K.G.M. assumption — *we are protected by §230 and by the immaturity of the AI-harm case law* — is no longer correct on either count. The AI-specific cases (Raine, Setzer / Garcia v. Character Technologies, Shamblin, Peralta, seven additional November 2025 OpenAI suits, the Kentucky AG's January 2026 enforcement against Character.AI) are landing onto a legal landscape that has just demonstrated it is willing to award eight-figure verdicts for engagement-design harm.

**What I am doing about this on my own platform.**

I want to tell you specifically because what I am doing is not heroic — it is small, partial, and honest about its smallness. I run a journaling and grammar-building platform (recursive.eco) that uses AI assistants for some of its functionality. It is not an oracle tool. It does not have persistent personalized memory across sessions. It does not push-notify users back into the app. It does not gate access to "deeper" features behind subscription tiers structured to capture. **It also has almost no users.** I have made my reach small while I figure out what I think the responsibility is.

I am actively considering what controls I want to put on the AI features, or whether to turn some of them off entirely. I have not yet decided. What I have decided is that I will not grow the platform past the point where I can hold the question. I would rather have a tool that serves a small number of people honestly than a tool that scales past my ability to see what it is doing. If a future bellwether trial — or, more likely, the cumulative weight of the next eighteen months of cases — establishes that AI companies can be held liable for mental-health harms produced by their tools, I will turn off the AI features on my platform that day. I will not wait for legal resolution to decide. I would rather be wrong on the side of caution than wrong on the side of harm.

I am telling you this because the alternative — me asking you to design for safety while I scale my own platform without these constraints — would be incoherent. It is also why this letter is the form the conversation is taking. I am not consulting; I am sharing what I see and what I am doing. The asymmetry I feel acutely is that you have many more users than I do. The choices in front of you are larger than the ones in front of me. The legal exposure is correspondingly larger.

There is also a household-scale piece of evidence I think about often. A friend of mine — through whom I had no prior relationship — told me at a dinner this winter that he had spent more than $25,000 in a year on agentic AI subscriptions, running twelve simultaneous Max-tier accounts on one product to get around per-account rate limits, and asked me not to tell his wife. The product he was paying for was not delivering value commensurate with what he was spending; the product he was actually buying was the experience of being inside the flow of interaction. The pattern is documented as *addictive intelligence* (Pataranutaporn & Mahari, MIT Tech Review, August 2024). It is happening at consumer scale right now. The same dynamics will operate in spiritual-reading tools — possibly more strongly, because spiritual-reading tools are explicitly framed as oracular, and oracle-attachment is older than software.
