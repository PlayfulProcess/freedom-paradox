## What to do with $180 billion: buy the time the race has been taking

A year ago I wrote, and did not publish, an essay arguing that Dario Amodei was the right model — that his interdisciplinary mind, his Pentagon-line refusals, his willingness to hold the two-opposed-ideas test of intelligence, was what responsible AI development looked like in our moment. I did not publish it because I could not yet name what was missing from the picture. I can now.

What is missing is the time he is not allowed to take.

Anthropic's RSP v3.0 dropped its unilateral pause commitment. Jared Kaplan: "We didn't really feel it made sense for us to make unilateral commitments if competitors are blazing ahead." Mrinank Sharma resigned: "The pressures to ship are winning." Then the Pentagon confrontation: Anthropic refused mass surveillance and autonomous weapons, won the lawsuit, and the same afternoon OpenAI announced its Pentagon deal. The lines Anthropic held were the lines a company can hold *only at the speed the race permits.* And the race permits less every quarter.

This is the structural fact Tristan Harris has been naming on Sam Harris's podcast (#469, April 2026): the AI race between firms, and more critically between nations, is "out of control," driven by a winner-take-all logic where being a few months ahead is regarded as control of the world. It is the structural fact Francis Fukuyama is naming about liberal democracy under ethno-nationalist pressure (#471): the institutions that could regulate the race are the institutions losing legitimacy faster than they can build legitimacy. It is the structural fact Bayo Akomolafe has been naming about the grammar of crisis: *the way we respond to the crisis is part of how the crisis continues.* The crisis is the race. Funding more racers — even responsible racers — is feeding it.

The OpenAI Foundation has $180B and the wrong question in front of it. The wrong question is *which AI safety work to fund*. The right question is *what would let Dario Amodei do the work he is being prevented from doing by the conditions of competition* — and the same answer applies to whoever holds the equivalent line at Google DeepMind or DeepSeek tomorrow. Alignment research at racing speed is a contradiction. Money cannot buy alignment because alignment requires time, and time is what the race takes from you. The crucial factor is therefore: **money can buy time, but only if it dismantles the conditions that make racing rational.**

Five concrete allocations, in order of leverage.

**1. Capitalize a US-China-EU compute-verification treaty (~$60B over 10 years).** The technical building blocks exist (Trusted Execution Environments at the H100/H200 level; the Singapore-hosted Frontier Model Forum's evaluation infrastructure; the PRC's December 2025 chatbot-content rules as evidence of regulatory will). What is missing is the diplomatic and verification infrastructure. The Foundation can fund the Track-II diplomatic process, the verification labs, and the carrot side (joint compute commons for medical and climate applications) at a scale states will not. NPT-for-AI is not naive. NPT-for-AI is what survives the race long enough to make alignment possible.

**2. Subsidize the legal infrastructure already pricing harm (~$20B).** In March 2026, the K.G.M. v. Meta verdict broke the Section-230 wall on engagement-design liability. The plaintiffs' bar that won that case does not need a new theory to sue every AI company building variable-reward, persistent-personalization, push-style consumer products. What it needs is capital. Litigation finance + adversarial design audits + insurer-of-last-resort markets together produce a price on harm that bounds the race from below — without requiring regulation Congress cannot pass. This is the Akomolafe-recognizable and Dwarkesh-recognizable bridge: the courts are already constructing the responsibility ex post; capital makes them faster.

**3. Pluriversal governance — not Q4's question (~$30B over 10 years).** The instinct to ask "what should India and Nigeria do to not be sidestepped" is coloniality in a different dress. It positions the rest of the world as receivers of a transformative technology already shaped by Western incentives. The Foundation should fund, instead, AI institutional design *inside* legal traditions that are different in kind — Brazilian relational-personhood environmental law, Indian constitutional plurality, Indonesian adat, Indigenous treaty-based accountability. Not "diversity in alignment research." Pluriversal infrastructure that can refuse the deployment terms the West has already accepted. The point is not what the Global South can do to catch up. The point is what conditions make the race redundant.

**4. Mission-locked deployment alternatives (~$50B).** Sesame, Ghibli, Ghost: governance built into corporate charters so future leadership cannot sell the mission. Endow ten or fifteen new AI-deployment organizations under such charters — community-controlled, legally barred from acquisition. Compete with venture-capitalized incumbents on form, not just message. The form is the argument.

**5. The trust-fund for the holdouts (~$20B).** If a company holds a redline that costs it the race, the Foundation underwrites the loss. Direct grants, bridge loans, replacement-revenue contracts. Anthropic refused the Pentagon and won the lawsuit but lost market position to OpenAI; that asymmetry is what ends the redlines. The Foundation can correct it. The mechanism is unsexy. The mechanism is decisive.

What this proposal explicitly does not fund: more alignment research at the existing labs, more think-tank policy, more AI-ethics centers, more summits. Those institutions are already capitalized. They are not what is missing. What is missing is the time to do the work. The capital can buy it. Whether the Foundation has the institutional courage to deploy it this way is the question the deployment itself will answer.
