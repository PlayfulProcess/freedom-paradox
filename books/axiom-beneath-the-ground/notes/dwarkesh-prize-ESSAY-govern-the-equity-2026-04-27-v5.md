# Govern the Equity, Not the Cash
*Dwarkesh Prize Q3 — v5 (final draft, submission-ready)*
*~970 words*

---

Frontier AI is governed by a simple rule: restraint is punished. When Anthropic declined a Pentagon contract on safety grounds, it lost share to OpenAI, which took it. This is not an anomaly. It is the equilibrium. Labs that move fastest win distribution, revenue, and influence. Labs that hesitate fall behind.

At the same time, control over AI is concentrating. A handful of actors control most frontier compute, and a small set of labs sit on top of it. The question is no longer just how intelligent these systems become, but who has the authority to deploy them—and under what constraints. In that world, the OpenAI Foundation matters because it is one of the few institutions that can impose discipline without forcing a lab into unilateral disadvantage.

It is often described as a $180 billion pool of capital waiting to be spent. That is backwards. The Foundation does not sit on $180 billion in cash—it sits on equity. To turn that equity into cash, it would have to sell it, giving up the very control that makes it valuable. In a race toward AGI, that is a bad trade. The Foundation's role is not to spend. It is to govern.

That governance role is also constrained. Key commercial and technical rights are already committed, including long-term agreements with Microsoft that extend access to models and infrastructure. The idea of a clean "AGI moment" where control resets is, at best, uncertain. The Foundation cannot assume it will have decisive leverage later. Its leverage is what it can exercise now, on systems already being deployed.

This is why internal governance matters more than external intervention. Frontier AI is developing inside a competitive and geopolitical landscape where blunt regulation is difficult to calibrate. Overly restrictive rules risk shifting capability—and therefore power—to other actors. That does not make safety optional. It means that the most effective constraints, in the near term, are those that operate inside the institutions actually building and deploying these systems.

But the current governance structure has a gap. On paper, the Safety & Security Committee has authority over deployment decisions, and the board is supposed to prioritize the mission in safety-related questions. In practice, the key terms—what counts as a "safety and security" issue, what evidence is required to proceed, and how the mission applies in contested cases—are not defined with enough precision to be enforceable. When definitions are vague, authority becomes optional.

The result is predictable. Decisions with significant safety implications can be framed either way: as advancing the mission or undermining it. Without operational definitions, governance does not fail loudly. It fails quietly, by never being activated.

The Foundation's power is also not absolute. It cannot simply dictate outcomes in a system shaped by contracts, competition, and internal incentives. That is why leverage matters—and why moments of external pressure become important.

The lawsuit brought by Elon Musk should not be understood as a solution. Courts will not determine the future of AI. But the existence of the case creates something rarer: a moment where internal governance can be exercised under public scrutiny. It introduces uncertainty, surfaces internal records, and makes questions of control visible. In politics, external pressure is often used to reshape internal dynamics. This is no different. The opportunity is not to rely on the trial, but to use it—to assert authority while attention is high and positions are not yet fixed.

Against that backdrop, the Foundation should act in three ways.

**First, define what governance actually means in practice.**
The existing framework gives authority, but not clarity. The Foundation should specify what triggers review, what qualifies as a safety-critical deployment, what evidence is required, and what the "mission-first" test entails in real decisions. These definitions do not need to be perfect. They need to be binding. Without them, governance remains discretionary. With them, it becomes operational.

**Second, separate oversight from execution.**
If the same individuals sit on both the Foundation board and the operating company, governance collapses into alignment. Independence is not symbolic—it is functional. A board that cannot reach a different conclusion than management is not governing. It is agreeing. Structural separation creates the possibility of real intervention when incentives diverge.

**Third, exercise the authority on a decision that matters.**
Governance is only credible if it is used. The Safety & Security Committee's ability to halt or condition deployments must be demonstrated in practice, not assumed. High-stakes deployment decisions—especially those with national security, surveillance, or autonomous capabilities—are precisely where this authority should be tested. If governance cannot assert itself there, it will not assert itself anywhere.

None of this requires slowing the field unilaterally or abandoning competitive positioning. It requires something more specific: making it clear, through action, that not every decision will default to speed.

We often talk about AI in terms of intelligence—how capable these systems will become. But intelligence alone does not determine outcomes. Power does. And power, in practice, is institutional: the ability to make decisions that stick.

This is what the Foundation is for.

Not to fund safety from the outside, where money competes with incentives and loses. But to enforce it from within, where control over the asset can reshape those incentives directly.

The lever already exists. It is the equity.

The question is whether it will be used.
