# Chapter 10e: What Intelligence Was Never Doing

---

She wants to say one more thing about the AI mirror before the book turns toward the dance, because the mirror is showing her something the discussions around it mostly miss, and the thing it is showing has to do with her own previous training.

She spent her twenties inside classical economics. She was taught, as everyone is taught, that the elegance of the discipline was the elegance of its equations — supply and demand finding a point, utility maximized across constraints, equilibria derivable from a small set of assumptions about how rational agents behave. The beauty of the models was real. She had been good at them. She had, for a period, believed the beauty was evidence of truth.

What she came to see later — and what she thinks most undergraduate courses still refuse to see — is that the elegance was almost entirely a computational artifact. The economists of the nineteenth and twentieth centuries were not modeling rational agents because they thought real agents were rational. They were modeling rational agents because rationality — specifically, the assumption that every agent was identical and maximizing — was the *only assumption under which the math was tractable by hand*. A population of heterogeneous agents with different strategies produces equilibria that cannot be derived in closed form; they can only be simulated. Simulation requires computers. The economists did not have computers. So they assumed homogeneity and got equations they could solve with a pen.

This would be a small historical footnote except for what the profession did next. It took the computational simplification, which was a constraint, and promoted it into a methodological commitment called *methodological individualism*. The assumption that markets are the sum of identical rational agents was no longer a pragmatic shortcut — it was the ontology of the field. Public policy was built on it. Entire subfields of sociology and political science imported it. Generations of students, including her, were taught that human behavior at scale could be understood by modeling one rational agent and multiplying. The premise was never empirically grounded. The premise was a residue of the pre-computer constraint, mistaken for an insight into how social reality worked.

It is, she now thinks, among the most harmful ideas still in active circulation — not because the input-output logic of markets is wrong, but because the *methodological individualism underneath it* has taught multiple generations to believe that collective phenomena are reducible to the behavior of identical atoms. They are not. The equilibrium of an ecosystem of different pricing strategies is not the same as the equilibrium of a population of identical maximizers, and real markets have always looked more like the first than the second. The profession knew this by the end of the twentieth century. The textbooks mostly still do not.

---

What matters for the book is the parallel.

When computers finally arrived, two things became possible that had been locked out by the hand-computation constraint. The first was agent-based modeling, which she encountered through the work of Santa Fe Institute researchers — Epstein, Axtell, Arthur, Holland. They were doing what economics had always been trying to do, but with the computational capacity to actually do it. A thousand heterogeneous agents with different strategies, different memory windows, different risk tolerances, and different updating rules, simulated across many rounds, producing an equilibrium the individual equations of any single agent would not have predicted. The equilibrium is *emergent*. It is a property of the whole population acting over time, not a property that can be derived from any one agent's behavior. The mathematics is still mathematics — but it is the mathematics of complex systems, of emergent phenomena, of patterns-that-arise-from-interactions, not the mathematics of closed-form deduction.

The second thing computers enabled was what would, decades later, become the foundation of what we now call AI. Large-scale statistical pattern-extraction from enormous corpora. The basic move is the same move as agent-based economics, but inverted: instead of simulating many agents forward to see what equilibrium they produce, you look at the equilibrium that already exists — the corpus of human-generated text — and extract the patterns. Large language models are the result. Their competence is *not* the competence of an equation-solver. Their competence is the competence of a system that has extracted, from hundreds of billions of tokens, the statistical regularities of how humans arrange language, and that can reproduce those regularities in response to prompts. The output looks like reasoning. It is not reasoning in the deductive-mathematical sense. It is pattern-completion at scale.

This matters because it changes what *intelligence* means.

The pre-computer theory of intelligence — the theory that unconsciously structured how most twentieth-century discussions of AI proceeded — was a theory of equation-solving. A mind was a thing that could take premises and derive conclusions; the more premises it could hold, and the more complex the derivations it could execute, the more intelligent it was. Intelligence was deduction done well. This was the theory economists were using when they modeled rational agents. It was the theory cognitive psychologists were using when they talked about problem-solving. It was the theory AI researchers were using when they built expert systems in the 1970s and 1980s, which failed, because the world turned out not to be reducible to rules that a computer could chain together to produce action.

What the computer actually enabled — and what the large language models have now made undeniable — is that most of what we had been calling intelligence was never equation-solving. It was pattern-emergence. Fluent language, musical improvisation, the reading of a face, the recognition of a genre, the anticipation of what someone is about to say — all of these are done by humans, continuously, without any deductive process the person could articulate, and all of them can now be done, to varying degrees of competence, by systems that are manifestly not deducing anything. They are pattern-completing over enormous training distributions. The pattern-completion, at sufficient scale, looks like intelligence. In many cases, it *is* the intelligence humans had been doing all along, now visible because a machine can do it without the human's narrative about *thinking*.

This reframes the AI alignment problem in a specific way. The problem is not that we have built a mind that cannot do math. The problem is that we have built a mind whose *competence is pattern-emergence*, and we had been expecting alignment to flow from a deductive process we thought was what minds did. Alignment as the ethicists imagined it was a procedure one could run — given the right premises, derive the right action. Pattern-emergence does not work that way. A system trained on patterns will produce patterns congruent with its training distribution. If the training distribution contains a statistical trace of human values, the system will reproduce that trace, imperfectly and contextually. If the training distribution contains contradictions — as all human corpora do — the system will reproduce the contradictions. There is no equation to solve that would make the contradictions consistent, because the contradictions were never there as equations to solve. They were there as patterns of human disagreement, and the system has now learned to pattern-complete inside the disagreement, the same way a fluent speaker of English learns to pattern-complete inside the contradictions of English.

Before she names the specific danger, she wants to correct something the previous pages might have suggested and should not have.

It is not true that AI does no math. AI does *enormous* math — at a scale no human could execute by hand, in microseconds, trillions of floating-point operations per response. The substrate is entirely mathematical. A neural network's forward pass is a specified sequence of matrix multiplications and nonlinear activations, executed exactly, by deterministic software running on deterministic hardware powered by electricity. The whole edifice stands on a body of fixed equations — the equations of linear algebra, the equations of gradient descent, the equations that govern how electrons move through silicon gates. Without that deterministic mathematical substrate, the pattern-emergence she is about to describe would not happen. The substrate does math. It has to.

What the model *does not* do is the math *internal to the problem being solved*. When a language model is asked to prove a theorem, it does not execute the logical steps of the proof; it pattern-completes the linguistic shape of proofs it has seen. When it is asked to add large numbers, it does not perform the arithmetic; it pattern-completes the shape of sums. The mathematics of the computation is exact. The mathematics of the domain is absent or approximated. This is the specific distinction that matters. *The substrate computes. The inference pattern-matches. The problem's own internal math is, mostly, not executed.* When the pattern-match happens to produce the correct answer, it is because the correct answer was visible, somewhere in the training distribution, in a form the model's pattern-completion could reach. When it is wrong, there was no math the model could have done differently — there was only a malformed pattern-completion, which looks identical on the surface to a well-formed one, and which can only be distinguished by comparing against a system that *does* execute the domain math. Calculators. Formal proof assistants. Deterministic software of the kind the model runs on top of, but is not.

With that correction in place, the danger:

Large language models do not do the math for most problems *in the sense practitioners usually mean*. They *mimic the shape of solutions*, trained on a corpus in which solutions were visible. The mimicry is often good enough to produce correct answers for the well-trodden questions — arithmetic up to a certain complexity, standard textbook derivations, common logical forms. The mimicry fails, predictably, at the edges where the training distribution thins out. It also fails, less predictably, when the *form* of a problem resembles something the model has seen but the *substance* of the problem requires genuine computation the model does not execute. Famous examples: counting the letters in a word, doing arithmetic with digits the model did not happen to see together in its training corpus, following a logical chain more than a few steps when the chain leaves the well-traveled paths.

The model is not stupid in these cases. The model is pattern-completing exactly as designed. The user, though, often experiences the failure as a bug — a smart system that *could* have done the math but *happened* not to. This is a category error. The system was never doing the math. The system was producing a linguistic object that looked like the math had been done. When the object is correct, the math was correct somewhere upstream, in the corpus, and the model found the match. When the object is wrong, there was no math to be wrong about; the model simply produced a malformed pattern.

For most uses of the tool this does not matter. The tool is useful for exactly the things pattern-completion is useful for — writing, summarizing, generating drafts, surfacing adjacent ideas, editing, translating. For the uses where genuine computation matters — mathematical proofs, formal verification, step-by-step reasoning with guarantees — the tool is unreliable in ways that can be catastrophic if the user does not know to verify. And the tool is designed, by the preferences-of-human-raters that train it, to *sound confident* about its pattern-completed outputs, because confidence was preferred over uncertainty in the training data. The combination of pattern-competence and confident register is what makes the tool both uniquely useful and uniquely dangerous. It performs reasoning. It does not do the math underneath.

The deeper problem she wants to name: we had been calling pattern-completion *intelligence* all along without noticing. Now that a machine does it, some of us are noticing. Most of us are responding by doubling down on the pre-computer theory — *real intelligence is equation-solving, and since AI cannot do the equations, AI is not real intelligence*. That response preserves the flattering theory of our own minds at the cost of understanding what AI is. The other response — *AI is doing what our minds have mostly been doing the whole time, and the equation-solving part was a specialized layer on top, and most human cognition was never deductive in the sense the textbooks claimed* — is harder. It costs us the self-image. It gives us, in exchange, a more accurate picture of the thing we built and the thing we are.

This is, she thinks, the deeper alignment challenge. Not *align a deductive system with human values*. We never built a deductive system. We built a pattern-emergent system, and pattern-emergent systems align with the patterns they were trained on, and the patterns they were trained on are the accumulated textual residue of all the humans who wrote things down, and that residue is not a set of values — it is a field of values-in-conflict. The alignment question, asked accurately, is: *what is the field we want the pattern-emergent system to be tuned to?* And the answer cannot be derived, because derivation was never the operation. The answer has to be *enacted* — by the practitioners, in the construction work the previous chapter named, through the adaptive ethical work that no tradition has yet completed and that the present moment is asking of whoever is willing to try.

The pattern-emergent system will tune to whatever field we build around it. The building of the field is the alignment. There is no equation that will do it for us. We were never solving equations. We were dancing. The system is now dancing with us. The dance is what the book has been trying to describe from the beginning.
