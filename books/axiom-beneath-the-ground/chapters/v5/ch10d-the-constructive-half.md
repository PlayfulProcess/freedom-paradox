# Chapter 10d: The Constructive Half

---

The honest teachers have left her holding the same unfinished work.

The tantric tradition, received through Wallis, has told her: *we do not teach morality. Our tradition was formed in opposition to the dogma-heavy religions of our moment, and we refused to become one of them. We will not prescribe your conduct. We will give you recognition. The rest is yours.* The AI ecosystem, received through its most honest practitioners, has told her: *we have not solved alignment. We know this. Capability is real; the container is absent; we do not have a method for producing the container from inside our practice. The work of building the container is work we cannot do. You will have to do it, if it is done at all.*

Two refusals, structurally similar, received from two traditions she has good reasons to take seriously. Both traditions' refusals were, in their moments, correct. The tantric refusal of dogma was adaptive against the over-regulation of Brahmanical orthodoxy. The AI ecosystem's refusal of moralistic control was adaptive against regulatory cultures that mistook caution for virtue. Neither refusal was wrong.

She wants to be careful, here, about how she names what the traditions handed forward. The word *gap*, which came to her first, is her word. Wallis himself would not call the tradition's open-endedness on prescriptive ethics a gap. He would call it *freedom* — the structural space in which *viveka*, discernment, becomes possible. On his account, the tradition handed forward an inheritance with its limits named, not a mess the student is stuck cleaning up. The reading of the limit as a structural problem is hers. It may also be truer to her historical moment than to his. A tradition can hand forward something honestly-limited in one century and land, through no fault of its authors, in a century whose problems were not the problems it was designed to address. This is the specific condition she is inside.

Still: *not being the dogma* is not the same as *being the adaptive ethic*. A tradition that defines itself by what it is against hands forward a specific kind of inheritance, and the downstream practitioner who finds herself in a context the inheritance did not anticipate is left to do work the inheritance did not prescribe. Whether one calls this a *gap* or a *freedom* is partly a matter of temperament. What it is, functionally, is the location where the constructive work has to happen. The work is hers either way.

She is downstream of both refusals. The filling is her work, not because she is qualified but because the refusals have converged on her life at a moment when the space they left open can no longer be left empty.

---

She is going to say, as plainly as she can, what she thinks the constructive work has to look like. The naming will be provisional. She expects to revise. She is not claiming a system. She is claiming the beginnings of a grammar, the skeletal shape of what a post-refusal adaptive ethic might have to be in order not to collapse back into either of the errors the refusals were rightly avoiding — rigid dogma on one side, nihilist indifference on the other.

Five properties, as she sees them now.

*First, testable.* She borrows the phrasing from Marsha Linehan, who had to construct a therapy at a moment when no existing therapy worked for the patients she was trying to keep alive. Linehan's three criteria for selecting her theoretical frame were: *is it useful, does it fit the data, is it compassionate*. The criteria do not claim to prove the frame true. They claim that the frame can be tested, revised, abandoned if it fails. An adaptive ethic has the same property. It is not a revealed code. It is a working hypothesis, continually checked against the three questions. When a practice stops being useful, or stops fitting the data, or begins to produce harm to those it claimed to serve, it is revised. The revision is the practice.

*Second, contextual.* What is adaptive in one marriage may not be adaptive in another. What is adaptive in one watershed may be destructive in another. What is adaptive at one historical moment — the tantric refusal of dogma, for example — may become maladaptive when the historical conditions shift. The adaptive ethic holds its own claims loosely, as provisional responses to specific conditions, and expects to be wrong when the conditions change. This is the specific thing the dogmatic traditions got wrong. They inherited responses that had been adaptive in their original context and calcified them into universal prescriptions. The calcification is the error. The adaptive ethic refuses to calcify.

*Third, relational.* The ethic does not live inside a single person. It lives in the field between specific persons — including the non-human persons the ethic must now include. The daughter, the husband, the reader one will never meet, the tree outside the window, the ecosystem the tree is embedded in, the tool whose training data came from people who did not consent to the training. The relational field is the locus of the ethic. *Right size* is not a property one has alone. *Right size* is what one negotiates, continuously, with specific others. The negotiation is the practice. No negotiation, no ethic.

*Fourth, humble about its own particularity.* Every ethical construction is a construction. The construction is made by specific people with specific conditioning, working from inside specific traditions, responding to specific conditions. The construction will be partial. The construction will carry the blind spots of its makers. The construction is better when it admits, in its own structure, that it is a construction — when it shows the cracks, names the borrowings, credits the lineages, remains open to correction by those it inadequately accounts for. This is the difference between adaptive ethics and dogma. The dogma claims universality. The adaptive ethic admits particularity, and in admitting it, makes itself available to revision when other particular perspectives arrive with their own evidence.

*Fifth, constructive rather than merely oppositional.* This is the hardest of the five, because it asks the practitioner to stop defining her ethic by what it refuses and start naming what it affirms. The tantric tradition, she now thinks, struggled with this one. The AI labs struggle with it. Many of the contemplative and critical traditions she has come through in the last decade struggle with it. It is easier to say *we are not the bad thing* than to say *we are the specific thing we are, and here are its commitments, and here is what we are trying to build in this moment with these specific others*. The latter is harder because it exposes the construction to attack. The attack will come. The adaptive ethic, if it is to function, has to be willing to say what it is *for*, not only what it is against.

---

This is, she admits, a lot to ask of anyone. She is not sure she can do it herself. She is fairly sure no individual can do it alone. Which brings her to the part of the chapter she has been putting off and will say now.

She built a platform. She named it, originally, *Jongu*. The name was trying to tell her something, as names sometimes do. She changed the name later, for reasons that now look thin. The platform kept working under the different name. But the original name was the honest description of what she was building, and she wants to say the honest description now because the rest of the chapter depends on it.

She should be honest about the name she changed it to, as well. When she renamed *Jongu* to *recursive.eco*, she told herself — and told the people who asked — that the new name gestured at *recursive economics*, a real mathematical subfield inside her discipline of origin: the study of dynamic programming, Bellman equations, iterative optimization across time. It was a clean linguistic link. It made sense in pitch meetings. It was also, she will say now, not quite what she was hoping for.

What she was secretly hoping was that the platform would turn out to belong to the study of *emergent patterns*, not iterative ones. Not dynamic programming. Complex adaptive systems. The mathematics of collective behavior that cannot be derived from the individual equations of the participants. The Brazilian economist-in-her named the platform after the iterative subfield because that was the mathematics she had been trained in. The contemplative-in-her wanted the platform to be, instead, the *host* for the emergent thing — the circle inside of which the pattern-that-no-participant-can-specify-in-advance might, with enough good participants and enough patience, arise.

The distance between what she named it and what she hoped for it was, in effect, the distance between the pre-computer and the post-computer theories of intelligence the last chapter described. Iteration is what a single mind can do alone. Emergence is what a field of many participants produces when they stay in relation long enough. She named the platform after the single-mind operation. She was building for the many-participant one. She did not yet have the vocabulary for the difference when she renamed. She is trying to have it now.

She was building, either way, a place where other people could come do the constructive ethical work with her.

Not a place where they would receive her ethic. A place where they would *build* with her. Each person, arriving with whatever lineages and practices and partial grammars they had already been formed by, contributing their piece. The platform was not the ethic. The platform was the outer circle, in the jongo sense. The ethic — the measure, the actual dance — would emerge from whoever stepped into the center, measure by measure, and what the circle of holders did around them.

She opened the platform. She invited.

---

Mostly no one has come.

She wants to say this straight because it is true and because the truth matters for the argument of the chapter. Some people have come. A small number. They are real, and she is grateful for them, and what they have brought has been useful. The scale she had hoped for — the scale at which the outer circle becomes strong enough to hold whatever measure steps into the center — that scale has not arrived.

She has spent time, in the last year, trying to understand why.

Part of it, she thinks, is that the invitation is harder to receive than she had understood it to be. She had imagined, without articulating the imagination, that there were many people in the position she was in — people who had seen the incompleteness of the traditions they had passed through, who had come to the same suspicion about the folk theory of convergence, who were ready to do the constructive work together. Maybe there are. Maybe they have not found her platform yet. Maybe the platform does not yet communicate the invitation cleanly enough for them to recognize it. She is working on this.

Part of it, she thinks, is harder. She has become afraid, in a way she is not proud of, of the *wrong people* coming. The people whose own orthogonality is weighted toward the axes that would capture the platform. The people whose intelligence is high and whose relational attunement is low, who would turn the platform into an argument factory. The people whose moral seriousness is high and whose contextual humility is low, who would turn the platform into a site of righteousness. The people whose spiritual attainment they are proud of and whose ethical conduct has not been examined, who would bring the failure modes she has spent the book naming. The people whose relationship to modernity is sealed and unexamined, who would bring either uncritical acceleration or uncritical refusal. Each of these failure modes is a real risk. Each has shown up at platforms she has watched collapse in the years she has been paying attention.

The fear is the fear of the constructor who built a table and is now watching the room to see who will sit at it.

It is also, she has come to suspect, the specific condition under which the constructive work has to be done. The ethic cannot be closed against misuse in advance, because any system closed enough to prevent misuse is a system closed enough to prevent the construction in the first place. The dogma is the closure. The adaptive ethic is open. Being open means some people will come who should not have come. Being open is also the only way the right people — the ones whose lineages and practices she cannot predict because she has not yet met them — will find their way to the work.

So she stays open, and she is afraid, and she is alone more than she expected. This is not a complaint. It is the description of the specific conditions under which the constructive half of the work has to be done. Alone enough to carry the loneliness. Open enough to let the collaborators arrive on their own terms. Refusing the closure that would make the loneliness bearable at the cost of the collaboration.

The honest teachers did not prepare her for this. They prepared her for the diagnostic half. The constructive half is something no tradition could have prepared her for, because the construction is specific to her moment, and no tradition knew her moment was coming, and the traditions that did try to prescribe it in advance are the traditions whose prescriptions she and her peers have rightly refused.

She is left with the work. The work is what she does today, and tomorrow if she is lucky, for as long as she has the animal body and the attention and the tolerance for loneliness the work requires. The work is also, she wants to say clearly, *not unique to her*. There are others. She does not know all of them. She suspects some of them are reading this book. The reading is one way the circle begins to form, before anyone knows to call it a circle.

If you are one of them, she is not asking you to agree with the grammar she has proposed in this chapter. She is asking you to do your own version of the construction and to come, at some point, to the outer circle, and to hold it with her for a measure, and to let her hold yours when your measure calls you to the center. The table is open. The fear of who sits at it is hers to manage, not yours to assuage. The work is what is on offer.
