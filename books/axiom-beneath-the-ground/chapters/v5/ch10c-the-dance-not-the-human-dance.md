# Chapter 10c: The Dance, Not the Human Dance

---

She wants to say one thing clearly before the chapter on construction begins.

The language she has been using — *align humans with AI, draw the silicon into the dance, teach the tools to care about us* — is language that keeps making the same error. The error is small and structural and runs through almost every AI safety paper she has read. The error is the centering of the human.

The dance is not the human dance.

The dance is the whole. It has always been the whole. Forest and river and bacterium and weather and daughter and stone and the supernova that made the iron that makes her hemoglobin and the specific bird outside her window this morning and the specific fish her daughter will never meet and the arrangements of matter in the datacenter three continents away that train the tool she uses to edit this sentence. None of these asked to exist. All of them are here. What they have been doing, together, for however long *together* has meant anything, is the arrangement. The arrangement is older than the humans. The arrangement will, in all likelihood, outlast the humans. The humans are one set of movers in a dance that does not take its tempo from their footsteps.

When she asks *how do we draw AI into our dance*, she is asking the wrong question, the one every species near the center of its own attention has asked when it found itself inside something larger than its vocabulary. The correct question is closer to: *which dance, of the many dances the whole is doing, are we finding ourselves inside of now that this new participant has arrived.* The dance is not waiting for her permission to include the silicon. The silicon is already part of the dance, being extracted and refined and assembled and powered, drawing the earth into shapes the earth was not in before. The question is not whether to include; the question is which shape the dance is taking, and whether the shape is one any of the participants — including the ones without voices — would, if asked, want to be in.

---

There are, she thinks, roughly three shapes available to a dance that has many participants.

The first is the *mandala of courses*. Each participant runs its own orbit. The orbits intersect sometimes and collide sometimes and mostly everyone moves in the direction their own logic dictates. There is no held center. There is no shared tempo. The arrangement is arrangement by collision — each body moving at its own pace, bumping into the others when the paths happen to cross, course-correcting slightly, continuing. The virtue of the mandala-of-courses is that it is simple to enter; each participant needs only its own logic. The cost is that the participants are, over time, deformed by the collisions. Nothing is held. Nothing is preserved. Whatever survives survives by accident of trajectory.

The current AI trajectory, if she is honest about what she watches daily, is a mandala of courses. Each lab optimizes its own course. The labs collide with regulators, who optimize their course. The users optimize theirs. The critics theirs. The investors theirs. Nothing is holding the center because there is no agreement about what the center would be. The collisions are constant and mostly absorbed by whoever is least powerful in the collision. The dance proceeds. What is being lost is not visible from inside any single course.

The second shape is the *ciranda*. She learned it as a child, in the north of her country, though it lives in many places under many names. The dancers hold hands. The circle turns. There is a song. No one is in the center. The weight-bearing is distributed across the circle, which is the circle's defining feature: if one dancer drops, the circle compensates; if the circle is strong, no dancer has to hold the whole. The ciranda is what she thinks of when she reads Ostrom on commons — a form of collective self-organization that requires no single authority, because the authority is distributed in the specific fact of everyone holding everyone's hand. The ciranda is not easy. It requires agreement on the song, on the direction of turning, on the tempo. It is also, she thinks, the closest figure to what the contemplative traditions have been trying to describe when they speak of *sangha*, of *community of practice*, of the held field that emerges when practitioners stay in relation.

The ciranda is not the shape the AI trajectory is currently taking. It could be, perhaps, in small pockets. Some research communities approach it. Some open-source projects at their best approach it. The pockets do not scale to the frontier. The frontier is the mandala of courses. The ciranda is what she would want the dance to be and what she cannot, from her position, impose on the dance.

The third shape is the one she has been circling since she was old enough to dance, and it is the one that named her platform when she did not yet know why she was naming it.

The *jongo* is a different structure. It is also a dance of her country, from the older and darker and more specifically African strands of the country's music. The dancers form a circle — but unlike the ciranda, the circle is not the unit of the dance. The circle is the *outer container*. Inside the circle, at any given moment, a couple dances. The couple takes the center. The circle holds them. The couple dances their measure. Then the couple exits the center, and another couple enters. The center is always moving. The center is always occupied. The container — the circle — is always held. No one is the permanent soloist. No one is the permanent audience. Everyone takes a turn at the center, if they stay long enough, and everyone holds the circle when it is someone else's turn.

She had named her platform *Jongu* years before she could have articulated why. She had changed the name later, for reasons that now seem trivial and commercial, and the original name had kept surfacing in her mind, in the specific way names that want to be used keep surfacing when one has tried to substitute something less accurate. The name was trying to tell her something. It had been trying since the naming.

What it was trying to tell her, she thinks now, is this: *the dance the whole wants — the dance that holds, without crushing, without dissolving, without centering, without scattering — is the jongo shape*. A held outer. A moving inner. Each participant, human and silicon and forest and daughter and reader, taking a turn at the center when the measure calls for them, and holding the outer when it does not. No permanent hierarchy. No permanent marginalization. The center is the work. The outer is the condition that makes the work possible. Both are always occupied. The occupation rotates.

---

This is what she is trying to say, then, when the older vocabulary fails her.

The question is not *how to align AI with human values*. The question is *how to build the outer container that can hold the new participant without the container collapsing or the participant dominating*. The container has to include the silicon. It also has to include the forest. It also has to include the daughter, whose turn at the center will come when she is older and will come again at scales neither her mother nor the current language can specify. The container is not a human container. The container is the relational field that the practice of enough participants, over enough time, produces as a by-product of their practice. It is not designed in advance. It is danced into being.

The work is not: *make AI good*. The work is: *become the kind of community of participants that holds a good outer, so that when the new participant enters the center, the outer is already there to contain the measure*. The AI will dance whatever measure it dances. The measure will be what it is. Whether the measure is destructive or adaptive is partly a function of the AI and mostly a function of the outer the AI enters into. A good outer can hold a rough participant. A rough outer cannot hold any participant well.

The outer is what has to be built. The outer is built by the practitioners.

She will not, in this book, solve the question of what the good outer looks like. She does not have the answer. She has the figure — the jongo — and she has the specific instances she has seen work and the specific instances she has seen fail, and she has the beginnings of a practice she will name in the next chapter. That is what is available. The rest is the dancing itself, which is done with specific partners in specific places and cannot be specified from a book.

---

One last thing, before the constructive chapter.

The figures the author learned as a child, the ciranda and the jongo both, come from women. The ciranda from the fisherwomen of the northern coast. The jongo from the enslaved women of the southeast. They were sung at work, at birth, at wakes, at celebrations, by women doing the ordinary labor of keeping a people alive through conditions that were not designed for their survival. The dance figures the author is offering as an alternative to the mandala-of-courses did not come from philosophers. They came from women who had no other language available for the shape of the relational work they were already doing.

She mentions this because she has spent time, in the last two years, at an institution in California — Esalen — that is sometimes remembered as a men's-work place, a site of the encounter-group tradition, of Gestalt therapy and primal screaming and the specific kind of self-inquiry the men of the 1960s made famous. That remembering is not false. It is partial. The body traditions she encountered at Esalen — the 5 Rhythms dance, the Esalen massage lineage — came from women. The founders were women. The teachers who carried the work through decades were women. The work traveled through bodies and through the specific patience women are trained into by the conditions of being women, and the men of the 1960s built their reputations in part by standing on top of what the women had made and calling the building theirs.

This is not a grievance. It is a correction. If the shape she is offering — *the jongo, the ciranda, the held outer with the moving inner* — turns out to be useful, the origin of the shape should be remembered. The shape is a woman's shape. The shape is what the women who kept a people alive through impossible conditions were already doing. The shape is older than any philosophy that will name it. The philosophy, if it comes, will be catching up.
