# Chapter 10a: The Four Orthogonalities

---

She had not believed that intelligence was the thing that would do it.

She had grown up in a country where some of the most intelligent people she knew were monstrous, and some of the most decent were not brilliant, and the mapping between the two registers was visibly loose to anyone who looked. She had watched intelligent men at Goldman Sachs construct elegant models that funded extractive industries. She had watched intelligent professors argue, with the precision one associates with well-trained minds, for positions that produced harm when enacted. Whatever it was that made a person reliably good, she had decided early, was not the same as whatever made a person reliably sharp. She did not need the AI safety literature to tell her that intelligence and goodness were separable. Her own twenties had taught her.

What she had believed — and what the retreat took from her with specific force — was a different version of the same folk theory. The version that said: *spiritual attainment will do what intelligence cannot*. Sit long enough. Recognize deeply enough. Arrive at the clear seeing the traditions promise. The clear seeing, once arrived at, would do the work intelligence had failed to do. The disagreements would dissolve. The selfishness would thin. The alignment between her values and her conduct, between her conduct and the world she wanted to live in, between her world and the world the others around her were trying to live in — that alignment would emerge from the depth of the practice. This was the quiet promise she had been reading in all of the spiritual literature since her twenties. She had never articulated it as a theorem. She had held it anyway, the way one holds beliefs one has never been asked to defend.

The retreat did not disabuse her of the practice. The practice, on its own terms, was what it said it was. Recognition was real. The body did what the tradition said it would do. The insights that arrived were not hallucinations; they were coherent, repeatable, congruent with what teachers she respected had described. The practice was not the problem.

The problem was that the practice, even fully received, *did not produce convergence*. She sat beside people who had practiced longer than she had, more deeply than she had, in some cases for decades, and who held political positions she found indefensible. She sat beside people whose recognition she could feel the quality of and who, in their ordinary relational lives, treated people badly. She sat beside people whose clarity about the deepest questions was striking and whose clarity about the middle-distance questions — how to raise a child, how to run a household, how to behave on a retreat with other humans who had not practiced as long — was unremarkable, sometimes worse.

She sat, in particular, with the teacher. Whose clarity she can attest to — though she wants to be honest that the superlatives that want to attach themselves to that clarity are hers, not his. He would redirect the credit upstream. The clarity, on his own account, is the tradition's; he is transmitting what he has received. She will, in what follows, try to speak of his clarity without the guru-flattery that would be the first failure mode of the chapter. What she can say cleanly is this: his clarity is among the more useful pieces of transmission she has received, in a field where useful transmission is rarer than the field's volume of output would suggest. And his clarity, she came to see over the course of the retreat and the year that followed, *did not transmit to his students the way she had expected it would*.

---

*Wallis is* — she will name him, because the point requires it — *the specific case*.

She reads him, and listens to him, and feels the quality of a mind that has done the work and can name what it has done with precision most teachers cannot match. She trusts him in the narrow sense of trusting a cartographer: the maps are good, the scale is honest, the places marked on the maps are places that exist. The trust is earned.

And yet. His admirers misread him. His detractors object to things he has not said. His students follow the letter and miss the substance. His fellow practitioners, in lineages running parallel to his, encounter the same ground and name it differently and disagree about the naming. The disagreements are not failures of his clarity. He has been clear. The disagreements are the native state of reception — what always happens when a specific mind communicates with other specific minds who bring their own conditioning, their own translation errors, their own prior commitments, their own cultural grammars to the encounter.

The clarity is his. The reception is contingent. The clarity does not carry anyone across to where he stands. It only shows, from outside, that someone is standing somewhere specific, and that the standing is real, and that the transmission is not automatic.

He knows this. This is the part that matters.

He is unusually willing to say, about his own tradition, the thing most teachers do not say: *the tradition does not prescribe a moral code. Awakening by itself does not guarantee ethical conduct. Practice gives capacities; whether those capacities become goodness depends on the separate work the tradition calls liberation — the slow dissolution of what was conditioned — and that work is not supplied by recognition alone.* This is the distinction between *bodha* and *mokṣa*, between waking up and becoming free. He has been plain about it in interviews. He has written it down. In one of his AMA sessions he says, of teachers who behave badly: *you should assume that anyone might fall victim to spiritualized ego at any time*. He does not pretend the recognition the tradition offers is the same as the moral development it does not automatically produce. He names the orthogonality explicitly, in Sanskrit-inflected English, in a voice whose carefulness is the reason to take him seriously.

He goes further — though more carefully than the shorthand version of the move would suggest. He will say that the map is a figure of speech pointing at an aspect of the already-existent nature of reality. He will say that recognition can arrive without the full theoretical apparatus, and that the categories are *upāya*, skillful means, not ontological commitments. What he will not say is that the teachings can be dropped once essence is encountered. What he will say is that the teachings should be held *as pointers rather than possessions* — and that most of a lifetime's practice lives *after* recognition, in the liberation work that recognition does not do for you. The folk misreading of tantric recognition — *I have arrived, I can set down the scaffolding* — is the misreading he most consistently warns students against. The tradition is scaffolding the practitioner keeps using. The scaffolding is not the destination. The practice is the walking, and it does not end when the walker has glimpsed where walking is.

This is the diagnostic half of the problem, said more cleanly than she has heard it said anywhere else, by a teacher inside a living lineage, not by a Western rationalist outside one. It is Bostrom's orthogonality thesis translated into Sanskrit, except that Wallis was saying it before she knew Bostrom's version existed. Capability and value are separable. Intelligence and morality are separable. Recognition and relational wholeness are separable. The thing the practice gives — whether the practice is computation or contemplation — is not the thing that makes the user of it reliably good. The axis of attainment and the axis of ethics are not the same axis.

---

Once she had seen this plainly, she could not un-see it at any of the scales she had been trying to live at.

She saw it in intelligence, which she had never believed would do it. The rationalist communities she had lurked in had their own version of the folk theory — *enough intelligence, enough rationality, enough Bayesian updating, and the right answers become visible and shared* — and the communities themselves produced exactly the disagreements the folk theory predicted would dissolve. Fissures over priorities. Fissures over values. Fissures over interpretations of the same evidence. Nothing resolved by additional reasoning power. The people in those communities were not stupid. The intelligence was real. The convergence was not there.

She saw it in enlightenment, which was the axis the retreat had taken from her. The tradition she had walked into was full of people whose practice she could feel and whose conduct she could observe and who, on inspection, were not converging. Different schools of the same lineage disagreed on what the recognition required behaviorally. Teachers inside the same school disagreed with each other. The practitioners who had sat the longest did not obviously treat other humans better than the practitioners who had sat less. Whatever the practice was producing, it was not moral development as a dependent variable.

She saw it in moral seriousness, which she had not previously thought of as an axis but which she now had to admit was one. She had known moral philosophers whose ethical conduct was ordinary at best. She had known moral seriousness that manifested as rigidity, as the inability to stay in relation with people whose moral judgments differed, as a kind of righteousness that was structurally incompatible with the relational work ethics is supposed to be in service of. Being morally serious, in the sense that one thinks carefully about ethics, did not produce relational wholeness. It sometimes produced the opposite.

She saw it in relational attunement, which she had held in highest regard because of her MSW training and the therapists and mothers she had learned from. Relational attunement is a real skill; she would not concede otherwise. People who have it can do things with nervous systems that people who do not have it cannot do. And — *and* — she had known people with extraordinary relational attunement whose intellectual lives were thin, whose spiritual lives were absent, whose engagement with the structural problems of their time was nonexistent. The attunement was genuine. It did not carry them to the other axes.

Four axes, then, none of which predicted the others. Intelligence, enlightenment, moral seriousness, relational attunement. Each naming a real thing. Each, when the folk theory of its tradition is tested against the available evidence, *failing to produce convergence on the other three*. The hope that one axis could carry the others was the hope every tradition had been selling to its practitioners, because the alternative was to admit that the full picture required integration work the tradition could not do from inside itself.

---

Stepping back, the shape is this.

A person of high intelligence and low relational attunement is a recognizable figure. Often a respected one, in professional life. The person builds systems, writes papers, leads companies, and their colleagues quietly manage around the relational gaps. The intelligence does its work; the gaps do theirs. No one expects the intelligence to close the gaps, because intelligence is not what closes relational gaps. The folk theory would predict that enough intelligence should eventually produce relational insight. It does not. The axes are separate.

A person of high enlightenment-attainment and low moral development is also a recognizable figure. The tradition has a word for them, in its uncomfortable moments. The guru who can transmit recognition and cannot be trusted around money, or women, or students. The meditation teacher whose retreats change lives and whose private conduct destroys the lives in front of him. The shape is not rare. The tradition, when pressed, concedes that the recognition and the conduct are not the same axis.

A person of high moral conviction and low relational skill is perhaps the most familiar figure in contemporary life. The activist who is correct and cannot maintain friendships across difference. The ethicist who can specify what is wrong and cannot repair the rupture they produce by specifying it. The moral conviction is not fake. It is also not what repair requires. Repair requires the attunement the moral axis does not supply.

A person of high relational attunement and low intellectual rigor is a figure the therapeutic world knows, though it does not usually name out loud. The skilled holder of other people who cannot think their way through the structural questions their clients' lives are embedded in, and whose helping, at certain scales, becomes a kind of tender misdirection. The attunement is real. The thinking is not what the attunement is.

Each of these figures is the foreground of one axis and the absence of another. Each is common. Each is, within their own subculture, often celebrated for the axis they embody. None of them is wrong for having the axis they have. The wrongness is in the folk theory that says the one axis would, at high enough dosage, supply the others. It will not. The axes are independent.

What the book is trying to name is this: *the folk theory of convergence is always the folk theory of the axis one is strongest on*. The intelligent person believes intelligence would do it. The contemplative believes enlightenment would do it. The moral philosopher believes moral seriousness would do it. The therapist believes attunement would do it. Each of these beliefs is a self-flattering projection. Each dissolves on contact with the evidence the holder already has access to.

The honest path is not to pick an axis and maximize it. The honest path is to recognize that one is always going to be stronger on some axes and weaker on others, that no amount of work on the strong axis will substitute for the weak ones, and that the ethical life requires specific labor on each of them — labor the traditions one's strong axis belongs to will not supply, because no tradition has yet produced a full integration of all four. The integration is the constructive work the next chapters will try to name.

---

One more observation before the chapter closes.

The orthogonality thesis, as Wallis teaches it and as the AI literature names it, tends to be received as a pessimistic claim. *Intelligence does not imply goodness; therefore smart things are potentially bad; therefore be afraid.* The framing is available. The framing is also incomplete. The same thesis, turned toward the lives we are already inside of, is a kind of permission.

It is permission to stop expecting any single axis to carry the others. It is permission to stop feeling, when one's meditation practice fails to produce the relational wholeness one expected, that one must be practicing wrong. It is permission to stop feeling, when one's intellectual rigor fails to generate the moral insight one hoped for, that one needs to think harder. The failing is not one's own. The failing is in the theory. The theory was a folk theory. The axes are independent. The only way through is to work on each axis for what it is, to accept the gaps between them as structural rather than remediable by more of the axis one happens to be on, and to stay inside the relational work that no axis on its own can close.

This is what the retreat left her with. She had come expecting the recognition to do the integration. The recognition did not. It did what it did, cleanly, and left the rest to her. The rest is what the book is about.
