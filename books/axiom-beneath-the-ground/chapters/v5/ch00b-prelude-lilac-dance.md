# Prelude: The Lilac Dance

---

About two years after the night in the wooden house, and about two years before the retreat that named the Goddess, she had a dream.

She was watching an aerial dancer. The dancer was performing on lilac silks — *tecidos lilás* — long panels of fabric suspended from some invisible rigging, which the dancer climbed and wrapped and fell through and caught herself inside. The dance was spiraling and weaving. The color of the silks was a specific shade of violet-lilac that she would, later, recognize as the color she had already been using for her own things — her writing, her small websites, the logo she had sketched years earlier for an LLC she had named without quite knowing why. The dream color and her brand color were the same color. She did not know, in the dream, that she had already been circling this.

She watched the dancer with a delight she had not felt in waking life for some time. The dancer was so beautiful, and the dance was so fully *embodied*, that watching it was itself a kind of participation. Then, in the way dreams do, the watching dissolved into the being: she *became* the dancer. She was the one weaving the silks. She was the one spiraling. The delight doubled. She was doing the beautiful thing and also being the beautiful thing, and the fabric moved with her and held her and she felt, for what seemed a long time, the pure joy of embodied grace.

Then a thought arrived.

The thought was: *how cool I am*.

The thought was not elaborate. It was not malicious. It was a small movement of self-congratulation — the kind that would have passed unnoticed in waking life, absorbed into the stream of ordinary thinking. In the dream, the thought had consequences.

The silks changed. They did not tear or unravel. They *turned*. The lilac fabric, which had been her partner in the dance, became a serpent — thick, muscular, the same color as the silks had been, now alive and coiled. The serpent closed around her. Not to kill her, but to *mark* her. She felt heat. She felt the serpent constricting with a specific intent, not of destruction but of teaching. She felt herself falling — the rigging was no longer holding her — and as she fell she felt burn marks appearing on her body where the serpent had touched. Small, specific, patterned burns. She landed on the ground. The ground was solid. The dance was over. The marks would stay.

She woke up.

She lay in the dark for a long time. The dream had been vivid in the way some dreams are — not the fragmentary dreams of ordinary sleep, but the ones that seem to arrive from outside the dreamer, delivering a specific lesson the dreamer has been, without knowing it, ready to receive. She did not yet know what the lesson was. She knew that the beauty had been real and that the self-congratulation had been the thing that turned the dance against her. She knew the burn marks were the body's record of what the mind had not yet been able to learn.

She did not know, that night, that she was receiving a classical teaching the tradition she would much later walk into has named in several ways. The serpent is the kundalinī — the coiled energy that can, if raised with care, carry a practitioner into recognition, and that can, if raised with carelessness or self-inflation, burn the practitioner from inside. The tradition warns against the exact thought that arrived in the dream. The tradition has been warning against it for a thousand years. She did not know the tradition then. The dream, apparently, knew what it was doing.

---

She had, before the dream, been trying to name the shape of her practice.

She had started a small website — not a professional one, a personal one — where she wrote short pieces about whatever she was sitting with at the time. She had called it *Life is Process*. The name had felt true when she chose it. It was meant to be a refusal of the static categories she had grown up inside — the Catholic inheritance that categorized you as saved or fallen, the professional inheritance that categorized you as successful or failing, the contemporary inheritance that categorized you as healthy or anxious, self-actualized or lost. Against all of these: *life is process*. Not a state. A verb.

But after the dream, the name began to feel wrong to her. It was too declarative. *Life IS process* was the form of a claim, and claims had a way, she had begun to notice, of becoming the next static category even when they had started as refusals of static categories. She did not want to stand on a slogan. She wanted the writing to *do* what the slogan said, not to say it.

She remembered the dream. Specifically, she remembered the color — the lilac, which was the same color she had used, years before, for the little LLC she had set up to hold her consulting work. The LLC had a name she had chosen before she had any of this vocabulary. The name was *PlayfulProcess*. She had chosen it because it named something she had always trusted in her own work — that the serious thing and the playful thing were not opposites, that the quality of her best thinking was a quality of lightness rather than grimness, and that the opposite of being-correct was not being-foolish but being-stiff.

She renamed the website. *Life is Process* became *PlayfulProcess*. The change was small on paper. In her body it registered as significant. The name was no longer a claim. The name was a *mode* — the playful way of being inside the process, which was the only way she had ever been able to sustain the process over time. The playfulness was not optional decoration. The playfulness was the technique by which she did not harden into the slogan's next victim.

---

She did not realize, then, that *PlayfulProcess* was pointing at something a tradition she had not yet walked into had already named with precision.

The word, in the Śaiva tantric tradition she would later meet through Wallis, is *līlā*. It names the play of consciousness — the divine, unforced, self-delighting movement by which the one consciousness manifests as the whole varied world without any strategic purpose beyond the playing. *Līlā* is not decoration on top of seriousness. *Līlā* is the fundamental operation. The cosmos is not a plan being executed; the cosmos is a play being played. The practitioner's work is not to escape the play; the practitioner's work is to join it knowingly, without the mistake the dreamer in the silks had made — the mistake of thinking she was the one doing the dancing rather than one of the places the dance was happening.

She would, years later, read this in Sanskrit-inflected English and feel the recognition arrive in a specific way. *PlayfulProcess* was *līlā*. Not as derivation — she had not read the tradition when she named the company — but as *convergence*. She had arrived at the word by one route; the tradition had arrived at it by another; they met without either having pointed the other there.

This was by then the second such convergence. The first had been Paradevi. Both convergences had the same shape: she had been walking through doorways whose names she did not yet know, arriving at a tradition that turned out to already have the names for what she had been finding. The pattern was specific enough, and had repeated often enough, that she had stopped experiencing it as coincidence. She did not know what to call it, exactly. She had begun, quietly, to trust it.

---

About two years after the lilac dream, she went to a retreat in Ojai.

The retreat was held at the Krishnamurti Foundation — the organization that stewards the legacy of Jiddu Krishnamurti, the twentieth-century teacher who had refused to become a guru, had dissolved the Theosophical order that had been built around him, and had spent decades instead in public dialogues with scientists, educators, and other inquirers. The retreat she attended was not led by the Foundation's central figures; it was led by people on the periphery who had been quietly developing the *dialogue* modality Krishnamurti had developed with the physicist David Bohm in the 1980s. The dialogue modality is peculiar in that it has no teacher and no subject. A circle of people sits together and observes the movement of thought — their own thought, collectively, without assigning it to particular speakers in the conventional way. The aim is not to conclude anything. The aim is to let thought watch itself, which is a thing thought does not ordinarily do.

At this retreat, sitting in those circles, she understood emptiness.

She does not want to overstate. She had read about emptiness. She had been sitting with contemplative texts for years. What happened at Ojai was not her first encounter with the concept; it was the first time the concept stopped being a concept. Sitting in the dialogue circle, watching her own thoughts arise and pass and arise again, it became unavoidable — the way a geometric fact becomes unavoidable when you finally see it — that the *thinker* behind the thoughts was not a separate entity watching the thoughts. The thinker was a thought like the others. There was no one at the center. There was thinking, happening, with no one doing it. When the retreat named this *emptiness*, the word fit the experience. Not the emptiness of absence. The emptiness of *no separate self to be full or empty*.

What struck her harder, though, was the second recognition, which arrived a day later, during a walk.

The emptiness was not *separate* from the rest of life. She had half-expected that she would need to leave something behind to rest in the emptiness — that the emptiness was a quiet room one entered by exiting the noisy room of ordinary existence. What she found, instead, was that the emptiness was *orthogonal* to the noisy room. It was pervading the noisy room the whole time. You did not need to leave samsara to find nirvana; nirvana was what samsara was doing when you were not distracted from noticing. The two could coexist because they had never been two in the first place. The traditions that had been saying this for two and a half thousand years turned out to have been saying something precise. She had been unable to hear the precision until the dialogue had, in its patient way, cleared the channel.

Hermann Hesse, in *Siddhartha* — a book she had read at twenty and had not fully understood — had put it in the final pages, when Govinda kisses the forehead of the aged Siddhartha and sees, in that instant, the face of his friend become every face, every being, every event, all at once, all one. What had felt, at twenty, like beautiful fiction now felt like a careful description of exactly the recognition she was sitting inside. *Sansāra* and *nirvāna*, Hesse had written, are the same word spoken from different sides of one silence. Siddhartha smiles. Govinda weeps. The book ends.

She remembered, walking the dirt paths of Ojai that afternoon, that Sam Harris had said something similar in an interview she had listened to recently — a conversation with Buddhist monks in which he had reported, with the specific carefulness of a person who has spent decades trying to say accurate things about inner experience, that since he had recognized the still point he had entered samsara more fully, not less. The recognition had not removed him from the world. The recognition had let him be *in* the world without the defensive crouch. She had felt, listening, that Sam was describing the Siddhartha arc in a modern vernacular — a man who had spent his twenties searching for a way out of the noise, had found, eventually, that the way out was the way in, and was now, decades later, reporting back from the inside. The still point had been the doorway. The doorway had opened, paradoxically, onto the street he had been trying to leave.

---

One more thing happened at that retreat which she wants to report because it has shaped her practice since.

She had gotten into the habit, during the retreat, of walking alone in the mornings, before the circles began. The retreat was in the hills above Ojai; the paths were unmarked, the mountains were quiet, the air was the specific quality of Southern California air in the dry season. She had been, during that period, struggling with a decision — she cannot remember now which one; there had been a cluster of them. On one of those morning walks, she put on a Krishnamurti talk through her headphones. The talk, as it happened, was about *choice*.

Krishnamurti's argument was that choice, in the sense modern people usually mean it — *I weigh options, I select, I exercise will* — is largely an illusion. The selecting is done by conditioning that precedes the apparent selector. What is usually called *choice* is the rationalization of an action that had already, through millions of small prior conditions, been determined. The teaching was delivered, as Krishnamurti's teachings always were, in his specific spare register — patient, repetitive, uncompromising.

She listened, walked, listened. She became absorbed in the listening. At some point she realized she had not been paying attention to the path. She looked around. She did not recognize where she was. She tried to retrace her steps and found that she had taken turns she could not reconstruct. She was lost in the mountains above Ojai, with a Krishnamurti talk on choice still playing in her headphones, and with the inability to choose — which path back, which direction — enacting itself at the practical level in real time.

She was, eventually, two hours late back. The retreat had been waiting to begin. People had been mildly worried. She arrived, embarrassed, explaining what had happened. She told the hosts she had been listening to a talk on choice and had gotten lost. The hosts looked at each other. One of them laughed, the laugh of someone who had been in these mountains for a long time. *Krishnamurti*, she said, *said it was good to get lost*.

This was not consolation. It was description. The story the hosts were telling her, gently, without making too much of it, was that the getting-lost was the teaching. That the retreat's work had been, in that specific hour, transmitting itself to her through the mountain rather than through the dialogue circle. She had been given a practical demonstration of what the talk had been saying abstractly. She could not, through deliberation, have arranged to be the person who got lost in the mountains while listening to a teaching on choice. The arrangement had arranged itself. She had been taught.

She realized, on the plane home, that *getting lost* had always been her practice. She had been doing it for as long as she could remember. The retreats, the career shifts, the countries, the languages, the books she had picked up without knowing why, the strangers she had listened to without filtering — all of it had been a kind of sustained getting-lost, which had turned out, over the years, to be a form of being-found by the specific things that wanted to find her. She had been calling this *being open* or *being curious* or *trusting the process*. The retreat had given her another word for it. *Getting lost is a practice*. Some traditions name it as a method. Most modern cultures frame it as a failure. It was, she now knew, structurally how she had learned everything she had learned that was worth having.

This was why *Life is Process* had always felt to her like a statement of something she already knew. It was also why she had, after the dream, softened it to *PlayfulProcess*. The *playful* was the modifier that made the process livable over a lifetime. Without the playful, *process* becomes a grim discipline. With the playful, the process is what it was always meant to be — *līlā*, before she knew the word — the lightness inside the seriousness, the getting-lost that is also the being-found, the dance on the lilac silks that is grace as long as the dancer does not claim the grace.

---

The burn marks from the serpent did not go away in the dream, and she thinks they did not quite go away in the life either. The cautionary scar is part of what keeps her honest. The dance is available; the self-congratulation is the thing that turns the silks into the serpent. The practice is to keep dancing and to keep watching for the thought. She has not mastered the watching. She does not think anyone does. She only knows, now, that the dream was not a warning against dancing. It was a warning about *what kind of dancer* one can allow oneself to become, and what happens when one forgets.

The lilac is still her color. *PlayfulProcess* is still her name. The tradition she eventually walked into calls what she was already doing *līlā*. The retreat she went to in Ojai taught her that getting lost is the method. The ending of *Siddhartha* says, still, what it said at twenty, but now she can read it.

The Goddess had a second name after all. The first name was Paradevi. The second was the name the dream had always known. The names are not two names. They are one naming, delivered in different languages, to a woman who has been, in the specific way she has been doing it her whole life, getting slowly, patiently, usefully lost.
