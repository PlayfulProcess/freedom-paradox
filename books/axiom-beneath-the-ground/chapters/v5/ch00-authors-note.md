# Author's Note

---

> *I celebrate myself, and sing myself,*
> *And what I assume you shall assume,*
> *For every atom belonging to me as good belongs to you.*
>
> — Walt Whitman, *Song of Myself* §1 (*Leaves of Grass*, 1892 ed.; public domain)

---

A Tuesday morning. A park bench. My five-year-old running in and out of a sandbox. The voice in my earbuds was describing a kind of consciousness that is simultaneously beyond everything and pervading everything, and something in me said *yes* before I knew what I was saying yes to.

That *yes* is the reason this book exists. It arrived before any question I could name. It arrived as a pull — the oldest kind of wanting, the kind that moves before thought.

For years I had thought the pull was for something called value. I read the existentialists, the contemplatives, the therapists. I sat in rooms where people offered answers. None of them landed. What I eventually understood, long after the Tuesday on the bench, was that value was the moral question — *to whom should I direct what I have?* — and the pull beneath it was the spiritual one: *what is the "I" that directs at all, and is it even mine?* Value is a relative question. Freedom is an absolute one. They are not rungs on the same ladder. They are different floors.

---

I went to a retreat led by a teacher in the tradition of nondual Shaiva Tantra. I went because the wanting had become unbearable and I had run out of ways to hold it alone. What happened there was not a conversion. It was stranger. The practices worked — not as belief, but as something the body recognized even when the mind resisted.

The tradition I had stumbled into maps the self as nested layers. At the outermost: stuff — the world of things. Then body. Then heart-mind. Then life-force. Then a transcendent void. And at the center, consciousness itself — not a layer exactly, but the knowing that makes all the other layers knowable.

The Western philosopher I trusted most had given a name to what it takes to stand in the void: *courage*. The tradition I was now inside had a name for what lives at the center, beneath the void: *svātantrya* — autonomy, freedom. Not personal freedom. The freedom that is the nature of the whole field. I came to understand that the philosopher was describing the door and the tradition was describing the room. Courage is what you do when the ground has dissolved. Freedom is what the dissolved ground turns out to be made of.

I asked the teacher directly: are the teachings just a construct? The answer was five words this book circles: *It is, until you experience it.*

I have not fully experienced it. I may never. But something shifted, and the shift was not intellectual. The proof came on an ordinary evening: a system I had been building for months collapsed in an hour, and I put my daughter to sleep with grace. My husband noticed, because he knows this is not my default state. He also made a joke about the small image I had brought home for the bedroom altar — a joke that referenced a well-known documentary about a cult. I had heard the same reference from the teacher a few days earlier, who had named the problem with such documentaries: they make people stop searching, because one frame has consumed the whole category. My husband and the teacher had landed on the same punchline from opposite directions. They were both right.

---

Late in the retreat I asked the teacher a harder question. It was the question I had been carrying, in different forms, for most of my adult life. It concerned destruction — whether it matters, whether one life or one world leaving leaves a real hole, whether the whole notices.

He gave the answer the tradition gives. From the absolute view, nothing is lost, because nothing was ever separate to begin with. Consciousness does not suffer from the rearrangement of its forms.

And then, without softening it, he gave the other answer.

*There always is.*

There is always a hole.

Both answers are true. The book is the long form of learning to hold both — the absolute view that sees no loss, and the relational view that registers every loss as real. The tradition does not choose between them. It teaches the practitioner to live inside their simultaneity. That is harder than choosing. It is also more accurate to what a day actually feels like.

---

I keep returning to something else the teacher said about practice. It is not the temple that raises the energy. It is the open-hearted people who inhabit it. The form is neutral. What matters is who is in the room and whether their hearts are open or closed.

I need a container to open my heart. A community of open-hearted people is the best container I have ever experienced. But I am writing from the edge of that container — one foot in, one foot braced against the doorframe, the doubt still present, the body still settling.

This is not a book by someone who has arrived. It is a book by someone who cannot fully get rid of doubt and has chosen to write from inside it — and who found, while standing there, that freedom is not a property she owns but a field she is moving through, and that the only form of choice she has is the choice of how she relates to it.

---

A note on method. The frameworks in this book are asked three questions: is it useful? Does it fit the data? Is it compassionate? These are not my questions. They are Marsha Linehan's three criteria for selecting her biosocial theory of borderline personality disorder — that a theory must guide treatment implementation, engender compassion, and fit the research data. She used them to choose a single theory. I adopt them, at the same theory-selection level, as a filter for any framework this book engages. A clinician who built her way out of a condition she had suffered from arrived, through the discipline of staying alive, at a meta-criterion that maps onto a thousand-year-old contemplative tradition on the other side of the world. I use her filter because it is the most honest one I have found. If a framework in this book stops meeting the three tests, it should be revised. That includes, perhaps especially, the frameworks I am most attached to.

A note on voice. The rest of the book is written in close third person. A woman who is not quite me walks through the question. I appear only here, and briefly at the end. The point is not the person inside the experience. The point is the argument the experience carried. Let her carry it.
