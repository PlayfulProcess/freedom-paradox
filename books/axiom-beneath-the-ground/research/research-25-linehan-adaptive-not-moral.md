# Research 25 — Linehan on adaptive vs. moral framing

## Critical finding (affects book attribution)

The "useful / fits the data / compassionate" triad often attributed to Linehan as a clinical thought-evaluation filter is **not** that. Linehan's actual formulation is her **three criteria for selecting her own biosocial theory of BPD**. Her exact 2015 wording:

> "It had to **(1) guide treatment implementation, (2) engender compassion, and (3) fit the research data**." (*DBT Skills Training Manual*, 2nd ed., Guilford, 2015; also Linehan & Wilks, "The Course and Evolution of Dialectical Behavior Therapy," *American Journal of Psychotherapy* 69, no. 2 [2015], rephrased as the model must be "capable of guiding effective therapy, nonpejorative for the client, and compatible with current research data.")

Any account framing this triad as a clinical skill for evaluating cognitions misattributes a **metatheoretical** criterion (for choosing a theory) to a **clinical** technique.

**For the book:** the current Author's Note phrasing ("The frameworks in this book are asked three questions: is it useful? Does it fit the data? Is it compassionate?") is structurally accurate because it applies Linehan's criteria at the **theory-selection level**, which is exactly what she did. The book is not claiming to quote her clinical skill; it is adopting her meta-framework for choosing among competing theoretical frames. The attribution just needs to be made explicit: these are her criteria for choosing her biosocial theory of BPD, adopted here as a filter for the frameworks this book engages.

## Section 1 — The 1993 manual's structure and adaptive framing

*Cognitive-Behavioral Treatment of Borderline Personality Disorder* (Guilford, 1993, ISBN 0-89862-183-6). Most philosophically relevant chapters:
- **Ch. 2, "Dialectical and Biosocial Underpinnings of Treatment"** (p. 28)
- Ch. 3, "Behavioral Patterns: Dialectical Dilemmas" (p. 66)
- **Ch. 4, "Overview of Treatment: Targets, Strategies, and Assumptions in a Nutshell"** (p. 97)
- Ch. 7, "Dialectical Treatment Strategies" (p. 199)
- Chs. 8 and 9, validation and problem-solving core strategies (pp. 221, 250)

Adaptive-not-moral framing is well-grounded. BPD criterion behaviors, including self-harm and suicide attempts, are conceptualized as **attempts to regulate emotion** or natural consequences of emotion dysregulation — not character flaws. The biosocial theory was deliberately constructed to be **nonpejorative**.

## Section 2 — Dialectical stance

Chapter 2 frames dialectics around three propositions that recur verbatim in DBT training materials drawn from the 1993 text:
- Reality has **interrelatedness and wholeness** ("the whole is more than the sum of its parts")
- Reality is composed of **internal opposing forces whose synthesis evolves a new set of opposing forces**
- **The nature of reality is continuous change**

Clinical translation: **acceptance AND change** as the central tension — the dialectical heart of the treatment.

## Section 3 — Radical acceptance (location matters)

Canonical 2015 source: **Distress Tolerance Handouts on Reality Acceptance Skills, pp. 342–344** of *DBT Skills Training Handouts and Worksheets*, 2nd ed. (Guilford, 2015).

Most-quoted formulations — "Pain is pain. Suffering, agony, are pain plus non-acceptance. Radical acceptance transforms suffering into ordinary pain" and "Radical acceptance rests on letting go of the illusion of control and a willingness to notice and accept things as they are right now, without judging" — are from these handouts and from the 2021 memoir *Building a Life Worth Living*.

The 1993 book uses the term, but the most-quoted phrasings are **2015 onward** and should be cited accordingly.

## Section 4 — Nonjudgmental stance

The mindfulness "how" skills in the 2015 manual encode the stance directly:
- "Nonjudgmentally — see, but don't evaluate as good or bad. Just the facts"
- "Effectively — do what works"

(from Mindfulness Handout 5 and 5a; the 1993 versions appear in the companion *Skills Training Manual*.)

"Doing the best they can" + "need to do better" is the cleanest example of Linehan's substitution of dialectical framing for moral framing — neither blame nor excuse.

## Section 5 — The three filters: sourcing analysis

**Critical for the book's attribution.**

- **Is "useful / fits the data / compassionate" Linehan's verbatim clinical formulation?** No.
- **Are these three criteria present in Linehan's writing?** Yes — as her three criteria for **choosing her biosocial theory** of BPD.
- **Exact wording:** "(1) guide treatment implementation, (2) engender compassion, and (3) fit the research data" (2015 *DBT Skills Training Manual*); rephrased in Linehan & Wilks 2015 as the model must be "capable of guiding effective therapy, nonpejorative for the client, and compatible with current research data."
- **Is this a clinical skill for evaluating cognitions?** No. It is a **metatheoretical criterion** for theory selection.

The book's current Author's Note language — "The frameworks in this book are asked three questions: is it useful? Does it fit the data? Is it compassionate?" — is **correct** because it applies Linehan's criteria at the theory-selection level (to frameworks), which is exactly what she did. The attribution line should be sharpened: "These are Marsha Linehan's three criteria for selecting her biosocial theory of BPD. I adopt them as a filter for any framework this book engages."

## Section 6 — Biographical context

1961: age 17, hospitalized at the Institute of Living, Hartford CT. Experienced severe self-harm (cutting, burning, head-banging), repeated seclusion.

Made a vow in the Institute's chapel: to get herself out of hell and then go back in for others.

2011, June 23: disclosed her psychiatric history publicly for the first time, in the *New York Times* (Benedict Carey, "Expert on Mental Illness Reveals Her Own Fight"). Returned to the Institute of Living to speak.

2020: memoir *Building a Life Worth Living* (Random House).

## Section 7 — Assumptions about patients (Ch. 4, pp. 106–107)

The full list, verifiable to exact pages in the 1993 Guilford text:

1. "Patients Are Doing the Best They Can" (p. 106)
2. "Patients Want to Improve" (p. 106)
3. **"Patients Need to Do Better, Try Harder, and Be More Motivated to Change" (p. 106)** — the dialectical counterweight to 1 and 2, frequently dropped by popularizers
4. "Patients May Not Have Caused All of Their Own Problems, but They Have to Solve Them Anyway" (p. 107)
5. "The Lives of Suicidal, Borderline Individuals Are Unbearable as They Are Currently Being Lived"
6. (not separately verified here — typically about patients needing to learn new behaviors)
7. "When patients drop out of therapy, fail to progress, or actually get worse while in DBT, the therapy, the therapist, or both have failed."

**Any account citing Assumption 1 without Assumption 3 misrepresents the dialectic Linehan built in.** Both are needed.

## Section 8 — Hegel and Zen as cited sources

Linehan's own historical account (Linehan & Wilks, 2015) is explicit that dialectics **arrived later** than the behavioral and Zen components: the framework "emerged with a chance encounter with the philosophical concept of dialectics."

- **Zen** is the second root, supplying acceptance-side skills. Mindfulness vocabulary borrowed from Ellen Langer (1989) and Thich Nhat Hanh (1976). Reality-acceptance skills drew on Gerald May (1987).
- **Hegel/Marx:** the thesis-antithesis-synthesis vocabulary is Hegelian in lineage, and developmental-psychology dialecticians like Basseches and Riegel are cited in surrounding literature, but **no direct quotation of Hegel or Marx** was verified in Chapter 2 of the 1993 manual. `[RESEARCH NEEDED: direct primary-source verification of any Hegel/Marx citation in Ch. 2 of the 1993 book]`

The safe formulation: Linehan adopted "the philosophical concept of dialectics" pragmatically as a clinical worldview, integrating behavioral science with Zen-derived acceptance; the philosophical genealogy was filled in later by commentators.

The synthesis of **emotion mind** and **reasonable mind** into **Wise Mind**. Formulation in the 2015 *DBT Skills Training Manual*, Mindfulness module.

## Section 10 — Notes for the book's argument

**Linehan's three criteria map onto the Tantric *pramāṇas* (means of valid cognition) the book already references:**
- Guide treatment implementation ≈ direct-experience criterion (the framework must illuminate experience)
- Fit the research data ≈ inferential coherence (the framework must cohere with what can be verified)
- Engender compassion ≈ the ethical / dignity criterion (the framework must reduce suffering, not inflict it)

The mapping is not perfect but structurally genuine — and the book was already reading Linehan this way in v3 Ch2.

**On the "caused AND responsible" stance as ground for the book's free-will dialectic:** Linehan's signature formulation — patients may not have caused all their problems but have to solve them anyway; they are doing the best they can *and* need to do better — is the clinical analogue of the "not total free will, not none" argument worked out in research-24b. The philosophical dialectic (Kybalion Ch. XII, Chrysippus's cylinder, Nāgārjuna's middle) and the clinical dialectic (Linehan's assumptions) name the same shape from different registers.

**Three fixes for the book's current drafts:**
1. Author's Note: sharpen the attribution so it's explicit that the three criteria are Linehan's theory-selection criteria, adopted here as a framework-selection filter.
2. Ch3 (Citta/filter): present Linehan's three criteria in her actual language (guide treatment implementation, engender compassion, fit the research data) before the book paraphrases them as "useful / fits the data / compassionate" — so the reader sees both and the paraphrase is honest.
3. Ch3 (Citta): drop any claim that this is a clinical technique for evaluating thoughts; present it as a metatheoretical commitment.
