## Core Finding

The VBT's closing section (vv. 142c-162) neither resolves the practice/beyond-practice paradox nor simply preserves it. It performs a progressive dissolution of the subject-object structure required for practice, conducted through the language of practice itself. Five major scholars (Singh, Silburn, Baumer, Dyczkowski, [T]) agree: the closing COMPLETES the arc rather than contradicting it. No separate textual layer.

## The Architecture: 23 + 112 + 23

[T]'s structural observation: the 112 practices are framed by exactly 23 opening and 23 closing verses — deliberate symmetry. Verse 154 (breath) echoes verse 24 (foundational practice), creating a full circle. The text enacts what it teaches.

## The Goddess's Question (vv. 142c-144a)

"If this formless, all-pervading reality is the Supreme, then who recites, what is the recitation? Who is meditated upon, who is worshipped, and who is satisfied?" Devastating because it comes AFTER 112 practices, not before.

## Bhairava's Answer: Six Reinterpretations

1. Japa → "ever greater meditative absorption" (bhāvanā)
2. Mantra → "spontaneous resonance of self-awareness" (svayaṁ nāda)
3. Dhyāna → "mind that has become motionless, free of forms"
4. Pūjā → "mind made firm, dissolving into thought-free ultimate void"
5. Homa → offering elements/senses/mind into fire of dissolution
6. Snāna → immersion in one's true nature as "simply Freedom, Joy, and Awareness"

## The Three-Stage Dialectic

Stage 1 (v. 144b): External worship is coarse (sthūla)
Stage 2 (vv. 145-152): True worship is inner dissolution
Stage 3 (v. 153): Even "inner worship" cannot stand — worshipper, worshipped, and offerings are One. "How then can we use the term 'worship'?"

## The Key Paradox: Verse 148

AFTER dissolving the subject-object structure, Bhairava quietly reinstates practice: one committed to even one practice "progresses day by day" toward "nourished fullness" (bharitākāratā). The text dissolves practice then reinstates it in the same breath.

## Five Scholars, Same Arc

- **Singh:** Classifies via upāya hierarchy. Closing = anupāya (no means). Practices were always pointing here.
- **Silburn:** Culmination of pedagogical arc from effort → effortless recognition. Key concept: kramamudrā.
- **Baumer:** "Everything can be used for attaining God consciousness." Interiorization, not negation.
- **Dyczkowski:** V. 153 is the key summary. Spanda doctrine: consciousness is intrinsically dynamic — practice reveals the vibration already occurring.
- **[T]:** "Only God can worship God." Practices are "radically re-interpreted in a nondual mode," not thrown out.

## Bhāvanā: The Bridge Concept

Óscar Figueroa (2017): bhāvanā operates as both human faculty and divine power. "Bhāvanā is the means and the goal; it is a progressive human effort and, at the same time, an ongoing divine event." The practitioner contemplates the Goddess; the Goddess contemplates through the practitioner.

THIS resolves the axiom question at the structural level: practice is not something a separate self performs. It is consciousness's own self-recognition using the FORM of practice.

## The Upāya Framework as Master Key

Abhinavagupta's Tantrasāra Chapter 2: "The whole network of methods could not reveal God. Can a pot illumine the thousand-rayed sun?" The VBT's closing is the scripture's own enactment of this principle — from within a text made entirely of methods.

The four-stage movement:
1. Āṇavopāya: ritual practice (the 112 dhāraṇās at their most effortful)
2. Śāktopāya: introspective inquiry using the practices
3. Śāmbhavopāya: recognition that practitioner and deity are one
4. Anupāya: recognition that there was never a separate self to practice

## Verse 152: The Essence

Self's essence = svatantra-ānanda-cin-mātra-sāraḥ — "simply Freedom, Joy, and Awareness." True purification = immersion in this nature.

## The Final Image

The Goddess says she is "completely satisfied" (tṛptā) — the very satisfaction questioned as impossible. Then she embraces Śiva. Not dissolution of practice into philosophy but union of energy (Śakti) and awareness (Śiva) that was always already true.

"The paradox is not a problem to solve but the texture of awakening itself."

## For the Axiom Book

This research confirms the book's central claim: the tradition HOLDS the paradox rather than resolving it. The 112 practices are simultaneously methods and descriptions of what is already happening. The axiom (hold the View) and the recognition (see what was always true) are not contradictory — they are the same movement described from different positions within the upāya hierarchy.

The closing line of the research captures it perfectly: "The text does not choose between practice and beyond-practice. It demonstrates that the ascent through practice IS the descent into recognition."
