## A) Reincarnation as karmic momentum, not soul transmigration

### The author's recollected argument

In the goodbye conversation at the end of the April 2026 retreat, the author asked Hareesh about reincarnation — specifically, *if* consciousness is consciousness (the position he had developed earlier in the retreat using the body-switching thought experiment, two days prior), then why does the tradition invest in explaining reincarnation? Hareesh answered that reincarnation is different from the body-switching thought experiment: in reincarnation, *there is a pattern there*. There is karmic continuity — a structured momentum — that distinguishes rebirth from random consciousness shuffling.

### The corpus passage

From *Ask Me Anything: What Happens After We Die? And more* (Hareesh Wallis, recorded 2020-10-14, ~52 min mark).

> "All Indian traditions all take reincarnation as, you know, as read, meaning of course of course that. But then what does reincarnation actually mean? That's an interesting question, right? Because according to some schools, there's an individual soul. There's a kind of essence, a monad, and this essence transmigrates from body to body. According to the Buddhists, that's not true at all. That's total nonsense, they say. That in fact, there's just a kind of *pancha skandha*. It's this packet of interrelated energies of consciousness, perception, sensation, and cognition. And this energy, just like any energy in the universe — like a bolt of lightning or a wave through water — it has a momentum. And until something checks that momentum... a wave can travel through water for hundreds of miles before it reaches a shore and breaks. And in the same way, you are a kind of interrelated bundle of energies where there's a causation involved, whereby the earlier version of you is the cause of the present version of you, and the present version of you is the cause of a later version of you. But there is no essence to you which is going through this whole time. It's just an antecedent cause."

> "There's no reason from the traditional Indian or Asian or indeed pre-modern point of view generally — no reason to suspect that the momentum of this bundle of energies would be terminated by physical death. You have all this momentum of desire and cognition and engagement and karma. This is a big reason the Indian tradition taught reincarnation — because karma is in play. It's in motion. The consequences of your actions are coming back at you. You don't get to escape that just by dying."

> "So there's this sense of the psychic momentum to your bundle that spills over into another body and another and another, until it loses its momentum. And that's exactly what Nirvana is. That's exactly what enlightenment is in the Buddhist tradition — to lose that psychic momentum."

> "Now, the Shaiva tantric view is somewhat midway between these two views — and I'm talking about the non-dual tantric view, because the dualistic tantric view adheres to this idea of a monadic soul on its own individual journey. Whereas the non-dual tantric view says, well, there's the appearance of a soul. The infinite consciousness contracts into all these different loci of awareness temporarily. And that is something like a soul, but it's not — because each of these points of contraction ultimately completely merges back into the formless whole."

## B) Suicide: not a sin (in the Catholic sense), just karmically ineffective

### The author's recollected argument

At the goodbye, the author asked Hareesh "the ultimate question" — what about suicide? Hareesh's reply, as the author recalls: *it is not like Catholicism; in this tradition it is not a problem.* But — *don't injure the body; that conveys karma.* The suicide question is not a theological prohibition in this tradition; it is a karmic-mechanics question. Catholicism frames suicide as a sin against God (mortal, damnable). The non-dual Shaiva tantric frame is that suicide does not solve the underlying issue (the *samskaras* and karmic momentum carry forward) and adds the karma of injury to the body.

### The corpus passage

From *Ask Me Anything Satsang Sept 20* (Hareesh Wallis, ~19 min mark), where Hareesh is responding to a question about working with *samskaras*:

> "If it was about just resolving samskaras, I would say kill yourself, because that resolves most of them right there from this life. You know, you carry over a few — a handful — to the next life, maybe. But it's not just about resolving samskaras. That is not how we awaken."

> "Resolving samskaras does play a part in the awakening process — which is that once you've recognized your true nature, digesting and resolving samskaras helps you to abide in your true nature as your default state. Because samskaras, as it were, these unresolved past experiences hanging out in your system energetically, they tend to pull you out of abiding in essence nature and back into habitual modes of existence or modes of perception. So they constitute a kind of gravitational pull, and that's why many people need to resolve them. But, again — from a spiritual point of view, and this is a spiritual point of view, it's different from a psychotherapeutic point of view, it's different from a 'how to be a functional human or at least look like one' point of view — this is a spiritual point of view in which the question is the wrong question until you've recognized your true nature, really viscerally recognized."

**What this confirms:** The framing the author recalls is internally consistent with Hareesh's recorded position. Suicide is treated dispassionately as a karmic-mechanics question (not a sin), and his answer is essentially: it doesn't solve the underlying issue, and you carry forward the unresolved bundle (plus, by implication and per the author's recollection of the goodbye, the additional karma of body-injury). The Catholic-style absolute prohibition is absent from the tradition; the practical "do not" is grounded in karma, not damnation.

## C) "You always will [leave people counting on you]" — parental responsibility and the impossibility of control

### The author's recollected argument

The author followed up at the goodbye: *what if you leave a whole of people counting on you?* Hareesh replied: *you always will.* You cannot complete the obligation; you cannot guarantee being there. This is not a failure to be solved; it is the structural condition of relational life under a finite body.

### The corpus passage

From the same *What Happens After We Die* video, in response to a question from a parent (Amaraja) who was "content with death coming my way until I had a child":

> "It may not be any consolation, but I would just invite you to contemplate. The universe can't get this wrong. The death thing — the death thing is the most important part of life. There's no way to prove this and you don't have to believe me, but there's no such thing as premature death. That's just a human mental construct. If somebody dies sooner than we expect, oh, they went before their time. No — there's no before. How could anything happen before its time? There's no such thing. The universe can't get it wrong. Whenever it happens, it's the right time."

> "As a parent: 'Oh, but I want to be there for my child.' And I'm sure you will be. You live a healthy lifestyle, Amaraja, and you're going to be — you're likely, very likely. But you should always be prepared, because it could come any moment. Could be tomorrow, whatever. But very likely you're going to be there for your child through to adulthood."

> "But even if you're not, that's the right thing. And — even if you're alive, even if you live the longest possible life, you are not in control of the destiny of your child. And you know that. But it's the scariest thing for a parent to face — the truth that you are not in control of the destiny of your child and you can't be. And life has designed it that way. So it's not wrong. For some people, that's a harder thing to accept than their own death. And it's really worth accepting. Every parent I know who's done it, it's the most life-changing thing for them. To truly, deeply accept that they're not in control and couldn't be in control of the destiny of their child, no matter how well they parent. No matter what they do."

**Connection to the recollected "you always will":** The corpus passage substitutes "you are not in control of the destiny of your child" for the more compressed "you always will [leave people counting on you]," but the underlying point is the same: the parental obligation cannot be completed, the relational accounting cannot be balanced, and the willingness to be present is not the same as the ability to guarantee presence. The shorter form Hareesh used in the goodbye reads as a personal compression of the public teaching — exactly the kind of formulation a teacher would arrive at after years of saying the longer version.

## D) Consciousness switching bodies — the thought experiment from two days earlier

### The author's recollected argument

Two days before the goodbye, in a different conversation at the retreat, Hareesh made the argument: *if consciousness randomly switched bodies, we would not even realize we changed bodies — consciousness is consciousness.* Later, when someone else referenced this argument, the author intrusively shouted "what would" — the body refusing the conceptual clean answer even as the head accepted it. (That Hareesh remembered the thought-experiment two days later, at the goodbye, and built on it when answering the reincarnation question, is a small note on his pedagogical attention.)

### The corpus passage

From the same *What Happens After We Die* video, the Shaiva tantric account of the apparent self:

> "What you consider as myself is actually just the one consciousness experiencing itself from a particular vantage point. But your essence nature has no intrinsic difference whatsoever from the essence nature of all beings. This is the part that's a bit of a disappointment as it were to Westerners, who really in general want to believe there's something uniquely special about me — like I am God manifest as [insert name here] and that is a unique vibration. No. This tradition says essence nature is one and the same in all beings. There's no your essence nature as opposed to my essence nature. But what is unique is expression. There has never been a set of life experiences identical to the ones had by Hareesh, and there never will be again. But my essence nature is exactly the same as yours. There is no difference."

**What this confirms:** The body-switching thought experiment is a teaching device drawn directly from this position. If essence nature is identical across beings and only expression differs, then a hypothetical switch of bodies would not register at the level of essence-nature — only the expression-stream would change, and the "I" that registers experience would have no way to compare across the switch. Same passage, more philosophical framing.

### Retreat-day corroboration (added 2026-05-11)

The Cali Retreat 26 transcripts contain the underlying death-teaching at **Day 2 Session 2**, in response to a participant who asked what happens when we die. Wallis answered with the "consciousness-only view": the brain is a *consciousness transceiver* — it does not make consciousness but receives, modulates, and transmits it; damage to the transceiver affects experience without altering consciousness itself. He then cited Eben Alexander's *Proof of Heaven* NDE — "in the form it had before the editors got to it" — as evidence of what consciousness can produce when not mediated through a physical body. He was careful to insist the book should *not* be read as an account of what we experience after death; from this tradition's point of view, NDEs show consciousness's autonomy from the body, not heaven. Later in the same session he taught the inverse: *what you are is space for the world* (Douglas Harding's headlessness) — that we conceptualize ourselves from the third-person perspective only by mistake.

Two days later, at **Day 4 Session 2**, Barry returned to this teaching to share what happened in his body after he heard the previous-retreat version. He recounted Wallis's earlier framing — the more striking thought-experiment form: *"if you swap consciousness with someone, you wouldn't notice, because your thoughts and your brain and everything are in this body"* — and reported the experience that followed: turning around in the room and seeing Paradevi's eyes in everyone, recognizing one being receiving all the experiences. Wallis asked, "How does it relate to the first part about the consciousness switching?" Barry started to continue. The author intrusively interrupted at that point — her body shouting out a partial phrase ("what would —") before the head could moderate. Wallis preserved the container by saying, *"Well, let's finish with Barry."* Barry then completed his share with an antidote framing (*I am everyone else and everything in this room except for this*), and Wallis closed the moment with the Lojong principle: *liberate the antidote in its own place* — use it, but don't get stuck with it.

The intrusion itself appears in the transcript as a brief partial vocalization and is not transcribed verbatim. It is the author's witness that the words she shouted began with "what would." The body was refusing the conceptual clean answer in real time, even as the head had been following the teaching. Wallis's *"let's finish with Barry"* is the same container-preservation gesture he used during the Claude/ChatGPT moment on Day 3 (now rendered in ch06): the content of the moment is received, but the form of the interruption is gently returned to its proper place.
