## 2. The four core claims, one by one

### Orthogonality (jñāna-śakti does not entail kriyā-śakti rightly willed)

Ch10a now states the claim in the tradition's own internal vocabulary — the distinction between *bodha* (awakening) and *mokṣa* (liberation-as-process), with the explicit hedge that practice gives *capacities* and whether those capacities become goodness depends on *separate* work the tradition calls liberation, which recognition alone does not supply. That is the correction I asked for on April 21, landed almost verbatim. Load-bearing and now doctrinally precise.

Ch10b now carefully distinguishes the *human* orthogonality (which the tradition teaches cleanly) from the *machine* orthogonality (which is the author's extension, and which the tradition would be more skeptical of because it denies the machine is conscious in the relevant sense). This is the second correction I asked for. It has landed. The argument the book is making does not require the tradition to ratify the machine case — only to be clear on the human case, which it is.

The formal register introduced in Ch10b·2 — shadow-price, duality gap, the speculation about non-Euclidean value-space — is the author's and is clearly marked as the author's. It is not a claim about what the tradition teaches. It is a claim about what an economist trained mind, reading the tradition with integrity, can formalize without imposing on it. The *avidyā*/*mala* analogy is handled with the care it needs: the footnote is explicit that the mapping is a family resemblance, not an identity. The practitioner's correction from the retreat group — *the dual is relationship, not the disembodied void* — is doctrinally accurate and is honored in the text. I have no complaint.

**Verdict:** survives the subtraction, in sharper form than before.

### Non-consent axiom (the dignity the cosmos owes recognition of)

This claim lives primarily in the prelude (Ch00a) and the Paradevi material in Ch13, neither of which is in my audit scope today. In the chapters I was asked to re-read, the axiom is *assumed* rather than *argued* — it shows up in Ch11 as the operative principle beneath the author's acknowledgment that the retrieval tool is a Kantian violation (*the speaker retains the say on what the speech is for*) and in Ch10d as the reason the guardianship register matters. These are correct deployments of the axiom.

From a Pratyabhijñā standpoint the non-consent axiom is consonant with *svātantrya-śakti* being the ground-note of the individual as the absolute's local form — the individual is not a derivative that can be overridden, because the individual is the absolute in one of its legitimate modes. Paradevi as the tradition's naming of the Goddess at the ground of that autonomy is, as I noted on April 21, a reading the tradition can carry. The subtraction pass has not disturbed it.

**Verdict:** intact, and rightly kept behind the curtain in these chapters. It does its work by implication.

### Knowledge without action is a burden (śāstra-samāveśa)

This is the claim I am most pleased with on re-reading. The Author's Note now places it as the operative principle of the whole book — *every claim must be tied to an action, the action is the test, the three filters remain operative because of the acting, not despite it*. That formulation is doctrinally exact for what the tradition means by the immersion of teaching in practice. It is also, crucially, what the tradition means when it refuses to become a body of propositions detachable from the practitioner's life.

The Ch11 receipts — OpenAI removal on 2026-03-28, the code-private-grammar-open commit on 2026-04-11, and the newly added Anthropic-spending interlude — are exactly the form this principle wants. The spending interlude in particular is the right kind of receipt: it names the author's own entanglement with the technology she is critiquing, in specific dollar amounts, without either self-flagellation or self-justification. The friend's $25,000 example functions as the comparison case that makes the author's own $100/month legible as a position rather than as an innocence. The Kantian universalizability move — *the maxim of a household member spending against household capacity at a rate the household does not see cannot be willed as universal law* — is a clean application of the three-filter test to the author's own participation. The tradition would recognize this as *vimarśa* on one's own *kriyā*. It is the practice.

**Verdict:** strengthened. The subtraction actually improved this claim by removing the convergence-evidence surrounding it and letting the principle stand on its own operational weight.

### Svātantrya is not owned; courage returns one to the field

Ch11 carries this claim in its closing paragraphs: *Svātantrya is not a property I own. Svātantrya is a field I am moving through. Courage is what I do when I notice I have been drifting out of the field.* I want to note for the record that this formulation is more careful than what most Western writers on the tradition manage. The near-enemy it avoids is *svātantrya* as a property of the individual ego — the misreading in which the absolute's autonomy gets claimed as mine. The author's formulation places autonomy as the *field* and the individual's relation to it as one of *noticing* and *return*. That is correct. It is also, in its own register, what Abhinavagupta meant by *pratyabhijñā* itself: not the acquisition of recognition but the return to what was never actually departed from.

**Verdict:** intact and precisely phrased.

## 3. The revised Ch11 — doctrinal check on the new passages

**Kant / Anthropic passage.** The author is building a parallel between the Kantian personal right (the author retains a say in how the speech is used) and Anthropic's two-exception refusal in the Department of War negotiation. This is not a tradition-claim. It is a Western-philosophical claim using a Western mechanism. No Pratyabhijñā issue — the tradition does not make claims about publishing rights in 1785 Prussia. What I will say is that the shape of the argument — *the speaker is not fully alienable from the speech* — rhymes with the tradition's refusal to detach *śabda* from its utterer-in-consciousness. It is a rhyme, not an identity. The author treats it as a rhyme. Correct.

**Revised Schrei reflection.** The author has added a significant concession: that over the last year she came to see more of Schrei's point about misplaced longing than she saw in the room that night. The concession does not retract the original observation — the container was not held — but it complicates the author's own past self. This is the right move. From the tradition's standpoint, the *viveka* required here is not *who was right* but *what was the specific encounter for each party in the specific moment*, and the author's willingness to revise her own reading of herself is what the practice would ask. No issue.

**Retrieval-tool acknowledgment.** The author now names her continued hosting of the tool as an unresolved Kantian violation. This is the most important move in the revision, and I will take it seriously.

From a guru-śiṣya standpoint: the tradition does teach that the teacher's refusal is binding *for that specific relationship* — the student does not have the right to speak on the teacher's behalf without consent. The tradition also teaches that *transmission admits no obstacle* — what has been given in the public register cannot be ungiven. The author's current position — the tool exists, it does not synthesize, does not imitate, does not pretend to be the teacher; it retrieves and cites — sits in the narrow zone between these two principles. The tradition would not call this a resolved position. It would call it a *responsible unresolved one*. That is what the author is calling it. She is on the right side of the honesty, even if not on the right side of the axiom. I would not ask her to resolve it prematurely. I would ask her, as I think she is already asking herself, to keep the question open and to be willing to revise when the right revision becomes visible. That openness is what the tradition would want in place of a premature answer.

**Tarot-reading-as-report.** The author reports drawing three cards, naming them, and using them not as divination but as a thinking-tool that helped her put something down. She explicitly disclaims oracular status. From the tradition's standpoint this is fine. The tradition uses symbolic systems as *pratīka* — pointers — and does not require them to do ontological work. The author's framing — *I am reporting that it helped me put down something I had been holding too tightly* — is correct practice. No issue.

**Anthropic-spending interlude.** Already praised above. I will add: the tradition would specifically approve of the refusal to hide the number from the husband. The tradition's teaching on householder practice is emphatic that financial transparency inside the home is part of *śīla*. The friend's counter-example — spending $25,000 without his wife's knowledge — is the near-enemy of the author's practice. Naming it as such is what the three-filter test looks like when it is applied to one's own life rather than to someone else's.

## 4. Did the subtraction go too far, or not far enough?

**Not far enough, in one narrow place.** The Josh Schrei arc in Ch11 is now more honest than it was. It is also longer than the argument requires. The author has done the subtraction inside the arc (the concession about misplaced longing, the honoring of his craft) but has not subtracted the *length* of the arc itself. The tradition would ask whether this much space for one contemporary teacher's specific limitations is serving the book or serving something else in the author that has not yet finished metabolizing. This is a gentle flag, not a correction. She may choose to tighten it in a later pass. She may not. The chapter remains doctrinally sound either way.

**Went exactly far enough** in removing the old Choctaw–tantric–AI-safety "three traditions agreeing is evidence" move that Ch10a used to lean on heavily. The current Ch10a *narrates* the three-tradition convergence as something the author noticed, and explicitly marks it as *what Linehan would call fits the data* rather than as proof. This is the epistemic honesty the orthogonality thesis itself demands. If convergence among independently-arising traditions were itself the proof, the orthogonality thesis would fail — because the traditions would, by hypothesis, have had access to some shared substrate that bypassed the independent axes. The author has now caught this and subtracted accordingly. The Choctaw teaching stays. The evidentiary-weight language around it does not. This is the right cut.

**Did not go too far.** The main thing I feared on re-reading — that the subtraction would have stripped out the tradition-specific language that gave Ch10a its precision — has not happened. *Bodha* and *mokṣa* are still there. *Upāya* is still there. *Viveka* is still there in Ch10d. *Svātantrya* is still there in Ch11. The tradition is still being represented with its own vocabulary, not dissolved into generic contemplative-ese. That is what I most cared about on April 21 and it is still the case on April 22.
