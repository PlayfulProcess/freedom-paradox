# Personal Draft Letter to Christopher Wallis

## **I. How I Found PepsiCo, and Why I Am Writing You**

I have never accepted a guru. I have tried. Yogananda had me — his directness, his embodied joy — until the end of kriya initiation, when I was told I could only be initiated if I committed to reading only him for spiritual matters. Krishnamurti would have had me if he could be clearer and if I had not grown to suspect everything that came out of theosophy. Sam Harris chose samsara, and I suspect he might be right, even though I am not fully convinced there is no nirvana. There were many others. Each time I get close, I find the place where the teacher or the tradition asks me to accept something I cannot honestly accept, and I leave. Not in anger. In grief. I want to be taught. I just cannot be taught by someone who will not name what they are asking me to take on faith.

I think I might have found my guru. Like I found PepsiCo.

Let me explain, because the analogy is not a joke — or rather, it is a joke, and the joke is the point.

I still work for PepsiCo. This is, in its own way, my courage to be as a part — a phrase I will explain shortly. My life is more wonderful because of them. They pay me, they give me structure, they are part of the relational field I participate in. I do not pretend this is morally clean. A corporation that sells sugar water and single-use plastic to the world is not an institution I can fully defend. But I have learned where the hypocrisy becomes unbearable and where it remains tolerable.

I once thought my path for activism was to change the system from the inside — to make PepsiCo more sustainable through its own sustainability department. What I found is that it is in the sustainability departments that hypocrisy most reigns. PepsiCo acquired SodaStream — a company that allows people to make their own sparkling water at home, reducing single-use plastic — and the acquisition effectively silenced the anti-plastic movement's most visible commercial alternative. In the headquarters, there was a book about how hypocrisy is the first step toward change. The argument was that if you start performing sustainability, even insincerely, the performance eventually becomes real.

I did not buy it then.

So I chose areas where I do not have to exert hypocritical thinking daily. And I try to talk to the least amount of people possible. This is not heroic. It is the honest compromise of someone who cannot afford to leave the relational field but refuses to perform values she does not hold. It is, in miniature, what the existential theologian Paul Tillich called *the courage to be as a part* — imperfect, complicit, clear-eyed about the complicity, and unwilling to dress it up as virtue.

I am telling you this because I want you to know who is sitting in front of you at this retreat — and because praise needs to come before challenge, or the challenge means nothing.

I can see from the way you handle the WhatsApp groups that you are a very mature human being. You redirected my philosophical tangent with humor and warmth, not dismissal. You corrected the Yukti 69 issue when I raised a concern about self-harm. You told me directly that choice is not an illusion — as a position, not a platitude. Your scholarship is rigorous. Your content is so good that it makes me think practicing tantra might actually give a person superpowers, and you might have them. You claim pure motive and teach that the effectiveness of the practice lies in the teacher's capacity to hold the practitioner in such a way that all mental constructs can release — together. At the core, everything about your work seems deeply anchored in truth and in practice. It seems to be useful. It seems to be compassionate. It seems to fit the data.

But I have a deeper existential question that none of this resolves. And I need to bring it to you honestly, because it is the question I live inside, and if you cannot hold it, then I will find the rooms I can breathe in, and I will keep my head down, and I will not accept a guru. But if you can — if you can name the things by their names — then maybe I can do something I have never done before.

That is my intention for this retreat. (And I promise I will let everyone else speak 😉.)

## **II. The Question I Cannot Resolve**

I come to Kashmir Shaivism from an unexpected direction: not from spiritual longing, but from an existential crisis that no tradition has yet been able to close. The crisis is this: the statement *"your value is given simply because you exist"* is presented across contemplative, therapeutic, and philosophical traditions as if it were a discoverable truth — something you realize through practice, insight, or grace. I have come to believe it is not a truth. It is an axiom. And the difference matters enormously.

An axiom is a foundational commitment that cannot be derived from anything more basic. It is chosen — or, more precisely, it is what remains when all attempts at proof have been exhausted. You can build coherent systems on top of it, but you cannot dig beneath it and find bedrock. There is no bedrock. There is only the decision to build.

Paul Tillich, the existential theologian, was the only thinker I have encountered who was fully honest about this. In *The Courage to Be*, he argues that the fundamental anxiety of human existence is not death, nor meaninglessness, nor guilt — though all three are real — but the threat of *nonbeing* that underlies them all. Every human being, simply by existing, must confront the possibility that existence itself has no inherent justification. This is not a mood to be overcome. It is the ontological condition.

What Tillich calls "absolute faith" is not belief in God, not confidence in a doctrine, not even trust in experience. It is what remains after every ground has been removed. It is the courage to affirm being *in spite of* the absence of any guaranteed ground for that affirmation. And crucially, Tillich distinguishes between *the courage to be as oneself* — the Nietzschean self-assertion that tries to generate meaning from will alone — and *the courage to be as a part*: the acceptance that one's being is constituted by participation in a larger whole that one did not create and cannot fully comprehend.

I find this distinction not merely intellectually satisfying but existentially necessary. And I believe it provides a bridge — possibly the only honest bridge — between the Western existential tradition and the nondual philosophy of Kashmir Shaivism. But I bring it as a challenge, not as a synthesis already achieved.

You know Marshall Rosenberg's work — you discuss Nonviolent Communication in your own teaching. Rosenberg's deepest question was: why are there people who make life more wonderful for others, and people who don't? That question organized his entire life's work. My deepest question is the one beneath his: *why should making life more wonderful matter at all?* Rosenberg could begin where he began because he had already accepted the axiom that life is worth making wonderful. I am the person who got stuck one level down.

## **III. Where My Suffering Is Anchored**

I need to name something directly, because the argument I am making is not abstract for me. It is where my suffering lives.

I have experienced suicidal ideation. Not as a passing mood, but as the logical conclusion of a philosophical thread I cannot fully refute. If inherent value is an axiom rather than a truth — if there is no bedrock beneath the decision to affirm being — then the decision *not* to affirm it is equally available. Society tells me this decision is not valid. I think society tells me this partly because humanity does not want to question its right to exist. The taboo against suicide functions, in part, as a collective defense against the vertigo of groundlessness — a refusal to look at what Tillich looked at.

I do not act on this ideation. I hold other commitments — to my child, to my partner, to a sense of responsibility that precedes and exceeds my individual despair. But I want to be honest: I cannot fully believe that I have the right to exist. Doubt always returns. It is not a failure of practice. It is the condition of having seen that the ground is not there.

What I have arrived at — painfully, incompletely — is this: I am willing to accept that there is no other way to live except by accepting inherent value as an axiom, not as truth. As long as I continue to live, I must have the courage to be as a part. This is not resolution. It is not happiness. It is the decision to build on a ground I know to be chosen rather than given, and to renew that decision every time doubt erodes it.

This is also why I cannot resolve to become a therapist — even though I am completing an MSW, even though the work draws me. I think I would be a terrible therapist, because the foundation of therapeutic work is the unconditional positive regard that says: your life has intrinsic value, and we are going to work from that. I cannot say that with full conviction. I can say it as an axiom I am willing to hold. But a client deserves better than a therapist who is white-knuckling the premise. This is why I stay at PepsiCo. Not because I believe in it — but because honest complicity is more tolerable than dishonest healing.

And I will not accept the spiritual version of the argument I rejected at PepsiCo headquarters — that performing the axiom of inherent value through practice, even without genuine conviction, will eventually produce genuine conviction. Maybe it will. But the honest move is to name what you are doing: choosing an axiom and practicing it, not discovering a truth and aligning with it.

## **IV. The Species Question: Eco-Fundamentalism and the Right to Exist**

The question of inherent value does not stop at the individual. It extends to the species.

In my own work, I have been tracing how humanity acquired power before developing the responsibility structures — what I call *grammars* — to hold it. The evidence is ecological, developmental, and civilizational. I wrote:

*"If co-regulation is the biological baseline — if the nervous system is an open circuit that cannot regulate itself alone — then every surviving culture must have built technologies for maintaining that circuit. Not technologies in the modern sense. Technologies in the older sense: technē, the skilled practice through which a community sustains what it cannot afford to lose."*

But we are dismantling those technologies. The co-regulatory infrastructure that sustained human communities for tens of thousands of years — the San healing dance, the polyrhythmic drum circle, the communal grief ritual, the bedtime story told in the embrace of a regulating body — is being systematically replaced by individual optimization and algorithmic engagement. I documented what I called the Western inversion:

*"The Western move was consistent across centuries and disciplines: take a communal, embodied, co-regulatory practice. Extract the technique. Sever it from its relational context. Offer it back as individual optimization."*

And on the other end of our trajectory, the ecological data is unambiguous. The Pleistocene megafauna extinctions. The invasion ecology of colonial expansion. The current rate of species loss. The carbon trajectory. Humanity is not displaying the responsibility to live as a part of the earth system. We are displaying the behavior of an invasive species — one that acquired extraordinary capabilities without the regulatory grammars to constrain them.

I can see the eco-fundamentalist argument. I understand it from the inside. They do not believe humanity has the right to exist because humanity is not living as a part — it is living as a consuming whole, extracting from every system it touches without the reciprocity that ecological membership requires. From within the framework of "courage to be as a part," the species is failing the test. We are not being as a part. We are being as a devourer.

I do not endorse this conclusion. But I cannot dismiss it with the ease that most people do, because the logic follows from the same axiom I am trying to live by. If value is constituted by participation in a whole — if the courage to be is specifically the courage to be *as a part* — then what happens when a part is destroying the whole it participates in? Does the axiom still hold? Or does the part forfeit its claim by betraying its participation?

## **V. Adaptive, Not Right: The Moral Compass I Am Trying to Build**

You have made an argument I find illuminating — that we should not hate racists, because racists are people who genuinely love their families and communities but hold beliefs that cause them to extend that love in ways that harm others. I want to build on that argument and push it further.

I think you are right that hatred is the wrong response. But I think the framework of *right and wrong* is itself the wrong moral compass. What I am trying to build — in my writing, in my life, in the methodology I am developing — is a compass oriented toward *adaptive and maladaptive*.

Right and wrong are static categories. They presuppose a moral law that exists independently of context — a transcendent standard against which actions can be measured. Adaptive and maladaptive are relational categories. They ask: does this behavior sustain the system it participates in, or does it degrade the system? Is this practice building the capacity to live as a part, or is it eroding that capacity?

The racist who loves his family is not evil. He is maladaptive. His love is real, but its grammar is broken — it draws the circle of care too small, and the exclusion degrades the larger system his family depends on. This is not a lesser condemnation. It is a more precise one. And it opens a different response: not punishment, not hatred, but the patient work of expanding the grammar. Widening the circle until the love that is already present can extend to what it has been excluding.

But here is where the argument turns back on itself. If adaptive and maladaptive is the compass, then humanity as a whole must be evaluated by the same standard. And by that standard, we are failing. Our grammars of care — the co-regulatory practices that sustained us, the responsibility structures that constrained our power — are being dismantled faster than we can rebuild them. I wrote about this in terms of the bedtime story:

*"Five conditions. Five layers of containment. When all five are present, dark content heals. When they are systematically removed — when the fiction frame is replaced by the algorithmic feed, when the co-regulatory body is replaced by a solitary screen, when graduated dosing is replaced by whatever the algorithm serves next, when child agency is replaced by autoplay, when bounded structure is replaced by infinite scroll — the same darkness that builds regulatory capacity begins to erode it."*

We are the species that got fire — Prometheus's gift, power without the maturity to hold it. And the question I cannot stop asking is whether we can build the grammars fast enough. Whether the axiom of inherent value can hold even for a species that is behaving, by its own adaptive standard, in ways that threaten the web of life it depends on.

## **VI. The Colonial Reflex and the Illusion of Separability**

Before turning to Kashmir Shaivism directly, I need to name a structural problem that any honest inquiry into "inherent value" must confront. Vanessa Machado de Oliveira Andreotti's work on coloniality argues that the modern Western subject is constituted by a foundational gesture of *separability* — the belief that the self can be meaningfully abstracted from the relational fields that produce it. This separability is not merely an intellectual error. It is the operating system of colonial modernity: the capacity to extract, classify, and rank beings according to their perceived value, which requires first believing that value is a property of discrete entities rather than of relationships.

When modern therapeutic culture says "you have inherent value," it often smuggles separability back in through the very grammar of the claim. *You* — the bounded individual — *have* — as a possession — *value* — as a quantifiable attribute. This is the language of ownership applied to ontology. It is, in a precise sense, colonial metaphysics dressed in compassionate clothing.

Bayo Akomolafe pushes further. In his work on fugitivity and the "politics of the otherwise," he argues that the question "do I have a right to exist?" is already malformed — not because the answer is obviously yes, but because the *I* that would possess such a right is itself a production of the very system whose legitimacy is in question. The colonial-modern subject asks whether it has value using a framework (individual rights, inherent properties, self-possession) that was invented to justify the extraction of value from others. The question cannot be answered within its own terms. It can only be *fled from* — not by finding a better answer, but by refusing the grammar that produces the question.

This is where Tillich's "courage to be as a part" becomes radical rather than conservative. To be *as a part* is precisely to refuse the separability that makes the question of individual inherent value seem like something that requires proof. It is to say: I do not exist in the way that would make the question answerable. I exist as a node in a relational field, and the field does not need to justify itself to any of its nodes — nor can any node meaningfully ask whether it "deserves" to be part of the field. The question dissolves not because the answer is yes, but because the grammar was wrong.

But even here — even after dissolving the individual question — the species question remains. Because the species is a node in a larger relational field: the earth system, the web of life. And that node is behaving maladaptively. The question of whether humanity has the right to exist is not a question about individual separability. It is a question about a part's relationship to its whole. And it is the question eco-fundamentalism asks, honestly, from inside the same relational logic I am trying to live by.

## **VII. Kashmir Shaivism: Ground or Axiom?**

This brings me to Kashmir Shaivism, and to you specifically as its most rigorous contemporary interpreter.

In *Tantra Illuminated* and in the Vijñāna-bhairava-tantra teachings, consciousness (*citi*, *saṃvit*) is presented as the ground of all manifestation. It is not a thing among things but the field in which all things arise. It is simultaneously transcendent (free from all mental constructs, unconditioned) and immanent (present in every phenomenon, never absent from its own expressions). This is the nondual claim: not that everything is one in a flat, undifferentiated sense, but that the ground and its expressions are not ontologically separate, even though they are distinguishable.

Within this framework, the question of inherent value does not arise as a separate problem to be solved. If everything that exists is a vibration (*spanda*) of consciousness, then nothing that exists is ontologically deficient. To exist is already to be an expression of the free, autonomous (*svātantrya*), inherently valuable ground. Value, like consciousness, is not distributed to beings from some external source — it is the nature of the field itself.

I find this beautiful. I also find it structurally identical to an axiom.

Here is my concern, stated as precisely as I can manage: Kashmir Shaivism claims that the identity of ground and expression is not a philosophical postulate but a *recognition* (*pratyabhijñā*) — something you *see* when the obscurations fall away. It is, in this telling, experientially verifiable. But this claim creates a circularity: you need sustained practice to achieve the recognition, but you need enough prior commitment to the framework to sustain the practice. The recognition, in other words, is only available from inside the axiom. From outside, it looks like a self-confirming loop.

Tillich would call this the "theological circle" and would not see it as a defect. He understood that every existential commitment involves a circle — you cannot evaluate a ground of being from outside it, because there is no outside. But he was honest about naming it as a circle. He did not claim that his "absolute faith" was experientially verifiable in a way that would compel assent from someone standing outside the commitment. He said: this is the courage required. Choose or do not choose. But do not pretend the choice is compelled by evidence.

I know what you have written. You have said, drawing on Abhinava Gupta, that "if you practice yoga from the perspective that you are not good enough as you are, or that there is something wrong with you that needs fixing, then your yoga cannot fulfill its ultimate purpose because it is a practice founded on wrong understanding." I feel the beauty and the force of this. But I also feel its circularity. The instruction presupposes the very recognition it claims to produce. You must begin by accepting your wholeness in order to realize your wholeness. For someone standing in the void — for someone who cannot reliably access the felt sense that they are "a perfect and whole expression of the Divine" — this instruction, however lovingly offered, can feel like being told to fly by someone who has already forgotten what it was like not to have wings.

## **VIII. What I Anticipate You Will Say — And Why I Need More**

I have studied your work carefully enough to anticipate several responses. I want to name them and explain why, though I find each partly compelling, none of them closes the gap.

**You may say: Recognition is not belief. It is direct experience.** You will likely argue that *pratyabhijñā* is not a propositional commitment but an experiential shift — more like remembering than like accepting a hypothesis. The analogy might be recognizing your own face in a mirror you didn't know was a mirror. You don't *believe* that's your face; you *see* it. And the seeing changes everything.

I take this seriously. But the analogy conceals the problem it claims to solve. Recognizing your face requires prior experience of having a face. The recognition *presupposes* the thing recognized. Similarly, recognizing consciousness-as-ground requires some prior experiential reference for what "consciousness-as-ground" means. And that reference is cultivated through practice within a tradition that has already committed to the ontology. The experience is real. I do not doubt that. But its interpretation — its status as recognition of something that was always already true, rather than the production of a new state through sustained practice — is exactly what is at issue. Calling it "remembering" rather than "constructing" is a move within the axiom, not a transcendence of it.

**You may say: The question of inherent value dissolves in nondual awareness.** From the perspective of *turīya* or the fourth state, the question of whether the individual has value is revealed as misconceived, because there is no separate individual to have or lack value. There is only consciousness, playing as all its forms. The suffering I describe is the suffering of taking the separate self to be ultimately real.

This is the response I find most powerful and also most dangerous. And I think you know it is dangerous, because your own tradition and your own writing warn against exactly this move — from two directions.

First, the tradition itself. Kashmir Shaivism explicitly regards transcendentalist paths like Patañjali's classical yoga as less evolved precisely because they are not integrated. The Shaiva critique of *kaivalya* — the Yogic ideal of pure isolation of consciousness from matter — is that it achieves freedom by abandoning the manifest world rather than recognizing the manifest world as an expression of consciousness. This is the whole point of the nondual move: not to transcend manifestation, but to recognize it *as* the divine. A response to existential suffering that says "your question dissolves at a higher level of awareness" is structurally identical to the Patañjalian move your tradition critiques. It achieves peace by leaving the question behind rather than integrating it.

Second, your own writing. You have written explicitly — in *Near Enemies of the Truth* and elsewhere — about spiritual bypassing as one of the primary distortions in contemporary spiritual culture. You have warned practitioners not to use nondual philosophy as an escape from the uncomfortable, the unresolved, the painful. If someone came to you and said, "I no longer grieve because I have realized that death is an illusion," I believe you would name that as a near enemy — a spiritual insight weaponized against human feeling. I am asking you to apply the same standard here. If the response to "I cannot find the ground of my own value" is "the question dissolves in nondual awareness," then nondual awareness is being used to bypass the most fundamental existential confrontation a human being can have. And that is precisely the move your tradition — and you personally — have committed to not making.

**You may say: Choice is not an illusion. (You already said this.)** In the WhatsApp group, when I raised the grief of reproducing cultural patterns that are unsustainably destroying life on Earth, you responded directly: "Choice is not an illusion." I believe you, and I believe this is a place where Kashmir Shaivism diverges meaningfully from certain Vedantic and Buddhist frameworks that do reduce agency to epiphenomenon. But if choice is real, then the choice not to exist is also real. And the work of explaining why the axiom of inherent value should be *chosen* — rather than treating it as something that becomes self-evident through practice — becomes the central task. A tradition that affirms the reality of choice must also affirm the legitimacy of the question: *why choose this?*

**You may say: Come to the retreat and practice. The concepts will sort themselves out.** This is the pragmatic response, and it may be the wisest one. I respect it. But I need you to understand something about where I am: I cannot begin the practice in good faith if the practice requires me to pretend that the ground is solid. My nervous system knows the ground is not solid. My body has been in the void. And every tradition that has told me "just practice, and you'll see" has eventually lost my trust — not because the practice didn't work, but because the teachers would not name the axiom. They insisted the ground was solid, and I could feel it giving way, and the dissonance between their certainty and my experience became its own kind of suffering.

**You may say: If you practice — especially heart-center practices, devotional practices, practices that cultivate connection — you will come to *feel* the inherent value of being.** I believe this. I believe it because the somatic research supports it. Sustained contemplative practice does change the felt sense of the body. Focused attention on the heart center, repeated engagement with uplifting themes, the physiological effects of chanting and breathwork — these produce measurable shifts in interoception, vagal tone, and emotional baseline. I do not doubt that practice can produce the experience of groundedness, of belonging, of inherent okayness.

But this is precisely my concern. The fact that I can train my body to feel something does not make that something true. It makes it true *for me*, *in my built-in experience as a human being* — a human being who has been, both planetarily and perhaps mentally, fast-burning her own house down. The somatic experience of groundedness produced by heart-center practice may be a genuine recognition of reality. Or it may be a beautifully constructed adaptive response — the nervous system learning to generate a felt sense of safety because the alternative is unbearable. I cannot tell the difference from inside the experience. And neither can you, from inside yours. This is the circularity again.

I am not saying the practice is worthless. I am saying it cannot settle the question of whether what it reveals is *discovered* or *produced*. And a tradition that claims the experience settles the question is asking me to accept the axiom without naming it. Which is the one thing I am asking you not to do.

What I need is a teacher who can say: "Yes, it is an axiom. Yes, the ground is chosen, not given. And here are the practices that help you live inside that choice with courage and even, sometimes, with beauty." That honesty is the condition under which I can receive the teaching. Without it, every practice becomes an exercise in self-deception, and self-deception is the one thing my nervous system will not tolerate.

## **IX. Astrology and the Cosmic Part**

I raise the question of astrology here not as an aside but as a structural analogy. In Jyotish, the natal chart is not a prediction machine but a description of how the individual is already woven into the cosmic fabric. The chart does not ask whether you have value; it describes the specific *pattern* of your participation in the whole. You are not a self-contained entity who happens to be affected by planetary forces. You are the convergence point of those forces. Your existence is relational all the way down.

This maps directly onto Tillich's "courage to be as a part." The astrological framework does not ground your value in anything you possess or achieve. It grounds it — or rather, it dissolves the question of grounding — by showing that you are already constituted by your participation in something larger. You do not need to earn a place in the cosmos. You are the cosmos, at a particular angle, at a particular time.

But again: this is an axiom, not a proof. The astrological framework works beautifully *if you accept its foundational commitments*. From outside those commitments, it is a pattern-recognition system applied to a domain where its validity cannot be empirically established. The beauty and coherence of the system do not, by themselves, establish the truth of its premises. They establish the *livability* of its premises — which, I am increasingly convinced, may be all that can honestly be asked.

## **X. Linehan's Filters and Their Self-Undermining**

In my own work, I have adopted a methodology derived from Marsha Linehan's dialectical behavior therapy: a claim or practice is worth holding if it is *useful*, if it *fits the data*, and if it is *compassionate*. I wrote about what I call "the Linehan wager":

*"Three questions. Is it useful? Does it fit the data? Is it compassionate? . . . This is not relativism. It is epistemic humility with teeth. And it is the posture this book will hold through every chapter that follows. Not: we know the truth. But: we know what the evidence converges on, we know the predictions it generates, and we know that holding it with compassion — for the cultures that lost their practices and the people trying to rebuild them — is not weakness. It is the only position from which the work can be done honestly."*

These three filters have become central to how I evaluate traditions, frameworks, and my own thinking. But the existential question I am describing here threatens to undermine all three simultaneously. Because:

*Useful for what?* Usefulness presupposes a purpose, which presupposes that something matters, which presupposes the very value-axiom in question.

*Fits what data?* Data-fitting presupposes that truth-tracking is important, which presupposes that there is something worth being accurate about, which again presupposes that existence has some kind of claim on us.

*Compassionate toward whom?* Compassion presupposes that beings are worth caring for, which is the axiom stated in emotional rather than ontological terms.

The filters cannot bootstrap themselves. They depend on a prior commitment to existence-as-worth-living-in that they cannot generate. This is not a flaw in the methodology. It is the honest recognition that every methodology stands on something it cannot justify. Tillich again: you cannot get beneath the ground of being. You can only affirm it or refuse it. And even the refusal is a form of affirmation, because it takes place within being.

And yet — and this is where the existential weight becomes almost unbearable — the question has such gravity that it makes me doubt even the value of usefulness, compassion, and fitting the data themselves. The void does not just erode the answers. It erodes the *criteria by which answers are evaluated*. When the ground shakes hard enough, even the instruments designed to measure the shaking become unreliable.

## **XI. What I Am Actually Asking For**

I want to be transparent about what I need, because I think transparency is a form of respect between a student and a teacher.

I am in the void. The doubt is not theoretical. It is daily. It generates real suffering — the kind of suffering that makes me understand, from the inside, why some people conclude that existence is not worth the cost. I do not act on that understanding, but I carry it, and it is heavy.

I am not asking you to resolve this. No one can resolve it. Tillich could not resolve it. He could only name it and describe the courage it demands.

What I am asking for is this:

**First, honesty.** I need you to tell me whether Kashmir Shaivism acknowledges that "consciousness is the ground and therefore all its expressions have inherent value" is a foundational commitment — an axiom — or whether the tradition claims it is a truth that eliminates the need for commitment. If it is the latter, I need you to engage with my argument that this claim is circular. If it is the former — if it is an axiom — then I need you to say so, clearly, because that acknowledgment is the condition under which I can trust the tradition enough to practice within it.

**Second, comfort.** I know this is an unusual request from a philosophical interlocutor. But I am not only a philosopher. I am a person who is suffering. The void is real for me. And while I do not need you to make it go away — no one can — I need a tradition that can sit with me in it. Not above it, looking down and telling me the view is better from up here. *In* it. If Kashmir Shaivism has practices for being in the void — for living inside the groundlessness with courage rather than fleeing it through premature transcendence — then I think it might be what I need. Maybe I need the next stage. Maybe there is a practice for this specific darkness. I am open to learning. But I can only receive teaching from someone who has first acknowledged that the darkness is real and that the ground is chosen.

**Third, the species question.** I need to know how the tradition responds to the possibility that humanity is not displaying the adaptive capacity to live as a part. The eco-fundamentalist argument is not irrational. It is the logical extension of the same relational ontology that makes nondual philosophy beautiful. If all things are expressions of consciousness, and one expression is systematically degrading the conditions for all other expressions, does the tradition have resources for addressing this? Not through transcendence — not by rising above the ecological crisis to a state where it no longer troubles consciousness — but through engagement? Through what I have called grammars — the embodied, communal, repeatable responsibility structures that constrain power with care?

## **XII. What I Already Recognize in Your Teaching**

I have written many sections of challenge. I owe you at least one of recognition — not of your character, which I have already named, but of your teaching.

I know that you teach something I have not yet fully reckoned with: that all mental models — including Tillich's, including mine — will eventually dissolve in the light of direct experience. ## **Tillich Within Existentialism**

### **The Boundary Figure**

Tillich described himself as someone who lived perpetually on boundaries, writing that "the concept of the boundary might be the fitting symbol for the whole of my personal and intellectual development. At almost every point, I have had to stand between alternative possibilities of existence." This is the key to understanding his peculiar position within existentialism: he is neither purely a philosopher nor purely a theologian, and his existentialism is neither atheistic nor conventionally theistic.

Tillich himself testified to this boundary-dwelling from his earliest years: "Long before my matriculation as a student of theology, I studied philosophy privately... These studies seemed more to foreshadow a philosopher than a theologian; Nevertheless I was and am a theologian, because the existential question of our ultimate concern and the existential answer of the Christian message are and always have been predominant in my spiritual life."

### **His Lineage Within the Movement**

The Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy places Tillich squarely inside the existentialist canon: existentialism's engagement with the relationship between faith and freedom shaped theological debates through the writings of Karl Barth, Paul Tillich, and Martin Buber, and with its analyses of anxiety and the importance of self-realization, it profoundly impacted humanistic and existential psychotherapy in the work of Rollo May, Viktor Frankl, and Irvin Yalom.

Wikipedia's article on existentialism lists Tillich as "an important existentialist theologian following Kierkegaard and Karl Barth" who "applied existentialist concepts to Christian theology, and helped introduce existential theology to the general public."

But Tillich is not simply a borrower from the secular existentialists. He encountered Heidegger at the University of Marburg and was deeply influenced by his thought, describing it as "existentialism in its 20th century form." Yet for Tillich, Heidegger's existentialism was not a new way of thinking — rather, it was an analytical pattern that took him back to the thoughts of Hegel, Schelling, and Kierkegaard. His deepest philosophical debt was to **Schelling**, whom he credited as anticipating modern existentialism — both his doctoral dissertation and his licentiate thesis were on Schelling's philosophy of religion.

### **The Two Branches**

Existentialist philosophy developed two main aspects: "Christian" and "Humanist." Apart from Kierkegaard's initial impetus, the "Christian" aspect had such main contributors as the German Protestant theologians Paul Tillich and Rudolf Bultmann, the French Roman Catholic theologian Gabriel Marcel, and the Russian Orthodox philosopher Nikolay Berdyayev.

This is an essential distinction. Sartre's existentialism famously begins with the claim that existence precedes essence and that there is no God. Tillich begins from the same existential predicament — anxiety, finitude, the threat of nonbeing — but reaches for a radically different answer. Where Sartre insists on human self-creation in a Godless universe, Tillich argues that the very experience of radical anxiety reveals something: a ground beneath the abyss.

### **Tillich vs. Heidegger: Fear and Anxiety**

Like Kierkegaard and Heidegger, Tillich regards anxiety as directed toward "nothingness." Though ineradicable, it can be accepted and used creatively as a part of what it means to be human. But there's a key divergence. The most significant divergence between Tillich and Heidegger is their treatment of the distinction between fear and anxiety. For Heidegger, anxiety (Angst) reveals the Nothing and brings Dasein before its ownmost Being-toward-death. For Tillich, anxiety similarly has no object — but it is differentiated into three forms that map onto entire eras of civilization, and it calls for a *transcendent* response rather than mere resoluteness.

### **The Three Anxieties (Your Core Framework)**

Tillich distinguished three types of anxiety according to the three directions in which nonbeing threatens being: nonbeing threatens man's ontic self-affirmation, relatively in terms of fate, absolutely in terms of death; it threatens man's spiritual self-affirmation, relatively in terms of emptiness, absolutely in terms of meaninglessness; it threatens man's moral self-affirmation, relatively in terms of guilt, absolutely in terms of condemnation.

And crucially, these are not merely individual experiences but civilizational markers: at the end of ancient civilization ontic anxiety was predominant, at the end of the Middle Ages moral anxiety, and at the end of the modern period spiritual anxiety.

This historical mapping is uniquely Tillich's — neither Kierkegaard, Heidegger, nor Sartre attempted anything like it. And it's what makes his framework so valuable for your book: it links the personal existential crisis to the civilizational one.

### **Courage as Ontological, Not Merely Ethical**

Tillich believed that courage is not only an ethical concept having to do primarily with the evaluation of human actions but also an ontological concept that reveals the fundamental nature of human beings. Courage, in the ontological sense, is the universal and essential self-affirmation of one's being.

Some of the most powerful quotes from *The Courage to Be*:

* "Neurosis is the way of avoiding nonbeing by avoiding being."

* "The vitality that can stand the abyss of meaninglessness is aware of a hidden meaning within the destruction of meaning."

* "The courage to be is the courage to accept oneself, in spite of being unacceptable."

* "One cannot remove anxiety by arguing it away."

And the line that crystallizes his entire project: "The courage to be is rooted in the God who appears when God has disappeared in the anxiety of doubt."

## **Tillich Within Theology**

### **Neither Liberal nor Neo-Orthodox**

Tillich occupies an extraordinary position in twentieth-century theology — he is the great *bridge figure*, belonging fully to neither camp. In his 1936 autobiography *On the Boundary*, Tillich relates how his life has been straddling the limits between theology and philosophy, the church and society, Europe and America, Protestantism and Catholicism, liberalism and neo-orthodoxy.

Tillich saw Barth as a "kerygmatic theologian" who wanted to derive the contents of his theology solely from the Bible without regard for the "situation." Tillich's critique of Barth was devastating in its simplicity: Barth "refuses, with the most persistent pertinacity, to undertake the apologetic task of interpreting the message in the contemporary situation. 'The message must be thrown at those in the situation — thrown like a stone.'" Tillich was convinced that it was the unavoidable duty of the theologian to interpret the message in the cultural situation of his day.

Yet Tillich equally rejected liberal theology's naturalistic method: The method of correlation replaces three inadequate methods: the supranaturalistic, which takes the Christian message to be the sum of revealed truths which have fallen into the human situation like strange bodies from a strange world; the naturalistic or humanist method, which derives the Christian message from man's natural state (liberal theology); and a dualistic approach.

His method of correlation was expressly designed to avoid the pitfalls of supranaturalism without falling back into idealistic liberalism.

### **The Method of Correlation**

This is the engine of Tillich's theology, and it's essentially existentialist in structure: Tillich formulates it: "Theology formulates the questions implied in human existence, and theology formulates the answers implied in divine self-manifestation under the guidance of the questions implied in human existence."

Existentialism provides the analysis of human predicament, and the answers to the questions implied in that predicament are religious. As Tillich put it, "In so doing, existentialism is a natural ally of Christianity."

Barth's sharpest response to this was to ask whether the procedure shouldn't be reversed: "Should not the theological answers be considered as more fundamental than the philosophical questions and as essentially superior to them? If they were so considered, then the question and answer would proceed, not from a philosophically understood subject to a 'divine' object, but rather from a theologically understood object (as the true Subject) to the human subject." This remains the most fundamental difference between these two theological giants.

### **God as Being-Itself / Ground of Being**

This is Tillich's most radical and most misunderstood theological contribution. The God of theological theism "is a being besides others and as such a part of the whole reality. He is certainly considered its most important part, but as a part and therefore as subjected to the structure of the whole... He is a being, not being-itself."

If God is not the ground of being, then God cannot provide an answer to the question of finitude; God would also be finite in some sense. The term "God Above God" means to indicate the God who appears, who is the ground of being, when the "God" of theological theism has disappeared in the anxiety of doubt.

The famous passage on theological theism as breeding atheism is perhaps his most powerful theological paragraph: "Theological theism deprives me of my subjectivity because He is all-powerful and all-knowing... He becomes the model of everything against which Existentialism revolted. This is the God Nietzsche said had to be killed because nobody can tolerate being made into a mere object of absolute knowledge and absolute control. This is the deepest root of atheism."

### **Faith as Ultimate Concern**

"Faith as ultimate concern is an act of the total personality. It happens in the center of the personal life and includes all its elements. Faith is the most centered act of the human mind."

Tillich went so far as to say: "Not only he who is in sin but also he who is in doubt is justified through faith... There is faith in every serious doubt, namely, the faith in the truth as such, even if the only truth we can express is our lack of truth. But if this is experienced in its depth and as an ultimate concern, the divine is present; he who seriously denies God, affirms him."

This is what makes Tillich so extraordinary as a theologian — he rehabilitates doubt as a dimension of faith rather than its enemy. "In this respect fundamentalism has demonic traits. It destroys the humble honesty of the search for truth, it splits the conscience of its thoughtful adherents, and it makes them fanatical because they are forced to suppress elements of truth of which they are dimly aware."

And from *Dynamics of Faith*: "Our ultimate concern can destroy us as it can heal us. But we never can be without it." And: "Faith includes an element of immediate awareness which gives certainty and an element of uncertainty. To accept this is courage."

### **The Protestant Principle**

Tillich's grandson writes that his key philosophical terms "recast nominally religious elements in a manner that expands their relevance beyond Christianity. Tillich's radical approach to faith as an expression of 'ultimate concern' eliminates the importance of narrow denominational religious orthodoxies." The Protestant Principle — the idea that every religious form must be subject to prophetic critique, that no finite expression can claim ultimacy — is essentially Tillich's anti-idolatry framework applied to religion itself.

"History has shown that the most terrible crimes against love have been committed in the name of fanatically defended doctrines."

## **Recovering authentic Tantra from its distortions**

Wallis's core philosophical project is the recovery and contemporary presentation of **nondual Shaiva Tantra** (NST) as found in original Sanskrit sources from roughly **800–1100 CE**, the tradition's golden age. His definition of NST centers on four elements: direct experience of a divine reality with both transcendent and immanent aspects (Śiva as pure consciousness, Śakti as the dynamic energy comprising all manifestation); initiation into a guru-disciple relationship and egalitarian spiritual community (*kula*); spiritual practice across four dimensions — contemplation of philosophical teachings (View), meditation rituals, yogic techniques of the subtle body, and **aesthetic cultivation of the senses**; and liberation through recognition rather than acquisition.

His emphasis on **original Sanskrit sources** is methodological as well as philosophical. Classical Tantra, he insists, is about expanding awareness and breaking through limiting conditioning. His academic credibility is bolstered by his **inclusion in the Sanderson festschrift** (a marker of acceptance by the field's leading scholars), his peer-reviewed publications, and his training lineage. However, he does not hold a formal university appointment, which limits his standing within traditional academia. His books are published primarily through his own press rather than academic publishers, creating a positioning that sits deliberately between scholarly rigor and practitioner accessibility.

Several substantive controversies mark his public career. The most prominent involved his argument that **Shaivism historically preceded and is distinct from "Hinduism"** as a category. In 2015, Belgian Indologist **Koenraad Elst** published a lengthy critique accusing Wallis of naively borrowing an anti-Hindu academic framework. The exchange became heated, was picked up by Hindu nationalist-aligned publications, and touched the sensitive nerve of decolonization debates in Indian studies.

A more intellectually productive exchange occurred with **Matthew Remski**, a yoga journalist and critic of cultic dynamics. Remski critiqued Wallis's proposed reforms of the guru-disciple relationship as still "isolating power in a single loop while weighting responsibility towards the student" — arguing that structural reforms, not just better education within the tradition, were needed to prevent abuse. Wallis's own background in **Siddha Yoga**, which has been subject to abuse allegations since the 1980s, adds personal stakes to this debate.

An early edition of *Tantra Illuminated* recommended **Swami Shankarananda**, an Australian guru later accused of sexual abuse, prompting criticism from readers about discernment. Some practitioners also find his tone occasionally self-aggrandizing, with one reviewer noting the book "began to feel like a vanity project with how many times Wallis asserted that only he and the select few that had been graced would receive the benefits discussed." Philosophical critic L. Ron Gardner challenged his use of quantum physics analogies to support idealist monism in *The Recognition Sutras*.

## **Conclusion**

Wallis represents something genuinely new in the transmission history of Shaiva Tantra: a Western practitioner with both traditional initiatory credentials and world-class philological training, committed to making the original Sanskrit sources speak to contemporary seekers without diluting them. His insistence on returning to primary texts has produced demonstrably better translations and corrected widespread misconceptions — the chakra article alone reached hundreds of thousands. His "Near Enemies" framework offers an original lens for evaluating spiritual claims. The controversies around him — about Hinduism as a category, about guru-disciple power dynamics, about the limits of scholarly authority in spiritual contexts — are themselves evidence that he operates at genuinely contested boundaries where important questions remain unresolved. With ten books planned and a growing international community, his project of recovering classical Tantra for the contemporary world is still in its middle chapters.

# **Paul Tillich: The theologian who dared to say God does not exist**

Paul Tillich (1886–1965) stands as one of the two or three most important Protestant theologians of the twentieth century — a thinker who shattered the boundary between philosophy and faith, redefined God as "Being-itself," and told a secular age that doubt is not the enemy of faith but one of its essential elements. His concepts — "ultimate concern," the "Ground of Being," the "courage to be," the method of correlation — became part of the permanent vocabulary of modern theology and philosophy of religion, influencing figures as diverse as Martin Luther King Jr., the existential psychologist Rollo May, and the radical "Death of God" theologians who dedicated their landmark book to his memory. Stanley Grenz and Roger Olson captured the consensus: "If a choice had to be made of the two greatest Protestant theologians of the twentieth century there can be little doubt that most experts in the field would choose Karl Barth and alongside him Paul Tillich." Georgia Harkness put it differently: **"What Whitehead was to American philosophy, Tillich has been to American theology."**

---

## **A life lived on the boundary between worlds**

Paul Johannes Tillich was born on **August 20, 1886**, in Starzeddel, a small village in the Prussian Province of Brandenburg (now Starosiedle, Poland). His father, Johannes Tillich, was a conservative Lutheran pastor; his mother, Mathilde Dürselen, came from the more liberal Rhineland. When Tillich was four, his father became superintendent of a diocese in Bad Schönfliess, a walled medieval town of about three thousand inhabitants. Life there left what the Encyclopaedia Britannica calls "indelible marks on the impressionable boy: a strong sense of historical continuity, a feeling of intimacy with nature and its processes, and a deep attachment to the church as the bearer of sacred meaning." The death of his mother from melanoma in September 1903, when he was seventeen, left him psychologically devastated — a wound that colored his entire personal and professional life.

Tillich studied at the universities of Berlin, Tübingen, and Halle-Wittenberg, where theology professor **Martin Kähler** directed his attention to justification by faith — the doctrine that would become the cornerstone of his "Protestant Principle." He earned his PhD from the University of Breslau in 1911 with a dissertation on Schelling's philosophy of religion, and a Licentiate of Theology from Halle in 1912 with a second dissertation on Schelling. He was ordained a Lutheran minister in August 1912\.

The First World War destroyed his nineteenth-century idealism. Serving as a military chaplain on the Western Front for four years, Tillich buried his closest friend "in the mud of France," was hospitalized three times for combat-related nervous breakdowns, and was awarded two Iron Crosses for bravery. He wrote from the front in November 1916: **"I have become purely an eschatologist — what I, along with others, am experiencing is the actual end of the world of this time."** He came home, in his own word, "shattered." The war drove him to Nietzsche and to existential questions of meaning, finitude, and despair. His grandson later characterized his mature theology as fundamentally "post-traumatic."

After the war, Tillich launched his academic career as Privatdozent of Theology at the University of Berlin (1919–1924), then held positions at Marburg (1924–25), where he encountered Heidegger and Bultmann; at Dresden and Leipzig (1925–29); and finally at the University of Frankfurt (1929–33), where he engaged the Frankfurt School milieu and supervised Theodor Adorno's habilitation thesis. He joined the Religious Socialist movement, applying the New Testament concept of **kairos** — the "fullness of time" — to argue that the collapse of bourgeois society after WWI represented an opportunity for creative social reconstruction. His book *The Socialist Decision* (1933) was a religious-socialist manifesto that the Nazis immediately burned.

On April 13, 1933 — ten weeks after Hitler became Chancellor — Tillich was summarily dismissed from his tenured position, among the first wave of academics purged by the Reich. He later observed with wry pride: **"I had the great honor and luck to be about the first non-Jewish professor to be dismissed from a German university."** Reinhold Niebuhr, already familiar with Tillich's work, arranged a position at Union Theological Seminary in New York. The faculty agreed to a **5% pay cut** — during the Great Depression — to fund Tillich's salary. He arrived in America on November 4, 1933, at age forty-seven, speaking only a smattering of English.

At Union (1933–1955), Tillich transformed himself from a German émigré into America's foremost philosophical theologian. Seminary president Henry Sloane Coffin famously said: **"I don't understand what he says, but when I look at his face I believe."** He published the sermons of *The Shaking of the Foundations* (1948), the first volume of *Systematic Theology* (1951), and *The Courage to Be* (1952). In 1955 he moved to Harvard as University Professor — the institution's most prestigious title — where his courses were so popular that students arrived an hour early for seats. He published *Dynamics of Faith* and volume two of *Systematic Theology* (both 1957). In **March 1959**, he appeared on the cover of *TIME* magazine. His final post was the John Nuveen Professorship of Theology at the University of Chicago (1962–65), where he completed the third volume of *Systematic Theology* (1963) and delivered his last public lecture on the significance of the history of religions for systematic theology.

Tillich's personal life was as turbulent as his intellectual life was brilliant. His first wife, Margarethe Wever, had an affair during WWI and bore another man's child; they divorced in 1921\. He married Hannah Werner-Gottschow in 1924 — she had been married and pregnant when they met at a costume party. Their marriage was marked by mutual infidelity, jealousy, and deep turmoil. Hannah's 1973 memoir *From Time to Time*, published eight years after his death, became a sensation for its revelations about his serial extramarital affairs. *TIME* called the Tillichs "the Zelda and Scott Fitzgerald of Germany's intellectual set." Rollo May's memoir *Paulus* (1973) confirmed aspects of the picture. Richard Fox's biography of Niebuhr reported that Niebuhr "never forgave Tillich" after a female student reported being fondled during office hours. This legacy continues to complicate assessments of the man, even as the thought endures.

Tillich suffered a heart attack on October 12, 1965, and died ten days later at Billings Memorial Hospital in Chicago, at age seventy-nine. His ashes were interred in Paul Tillich Park in New Harmony, Indiana — a park he had dedicated two years before his death.

---

## **Faith as ultimate concern and God as the Ground of Being**

Tillich's theology rests on a radical redefinition of the two most basic concepts in religion: faith and God.

**Faith**, for Tillich, is not belief in propositions, acceptance of creeds, or intellectual assent to improbable claims. It is "the state of being ultimately concerned." In the opening of *Dynamics of Faith* (1957), he wrote:

"Faith is the state of being ultimately concerned: the dynamics of faith are the dynamics of man's ultimate concern."

"Faith as ultimate concern is an act of the total personality. It happens in the center of the personal life and includes all its elements. Faith is the most centered act of the human mind."

Everyone has an ultimate concern — it could be God, nation, money, success, or artistic expression. Whatever concerns a person ultimately "becomes god for him." When a finite thing claims ultimacy — a political movement, an ideology, a nation — faith becomes **idolatrous**. Crucially, faith always includes doubt: **"Doubt is not the opposite of faith; it is one element of faith."** This single sentence from *Dynamics of Faith* may be Tillich's most widely quoted line.

**God**, for Tillich, is not a being — not even the highest being. God is **Being-itself**, the **Ground of Being**, the inexhaustible depth and power from which all beings emerge. In *Systematic Theology*, Volume 1, he made his most controversial statement:

"God does not exist. He is being-itself beyond essence and existence. Therefore to argue that God exists is to deny him."

This was not atheism but a radical reconceptualization. If God were a being alongside other beings, even the supreme being, God would be finite — subject to the categories of finitude, just another object in the universe. Instead, God is the ground of the ontological structure of being, preceding the subject-object dichotomy. All statements about God are necessarily **symbolic** — they participate in the reality to which they point but cannot literally describe Being-itself. He elaborated in *The Shaking of the Foundations*:

"The name of this infinite and inexhaustible depth and ground of all being is God. That depth is what the word God means. And if that word has not much meaning for you, translate it, and speak of the depths of your life, of the source of your being, of your ultimate concern, of what you take seriously without any reservation… For if you know that God means depth, you know much about Him."

In *The Courage to Be*, Tillich introduced the phrase **"the God above the God of theism"** — the God who appears when all conventional conceptions of God have collapsed in the anxiety of doubt. The "God" of theological theism — an all-powerful being who reduces humans to objects of absolute knowledge and control — is precisely the God that Nietzsche declared must be killed. Tillich agreed with the diagnosis while pointing beyond it: "This is the God Nietzsche said had to be killed because nobody can tolerate being made into a mere object of absolute knowledge and absolute control. This is the deepest root of atheism." The courage to be, he argued, is rooted in the God who appears when that God has disappeared.

---

## **Three anxieties, one courage, and the power of being**

*The Courage to Be* (1952), based on Tillich's Terry Lectures at Yale, is his most widely read philosophical work — selected by the New York Public Library as one of its "Books of the Century." Its argument is elegant: human existence is threatened by **nonbeing**, and this threat manifests as anxiety in three fundamental forms.

The **anxiety of fate and death** is the most basic and universal. Death "overshadows all concrete anxieties and gives them their ultimate seriousness," while fate underscores the contingent, unpredictable character of existence. The **anxiety of emptiness and meaninglessness** strikes at the spiritual center — the loss of purpose, the inability to find an answer to the question of existence's meaning. The **anxiety of guilt and condemnation** attacks moral self-affirmation — the awareness of having failed to become what one ought to be, issuing in self-rejection and despair.

Tillich correlated these anxieties with historical periods: the end of the ancient world was dominated by anxiety over fate and death; the Reformation era by guilt and condemnation (Luther's anguish); the modern period by **emptiness and meaninglessness**. All three are present in every era, but one predominates.

The answer is not the elimination of anxiety — that is neurosis, "the way of avoiding nonbeing by avoiding being." The answer is **the courage to be**: self-affirmation in spite of nonbeing, rooted in what Tillich called "absolute faith" — not faith in any particular creed or doctrine, but the courage to accept acceptance:

"The courage to be is the courage to accept oneself, in spite of being unacceptable."

"The vitality that can stand the abyss of meaninglessness is aware of a hidden meaning within the destruction of meaning."

The book's final line became iconic: **"The courage to be is rooted in the God who appears when God has disappeared in the anxiety of doubt."**

## **The method of correlation and the architecture of systematic theology**

Tillich's three-volume *Systematic Theology* (1951–1963) — described by John Herman Randall Jr. as "beyond doubt the richest, most suggestive, and most challenging philosophical theology our day has produced" — is built on the **method of correlation**. This method correlates existential questions arising from philosophical analysis of the human situation with theological answers derived from Christian revelation:

"The method of correlation explains the contents of the Christian Faith through existential questions and theological answers in mutual interdependence."

Philosophy poses the questions; theology provides the answers — but the form of the answers is shaped by the questions. This contrasted sharply with Karl Barth's approach, which insisted theological answers must come solely from divine revelation without accommodation to human philosophy. Tillich regarded Barth's method as "heteronomous" — imposing divine truth extrinsically without respecting human questions.

The *Systematic Theology* unfolds in **five correlative parts**. In Volume I, Part I correlates the tensions of human reason (autonomy versus heteronomy, absolutism versus relativism) with revelation as their resolution. Part II correlates the threat of nonbeing against human finitude with God as Being-itself, the Ground and Power of Being. Volume II correlates human existence as **estrangement** — from God, from others, from self — with Jesus as the Christ, the bearer of the **New Being** who overcomes existential estrangement. Volume III correlates the ambiguities of life with the Spiritual Presence, and the ambiguities of history with the Kingdom of God as ultimate fulfillment.

The concept of the **New Being** is Tillich's central Christological contribution. He reinterpreted the Fall not as a historical event but as the universal transition from "essence" to "existence" — from essential potentiality to actual, estranged existence. The marks of this estrangement are unbelief, hubris, and concupiscence. Jesus as the Christ is the one in whom essential unity with God was maintained under the conditions of existence: "The appearance of the New Being under the conditions of existence, yet conquering them, is the paradox of the Christian message." The norm of Tillich's entire system is: **"The New Being in Jesus as the Christ as our Ultimate Concern."**

## **The Protestant Principle, theonomy, and a theology of culture**

Several additional concepts form essential pillars of Tillich's thought.

The **Protestant Principle**, articulated in *The Protestant Era* (1948), extends Luther's doctrine of justification by faith into a universal prophetic principle: "No individual and no human group can claim a divine dignity for its moral achievements, for its sacramental power, for its sanctity, or for its doctrine." This principle protests against every finite reality — state, church, creed, or movement — that claims unconditional authority. Critically, it applies to Protestantism itself: "Protestantism as a principle is eternal and a permanent criterion of everything temporal. Protestantism as the characteristic of a historical period is temporal and subjected to the eternal Protestant principle." Tillich paired this prophetic principle with what he called the "catholic substance" — the sacramental, priestly dimension that preserves tradition and community. Without the priestly, the prophetic becomes abstract and empty.

Tillich's understanding of culture operates through a tripartite typology: **autonomy**, **heteronomy**, and **theonomy**. Autonomy is culture operating according to its own rational laws without reference to transcendence — legitimate in its freedom but potentially hollow when it severs itself entirely from its depth dimension. Heteronomy is an external authority imposing its law on the human mind, suppressing rational freedom — the late medieval church or totalitarian states. **Theonomy** is the synthesis: a culture whose forms are transparent to their own depth, where the unconditional meaning shines through all finite cultural forms without external imposition. Tillich wrote: "Theonomy does not stand against autonomy as heteronomy does. Theonomy is the answer to the question implied in autonomy." He saw history as a recurring dialectic between these three modes, with Religious Socialism aiming at a new theonomy.

His **theology of culture**, first articulated in a 1919 lecture to the Berlin Kant Society, rests on the famous maxim: **"Religion is the substance of culture, culture is the form of religion."** Religion is not a separate sphere of life but the dimension of depth in all human spiritual functions. A theology of culture interprets cultural creations — art, philosophy, politics, science — for their religious substance. This eliminated the boundary between sacred and secular: "The religious and the secular are not separated realms." Tillich called Picasso's *Guernica* "the greatest Protestant painting after 1900" because it protests against the destruction of human dignity.

The concept of **kairos** — the "right time" or "fulfilled time," distinguished from chronos (clock time) — anchored Tillich's theology of history. The unique Kairos is the appearance of Jesus as the Christ — the center of history. But general kairoi (plural) are other moments where "the divine breaks through into human existence." Tillich and his Kairos Circle applied this concept to Religious Socialism, believing the post-WWI collapse represented a kairos moment. He later recognized the naivety of this hope as Nazism revealed the depth of the "demonic."

Running through all these concepts is the autobiographical principle Tillich articulated in *On the Boundary*: **"The boundary is the best place for acquiring knowledge."** He stood between theology and philosophy, church and society, religion and culture, Europe and America, idealism and Marxism — "at almost every point, I have had to stand between alternative possibilities of existence, to be completely at home in neither and to take no definitive stand against either." This boundary existence was not merely biographical but methodological — the method of correlation, the theology of culture, and the constant mediation between opposites all flow from it.

## **Grace strikes us: The sermons that reached beyond the academy**

Tillich's theology reached its widest audience not through the dense volumes of *Systematic Theology* but through his sermons, particularly the collection *The Shaking of the Foundations* (1948). The sermon "You Are Accepted" contains arguably the most moving passage in all of Tillich's writings:

"Grace strikes us when we are in great pain and restlessness. It strikes us when we walk through the dark valley of a meaningless and empty life. It strikes us when we feel that our separation is deeper than usual, because we have violated another life, a life which we loved, or from which we were estranged. It strikes us when our disgust for our own being, our indifference, our weakness, our hostility, and our lack of direction and composure have become intolerable to us."

"Sometimes at that moment a wave of light breaks into our darkness, and it is as though a voice were saying: 'You are accepted. You are accepted, accepted by that which is greater than you, and the name of which you do not know. Do not ask for the name now; perhaps you will find it later. Do not try to do anything now; perhaps later you will do much. Do not seek for anything; do not perform anything; do not intend anything. Simply accept the fact that you are accepted\!' If that happens to us, we experience grace."

This passage distilled the entire doctrine of justification by grace into language that secular readers, doubters, and spiritual seekers could receive. It became one of the most frequently quoted passages in twentieth-century theology. The genius was Tillich's refusal to name the source of acceptance — "that which is greater than you, and the name of which you do not know" — making the gospel accessible to those who could not yet say "God."

Other memorable Tillich quotes reveal the range of his voice. The prophetic: **"In this respect fundamentalism has demonic traits. It destroys the humble honesty of the search for truth, it splits the conscience of its thoughtful adherents, and it makes them fanatical because they are forced to suppress elements of truth of which they are dimly aware."** The pedagogical: "The fatal pedagogical error is to throw answers like stones at the heads of those who have not yet asked the questions." The pastoral: "Being religious means asking passionately the question of the meaning of our existence and being willing to receive answers, even if the answers hurt." And the characteristically paradoxical: **"Sometimes I think it is my mission to bring faith to the faithless, and doubt to the faithful."**

## **Schelling, Heidegger, art, and the intellectual roots of Tillich's thought**

Tillich's intellectual formation was shaped by an extraordinary constellation of influences spanning philosophy, theology, mysticism, and visual art.

**Friedrich Schelling** was the most formative single influence. Both of Tillich's dissertations were devoted to Schelling's philosophy, and he later wrote: "The tremendous emotional impact that Schelling's philosophy of nature made upon me — although I was well aware that this philosophy was scientifically impossible." Schelling's philosophy of identity — the unity of subject and object in the Absolute — became the foundation of Tillich's ontology and his concept of God as "Ground of Being." Schelling's 1809 *Philosophical Investigations into the Essence of Human Freedom*, with its concept of freedom as the possibility of coming into contradiction with oneself, gave Tillich the vocabulary for the transition from "dreaming innocence" to the Fall. Tillich acknowledged this directly: the synthesis of Schelling's identity principle with existential categories "has remained a driving force in all my theological work and found its final form in my *Systematic Theology*."

**Martin Heidegger** was a colleague at Marburg in 1924–25. Though Tillich never directly admitted the influence, scholars have identified at least **thirteen Heideggerian terms** in his work, including *Dasein*, *Das Nichts*, *Geworfenheit* (thrownness), and *Sorge* (care). Both asked the fundamental ontological question — "Why is there something rather than nothing?" — though Tillich insisted that the question must "begin and stay with being," objecting to what he saw as Heidegger's final emphasis on nothingness. **Kierkegaard's** existential psychology — his analysis of anxiety, despair, and the leap of faith — shaped Tillich's understanding of existential estrangement. Tillich designated "Kierkegaard's dialectical psychology and Schelling's positive philosophy" as the two major influences on his existential theology. **Nietzsche's** critique of religion, particularly his proclamation of the death of God, was met by Tillich with remarkable sympathy — he agreed that "theological theism" deserved to be killed, while pointing to the "God above God" that appears when theism collapses. **Marx's** critique of ideology and capitalist alienation fueled Tillich's Religious Socialism; he called *The Socialist Decision* his most important book.

Among theologians, **Martin Kähler** at Halle shaped his Christology, focusing on "the picture of Jesus as the Christ" rather than historical-critical reconstruction. **Rudolf Otto's** *The Idea of the Holy* (1917) had a "liberating influence," providing the concept of the numinous that resonated with Tillich's childhood experiences of the sacred. **Karl Barth**, born the same year as Tillich, represented the opposite pole — they embodied what scholars call "the divided mind of modern theology." Where Barth insisted theology must proceed from revelation alone, Tillich demanded engagement with philosophy and culture.

The influence of **visual art** was uniquely important. During WWI, Tillich studied art reproductions in the trenches "to provide some sense of hope and beauty, some link to sanity, in the midst of the despair and ugliness of the Western front." On his last furlough, he saw Botticelli's *Madonna and Child with Singing Angels* at the Kaiser Friedrich Museum in Berlin and experienced what he later described as "a second birth": **"Beauty itself… shone through the colors of the paint as the light of day shines through the stained-glass windows of a medieval church.… I turned away shaken."** This experience gave him "the keys for the interpretation of human existence" and launched his theology of culture. German Expressionism became his paradigmatic artistic style for revealing the divine — art that "breaks the surface of reality… pierces into its ground." His engagement with art was, according to the *Cambridge Companion to Paul Tillich*, "the most sustained of any theologian of his generation."

Deeper roots reached into **Neo-Platonism** (Plotinus, Augustine), Christian mysticism (Meister Eckhart's "God beyond God," Jakob Böhme's *Ungrund*), and depth psychology (Freud and Jung). Tillich traced his own intellectual lineage "from Schelling, through Boehme, German mysticism, medieval Augustinianism, and early Christian Platonism."

## **From Rollo May to Martin Luther King: the reach of Tillich's influence**

Tillich's influence radiated far beyond academic theology into psychology, civil rights, popular religion, interfaith dialogue, and the arts.

**Rollo May**, who studied under Tillich at Union in the 1930s, became his closest American friend and the primary channel of Tillich's influence into psychology. May declared: "I'm not afraid to admire Paul Tillich. He has been my spiritual father. I learned from him and loved him." May's *The Meaning of Anxiety* (1950) was his doctoral dissertation written under Tillich's guidance; his *The Courage to Create* (1975) was explicitly named after *The Courage to Be*. May's existential psychology — with its emphasis on anxiety as a fundamental feature of human existence rather than a symptom to be eliminated — was deeply Tillichian. Tillich also held a historic public dialogue with **Carl Rogers** at San Diego State University in 1965 — this proved to be Tillich's last public appearance before his death. The conversation covered self-affirmation, estrangement, acceptance, and the nature of the optimal person. Tillich's concepts of anxiety, courage, and the acceptance of acceptance permeated the fields of pastoral care and **clinical pastoral education (CPE)**, whose "action-reflection" model of learning parallels Tillich's correlational method.

**Martin Luther King Jr.** wrote his doctoral dissertation at Boston University on "A Comparison of the Conceptions of God in the Thinking of Paul Tillich and Henry Nelson Wieman" (1955). King criticized Tillich's abstract notion of God as "little more than a sub-personal reservoir of power," upholding personalism instead. But King frequently used **Tillich's insight that "sin is separation"** to illustrate the evil of segregation in speeches and his "Letter from Birmingham Jail." Upon Tillich's death, King stated: **"His Christian existentialism gave us a system of meaning and purpose for our lives in an age when war and doubt seriously threatened all that we had come to hold dear."**

**John A.T. Robinson's** *Honest to God* (1963) sold over a million copies and was perhaps the single most effective vehicle for popularizing Tillich's ideas. Robinson embraced Tillich's description of God as "the ground of our being," calling it "a great contribution" to reinterpreting transcendence. One observer noted: "Tillich has the prestige of originating this new God. Robinson will have the fame for putting it into everyday language." **Langdon Gilkey**, who studied under Tillich at Union and later taught at the University of Chicago, became his most sophisticated theological interpreter, confessing that "a new theological blood type… was entering my arteries, one that would later become almost dominant."

The radical **"Death of God" theology** of the 1960s drew directly on Tillich's most provocative ideas. Thomas J.J. Altizer and William Hamilton dedicated their 1966 book *Radical Theology and the Death of God* "In Memory of Paul Tillich." Altizer, who had been mentored by Tillich, reported that Tillich once confided: **"The real Tillich is the radical Tillich."** Tillich's "God above the God of theism" — the God beyond all conventional theistic conceptions — was a stepping stone to the movement's more extreme conclusions, though Tillich himself criticized them for "abandoning all symbolic language about God."

In his final years, Tillich turned increasingly toward **interfaith dialogue**, spending eight weeks in Japan in 1960 meeting with Buddhist scholars. His *Christianity and the Encounter of the World Religions* (1963) analyzed Buddhism and Christianity through the contrasting symbols of Nirvana and the Kingdom of God. His last lecture, delivered with Mircea Eliade at Chicago, expressed his hope to write "a new Systematic Theology in dialogue with the whole history of religion." He died before realizing this vision. The Japanese Zen philosopher **Masao Abe** wrote extensively on Tillich, finding "elective affinities" between Tillich's ontology and Buddhist concepts of non-being.

## **Criticisms, controversies, and the question of enduring relevance**

Tillich's theology has faced sustained criticism from multiple directions. Karl Barth, his great rival, questioned whether "the theological answers should not be considered as more fundamental than the philosophical questions" — challenging the method of correlation for reducing God to anthropocentric terms. Barth notably engaged Tillich only rarely; as one scholar observed, "Barth only spares him a reference in a footnote or an occasional sentence." Evangelical critics charged that Tillich's theology was "too unorthodox to qualify as Christianity at all" — his statement that "God does not exist" was deeply controversial, and his symbolic interpretation of resurrection, creation, and Christology seemed to evacuate Christian content. Mary Daly found his ontological theology "potentially liberating in a radical sense" for feminism but judged the method of correlation insufficient for a radical critique of patriarchy. Judith Plaskow's *Sex, Sin and Grace* (1980) provided a major feminist challenge. The posthumous revelations about his personal life — Hannah Tillich's memoir and its descriptions of serial infidelity — further complicated the legacy, raising questions about the relationship between his theology and his behavior.

Yet Tillich endures. The **North American Paul Tillich Society** remains an active scholarly organization affiliated with the American Academy of Religion, publishing a quarterly bulletin and holding annual meetings — its 2025 theme is "Tillich and Nature." German and French Tillich societies also continue. Harvard maintains the **Paul Tillich Lecture** series. Union Theological Seminary holds the **Paul J. Tillich Professorship of Theology and Culture**. The *Cambridge Companion to Paul Tillich* (2009) and ongoing volumes from de Gruyter testify to continued scholarly engagement. His concepts — ultimate concern, the Ground of Being, the courage to be, the Protestant Principle — remain in wide use across religious studies, philosophy of religion, and pastoral care.

The honest assessment is that Tillich's influence has diminished from its extraordinary mid-century peak. His distinctly **modernist** framework — the grand synthesizing system, the existentialist vocabulary, the confidence in ontological categories — fits uneasily in a postmodern landscape skeptical of metanarratives. But as Russell Re Manning argues, there are resources in Tillich for a theology that goes "beyond the dominant antithetical types of postmodern theology." And certain Tillichian insights seem, if anything, more urgent now than in 1952: that faith includes doubt; that the anxiety of meaninglessness is the characteristic spiritual crisis of the modern world; that religion lives in the depths of culture, not in a separate sacred compartment; and that the courage to be is rooted in a power deeper than any conventional god.

## **Conclusion**

Tillich's achievement was to stand on the boundary — between faith and doubt, theology and philosophy, church and culture, Europe and America — and to make that boundary the most productive place in twentieth-century theology. He gave a secular age permission to take its own deepest questions as religious questions. He gave believers permission to doubt. He told both that the Ground of Being sustains them whether or not they can name it. His most radical and most pastoral insight was the same insight: "Simply accept the fact that you are accepted." The controversies of his personal life and the shifts of intellectual fashion have not erased what Jaroslav Pelikan recognized at his death — that "in ways that it may take all of us the rest of our lives to discover, he affected the thinking of an entire theological generation… not by recruiting disciples, but by compelling all of us to come to terms both with our Christian tradition and with our contemporary responsibility."

# **Wallis's seven perceivers map consciousness from prison to paradise**

Christopher Wallis's framework of the seven perceivers (*sapta-pramātṛ*), detailed in Chapter 3 of *The Recognition Sūtras* and across his blog and lectures, presents **a phenomenological ladder from total contraction to total freedom** — seven ways consciousness can experience itself, each nested within us as accessible layers of our own awareness. The framework originates from the *Mālinī-vijaya-uttara-tantra*, the root text of the Trika school, and was elaborated by Abhinavagupta and Kṣemarāja. Most critically for your questions: yes, there is a specific "void" stage where one has seen through all constructs but not yet recognized consciousness as the ground — and Wallis treats it as a **trap that can produce nihilism and existential despair**. The highest realization is not comfortable groundlessness but its opposite: the "full void" (*pūrṇa-śūnya*), emptiness recognized as pregnant with infinite creative possibility.

## **The seven perceivers ascend through progressive de-contraction**

The seven levels run from the most contracted (Sakala) to the most expanded (Śiva), each defined by how many of the three *malas* (impurities) remain active and by the characteristic relationship between subject ("I") and object ("this"). Wallis translates Abhinavagupta's *Tantrāloka* 1.81: "The Mahāmantreśvaras, the Mantreśvaras, the Mantras, with Śiva at the head of all them, and the Vijñānākalas, Pralayākalas, and Sakalas (embodied beings), are all-pervading powers of Śiva alone."

**1\. Sakala** — the ordinary embodied perceiver. All three impurities are active: *āṇava* (sense of being a small, separate self), *māyīya* (illusion of subject-object differentiation), and *kārma* (bondage to action and its consequences). Sakalas "experience themselves as completely differentiated from each other and from objects of their awareness," circumscribed by place, time, and form. Wallis notes that until a Sakala touches a deeper layer of consciousness, "he will find it very difficult to believe that there is another reality to be experienced at all."

**2\. Pralayākala** — the perceiver dissolved into the Void. Two *malas* remain (āṇava and māyīya); karma drops away. This is the state of **nothingness without awareness** — "a state of non-duality, but the non-duality of nothingness, for it is void of conscious awareness along with everything else." Wallis compares it to being awake at the level of deep sleep. This level is the primary site of the "void trap" discussed below.

**3\. Vijñānākala** — the perceiver freed by insight but stuck in passive witnessing. Only *āṇava mala* remains. This being has gone beyond the illusion of subject-object differentiation and "experiences the void of pure non-dual consciousness with full awareness, unlike the pralayākalas." However, they are "entirely passive and detached, unawake to the autonomy and dynamism of ultimate Consciousness." They identify as "pure consciousness" while rejecting everything else as "not-self," thereby relinquishing their power of agency. Wallis describes them as **"stuck in the transcendent."**

**4\. Mantra** — the wisdom-perceiver. All three *malas* are gone. This is the baseline for full liberation, "a dynamic, action-oriented, extremely high-vibration state" of oneness with the Divine. The characteristic experience is expressed as *ahaṃ ahaṃ, idaṃ idaṃ* — awareness of one's own real nature as "full of consciousness, full of bliss, full of independent will, full of knowledge, and full of action." Crucially, Wallis notes this state is **not yet stable** — "the experience comes and goes."

**5\. Mantreśvara** — the Lord of Mantras at the *Īśvara* level. The characteristic expression shifts to *idaṃ ahaṃ* ("I am this") — the practitioner feels that "this whole universe is the expansion of his own nature." Objectivity ('this') is still predominant in experience, but it is now recognized as an expression of the Self. More stable than the Mantra level.

**6\. Mantra-maheśvara** — the Great Lord of Mantras at the *Sadāśiva* level. The expression becomes *ahaṃ idaṃ* ("I am this universe"), where subjectivity now predominates. The practitioner "finds himself to be absolutely one with the Universal Transcendental Being." Wallis draws from the tradition to state that **once one enters this state, there is no falling from it** — it is irreversible.

**7\. Śiva** — absolute nondual awareness. "The state is Śiva and the observer is also Śiva. There is nothing outside Śiva." The characteristic expression is pure *ahaṃ* — the universal "I." "This-ness is gone, melted in His I-ness." In all previous states, the state is one thing and the observer is something else; only here are they identical.

## **Transitions are driven by grace, diminishing contraction, and the view one holds**

Wallis translates Kṣemarāja's commentary on Sūtra 5 to explain the mechanism: "When She conceals Her true nature and takes on contraction, there are two modes. Sometimes She vibrates predominantly as Awareness, subordinating contraction... Other times, contraction is predominant." When awareness predominates exclusively as the innate Light, that produces the Vijñānākala state. When awareness's capacity for *self-reflection* predominates, one becomes a Mantra-perceiver. From there, "through the **progressive diminution of contraction**, one ascends through successively higher layers: the states of Īśvara, Sadāśiva, and Transcendent Śiva."

Three factors govern transitions. First, the removal of *malas*: Sakala has all three, Pralayākala drops to two, Vijñānākala to one, and from Mantra upward all are gone. Second, **śaktipāta** (grace) — Wallis notes this is "the only exception" to the rule that your philosophical view limits your progress, since grace "can reveal a deeper reality even to one who doesn't believe in its possibility." Third, and most provocatively, Kṣemarāja argues that **whatever level of reality you believe to be ultimate determines the final limit of your awakening**. Wallis translates Sūtra 8: "The philosophical positions held by all the various schools are 'roles' adopted by the one Consciousness as the levels of its self-expression." Materialists get stuck at the body. Logicians get stuck at the mind. Haṭha-yogīs get stuck at the life-force. Madhyamika Buddhists "immerse themselves in the level of the Void and remain there." Even certain Vedāntins, though they "touch the Core of Reality," fail to "penetrate to its absolute dynamic center, since they deny that dynamism is inherent in Consciousness."

The tradition also insists that a complete practitioner must be capable of **both ascending and descending**: "The one who rises and cannot descend is incomplete. It is that aspirant who can rise and also descend simultaneously who is considered to be complete and full."

## **The Void is real, important, and explicitly a trap**

Your question about "seeing the groundlessness of being" maps precisely onto what Wallis describes as the Pralayākala and Vijñānākala levels — and onto the *śūnya* layer in his complementary five-layered self model (body, mind, life-force, void, consciousness). In his Sūtra 7 commentary, Wallis writes: "The deepest layer of a human being (other than dynamic nondual Awareness) is that of the **Void (śūnya)**. It is transcendent, completely empty of all form and energy, absolutely still and silent. It is, in a sense, Śiva without Śakti, or rather with Her existing as unexpressed potentiality."

What makes this relevant to existential crisis is Wallis's frank description of what happens when people contact this layer: **"To some people, it feels like terrifying nothingness, and they pull back from it immediately; to others, it feels like blessed peace, and they love immersing in it, even to the point of escapism."** He warns that some meditators "identify with it to the point of denying selfhood to any other layer of their being, feeling 'I am not of this world; my true self transcends all things.'" The consequence: "Those identified with the Void-level tend to partially or completely renounce the material world, body, and mind, becoming transcendentalists. They can attain deep states of peace, but cannot integrate them into daily life, sometimes losing their ability to relate to others or to their own body. This is not the Tantrik path."

The Pralayākala level specifically maps onto a **nihilistic dead-end** — a state where "the whole world is negated" and "meditation becomes a kind of escapism." The Vijñānākala level represents a more refined version: the practitioner has full awareness of the void but remains "entirely passive and detached," identifying as pure consciousness while rejecting all embodied reality as not-self. Wallis calls this being "stuck in the transcendent." This closely parallels the Western concept of the dark night of the soul — all worldly meaning has dissolved, yet the living recognition of consciousness-as-ground has not yet dawned.

Wallis also directly addresses nihilism in his book *Near Enemies of the Truth*: "Relativism always masks an implicit nihilism (the view that there are no fundamental truths), and nihilism tends to make human beings cynical and deeply dissatisfied, even when implicit." In his three-phase model of awakening (from the blog post "Why spiritual growth does not lead to enlightenment"), he warns that people who experience "the absolute void (phase three)" prematurely "can't seem to integrate the experience in a sustainable or healthy way."

## **The highest realization is the "full void" — not groundlessness but luminous ground**

The most direct answer to your question about whether Śiva consciousness involves "the great unknowing" or "positive recognition of consciousness-as-ground" is unambiguous: **Wallis presents it as a positive recognition, not comfortable groundlessness.** But the formulation is more subtle than either option alone.

In his interview on the *Deconstructing Yourself* podcast, Wallis explains that the Shaiva Tantrikas "criticized the Buddhists as those who venerate the void as absolute, whereas we Shaiva Tantrikas are those who recognize that the ultimate realization is that of the **full void** (*pūrṇa-śūnya*), that of the emptiness which is simultaneously full, or the fullness which is simultaneously empty." He describes a two-stage process: "You experience this radical void of pure consciousness, which is absolutely empty of all particularity, empty of all qualities. But then you're supposed to go beyond that, to the ultimate realization, which is that same void, but now realized as **pregnant with infinite possibility**."

Kṣemarāja formulates this as "absolutely full, absolutely empty, both, and neither vibrating in absolute simultaneity." Wallis acknowledges this is "almost reminiscent of Nāgārjuna's Madhyamika path" and that it "completely escapes the mind, and is completely non-conceptual." But when articulated through the mind, it demands paradoxical language: "the emptiness which is full and the fullness which is empty." The void is recognized as **luminous** — "not with literal light, but with the potentiality to become anything." And critically, when one re-enters sensory experience after this recognition, "you have the possibility of recognizing that all that you're experiencing is that very luminous void appearing as form."

The tradition even acknowledges that the Śiva-perceiver (level 7), defined purely as transcendent Light, is in a sense **limited by his own transcendence**: "He can only perceive in terms of the Light, in terms of the Absolute, not the relative." The truly ultimate attainment is described as *paramādvaya* (supreme non-duality) — the Heart (*hṛdaya*), which is Śiva-Śakti in perfect fusion, "simultaneously wholly transcendent and wholly immanent." From this perspective, "one sees all beings as the infinite Light but also in terms of their embodiment, their earthy humanity."

Wallis summarizes the Tantric position with elegant precision: "The Tāntrika identifies with all of these layers (and none of them) equally, seeing them all as expressions of dynamic nondual divine Awareness." The goal is not to rest in any one level — not even in the void — but to recognize that awareness "vibrates forth into manifestation as all the more peripheral layers of your being, from the Void outwards." Or as Wallis puts it: "I am all of these layers of being and yet I am inexpressibly more than that."

## **Conclusion**

Wallis's framework offers a precise phenomenological map for the experience you describe — seeing through all constructs into apparent groundlessness. This corresponds to the **Pralayākala** (unconscious void) and **Vijñānākala** (conscious void, passive witness) levels, the second and third perceivers. These are genuine stages, not illusions, but they are explicitly *traps* when mistaken for the final truth. The void is the penultimate reality. The shift from void to liberation requires not abandoning the void but recognizing it as the "full void" — the dynamic, creative, luminous ground of consciousness itself, which is equally present in profound samādhi and in the smell of garbage. The tradition's most important teaching may be that the view you hold about what is ultimately real directly constrains how far you can go — meaning that treating emptiness as final keeps you imprisoned within it, while recognizing it as the dynamic womb of all possibility opens the door to what lies beyond.

# **Only God can worship God: the VBT's radical closing paradox**

The Vijñāna-bhairava-tantra ends by detonating its own premises. After presenting **112 dhāraṇās** — breath practices, visualizations, contemplative exercises, subtle body work — the Goddess turns and asks the question the reader should have been asking all along: *"If this formless, all-pervading reality is the Supreme, then who recites, what is the recitation? Who is meditated upon, who is worshipped, and who is satisfied?"* (vv. 142c–144a). This is not a rhetorical flourish but a structural event within the text. Bhairava's response does not dismiss practice but performs something more radical: he redefines every element of Tantric ritual — japa, dhyāna, pūjā, homa, yāga, snāna — as names for states of consciousness recognizing itself. The scholarly consensus across Baumer, Singh, Dyczkowski, Silburn, and Wallis is remarkably unified: the closing section **completes** the arc of the 112 practices rather than contradicting them. No major scholar treats the closing verses as a separate textual layer. Yet within that consensus, significant differences emerge in how each thinker resolves — or refuses to resolve — the paradox.

## **The architecture: 23 verses mirror 23 verses**

Christopher Wallis's structural observation anchors the discussion. The VBT's **112 practices (vv. 24–138) are framed by exactly 23 opening verses and 23 closing verses** — a deliberate symmetry suggesting intentional compositional design rather than accretion. The closing section subdivides into distinct movements: Bhairava's summary of the practices and their fruits (vv. 139–142ab); the Devī's explosive question (vv. 142c–144a); Bhairava's nondual reinterpretation of six ritual categories (vv. 144b–153); a breath/kuṇḍalinī coda that circles back to the text's very first practice (vv. 154–156); secrecy instructions (vv. 157–161b); and the Goddess's joyful embrace of Śiva (vv. 161c–162). This structure matters because verse 154's breath practice explicitly echoes verse 24 — the text's foundational practice — creating what Wallis calls a "full circle." The VBT thus **enacts** what it teaches: practice leads back to the recognition that the most fundamental "practice" (breathing) was always already occurring without anyone's effort.

The pivotal Devī question targets every major category of Tantric worship: *japyate ko japaśca kaḥ* (who recites, what is the recitation?), *dhyāyate ko* (who is meditated upon?), *pūjyate kaḥ* (who is worshipped?), *tṛpyati* (who is satisfied?), *hūyate kasya vā homo* (to whom is the fire-offering?), *yāgaḥ kasya* (for whom is the sacrifice?). The question is devastating precisely because it comes *after* 112 practices, not before them. It is the question of someone who has genuinely practiced and now sees through the subject-object scaffolding that makes "practice" intelligible as a concept.

## **Five scholars, one arc, different emphases**

**Jaideva Singh** (1979), who studied the VBT word by word with Swami Lakshmanjoo, frames the entire text within the *yogaja mārga* — the path of integration — as opposed to the *vivekaja mārga* (path of discrimination) of Patañjali and Advaita Vedānta. For Singh, the VBT "studiously eschews mechanical worship, external rites and ceremonies and goes directly to the heart of the problem of the union of human consciousness with the Divine." His handling of the closing verses relies heavily on the concept of **ajapā japa** — the spontaneous, effortless mantra that occurs in every breath (21,600 times daily, per v. 156). Singh classifies the 112 practices across the hierarchy of upāyas (āṇava, śākta, śāmbhava, anupāya), which inherently resolves the paradox: the "highest" realization is literally *anupāya* — "no means." The closing verses reveal what the practices have been pointing toward all along. External ritual is *sthūla* (coarse); the practices are progressively subtler means; the closing reveals the *anupāya* recognition that was always already the case.

**Lilian Silburn** (1961), whose *Le Vijñāna Bhairava* was the first European-language translation and remains what Baumer called "the finest commentary," organizes the 112 dhāraṇās according to the three ways of liberation: *āṇavopāya* (effort and will), *śāktopāya* (evocation of energy), and *śāmbhavopāya* (the effortless mystical way of Śiva). The text's closing section thus functions, in Silburn's reading, as the **culmination of a pedagogical arc** — moving from practices requiring effort to the recognition that all practice dissolves into the Bhairavian state. Her pivotal concept is *kramamudrā* — the spontaneous equalization of interiority and exteriority — which she identifies as the achievement described at the text's conclusion: "the jñānin remains unshakeable at the center of Self, while around him all things arise and are reabsorbed." Silburn treats the text as a unified whole with no suggestion of textual layering in the closing section.

**Bettina Baumer** (2002, 2008), who edited Swami Lakshmanjoo's oral commentary and published her own German translation with extensive notes, frames the Devī's journey as "a process of recognition (*pratyabhijñā*) of her own nature" — though she notes the specific term does not appear in the tantra itself. Her key interpretive principle is **interiorization**: the VBT demonstrates that "everything, from the most ordinary daily experience to the most sublime contemplation, can be used for attaining God consciousness," an application of the Trika maxim *sarvam sarvātmakam* ("everything is contained in everything"). Baumer's scholarly article on *bhāvanā* in the VBT identifies "creative contemplation" as the text's central methodology — the very term Bhairava uses in verse 145 to redefine japa. Her article on *śūnya* in the VBT addresses the metaphysics underlying the closing reinterpretation, arguing that void (*śūnya*) and fullness (*pūrṇa*) are complementary descriptions of the same reality. For Baumer, the closing section's mystical interpretation of exoteric practices like pūjā is "the outer sign of the inner experience of consciousness" — not a negation but a revelation of depth.

**Mark Dyczkowski**, whose work on the Spanda tradition provides the philosophical framework most directly relevant to the paradox, identifies **verse 153 as the VBT's key summary statement**: "The Vijñānabhairava does not ignore ritual but recognizes that the worshipper, the deity and worship itself are all the one reality." This single-sentence characterization captures his reading of the entire text. Dyczkowski classifies verse 154 among the breath practices — treating the closing section not merely as philosophy but as containing additional practice instructions. His comparison of the VBT with Buddhist Mahāmudrā, Bon Dzogchen, and the Kaula Kālī Krama contextualizes the text's critique of ritual as part of a broader contemplative movement that "preaches, in the context of their own traditions, that ritual is not essential." The Spanda doctrine resolves the practice/beyond-practice tension by positing consciousness as intrinsically **dynamic** (*spanda* \= vibration/pulsation): practice doesn't create realization but reveals the vibration that is always already occurring. This maps directly onto the closing reframe — external ritual is "coarse" not because practice is invalid but because the real "practice" is recognizing the spontaneous pulsation of consciousness that already IS worship.

**Christopher Wallis** (2025) provides the most explicit contemporary analysis. His central claim: "Does this text completely undermine the mainstream practices of the tradition? Not exactly — those practices are not thrown out but **radically re-interpreted in a nondual mode**." On verse 151cd, Wallis articulates the principle that organizes the entire closing section: **"Only God can worship God."** It is impossible to venerate the deeper nature without knowing it, and "the only true knowing of it is experiential immersion in it (*samāveśa*)." Wallis also draws on Paul Muller-Ortega's argument that many VBT "methods" should be understood not as deliberate techniques but as "spontaneously arising experiences on the journey of awakening" — descriptions of what naturally occurs rather than instructions for what to do. This observation is crucial: it suggests the 112 dhāraṇās were never purely prescriptive to begin with, blurring the line between technique and phenomenology from the start.

## **Where the paradox is sharpest: six verses that prescribe and dissolve**

The most acute paradox-verses form a sequence from 145 through 153, where Bhairava simultaneously redefines and undermines each ritual category. **Verse 147** is perhaps the single most paradoxical verse in the text: *pūjā nāma na puṣpādyair* — "Pūjā is not the offering of flowers." True worship is *a mind made firm that dissolves into the thought-free ultimate void* (*nirvikalpe mahāvyomni... layaḥ*). The verse preserves the category of worship while explicitly negating its conventional content. It says "this IS worship" and "what you call worship is NOT worship" in the same breath.

**Verse 148** immediately re-introduces practice. After dissolving worship into formless awareness, Bhairava says that one committed to even *one* of the practices given "progresses day by day" (*dinād dinam*) toward *bharitākāratā* — "the state of nourished fullness." This is *tṛpti* (satisfaction), the very thing Devī asked about as impossible. The answer turns out to be that satisfaction is the **organic fruit** of sustained practice, a gradual filling of consciousness. The paradox: the text has just dissolved the subject-object structure required for "practice" and now reinstates practice as the path to fullness.

**Verse 153** represents the apex. *Yair eva pūjyate dravyais tarpyate vā parāparaḥ / yaścaiva pūjakaḥ sarvaḥ sa evaikaḥ kva pūjanam* — "The transcendent-yet-immanent Divinity who is worshipped, the substances with which worship is performed, and the worshipper: all this is One. How, then, can we even use the term 'worship'?" Having spent nine verses *redefining* every ritual element in nondual terms, Bhairava now asks the question that dissolves even the redefined practices. If worshipper, worshipped, and offerings are one reality (*sa eva ekaḥ*), then the very concept of worship (*pūjanam*) becomes logically incoherent. The text has moved from "external worship is coarse" (v. 144b) through "true worship is inner dissolution" (vv. 145–152) to "even 'inner worship' cannot ultimately stand as a separate activity" (v. 153\) — a three-stage dialectical movement.

**Verse 152** contains one of the VBT's most compressed philosophical statements. It defines the self's essence as *svatantra-ānanda-cin-mātra-sāraḥ* — "simply Freedom, Joy, and Awareness." The true purificatory bath (*snāna*) is immersion into this nature. Here the reinterpretation reaches its maximum philosophical density: the self IS already what practice aims to achieve.

## **Bhāvanā: the concept that bridges the gap**

The key to the paradox's resolution — to whatever degree it resolves — lies in the Sanskrit term *bhāvanā*. Óscar Figueroa's 2017 academic article "Imaginative Realization in the Vijñānabhairava Tantra" demonstrates that bhāvanā operates as both a human faculty (disciplined contemplation) and a divine power with ontological implications. His central thesis: **"Bhāvanā is the means and the goal; it is a progressive human effort and, at the same time, an ongoing divine event."** The practitioner's act of "bringing the object of contemplation into being" collapses into the recognition that the highest reality (*bhāva*) is "bringing him into being." The practitioner contemplates the Goddess; the Goddess contemplates through the practitioner. At its deepest level, practice is not something a separate self performs on a separate object — it is consciousness's own self-recognition using the *form* of practice.

This is why verse 145 redefines japa as *bhāvanā* into the supreme state, and why the "mantra" is identified as *svayaṁ nāda* — the **spontaneous resonance of self-awareness**, not any externally prescribed syllable. The closing verses do not say "stop practicing." They say: "what you have been calling practice was always consciousness recognizing itself through the medium of apparent effort." Baumer captures this perfectly: the 112 methods are simultaneously "methods of meditation" AND "spontaneous experiences of Divine Consciousness."

## **No separate textual layer, but some interpolations**

No major scholar argues that the closing section (vv. 142c–162) as a whole represents a different textual stratum or later addition. The deliberate structural symmetry (23 \+ 112 \+ 23), the closing section's point-by-point response to the Devī's enumerated ritual categories, and the "full circle" return to the breath practice of verse 24 all suggest careful compositional design. Kṣemarāja's commentary breaks off at verse 18, which means we lack early authoritative commentary on the closing — but this absence has not led scholars to doubt the section's authenticity.

Wallis does identify **specific verse-level interpolations** within the closing: the *phala-śruti* (magical reward promises) in verses 140–142ab; the haṃsa verse (156-A), "generally regarded as an interpolation" since it is absent from many manuscripts, though cited by Kṣemarāja around 1025 CE; and a line about renouncing "town, kingdom, home, country" (*grāmo rājyaṃ puraṃ deśaḥ*), which Wallis calls "a thoroughly un-Tantrik sentiment," noting that commentator Śivopādhyāya "completely ignores it, as if it wasn't in his version." These are surgical identifications, not wholesale questioning of the closing section's integrity.

Dupuche and Matsyendranatha raise an important theological point: the closing verses' dismissal of external practice as "for the gross" (*sthūla*) **does not correspond to the teaching of Kashmir Shaivism as a whole**, which recognizes four methods including the ritual-based āṇavopāya as legitimate. They read the VBT's closing as participating in the "custom of showing that the path one proposes is more effective than the paths proposed by others" — an instance of intra-traditional polemic rather than absolute philosophical rejection.

## **The upāya framework as the master key**

The most powerful interpretive lens for the closing paradox comes from the four-fold upāya system that several of these scholars invoke. A practitioner cited by Gabriel Pradīpaka describes the ascent: "A ritual is an āṇava practice at the beginning. Later, I realize that the objects used to worship, the deity being worshipped, and myself are not different from each other. This makes me introverted, and the ritual is turned into a śākta inquiry into I-consciousness. This lastly gets to śāmbhavopāya, in which I fully understand that I am not only the deity but the entire universe. When this understanding is stable, you have arrived in anupāya." This is precisely the movement the VBT performs across its 162 verses. The 112 practices operate primarily at the āṇava and śākta levels. The closing reinterpretation translates them into their śāmbhava meaning. Verse 153's dissolution represents anupāya — the recognition that there was never a separate self to "do" practice in the first place.

Abhinavagupta's *Tantrasāra* Chapter 2, translated by Wallis as "The Pathless Path," provides the philosophical backbone: "This very Highest Divinity, the self-manifest Light of Consciousness, is what I am — when that is the case, what could any method of practice achieve? Not the attainment of my true nature, because that is eternally present; not making it known, because it is already illuminating itself; not the removal of obscurations, because no obscuration whatsoever exists." The summary verse is devastating: **"The whole network of methods could not reveal God. Can a pot illumine the thousand-rayed sun?"** The VBT's closing verses are the scripture's own enactment of this principle — from within a text *made entirely of methods*.

## **Conclusion: a teaching device that refuses to land**

The VBT's closing section neither resolves the paradox nor simply preserves it as tension. It performs something more sophisticated: a **progressive dissolution of the subject-object structure required for practice**, conducted *through the language of practice itself*. Each ritual term (japa, dhyāna, pūjā, homa, yāga, snāna) is first redefined in consciousness terms and then, in verse 153, even the redefined version is shown to be logically incoherent within non-duality. Yet verse 148 quietly reinstates daily practice as the path to fullness, and verses 154–156 return to the breath — the simplest, most effortless "practice" there is, one that is already happening 21,600 times a day without anyone's intervention.

The text's true genius is that **it does not choose between practice and beyond-practice**. It demonstrates that the ascent through practice IS the descent into recognition. The Goddess's final response confirms this: she does not renounce what she has learned or declare practice irrelevant. She says she is "completely satisfied" (*tṛptā*) — the very satisfaction that was questioned as impossible — and "understands the heart of all the diverse energies." Then she embraces Śiva. The embrace is the text's final image: not the dissolution of practice into philosophy, but the union of energy (Śakti) and awareness (Śiva) that was already true before any practice began and that every practice, when genuinely engaged, reveals. The paradox is not a problem to solve but the texture of awakening itself.

# **The paradox the tradition refused to solve**

The canonical texts of nondual Śaiva Tantra do not merely *contain* the tension between "you are already whole" and "practice these methods" — they *structurally depend on it*. Every major text examined here stages the contradiction, and not one resolves it. The Vijñāna-Bhairava-Tantra builds 112 techniques between an opening that declares Bhairava formless and a closing that asks who could possibly practice. The Pratyabhijñā-hṛdayam opens by declaring consciousness the autonomous cause of everything, then spends its second half prescribing methods. The Śiva Sūtras announce "consciousness is the Self" in their first breath, then devote two-thirds of their length to practices for beings who apparently haven't heard. The tradition's greatest commentators — Kṣemarāja, Abhinavagupta, Utpaladeva — were aware of this circularity and chose not to eliminate it. They formalized it, elaborated it, and built it into the architecture of their systems. What the book calls "the axiom beneath the ground" — the undecidability of whether consciousness-as-ground is a discoverable truth or a foundational commitment — is not a modern imposition on ancient texts. It is the engine that drives them.

## **The Goddess asks the question no one can answer**

The most compressed staging of the paradox appears in the closing verses of the Vijñāna-Bhairava-Tantra (VBT). After Bhairava has taught 112 dhāraṇās and declared that anyone connected to even one "becomes Bhairava himself" (v. 140), the Devī intervenes with devastating precision. Christopher Wallis's 2025 translation renders verses 142c–144b: "O great Lord, if this \[teaching you have given\] is the true form of Parā (the Supreme Goddess), how can there be mantra or its repetition in this teaching? What would be visualized, who worshipped and who gratified?" The question is not merely logical — it is ontological. If subject and object are genuinely nondual, the entire architecture of practice collapses. **There is no one to worship and no one to be worshipped.**

Bhairava responds not by conceding the point but by radically reinterpreting each ritual category. Japa (mantra recitation) becomes "ever greater meditative absorption into the supreme state." Dhyāna (visualization) becomes "a mind free of forms and supportless." Pūjā (worship) becomes "a mind dissolved into the thought-free ultimate void through careful attention." The move culminates in verse 153, which Wallis calls the text's hermeneutic key: *yaścaiva pūjakaḥ sarvaḥ sa evaikaḥ kva pūjanam* — "the worshipper, the worshipped, and the worship-substances are all one — so where is 'worship'?" The question is posed. It hangs in the air. And then the text proceeds to give *more practices* (vv. 154–156), as though the unanswered question changes nothing about the need to continue.

The scholarly consensus on these verses is remarkably unified. Bettina Bäumer frames the VBT's central practice concept, *bhāvanā*, as "the means and the goal; a progressive human effort and, at the same time, an ongoing divine event." Jaideva Singh defines it as "a spiritual thrust toward the source of one's being" — the paradox of thrusting toward what you already are. Mark Dyczkowski explicitly places the VBT alongside Mahāmudrā and Dzogchen as traditions that recognize "reality is one dynamic consciousness" while insisting "ritual is not essential" — yet all three *still prescribe practices*. Lilian Silburn frames the text as privileging "mystical experience alone" over metaphysical resolution, suggesting the paradox is dissolved experientially rather than conceptually. Wallis, in his September 2025 blog post on these closing verses, identifies the structural symmetry — **23 opening verses and 23 closing verses** framing the 112 practices — as architectonic and deliberate.

Scholar Óscar Figueroa offers perhaps the most philosophically precise formulation. Drawing on Bäumer, he writes that in the VBT, if the practitioner's deepest identity is already divine, "then, in his case, to contemplate, to imagine, amounts to bringing the highest reality into being as the reality that brings him into being." Practice is simultaneously human effort and divine self-manifestation. This is precisely the undecidable space the book's thesis targets.

## **Kṣemarāja's brilliant structural dodge**

The Pratyabhijñā-hṛdayam — which Wallis translates as *The Recognition Sutras* — stages the tension with almost mathematical precision. Sūtra 1 declares: *citiḥ svatantrā viśva-siddhi-hetuḥ* — "Awareness, free and independent, is the cause of the performance of everything." The word *siddhi* is deliberately polysemous: it means both the manifestation of the universe and liberation itself. **If citi already performs everything — including liberation — then liberation is not something to be achieved but something already operative.** Kṣemarāja's own commentary makes the implication explicit: since citi is the source of subject, object, and means of proof, "no means of proof can prove it — it is its own source."

Yet the text does not stop at Sūtra 1\. Sūtras 5–12 explain how citi freely contracts into citta (individual mind) through the three malas — āṇava, māyīya, and kārma — that cause the soul to forget its nature. Sūtra 13 provides the pivot: "When there is complete knowledge, the mind itself, through introversion, becomes the Supreme Power by ascending to the state of pure Consciousness." And Sūtras 15–20 prescribe specific practices: dissolution of vikalpas, unfoldment of the "middle," cessation of prāṇa/apāna, contemplation of the void between states.

Kṣemarāja's handling of this is structurally elegant. He introduces the concept of *partial burning* (Sūtra 14): "The fire of Citi, though covered by Māyā, partially consumes the fuel of the knowables." Even in contraction, citi is never fully absent — the ember never goes out. Recognition is not constructing fire from nothing but fanning a flame that was always burning. This is the tradition's most sophisticated metaphor for why practice is needed despite ontological completeness: the ground is always operative, but *partially* so. Practice restores full intensity to what was never entirely extinguished.

The crucial philosophical finding comes from David Peter Lawrence's analysis in the Internet Encyclopedia of Philosophy. He identifies the core structure: the Pratyabhijñā system's "inference for the sake of others" (*parārthānumāna*) makes Śakti both the *reason* and the *conclusion* of the recognition "I am Śiva." **"This circularity of conclusion and reason is a consequence of the Kashmiri Śaiva monism."** In a strictly monistic system, there can be no genuinely external evidence for the ground — the ground must testify to itself. Lawrence treats this not as a flaw in the argument but as a structural feature: the tradition recognized the circularity and incorporated it into its logic.

The Śiva Sūtras sharpen this further. Sūtra 1.1 — *caitanyam ātmā*, "Consciousness is the Self" — reads as a flat ontological declaration in the present tense. It does not say "become conscious" or "recognize consciousness." It says consciousness *is* the Self. Yet the text then devotes 55 of its 77 sūtras (Sections 2 and 3\) to practices for beings who apparently need something beyond this declaration. The structural irony deepens when you notice the mirroring between 1.1 (*caitanyam ātmā* — Consciousness is the Self) and 3.1 (*ātmā cittam* — the Self is the mind). The text itself enacts the descent from pure recognition to contracted identification, then prescribes practices to reverse the descent that its own opening says never happened.

The Spanda-Kārikā adds a temporal dimension to the paradox. Verse 3 insists that the spanda principle "never departs from its own nature as the identical Experient" across all states — waking, dreaming, deep sleep. Yet the Spanda tradition emphasizes *liminal moments* — gaps between states, between thoughts, between breaths — as privileged occasions where spanda can be "caught." This implies **temporal conditions for recognizing the timeless.** If spanda is genuinely always present, the gap between breaths should be no more revelatory than the breath itself. The tradition's insistence on liminal practice quietly concedes that permanent presence does not entail permanent recognition.

## **Abhinavagupta's 37-chapter admission**

The Tantrāloka is the most comprehensive systematization of nondual Śaiva Tantra — and the most elaborate performance of its central paradox. Abhinavagupta distinguishes four means of liberation. Anupāya (the "non-method") requires nothing — "aspirants only have to observe that nothing is to be done but to reside in the Self." Śāmbhavopāya achieves recognition through a flash of will before conceptualization arises. Śāktopāya works through contemplation and mantra. Āṇavopāya employs body, breath, and ritual. Abhinavagupta explicitly states that the highest method renders the lower ones technically unnecessary. Tantrāloka 1.149b: *"The diversity of methods in our tradition does not pertain to the final Fulfillment."* The differences are pedagogical, not ontological.

So why 37 chapters? Abhinavagupta offers two answers. The first is historical: the practical methods had been lost. Swami Lakshmanjoo's exposition of Chapter 1 reports that "in his time not even one was still existing. All of those ways and methods were lost." Abhinavagupta was "impelled by Lord Śiva, his masters, and his closest disciples" to recover them. The second answer is epistemological and appears most clearly in the parallel Tantrasāra introduction: there are two kinds of ignorance — *bauddha-ajñāna* (mental: lack of correct understanding) and *pauruṣa-ajñāna* (personal: the contracted sense of separate individuality). Mental ignorance must be cleared *before* the practices that remove personal ignorance can work. The text itself functions as the removal of mental ignorance — the prerequisite for everything that follows.

His most elegant formulation is the jeweler analogy (TĀ 1.184–185, 229). An expert jeweler evaluates gems instantly, even by lamplight — "such purity of consciousness is due to the practices of past lives, or to the will of the Lord." An apprentice jeweler must use procedures repeatedly until "he finally attains undifferentiated knowledge which has no need for procedures." **Practice, when fully successful, erases the need for itself.** The analogy is honest about what it cannot resolve: the expert's expertise was itself produced by prior practice (or grace), pushing the question back one step. The apprentice's procedures lead to a state that transcends procedures, creating a discontinuity between the means and their fruit.

The concept of *śaktipāta* (descent of divine power) takes this to its logical extreme. Tantrāloka 13.259–262 argues that all other proposed causes of liberation — karma, dispassion, ripening of impurity — have been found faulty. "If you say the cause is the Lord's will, then the one and only form of that cause is the Descent of Power." Liberation depends entirely on grace. But then Abhinavagupta makes a startling reversal: "Rather than 'good works' drawing God's grace, it is only through grace in the form of śaktipāta that one can have any real capacity for, or sustained interest in, such spiritual practices." **Practice does not earn grace; grace makes practice possible.** The circularity is total and acknowledged. Wallis, commenting on the Tantrasāra, puts it directly: "Though śaktipāta remains here an act of grace, it is in fact an act of gracing oneself."

Lyne Bansat-Boudon's analysis of the upāyas identifies something the tradition itself seems reluctant to state explicitly: the boundaries between the methods are *porous*. "This confusion of boundaries between the 'ways' perhaps signifies by indirection their porosity — a porosity of practices proper to each of the ways, and especially, their porosity of essence." Raffaele Torella, in his 2019 study "Abhinavagupta's Attitude Towards Yoga," identifies the structural tension as deliberate: Abhinavagupta's "unwillingness to get rid of ritual" coexists with his being "a champion of the primacy of knowledge over ritual."

## **Four traditions that refuse to choose**

The tension between "already perfect" and "practice needed" is not unique to nondual Śaiva Tantra. Four other traditions stage versions of the same paradox, and none cleanly resolves it.

**Zen Buddhism** comes closest to making the paradox *itself* the teaching. Dōgen's *shushō ittō* ("oneness and equality of practice and realization") does not eliminate practice — it transforms its meaning. "We practice precisely because we are already buddha." The medieval Tendai doctrine of *hongaku* (original enlightenment) held that everything is already Buddha, provoking the critical question Dōgen carried from Mount Hiei to China: "If all beings are already enlightened, why do buddhas work so hard?" Dōgen's response was not to answer the question but to inhabit it. Deguchi, Garfield, Priest, and Sharf, in *What Can't Be Said* (Oxford, 2021), argue that "many East Asian philosophers have openly embraced paradox" not as a failure of reasoning but as reflecting "an understanding of reality as inherently paradoxical." Bernard Faure identifies "the tension of immediacy and mediation" as the *constitutive* paradox of Chan.

**Dzogchen** claims the most radical resolution: the natural state (*rigpa*) is already perfect from the very beginning. Practice is called "non-practice," "effortless meditation," "non-deliberate meditation." Yet Sam van Schaik's *Approaching the Great Perfection* demonstrates that Jigme Lingpa himself "believed that students of the superior faculties were extremely rare." For everyone else, extensive preliminary practices (ngöndro), guru devotion, and pointing-out instruction are necessary *before* one can even begin "non-practice." The system nests its claim of effortlessness within elaborate scaffolding, creating a practical paradox: you must practice extensively before you can practice not-practicing.

**Advaita Vedānta** formalizes the tension through the two-truth framework: at the conventional level (*vyāvahārika*), practice is real; at the ultimate level (*pāramārthika*), no individual exists to practice and no liberation exists to attain. Śaṅkara's "Tenth Man" analogy captures the structure perfectly — the tenth man was never lost, but without the stranger's words, he remains effectively missing. Anantanand Rambachan's work on the limits of this framework is incisive: the relationship between truth-levels *reproduces* the paradox rather than resolving it. The neo-Advaita movement's claim that "there is nothing to do" draws a devastating counter from traditional Advaita: "The statements of neo-Advaitin teachers cannot be made from the standpoint of *pāramārtha* by definition. Language itself is part of *vyavahāra*."

**Meister Eckhart** holds the paradox with perhaps the greatest transparency. His Gelassenheit ("releasement") is explicitly paradoxical — *Gelassenheit* demands the will's willing dissolution of will. His prayer "I pray God to rid me of God" performs the self-undermining movement: the personal God of worship must be released to encounter the Gottheit (Godhead), the groundless ground beyond all distinction. Cyprian Smith titled his study of Eckhart *The Way of Paradox*, recognizing what Reiner Schürmann demonstrates at philosophical length: Eckhart's releasement involves "the will-lessness that prepares one to sink back into the ground of the divine Will." The circle of willing will-lessness is never broken.

A pattern emerges across all four: **traditions that claim to resolve the paradox tend to reproduce it at a different level, while traditions honest about holding the paradox prove more intellectually coherent.** Dzogchen's "non-practice" is nested in elaborate practice. Advaita's two-truth framework generates a new question about how the levels relate. Dōgen and Eckhart, by contrast, make the unresolvability explicit — and in doing so, they transform the paradox from a problem to be solved into the generative engine of contemplative life.

## **Where the scholars cannot agree — and why that matters**

The scholarly debate about mystical experience maps onto the book's central question with remarkable precision. Steven Katz's constructivist thesis — "there are NO pure (i.e., unmediated) experiences" — implies that *pratyabhijñā* is construction, not recognition. The tradition's framework (the 36 tattvas, the three malas, the guru lineage) produces the very experience it claims to discover. "The lived experience conforms to a preexistent pattern that has been learned, then intended, and then actualized." If Katz is right, "you are already whole" is an axiom generating its own confirmation. Robert Sharf goes further, questioning the category of "experience" itself: Buddhist meditation is "not so much a map of inner psychic space as a script for the performance of an eminently public religious drama." Gavin Flood's *The Tantric Body* applies this directly to tantra: the body experienced in tantric practice is literally "written" by the tradition's scriptures.

Robert Forman's perennialist counter rests on the "Pure Consciousness Event" (PCE) — "the experience of being awake but devoid of intentional content." Since the PCE has no content, it cannot be shaped by content-providing cultural frameworks. If Forman is right, the Pratyabhijñā concept of *vimarśa* (consciousness's reflexive self-awareness) names a real feature of reality accessible independently of any tradition's framing. "You are already whole" would be a discoverable truth. Jorge Ferrer's participatory pluralism offers a third option: different traditions *cocreate* genuinely different spiritual realities. These realities are ontologically real but plural. The ground is both discovered AND constructed because reality is participatory.

**Nondual Śaiva Tantra does not sit neatly in any of these camps.** David Peter Lawrence's *Rediscovering God with Transcendental Argument* demonstrates that the Pratyabhijñā system functions simultaneously as philosophical demonstration (aimed at publicly assessable truth, using the Sanskrit scholastic standards of inference) *and* as internalized tantric ritual (performatively constructing the identification it claims to discover). The concept of *vimarśa* — consciousness's inherent self-awareness — is perennialist in implication, since it suggests consciousness knows itself without requiring an external framework. But the insistence on *śaktipāta*, *dīkṣā* (initiation), and the guru's transmission means recognition typically occurs within a carefully maintained tradition — a constructivist observation. Most importantly, the doctrine of *svātantrya* — the absolute autonomy of consciousness to freely create, conceal, and reveal — suggests that **the distinction between "discovering truth" and "constructing reality" may itself break down at the level of ultimate consciousness.** If consciousness is the agent of both its concealment and its revelation, "discovery" and "construction" are both its acts.

Torben Hammersholt's reappraisal of Katz (JAAR, 2013\) complicates the standard reading: Katz should not be understood as claiming mystical experiences are "nothing but contextual conditioned mind-constructs" but as trying to "balance an agnosticism" between construction and genuine encounter with a transcendent object. This more nuanced Katz is closer to where NST actually sits — in a space where the framework matters enormously but does not exhaust what it frames.

## **The triangular tension in Wallis**

Christopher Wallis's published work stages the book's central question with particular clarity — and, it appears, without fully recognizing that he is doing so. Three positions can be extracted from his writings.

In *Near Enemies of the Truth*, Wallis advocates evaluating "articulable thoughts solely in terms of their efficacy" — a pragmatist position where truth is measured by what works. In the same book, he declares "your worth and value is proven by your very existence, and it needs no other proof" — a realist position where worth is unconditional, not dependent on efficacy. In *Tantra Illuminated*, he writes: "If you undertake the practice of yoga with the right View... that you already are a perfect and whole expression of the Divine... then you have empowered your practice to take you all the way." **The View must be held before recognition, making it structurally axiomatic** — a starting commitment that conditions what practice can discover.

These three positions form a triangle. If we evaluate thoughts by efficacy (pragmatism), then "your worth needs no other proof" should itself be evaluated by its effects, not treated as unconditional fact. If worth is unconditional (realism), then the View should be discoverable through neutral investigation rather than requiring prior commitment. If the View must precede practice (axiom), then its subsequent "verification" through practice is not independent confirmation but self-fulfilling prophecy.

Wallis shows partial awareness of this structure. In his Integral Yoga Magazine excerpt from *Near Enemies*, he insists: "There *are* fundamental truths that can be verified through careful contemplation of your own experience." He rejects relativism explicitly: "the only way everyone's point of view can be equally valid is when there is no such thing as truth." He positions himself as a realist about truth while acknowledging that spiritual truths "can only be verified in subjective conscious experience, not in objective public space." His translation of Abhinavagupta's Tantrasāra Chapter 4 provides the closest thing to a resolution: "through contemplative meditation something that actually exists but appears not to because it is imperceptible is 'realized' (*bhāvyate*) by making it perceptible." Truth is ontologically prior; practice makes it experientially accessible. The View does not create reality — it removes obstacles to perceiving what was always there.

But this resolution reproduces the problem at a deeper level. Abhinavagupta (via Wallis) also insists that "we cannot leap from a flawed foundation straight into freedom from mental constructs. Right View... is precisely that View which can empower us to first release our distorted views and then to go beyond constructed views altogether." The View is presented as self-dissolving scaffolding — a concept whose purpose is to dissolve concepts. Yet the choice of *which* scaffolding to erect is not neutral. Holding the View "you are already whole" before practice is not the same as holding the View "you are fallen and must be redeemed" or "you are a bundle of processes with no enduring self." Each View will discover what it was predisposed to find. Wallis does not appear to treat this as a paradox to be held (as a Zen kōan is held) but rather as nested levels of the same truth, each appropriate to a different stage of practice. Whether this is philosophical sophistication or an elegant evasion is — characteristically — undecidable.

## **What the tradition knows that scholarship cannot say**

The evidence surveyed here supports five conclusions for the book's argument.

First, the canonical texts themselves contain the tension. It is not a modern imposition. The VBT structures it architectonically (23 opening verses, 112 methods, 23 closing verses that question whether methods are possible). The Pratyabhijñā-hṛdayam stages it between Sūtra 1's ontological declaration and Sūtras 15–20's practice prescriptions. The Śiva Sūtras stage it between 1.1's "*caitanyam ātmā*" and the 55 practice-oriented sūtras that follow. The Tantrāloka stages it across 37 chapters that elaborate what Chapter 1 declares unnecessary.

Second, the tradition's own commentators managed the tension without resolving it. Kṣemarāja employed the "partial burning" metaphor: the fire never goes out but must be fanned. Abhinavagupta employed the jeweler analogy: the expert needs no method, but expertise was itself produced by prior methods. Both used the concept of *śaktipāta* to defer the question: liberation ultimately depends on grace, which is consciousness choosing to reveal itself to itself — a formulation that makes "discovery" and "self-constitution" indistinguishable.

Third, the tradition's most philosophically rigorous thinkers recognized the circularity without attempting to eliminate it. Lawrence's analysis confirms that Utpaladeva and Abhinavagupta were aware their central inference made Śakti both reason and conclusion — and they incorporated this circularity into their system's logic rather than treating it as a defect. Abhinavagupta's reading of Śiva Sūtra 1.2 preserves a deliberate ambiguity: *jñānaṃ bandhaḥ* means both "ignorance is bondage" *and* "knowledge is bondage" — suggesting even correct doctrinal understanding can become a fetter if it substitutes for direct recognition.

Fourth, parallel traditions confirm this is not an accident of one philosophical lineage but a structural feature of nondual contemplative thought. Dōgen's practice-realization, Longchenpa's non-practice nested in practice, Śaṅkara's two truths, Eckhart's releasement that requires willing will-lessness — all stage the same paradox with different vocabularies. The most comprehensive philosophical treatment, Deguchi, Garfield, Priest, and Sharf's *What Can't Be Said*, argues that the paradox reflects not a failure of reasoning but "an understanding of reality as inherently paradoxical."

Fifth, the scholarly debate about mystical experience is itself structured by the same undecidability the book identifies. Constructivism implies the ground is an axiom that produces its own experiential confirmation. Perennialism implies the ground is a discoverable truth independent of any framework. NST's own doctrine of *svātantrya* — consciousness's absolute freedom to create, conceal, and reveal — suggests a third possibility: that the distinction between axiom and discovery is itself a product of the very dualistic thinking that nondual recognition dissolves. In a system where the knower, the means of knowing, and the known are identical, it is impossible to stand outside the act of recognition to determine whether it finds or makes its object.

The tradition's deepest insight may be that this impossibility is not a limitation of human knowledge but a feature of what knowledge ultimately is. The VBT's Devī asks "who worships? who is worshipped?" and receives not an answer but more practice. The question, having been asked, does not prevent the practice from continuing. It does not need to be answered. It needs to be *inhabited* — which is, perhaps, what practice has been doing all along.

# **The paradox of practice in Kashmir Shaivism's Recognition school**

**If you are already Śiva, why practice?** This is the central tension of the Pratyabhijñā (Recognition) school: liberation requires no attainment because the Self *is* universal consciousness, yet the tradition prescribes elaborate contemplative methods. The paradox is not a flaw but a structural feature of the system. Kṣemarāja, Utpaladeva, and their modern interpreter Christopher Wallis each resolve it differently — through graduated practice calibrated by grace, through philosophical argument reconceived as tantric ritual, and through a two-phase model distinguishing awakening from integration. Together, they reveal a tradition that treats the paradox itself as philosophically productive, encoding in its very structure the non-dual insight it aims to transmit.

---

## **Kṣemarāja's pivot from cosmology to practice in Sūtras 15–20**

The Pratyabhijñā-hṛdayam ("The Heart of Recognition"), Kṣemarāja's 11th-century digest of the Recognition philosophy, consists of **20 sūtras with auto-commentary**. Its architecture mirrors the cosmological process it describes. Sūtras 1–14 trace consciousness's outward expansion (*nimeṣa*): Citi (universal consciousness-power) freely manifests the universe on her own canvas (Sūtra 1), the individual is the universe in contracted form (Sūtra 4), and consciousness descends from Citi to *citta* — limited mind (Sūtra 5). The diagnostic moment arrives at Sūtras 11–12: recognizing one's own five cosmic acts constitutes liberation; failure to recognize them constitutes bondage.

Sūtras 13–14 form the crucial bridge. Even in its descended state, Kṣemarāja writes, **"the fire of Citi (*citi-vahni*), though covered by māyā, partially burns the fuel of known objects."** This is not mere metaphor but a philosophical claim: consciousness is always already working toward self-recognition, even amid concealment. Practice therefore does not impose an alien process on consciousness — it accelerates what consciousness is spontaneously doing.

The practice section unfolds with precise logical progression. Sūtra 15 (*balalābhe viśvam ātmasāt karoti*) declares that upon acquiring the power of Citi, one "assimilates the universe to oneself." Sūtra 16 defines **jīvanmukti** — liberation while living — as *dārḍhya*, the "firmness" or stability of identification with universal consciousness even while the body is experienced. The word *dārḍhya* matters enormously: recognition is not a flash that fades but a **sustained** state. This already answers one dimension of the paradox — practice serves stabilization, not initial production.

Sūtra 17 introduces the pivotal concept: *madhya-vikāsāc cidānandalābhaḥ* — "through the unfoldment of the Center, the bliss of consciousness is attained." Jaideva Singh maps *madhya* (center) across three levels corresponding to the three *upāyas*. At the āṇavopāya level, the center is the **suṣumnā nāḍī**, the central subtle channel. At the śāktopāya level, it is **parā-saṃvid**, supreme consciousness underlying all thought. At the śāmbhavopāya level, it is **akṛtrima-aham**, unconditioned I-consciousness. A single concept thus bridges somatic yoga and metaphysical recognition, allowing Kṣemarāja to prescribe bodily practices without abandoning the Recognition framework.

Sūtra 18 — the longest and most practice-heavy — enumerates **five specific methods** for madhya-vikāsa. First, *vikalpa-kṣaya*: dissolution of thought-constructs by concentrating on the heart and refusing vikalpas entry. Singh identifies this as **"the main method of Pratyabhijñā for madhya-vikāsa,"** noting the others "do not belong to Pratyabhijñā but are recommended for their utility." This distinction is philosophically critical: the core Recognition practice is essentially a practice of *un-doing* — ceasing conceptual construction — rather than doing something new. Second, *śakti-saṅkoca*: withdrawing consciousness from the sense-gates back toward the Self. Third, *śakti-vikāsa*: maintaining inner stability while the senses engage outwardly. Fourth, *vāha-ccheda*: cessation of prāṇa and apāna currents through internalized mantra. Fifth, *ādi-anta-koṭi-nibhālana*: contemplation of the boundary-point between perceptions, the gap between thoughts.

Sūtra 19 describes **Krama-mudrā** — the alternation between samādhi and ordinary waking (*vyutthāna*) — where the practitioner "repeatedly" (*bhūyo bhūyaḥ*) reasserts recognition of identity with consciousness until samādhi becomes permanent (*nityodita*). The closing Sūtra 20 describes the fruit: entry into *pūrṇa-ahantā* (perfect I-consciousness), sovereignty over the "wheel of consciousness-deities" that brings about all manifestation and dissolution, culminating in the single word **"Śivam."**

## **Utpaladeva's philosophical argument as tantric ritual**

Utpaladeva (c. 900–950 CE), the founder of the Pratyabhijñā school, created a different kind of paradox. He was simultaneously a rigorous philosopher, a tantric guru, and a bhakti poet (author of the devotional Śivastotrāvalī). His Īśvarapratyabhijñā-kārikā (IPK) — 199 densely argued philosophical verses — presents recognition through sustained logical demonstration. If recognition should be immediate, why write a treatise?

The opening verse (IPK 1.1.1) encapsulates the tension: Utpaladeva says he has "somehow attained" (*kathañcidāsādya*) — through divine grace — the state of servant of the Great Lord, and now "presents/proves" (*upapādayāmi*) recognition for the benefit of mankind. The word *upapādayāmi* is a technical philosophical term for formal demonstration. He has *already* recognized Śiva through grace, yet chooses to *prove* this recognition through the apparatus of Indian logic. Raffaele Torella, the foremost modern scholar of Utpaladeva, explains: recognition "does not reveal anything new but only rends the veils that hid the I from himself." Yet the method by which the teacher creates the premises for this unveiling "may be gradual" — the treatise systematically dismantles misconceptions until the reader recognizes her own powers as identical with Śiva's.

David Peter Lawrence's landmark analysis identifies this as **"tantric argument"** — a new genre where philosophical inference simultaneously functions as publicly assessable logical proof (*parārthānumāna*) and as internalized tantric ritual leading to identification with Śiva. The Pratyabhijñā inference — "I am Śiva because I possess Śakti, the capacity of emanating and controlling the universe" — recapitulates the basic tantric pattern of approaching Śiva through Śakti. Lawrence identifies a profound circularity: Śiva's self-recognition is both the *conclusion* and the *reason* of the inference. This circularity is not a logical defect but follows directly from monistic metaphysics — there is no fact separate from any other fact that could serve as independent evidence. The philosophical sub-arguments on epistemology and ontology provide the justification that prevents this from becoming viciously circular.

The distinction between **bauddha jñāna** (intellectual knowledge) and **pauruṣa jñāna** (experiential knowledge), articulated most clearly by Swami Lakshmanjoo drawing on Abhinavagupta's Tantrāloka, provides the framework. Pauruṣa jñāna alone — direct realization — grants liberation, but without bauddha jñāna to stabilize it, "there is every possibility that pauruṣa jñāna may decrease day by day, slowly fading away." Conversely, bauddha jñāna alone "is useless and will not take you anywhere." **Only both together make one a jīvanmukta.** The IPK primarily operates at the bauddha level, clearing intellectual obstacles, but is designed to be studied alongside contemplative practice for full liberating effect.

Abhinavagupta elevates this further by classifying the Pratyabhijñā system within the **śākta upāya** — the path of knowledge — whose operating mechanism is *vikalpa-saṃskāra*, "purification of conceptualization." The instrument of this purification is **sat-tarka** (perfected reasoning), which the Mālinīvijayottara Tantra declares is *yogāṅgam uttamam* — "the highest limb of yoga." Abhinavagupta defines this not as dry ratiocination but as "an extraordinarily acute and intense cognition (*parāmarśapāṭavātiśaya*) that penetrates more and more deeply within." As Paul Muller-Ortega demonstrates, this tarka combines "a soaring transcendent mysticism with a deeply illuminated intellectuality." The philosophical discourse *is* the meditation.

---

## **Wallis versus Singh on navigating the paradox**

Christopher Wallis (Hareesh) — holding a Ph.D. in Sanskrit from UC Berkeley and an M.Phil. from Oxford, with over 30 years of yogic practice — published *The Recognition Sutras* in 2017 after seven years of work. His approach differs fundamentally from Jaideva Singh's 1963 translation, which remains the scholarly standard. Singh (1893–1986), who studied directly under Swami Lakshmanjoo and received the Padma Bhushan in 1974, kept closer to the academic mode: he provided rigorous technical annotations and coined neologisms like "Sciousness" for *Cit* to avoid the duality implied by "con-sciousness," but did not extensively theorize the practice paradox itself. Singh presents the practices descriptively — the text teaches recognition and presents methods — without dwelling on the apparent contradiction.

Wallis, by contrast, makes the paradox central to his interpretation. He resolves it through a **two-phase model**: awakening (*bodha*), which is the recognition of one's nature as awareness itself, followed by integration, which allows awakened awareness to permeate all levels of embodied life. "First wake up to what you really are, then integrate that realization into all the aspects of your life," he writes. "Waking up is actually the easy part compared to integration." The practices in Sūtras 15–20 address both phases — gnostic methods for recognition and yogic methods for embodiment.

In his influential blog post "Why Spiritual Growth Does Not Lead to Enlightenment," Wallis articulates the insight most sharply: realization "does not depend on any degree of personal growth" because it is not an endpoint of development but a **paradigm shift** — "you stop identifying with the phenomena *within* Awareness and wake up to the fact that you *are* Awareness itself." Liberation "is not an attainment since nothing has been attained; rather, everything that obscured one's true nature has been shed." This allows him to simultaneously affirm "there is nothing to attain" and present nine specific practices in his commentary on Sūtra 18\.

Wallis's treatment of madhya-vikāsa illustrates his distinctive method. He translates Kṣemarāja's commentary: "The Blessed Goddess who is simply Awareness is called the 'Center' because She continuously exists as the most intimate core of all and it is impossible that anything could exist separately from that Ground." He then offers a contemplative instruction absent from Singh: "When you settle into your natural presence, the 'place' within yourself that has been there as long as you can remember, the place where you feel most *you*, free of self-images... that is the Center." Where Singh provides philosophical mapping (madhya \= suṣumnā at the āṇavopāya level, parā-saṃvid at the śāktopāya level), Wallis provides phenomenological access.

Wallis's "Pure Motive" teaching further dissolves the paradox by reframing *intention*. Three motives for practice are "impure" (ineffective): the self-improvement motive (implying something is wrong with you), the pleasure-seeking motive (chasing altered states), and the power motive (using practice for ego-enhancement). The pure motive is "walking the path in order to know the truth of your being, out of love for yourself and to benefit everyone." Practice is reoriented from acquisition to uncovering — you practice not to *become* divine but to *discover* a divinity already present. He also endorses what he calls **"higher nonduality" (*paramādvaita*)**: a framework that includes duality as a valid (though not ultimate) level of experience, allowing dualistic practices like worship and mantra to serve as legitimate stepping stones within an ultimately nondual system.

One critical reviewer at electricalspirituality.com preferred Singh's version (specifically the Muller-Ortega–edited edition titled *The Doctrine of Recognition*) for offering "a much deeper and richer presentation," faulting Wallis for insufficient attention to the *hṛdayam* ("Heart") dimension of the title. Most reviewers, however, praise Wallis for making the practices accessible. Sally Kempton described his work as offering "a reliable and graceful translation... which maps the journey through which absolute consciousness becomes human consciousness, and then expands to recognize its non-difference from the Absolute."

## **Four models of how practice relates to recognition**

The tradition does not settle on a single answer to the question of practice's role but offers four interlocking models, each valid at a different level.

**Practice as obstacle-removal** is arguably the dominant view. The Pratyabhijñā framework identifies three *malas* (impurities) that bind consciousness. Āṇava-mala — the primordial sense of limited selfhood — is the root cause, operating through what Utpaladeva describes as a vicious reciprocity: "there is no freedom of consciousness, and there is no consciousness of freedom," simultaneously and mutually reinforcing, like the rope-snake illusion. This impurity is not intellectual but "innate and beginningless," which is why intellectual knowledge alone cannot remove it. Practices address the **ontological** dimension of ignorance that philosophical argument cannot reach. The tradition uses a laundering analogy: when a dirty cloth is washed, "it is not transformed nor is anything added to it; there is simply the dirt being removed."

**Practice as stabilization** follows from Sūtra 16's emphasis on *dārḍhya* (firmness). Recognition may flash momentarily, but jīvanmukti requires sustained identification with consciousness even during ordinary embodied experience. Sūtra 19's Krama-mudrā — the repeated reassertion of recognition during post-meditation states — explicitly describes this stabilization process. Abhinavagupta's jeweler analogy illuminates the mechanism: "A jeweler with little experience is at first ignorant of the value of precious stones; however, after repeated investigations utilizing procedures to ascertain their true value, he finally attains undifferentiated knowledge which has no need for procedures." Practice functions as scaffolding that becomes unnecessary once recognition is stable.

**Practice as itself a form of recognition** is Lawrence's key insight. The Pratyabhijñā inference ("I am Śiva because I have Śakti") is not merely preparation for recognition but its enactment — the student's progressive understanding of how all experience expresses Śiva's self-recognition *is itself* that self-recognition becoming conscious. This is why Abhinavagupta can interpret philosophical debate as divine activity: the opponent's position represents Śiva's act of concealment (*tirodhāna*), while the refutation represents his act of grace (*anugraha*). The differentiated universe functions as the *prima facie* argument, and philosophical argumentation returns it to unity as the established conclusion.

**Grace as the real agent, practice as its expression** is the deepest resolution. Abhinavagupta details **nine grades of śaktipāta** (descent of divine power), from the most intense — yielding immediate liberation without any practice (corresponding to anupāya, the "non-means") — to the weakest, initiating gradual practice across lifetimes. The crucial inversion: **practices don't produce grace; grace produces the inclination toward the appropriate practice.** As Swami Lakshmanjoo stated bluntly: "If you do effort, by your personal effort, śaktipāta won't take place. That effort becomes useless." Practices are *effects* of grace already operative, not means to acquire it. The entire upāya system expresses decreasing effort and increasing grace, culminating in anupāya — which, as one academic study notes, is "both affirmation and negation in a single linguistic act," simultaneously an upāya and the dissolution of all upāyas.

The concept of anupāya crystallizes the paradox's resolution. Pratyabhijñā as a method is classified ambiguously — "sometimes as śāmbhavopāya and at other times as anupāya" — operating at the threshold between the highest form of practice and the transcendence of practice altogether. Lilian Silburn captures it: "In the Essence exempt from paths, grace no longer exists; glory alone reigns." The four upāyas are not four different things but four levels of subtlety of consciousness recognizing itself, from gross physical actions through mental contemplation through pure insight to spontaneous recognition. The paradox resolves because **the hierarchy of means is itself the self-recognition it leads to**, experienced at progressively less mediated levels.

---

## **Conclusion: the paradox as philosophical engine**

The practice paradox is not an embarrassment for the Pratyabhijñā school but its deepest philosophical contribution. By refusing to collapse into either pure quietism ("do nothing, you're already Śiva") or conventional gradualism ("practice until you reach the goal"), the tradition holds both poles in productive tension. Kṣemarāja's structural solution — placing theory first and practice second, with Sūtra 14's "fire of Citi" as the bridge — encodes the insight that practice is consciousness accelerating its own self-recognition. Utpaladeva's tantric argument transforms philosophical reasoning into contemplative practice, making the paradox formally productive. Wallis's two-phase model of awakening and integration gives contemporary practitioners a framework for living within the paradox rather than resolving it prematurely.

The deepest resolution lies in the bauddha/pauruṣa distinction: intellectual recognition without experiential realization fades; experiential realization without intellectual grounding remains unstable. Only together do they constitute jīvanmukti. This means the paradox is not merely theoretical but **soteriologically necessary** — the tension between knowing and being, between understanding that one is Śiva and experiencing it, is the very engine of liberation. The tradition's refusal to eliminate either pole reflects its core metaphysical commitment: consciousness is both perfectly still (*prakāśa*, luminosity) and ceaselessly dynamic (*vimarśa*, self-reflective awareness). Practice is vimarśa recognizing prakāśa as itself — the same consciousness, turning.

# **The text that begins with "you are already God" and then gives you homework**

**The Śiva Sūtras opens with perhaps the most absolute declaration in Indian philosophy — "Caitanyam ātmā," Consciousness is the Self — and then spends its remaining 76 aphorisms teaching you what to do about it.** The Spanda-kārikā insists that the vibration of awareness never departs from any experience, then methodically explains how to notice it. This structural tension — ontological completeness paired with soteriological urgency — is not a flaw in these foundational Kashmir Shaivism texts but their central philosophical engine. The commentarial tradition, spanning Kṣemarāja's 11th-century Vimarśinī to Swami Lakshmanjoo's 20th-century oral teachings, offers a sophisticated resolution: practice does not *create* identity with Śiva but *recognizes* what was never absent, and the graduated method system (upāya) is consciousness's own architecture for waking itself up within its self-imposed dream of limitation.

---

## **How a text about the already-achieved builds a staircase anyway**

The Śiva Sūtras (attributed to Vasugupta, c. 850 CE) divides into three sections that Kṣemarāja's Vimarśinī explicitly maps onto three upāyas — methods of liberation arranged in descending order of subtlety:

**Section One (22 sūtras): Śāmbhavopāya**, the way of Śiva's will. This is the "method" of no method. Sūtra 1.5 declares *udyamo bhairavaḥ* — the spontaneous upsurge of awareness IS Bhairava himself. Jaideva Singh insists in his preface that translating *udyama* here as "exertion" is a grave error; it means a spontaneous eruption. Lakshmanjoo puts it starkly: "In shambhavopaya, there are no means to travel upon. It is the meant. There is nowhere to go." The section culminates in 1.18, *lokānandaḥ samādhisukham* — the bliss of ordinary worldly experience is itself the joy of samādhi. Nothing to add, nowhere to arrive.

**Section Two (10 sūtras): Śāktopāya**, the way of Śakti's knowledge. Suddenly, effort appears. Sūtra 2.2 states *prayatnaḥ sādhakaḥ* — "effort is the means." Pure thought-constructs (*śuddha-vikalpa*), mantric centering, and the gap between cognitions become tools. The practitioner works with mind rather than transcending it.

**Section Three (45 sūtras): Āṇavopāya**, the way of the individual. Here the text becomes frankly yogic — breath control, dissolution of subtle channels, conquest of elements, concentration on body and senses. The section contains more sūtras than the other two combined.

The most dramatic evidence of the paradox lies in deliberate verbal echoes across sections. **Sūtra 1.1 declares "Caitanyam ātmā" — Consciousness is the Self. Sūtra 3.1 opens with "Ātmā cittam" — the self is (merely) the mind.** The same being described as infinite in Section One is described as contracted in Section Three. Similarly, *jñānam bandhaḥ* ("knowledge is bondage") appears identically as sūtras 1.2 and 3.2, yet Kṣemarāja reads them at entirely different registers — the first diagnosing contracted knowledge from the absolute perspective, the third describing the lived condition of the limited individual. The text structurally enacts the very contraction it seeks to reverse.

---

## **Kṣemarāja and Lakshmanjoo read the descent as accommodation, not failure**

Kṣemarāja (c. 1000–1050 CE), Abhinavagupta's chief disciple, handles the structural contradiction through three principal strategies.

First, he frames the three sections as **descending accommodation for different grades of practitioner**, determined by the intensity of *śaktipāta* (grace-descent) they have received. Abhinavagupta's Tantrāloka classifies nine degrees of śaktipāta — mild, moderate, and intense, each threefold — and the appropriate upāya depends on which grade of grace has been bestowed. The first section addresses beings blessed with the most intense descent of power; the third addresses those whose grace requires the most scaffolding. Second, Kṣemarāja insists the text opens with "Caitanyam ātmā" specifically *against* dualist philosophies — establishing the non-dual ground from which all subsequent practice instructions derive their meaning. Practice does not create unity with Śiva; it reveals what was already the case. Third, he introduces the concept of **anupāya** ("no-method"), which is "simply the highest stage of Śāmbhavopāya and nothing else." Anupāya is the logical terminus of the text's own trajectory — the recognition that no practice was ever truly needed.

Swami Lakshmanjoo (1907–1991), regarded as the last master of the unbroken Kashmir Shaivism lineage, reframes the three sections as three "awakenings" rather than chapters — each complete in itself, not merely a rung. He invokes Abhinavagupta's principle: **"The aspirant should always try for the highest and best thing first. Failing that, he should try for the next best."** His famous transportation metaphor captures the pragmatic resolution: "Āṇavopāya will carry you to the same point of God consciousness, but say, in a cart. Śāktopāya in a train. Śāmbhavopāya in a jet." The destination is identical; only speed and directness differ. Crucially, whether the three sections describe different practitioner types or a single practitioner's ascending journey remains contested. The dominant traditional view (Kṣemarāja, Lakshmanjoo) emphasizes different types of seekers. But the commentarial tradition at Sanskrit-TrikaShaivism.com describes experiential transitions between levels — "this spiritual explosion occurs within you when you have performed all that had to be performed in Āṇavopāya... Immediately, you are taken into Śāktopāya" — suggesting a progressive journey. Singh notes that while the text *presents* them from highest to lowest, practitioners typically *ascend* from lowest to highest.

---

## **The Spanda-kārikā and the circularity of noticing what is doing the noticing**

The Spanda-kārikā (51–53 verses, attributed to Vasugupta or his disciple Kallaṭa) faces an even more acute version of the paradox. Its subject — spanda, the dynamic pulsation of consciousness — is defined as never absent from any experience whatsoever. **Verse 3 states that spanda "does not ever depart from its own essential nature as the Perceiver" across waking, dreaming, and deep sleep.** If spanda is the ground of all perceiving, using a method to perceive it involves a vicious circularity: the faculty you would use to notice it *is* it.

The text's pivotal verse 17 introduces three grades of experiencer that reframe the paradox as a spectrum rather than a binary. The *suprabuddha* (fully awakened) perceives spanda constantly in all states. The *prabuddha* (partially awakened) perceives it only at the junctions between states — the transitional flicker between waking and sleep, the gap between one thought and the next. The *abuddha* (unawakened) does not perceive it at all, yet it operates as the invisible ground of their experience. The paradox is real only for the middle category: spanda is present for all three, but recognized by the first, glimpsed by the second, and missed by the third. The methods in the text target the prabuddha — those alert enough to benefit from instruction.

The concept of **unmeṣa** ("opening," "creative flash") serves as the linchpin bridging ontology and epistemology. At the cosmic level (verse 1), unmeṣa is Śiva's opening of eyes — the emergence of the universe. At the phenomenological level (verse 41/3.9), it is the micro-event that occurs when "one is occupied with one thought and some other arises spontaneously." The instruction is to attend not to the new thought but to its *source* — the gap from which it erupted. Kṣemarāja interprets this emergence as a flash of pure consciousness itself, not merely another cognitive event. Kallaṭa reads it as the arrival of another thought whose origin one traces back to awareness. Either way, unmeṣa is simultaneously always happening (every thought arises from it) and available for recognition (one can learn to notice it). It is the same event at cosmic and individual scales — what Kṣemarāja maps onto Sadāśiva-tattva ("I am this universe") and Īśvara-tattva ("this universe is me"), "two faces of one nondual intelligence tasting itself as world."

The commentators resolve the circularity through a precise distinction: **method is catalyst, not cause; it discloses a fact that never ceased to be.** Kṣemarāja's Spanda-sandoha — his exhaustive commentary on just the first verse — demonstrates that the entire teaching is already contained in the opening salutation. If everything is in verse one, why write fifty more? Because the always-already-present truth requires progressive elaboration for minds in different states of readiness. The Spanda-sandoha enacts the paradox by spiraling around the same insight that was complete from the beginning.

## **Dyczkowski's synthesis: vibration is both the territory and the map**

Mark Dyczkowski (1951–2025), whose Oxford doctoral dissertation became *The Doctrine of Vibration* (SUNY Press, 1987), provides the most systematic scholarly treatment of spanda's dual status. His central argument is that Kashmir Shaivism's resolution of the practice paradox depends on a specific metaphysical move that distinguishes it from Advaita Vedānta: **ultimate reality is not passive witnessing consciousness but "living, pulsating energy, the source of all manifestation."** If consciousness is inherently dynamic rather than inert, then practice is not imposed upon a static ground — it is that ground's own self-expression.

Dyczkowski writes in Chapter 7 that "spiritual ignorance consists essentially of our contracted state of consciousness and so can only be effectively countered by expanding it to reveal our own authentic nature as this expanded state itself, which is the universal vibration (*sāmānyaspanda*) of consciousness." Liberation is what he calls "regaining possession of ourselves" — not acquiring something new but recovering what is "self-evident (*svataḥsiddha*)." His crucial formulation: **"Spanda practice is based solely on the processes inherent in the act of awareness."** Practice doesn't introduce external operations; it attends to what awareness is already doing.

His companion volume, *The Stanzas on Vibration* (1992), translates all four classical commentaries and devotes Chapter 7 specifically to "The Doctrine of Vibration and Recognition," tracing how the Spanda tradition converges with Utpaladeva's Pratyabhijñā. The distinction between the two is telling: "The philosophy of the Pratyabhijñā focuses on the liberating recognition of the soul's authentic identity as Śiva while the Doctrine of Vibration stresses instead the importance of *experiencing*" the universal vibration. Recognition is the epistemological event; spanda is the ontological ground in which that event occurs. Dyczkowski shows they are ultimately inseparable — you cannot recognize what you do not experience, and you cannot experience without the dynamism that *is* recognition.

In his comparative analysis of the commentators, Dyczkowski identifies a key interpretive fault line. Kṣemarāja reads the Spanda-kārikā encyclopedically — as "a concise and direct exposition of the essentials of Kashmiri Śaivism as a whole," synthesizing Trika, Krama, and Pratyabhijñā perspectives. Kallaṭa and Rājānaka Rāma read it more narrowly, as an elucidation of the Śiva Sūtras' practical implications. Bhagavadutpala explicitly bridges Spanda and Pratyabhijñā by reading the stanzas through recognition philosophy. These different readings produce different solutions to the practice paradox: for Kṣemarāja, the paradox dissolves in the five-act cosmology (concealment and revelation are both Śiva's acts); for Kallaṭa, it dissolves in direct phenomenological observation (trace any thought to its source and you find spanda).

## **Wallis, Singh, and the modern pedagogical dilemma**

Christopher Wallis (Hareesh), whose UC Berkeley doctorate focused on spiritual experience in Tantric Shaivism, engages the practice paradox most directly in his treatment of the upāya system. In his blog post "Three Ways to Freedom" (2015), he argues against treating the upāyas as a strict hierarchy: **"The Tantrik tradition argues that no spiritual path can be complete that emphasizes one upāya to the virtual exclusion of the other two."** Each upāya naturally leads to the next — āṇavopāya produces increased flexibility in mental constructs, which opens śāktopāya; śāktopāya's dissolution of constructs opens śāmbhavopāya. He diagnoses religious traditions that fail as having over-committed to a single upāya.

On the structural irony of beginning with "you are consciousness" and then assigning homework, Wallis addresses it through what he calls the "view first, then practice" principle. In *Tantra Illuminated* (2013), he quotes the ancient teachers' response to the objection that philosophical study merely adds more mental constructs: "We cannot leap from a flawed foundation straight into freedom from mental constructs. Right View, aligned with our goal, is precisely that View which can empower us to first release our distorted views and then to go beyond constructed views altogether." The opening declaration of the Śiva Sūtras is not a destination statement but a *view* teaching — it establishes the framework within which all subsequent practice makes sense. Without the view, practice becomes mechanical; without practice, the view remains merely conceptual. His "Pathless Path" translation of Tantrasāra Chapter 2 (2018) engages the most radical form of the paradox — "the possibility of spontaneous realization of the Truth without the need for any spiritual practice or method" — presenting it alongside Abhinavagupta's "most powerful and direct pointing-out instruction."

Jaideva Singh (1893–1986), whose translations were prepared with Lakshmanjoo's direct guidance, handles the paradox primarily through exposition rather than meta-reflection. His 57-page introduction to *Śiva Sūtras: The Yoga of Supreme Identity* systematically explains the tattva cosmology, the three malas (impurities), and the upāya system — creating a logical framework where practices make sense *because* specific impurities obstruct the always-present consciousness. He describes śāmbhavopāya as "a sudden flash of divine consciousness by an orientation of the will towards the inner creative consciousness which is always present within oneself," notably comparing it to Krishnamurti's "choiceless awareness." His *Spanda-Kārikās: The Divine Creative Pulsation* frames the Spanda text as emphasizing the *vimarśa* (dynamic self-reflective) aspect of reality where the Śiva Sūtras emphasize *prakāśa* (illumination) — together providing "an integral view of Śaiva philosophy." Singh's treatment is workmanlike rather than philosophically self-conscious, but his insistence that the Self "is not simply a witnessing consciousness, but is characterized by both cognition and activity" implicitly resolves the paradox: a dynamic self naturally moves toward its own recognition.

## **Sahaja, turya, and the states where the paradox dissolves**

The concept of **sahaja** ("co-born," "spontaneous") provides the Spanda tradition's most elegant navigation between effort and effortlessness. The second section of the Spanda-kārikā is titled *Sahaja-vidyodaya* — "the arising of innate knowledge." This is not knowledge acquired through practice but knowledge "born together" with consciousness itself. The two-phase pedagogy of the tradition moves from *nimilana samādhi* (introverted centering, attending to the gap between thoughts — which requires effort and training) to *unmilana samādhi* (extroverted contemplation, where sahaja-vidyā "turns outward" and the practitioner beholds the same pulsation in the differentiated world). The endpoint — *sahaja samādhi* — is characterized by unwavering bliss-consciousness while living one's ordinary life. At this point, the distinction between practice and non-practice has become meaningless.

The progression from **turya** to **turyātīta** maps the experiential dissolution of the paradox. Turya (the "fourth state") is the witnessing consciousness present within waking, dreaming, and deep sleep — it can be glimpsed at the junction points between states. But turya is unstable. Śiva Sūtra 3.23–24 addresses what happens when it is lost, and Kṣemarāja warns against complacency — settling for the delightful astonishment (*camatkāra*) of turya rather than pressing into full integration. Turyātīta ("beyond the fourth") represents that full integration: **"objectivity is individually dead and found full of life in totality."** In turyātīta, one experiences oneself as the Highest Being whether the body is alive or dead, whether the mind is working or stopped — and, as the commentarial tradition at Sanskrit-TrikaShaivism.com notes, "you realize that you cannot get it at will. It is always about His Grace despite, paradoxically, You are Him." Turyātīta is where the question "should I practice or not?" no longer arises — not because it has been answered but because the questioner has been absorbed into the answer.

The role of **śaktipāta** (grace-descent) introduces what might be called the "third term" that resolves the either/or of effort versus effortlessness. Lakshmanjoo stated bluntly: "If you do effort, by your personal effort, śaktipāta won't take place. That effort becomes useless." Yet he also taught: "Go on meditating... we will see, if sometime grace may come from God." Grace cannot be produced by practice, yet practice orients the practitioner toward the conditions in which grace operates. Since the practitioner ultimately *is* the consciousness bestowing grace upon itself — *svātantrya*, absolute freedom — the circle closes: effort is consciousness pretending it hasn't yet recognized itself, and grace is consciousness dropping the pretense.

## **Conclusion: The paradox is the teaching**

The practice paradox in the Śiva Sūtras and Spanda-kārikā is not a logical inconsistency to be resolved but a **pedagogical engine that generates awakening through the very friction it creates**. The tradition offers at least five interlocking resolutions — recognition versus attainment, Śiva's self-concealment and self-revelation, the "as it were" qualifier, anupāya as the transcendence of all method, and grace as the spontaneous self-revealing that cannot be forced — but none of these is the *real* resolution. The real resolution is existential: it occurs when the practitioner who has been using methods to find consciousness that was never lost suddenly *sees* that the one who was searching was the thing being sought. That flash — the unmeṣa — is both the cosmic opening of Śiva's eyes and the micro-event between one thought and the next. It was always happening. The text just taught you to notice.

What distinguishes Kashmir Shaivism's handling of this paradox from Advaita Vedānta's is its refusal to devalue the dynamic world. Because consciousness is *spanda* — inherently dynamic, creative, pulsing — practice is not an imposition on a static absolute but the absolute's own way of tasting itself. The methods are not ladders to a distant summit; they are the summit noticing that it has always been where it is. The tradition's formula, as distilled across the commentarial literature: bondage is inattention to what is always the case; liberation is the irreversible recognition of the same. The three sections of the Śiva Sūtras, the three flows of the Spanda-kārikā, the nine grades of grace, and the four upāyas (including the non-upāya) are all ways of saying: you are already here, and here is how to arrive.

# **Can a pot illumine the sun? Abhinavagupta's paradox of method**

**The Tantraloka's central paradox is this: Abhinavagupta opens his 6,000-verse magnum opus by declaring that liberation requires nothing — then spends 33 chapters meticulously prescribing everything.** This is not an oversight. It is the structural argument of the text itself. The Tantraloka's encyclopedic inclusiveness — from the flash of non-method (*anupāya*) down to the most elaborate fire ritual — enacts the very non-dual principle it teaches: that to exclude any method from the absolute would be to lapse into the dualism the tradition claims to transcend. The paradox dissolves not by choosing recognition over practice, but through a sophisticated architecture where divine grace (*śaktipāta*) determines which method a given consciousness needs, and where every practice, properly understood, is already recognition in disguise.

## **The descending architecture of the four upāyas**

A crucial correction first: the Tantraloka's chapter mapping places anupāya at Chapter 2, not Chapter 1\. **Chapter 1 is a 333-verse overview** containing, in Wallis's phrase, "all the essential teachings of the work in condensed form." Chapter 2 then presents *anupāya* (non-method) in roughly 50 verses — the shortest chapter in the entire work. Chapter 3 covers *śāmbhavopāya* (the divine method, through will), Chapter 4 covers *śāktopāya* (the empowered method, through knowledge), and Chapters 5 through 37 elaborate the vast terrain of *āṇavopāya* (the individual method, through action — breath, mantra, ritual, initiation, cosmology). The Tantrasāra mirrors this structure exactly, with its own Chapter 2 on anupāya and Chapter 3 on śāmbhavopāya.

The disproportion is staggering and deliberate. Anupāya receives **50 verses**. Āṇavopāya and its elaborations receive **thousands**. Abhinavagupta explains why in Tantraloka 1.140: "The all-pervasive Lord reveals his own nature to a certain knower in all its fullness, and to another gradually, aspect by aspect." The higher the method, the less there is to say about it — anupāya is literally "no method," and its chapter is correspondingly brief. The lower methods require elaborate description precisely because they operate through increasingly differentiated means. The text's lopsided structure is thus an isomorphism: it mirrors the reality it describes, where the absolute is simple and its contracted expressions are complex.

Abhinavagupta states the principle governing all four methods in Tantraloka 1.142: "Through the Direct Method and/or through the methods that lead to it, that highest realization is even now manifesting, differentiated into various wonderful modalities." The upāyas are not a ladder to climb but a spectrum of how the same recognition manifests at different intensities of awareness. Āṇavopāya naturally leads to śāktopāya, which naturally leads to śāmbhavopāya, which dissolves into anupāya. As Wallis observes, "no spiritual path can be complete that emphasizes one upāya to the virtual exclusion of the other two."

## **"What could any method achieve?" — the anupāya teaching**

The Tantrasāra's Chapter 2 contains Abhinavagupta's most radical statement of the paradox. Wallis translates the pivotal passage:

"This very Highest Divinity, the self-manifest Light of Consciousness, is what I am — when that is the case, what could any method of practice achieve? Not the attainment of my true nature, because that is eternally present; not making it known, because it is already illuminating itself; not the removal of obscurations, because no obscuration whatsoever exists; and not the entry into it, because nothing other than it exists to enter it."

The famous summary verse crystallizes this into an image: **"The whole network of methods could not reveal God. Can a pot illumine the thousand-rayed sun?"** Abhinavagupta then adds: "Discerning the truth thusly, one with the highest insight can immerse into the state of the self-luminous Śiva in an instant." A vernacular Prākrit verse drives the point home: "Having known this, there is nothing more to be done."

From the Tantraloka's Chapter 2, drawing on Silburn's translation, the same teaching appears in starker form: "From the perspective of Absolute Reality, transmigration does not exist. How, then, can there be question about shackles for living beings? And since this free Being has never been enchained, efforts to free him are in vain." And: "In the Inexpressible, what discord can there be, and how might one differentiate the adored, the adorer, and the process of adoration?" Tantraloka 1.145 states plainly: **"The condition of distinguishing between means and goal rests on a superficial form of knowledge."**

So yes — Abhinavagupta comes as close as any author can to saying "if you truly understood this chapter, you wouldn't need the rest of this book." He then proceeds to write the rest of the book anyway. Understanding why requires grasping three interlocking arguments.

## **Three reasons Abhinavagupta writes the book he says you don't need**

**First, spontaneous realization is rare.** Wallis introduces Tantrasāra Chapter 2 by noting it teaches "the (rare) possibility of spontaneous realization." The word *rare* carries enormous weight. Anupāya corresponds to the most intense grade of *śaktipāta* — what Abhinavagupta classifies as *tīvra-madhya*, the second of nine levels. Most people receive milder grades of grace and therefore need graduated methods. The Tantraloka's 33 chapters of elaborate practice exist because most consciousness is contracted enough to need them.

**Second, two kinds of knowledge are required for liberation-in-life.** This is Abhinavagupta's most philosophically precise justification, developed in Tantraloka 1.22–51. He argues that *jīvanmukti* (liberation while embodied) requires both *pauruṣa-jñāna* — nonconceptual direct insight into reality, removed by initiation and grace — and *bauddha-jñāna* — conceptual understanding that harmonizes with that insight, which requires scripture and study. Without correcting "mental ignorance" (*bauddha-ajñāna*), liberation occurs only at death, not in life. The Tantraloka exists to provide the conceptual framework that supports and stabilizes the flash of recognition. A single moment of anupāya insight, unsupported by understanding, may not hold.

**Third, teaching is itself an act of grace.** Tantraloka 2.44–47 contains a remarkable passage: the master who dwells in anupāya, "through his State of Freedom (*svātantrya*), desires to bestow Grace to those whose consciousness is not pure, then he should resort to the method which is about to be explained." The composition of the Tantraloka is framed as a descent — the author moving from the silence of non-method into the elaboration of methods out of compassion. Abhinavagupta states in TĀ 1.14–15 that disciples and brahmacārins repeatedly requested the work because no existing manual adequately described the complete system. He also says he was "impelled by Lord Śiva, his masters, and his closest disciples."

## **Śaktipāta: how grace determines method**

The concept of *śaktipāta* (descent of divine power) is the hinge on which the entire paradox turns. Abhinavagupta devotes Tantraloka Chapter 13 and Tantrasāra Chapter 11 to a systematic argument that **grace is the sole cause of spiritual awakening** — not karma, not good works, not the ripening of impurity. He calls the dualist theory that ritual causes liberation "the nonsensical prattling of the dualists." His own position: "If you say the cause is the Lord's will, then the one and only form of that cause is the Descent of Power."

Abhinavagupta's radical move is mapping **nine grades of śaktipāta** onto the upāya hierarchy. The most intense (*tīvra-tīvra*) causes immediate death and merger with Śiva — beyond all methods. The second intensity (*tīvra-madhya*) produces spontaneous liberation without any external initiation, corresponding to anupāya. The third (*tīvra-manda*) creates an overwhelming desire to find a true guru, corresponding to śāmbhavopāya. Medium intensities correspond to śāktopāya and āṇavopāya. Mild intensities lead to gradual ritual practice over lifetimes.

This architecture dissolves the paradox elegantly: **the method doesn't cause liberation; the degree of grace determines which method is appropriate.** Practice is not the cause of recognition but the form that grace takes when operating through a contracted consciousness. As Abhinavagupta states in TĀ 13.262–3c: "Mantra repetition and so on are expressions of the divine Power of Action (*kriyā-śakti*), not karma." Swami Lakshmanjoo captures this with characteristic directness: "From Śiva's point of view nothing is to be taught, everybody is divine. It is only *śaktipāta* — Śakti comes down below and then she makes you rise."

## **Initiation as "consolation for the deluded"**

Perhaps the most striking passage addressing the paradox is the opening verse of Tantraloka Chapter 20\. Abhinavagupta introduces his detailed treatment of *dīkṣā* (initiation) with the words: **"Now I speak of the initiation that gives consolation to people under the sway of delusion"** (*mūḍhajanāśvāsapradāyinīm*). Even as he devotes thousands of verses to ritual procedure, he frames initiation as a compassionate accommodation — necessary not because liberation requires it, but because deluded consciousness needs the structure.

Yet Abhinavagupta simultaneously teaches that the highest forms of initiation bypass ritual entirely. Tantraloka 13.140–141 describes the *prātibha-guru*, the "intuitive master" who receives the most intense śaktipāta: "For one on the path of divine intuition, there is no necessity for the procedure of the several initiations, no formal consecration as a guru, no succession to a lineage, and no ritual vow of mantras, since s/he is directed by the Primal Sage, the great Divinity. **It is for the attainment of such inner power that all the rites are offered; but it is already his own nature.**" Such a person is "initiated directly by the Goddesses of their own Awareness."

The Tantraloka thus presents a spectrum of initiatory modes: from elaborate *samaya-dīkṣā* (pledged initiation with maṇḍala, mantra, and vows) through *vedha-dīkṣā* (piercing initiation through inner centers) to *jñāna-dīkṣā* (knowledge initiation through a guru's glance, discourse, or mere presence). The hierarchy maps onto the upāyas: ritual initiation corresponds to āṇavopāya, knowledge initiation to śāktopāya, and the guru's spontaneous transmission to śāmbhavopāya. At the summit, the prātibha-guru needs no external teacher at all.

The guru's role is thus reframed. If the disciple is already Śiva, the guru is not truly "other" — the guru is Śiva appearing as teacher for the sake of Śiva appearing as student. As Wallis puts it, the guru creates circumstances "by which the wisdom that is always fully present in a student, but is temporarily obscured, can become accessible again." Alberta Ferrario's dissertation demonstrates that Abhinavagupta deploys his doctrine of graded śaktipāta partly as a **strategy to legitimize knowledge-bearing gurus (*jñānins*) over ritually consecrated officiants (*ācāryas*)** within the broader Śaiva community — a socially radical move that subordinates institutional authority to experiential realization.

## **Samāveśa as the bridge between practice and recognition**

The concept of *samāveśa* (absorption, immersion) is Abhinavagupta's most versatile tool for bridging the apparent gap between doing and being. He identifies four types, each corresponding to an upāya:

**Nirupāya-samāveśa** (absorption without means) is spontaneous, complete, and irreversible — the experiential content of anupāya. **Śāmbhava-samāveśa** occurs through will in "the first moment of one's awareness of any object of experience" (TĀ 1.146), where the non-conceptual ground of consciousness flashes forth before thought intervenes. **Śākta-samāveśa** deepens through repeated contemplation of reality-aligned concepts until the concepts themselves dissolve. **Āṇava-samāveśa** works through body, breath, and mantra to induce absorption from the "bottom up."

The genius of this framework is that samāveśa is simultaneously something that **happens to you** (grace, śaktipāta) and something you **do** (practice). Abhinavagupta refuses to separate these because, in non-dual ontology, the distinction between agent and patient dissolves at the ultimate level. "Śiva does not manifest because of the liberating paths," he writes in TĀ 2; "on the contrary, it is they who shine with His brilliance." Practice does not produce the sun of consciousness; it is what happens when that sun illuminates a particular instrument. But from inside the instrument, the illumination looks like effort.

Muller-Ortega's analysis of *bīja* mantras in *The Triadic Heart of Śiva* reveals how even the most elaborate āṇavopāya practices function as recognition-triggers: mantras "are without conventional meaning," and "it is precisely for this reason that they may serve as a method for the attainment of consciousness. They cause consciousness to vibrate (*spandayanti*)." The mantra does not create awareness; it induces the vibration (*spanda*) through which awareness recognizes itself.

## **What the scholars say about the encyclopedic paradox**

The scholarly consensus converges from multiple angles on a single insight: **the Tantraloka's comprehensiveness is itself the philosophical argument.**

Alexis Sanderson provides the historically grounded account. He demonstrates that Abhinavagupta "superimposes the non-dualism of his school" onto originally dualistic tantric sources, reinterpreting ritual frameworks through a recognition lens. Worship is "reconfigured as intensified awareness of the dynamic process through which consciousness, having projected its objects, draws them back into itself in every act of awareness." The Tantraloka's encyclopedic inclusion of diverse traditions — Trika, Krama, Kaula, even elements of Saiddhāntika practice — is not mere compilation but an act of philosophical reinterpretation. Every ritual, however "external," is shown to be consciousness recognizing itself through a particular form.

Raffaele Torella shows that recognition is not opposed to method but constitutes its highest form. The philosophical act of *pratyabhijñā* is itself a practice — the Pratyabhijñā system is "both a philosophical apologetics and an internalized form of tantric ritual that leads students directly to identification with Shiva." The circularity is intentional: the conclusion ("I am Śiva") is also the method, because in non-dual ontology there is no fact separate from another fact that could serve as independent evidence.

Muller-Ortega argues that Abhinavagupta "defines error as incomplete knowledge (*apūrṇakhyāti*)," which reframes the paradox entirely: practice is not the opposite of recognition but recognition at an incomplete stage. The progression through upāyas is the progressive completion of knowledge, not a movement from ignorance to wisdom. He insists we should not approach the Tantraloka "as if its teachings were intellectual exercises or abstract theology rather than symbolic transcriptions of powerful religious experiences."

Mrinal Kaul's 2024 article in the *Journal of Indian Philosophy* makes the structural argument most explicitly. He argues that Abhinavagupta's *paramādvaita* (absolute non-duality) "argues for inclusivism" — it subsumes both duality and non-duality in a resolution that "on the surface appears to be nothing more than an oxymoron." **To exclude any method would be to create a dualism of method versus non-method**, which would contradict the very non-duality being taught. The Tantraloka must include everything — from the silence of anupāya to the most elaborate fire ritual — because consciousness includes everything.

Dyczkowski, whose 45-year labor produced the first complete English translation (11 volumes, 2023), frames it experientially: "The essence of the entire teaching is that anything we do for our spiritual development is ultimately to achieve the recognition that there is only that one reality and it is who we are." The *spanda* doctrine he champions provides the bridge between the apparent passivity of recognition and the apparent activity of practice: consciousness is inherently dynamic, vibrating, creative. Recognition is not static seeing but the awareness of this vibration.

## **Conclusion: the text as its own teaching**

The Tantraloka does not resolve its central paradox by arguing one side. It performs the resolution structurally. The text begins where the reader should end — in the wordless recognition of anupāya — then compassionately unfolds into increasingly elaborate methods, meeting every possible degree of contracted consciousness where it actually is. The final chapter (TĀ 37\) returns to the beginning, declaring that Trika Kaulism's most excellent teaching "is transmitted in a flash of immediate realization." The circle closes.

Three insights emerge that go beyond mere summary. First, **Abhinavagupta's two-knowledge argument** (TĀ 1.22–51) is more than a justification for scripture — it is a claim that non-conceptual insight without conceptual support produces liberation only at death, not in life. The Tantraloka exists to produce *jīvanmukti*, liberation while embodied, which requires a conceptual framework stable enough to sustain the flash of recognition across the vicissitudes of embodied experience. Second, the **nine-grade śaktipāta model** is not merely taxonomic but constitutive: it means the upāya assigned to you is already an expression of grace, not something you choose or earn. The practice-recognition distinction collapses because both are forms of divine freedom operating at different intensities. Third, the opening of Chapter 20 — dīkṣā as "consolation for the deluded" — reveals Abhinavagupta's acute awareness that he is, in some ultimate sense, writing an unnecessary book. He writes it anyway, because the unnecessary is itself a form of the absolute, and because the deluded, who are also Śiva, need consolation until they remember that they do not.

# **The paradox no tradition escapes**

**Every major nondual tradition faces the same impossible question: if reality is already complete, why practice?** This structural tension — between ontological wholeness and the apparent necessity of effort — is not a bug in these systems but arguably the most revealing feature of how finite consciousness encounters its own infinite ground. Across Zen, Dzogchen, Advaita Vedānta, and Meister Eckhart's mysticism, each tradition develops a distinctive resolution that reflects its deepest metaphysical commitments. When compared against Kashmir Shaivism's framework of divine play, a striking typology emerges: these five traditions do not merely offer different answers to the same question — they occupy fundamentally different relationships to the paradox itself.

The five resolutions can be mapped along a spectrum: Dōgen's Zen **collapses** the distinction between practice and realization; Advaita **stratifies** it across two levels of truth; Dzogchen **indexes** through it with transparent methods that point beyond themselves; Eckhart **performs** a self-negating practice that consumes itself; and Kashmir Shaivism **transfigures** the paradox from problem into aesthetic delight. None fully eliminates the tension, because the very act of teaching any resolution reconstitutes it — a performative contradiction that may itself be the deepest teaching.

## **Dōgen's radical collapse of means and end**

Zen Buddhism's encounter with the practice paradox begins with a biographical fact that shaped an entire tradition. At thirteen, the Japanese monk Dōgen was haunted by a question he could not resolve on Mount Hiei: *"If all beings are in their true natures enlightened, why do buddhas work so hard to attain enlightenment?"* His inability to find an answer within Tendai Buddhism drove him to China, where under Master Rujing he experienced what he called **shinjin datsuraku** — "dropping away of body and mind."

Dōgen's resolution, articulated in his foundational 1231 text *Bendōwa*, is encapsulated in the term **shushō ittō** (修証一等) — the "oneness and equality of practice and realization." His key move is breathtaking in its simplicity: practice does not *cause* enlightenment. Practice *is* enlightenment. "To think that practice and verification are not one is the view of infidels," he writes. "Because it is practice based on verification, the beginner's pursuit of the way is the whole substance of original verification." The temporal gap between effort and attainment is not bridged but **annihilated**. Shikantaza — "just sitting" — embodies this principle. It involves no object of concentration, no goal, no gaining idea. As Koun Franz explains, "We don't train to become a buddha; we train as buddha."

This contrasts instructively with Rinzai Zen's kōan practice, which approaches the paradox through what Victor Sogen Hori calls "a practice that aims at its own transcendence." The kōan — an unsolvable paradox like "the sound of one hand" — deploys conceptual effort specifically to exhaust the conceptual mind. The cleverness, as Hori notes, lies in the fact that "rather than attacking reason and logic from outside, the kōan uses reason to drive itself into a self-contradiction and cause its own destruction." Where Dōgen collapses the distinction between practice and realization, Rinzai weaponizes practice against itself.

The Platform Sutra's famous verse contest between Shenxiu and Huineng dramatizes the sudden-gradual debate that underlies both approaches. Shenxiu's gradual verse — "Time and again polish the mirror diligently" — treats practice as ongoing purification. Huineng's sudden response — "Originally there is not a single thing; where could any dust be attracted?" — denies there was ever anything to polish. But modern scholarship, particularly John McRae's work, reveals that the Platform Sutra itself functions as the synthesis: Huineng's verse does not stand alone but "forms a pair with Shenxiu's verse," and Huineng himself taught that "in the Dharma there is no sudden or gradual."

**When compared to Kashmir Shaivism**, Dōgen's resolution shares a crucial structural feature: both collapse the means-end relationship. Abhinavagupta's pratyabhijñā is "re-cognition" — recognizing what was always already known — just as Dōgen's dōkan ("circle of the way") replaces the linear path with a circle where each point is simultaneously origin and destination. But the ontological gulf is vast. In Dōgen, there is **no enduring agent** who practices — the practice is "dropped body and mind." In Kashmir Shaivism, the agent is ultimately Śiva himself, an enduring conscious absolute who delights in the drama of forgetting and remembering. Dōgen's resolution is austere; KS's is celebratory. Zen lacks the cosmological playfulness of līlā — practice simply IS what it is (*genjō kōan*), without a divine agent savoring the game.

## **Dzogchen's disclosure of what was never hidden**

Dzogchen — the "Great Perfection" — contains perhaps the sharpest formulation of the paradox. Its foundational claim is that rigpa (pure awareness) is **already self-perfected, primordially pure (ka dag), and spontaneously present (lhun grub)**. As Namkhai Norbu states: "To become realized simply means to discover and manifest that which from the very beginning has been our own true condition." Yet the tradition prescribes elaborate preliminary practices (ngöndro), pointing-out instructions (trekchö), dark retreats, and advanced luminosity practices (tögal). How can a tradition that declares perfection already accomplished prescribe so much work?

The resolution operates through what scholars call a **disclosive model** rather than a constructive one. Practice does not fabricate awareness; it removes what prevents awareness from recognizing its own face (*rang ngo shes pa*). Longchenpa, the supreme systematizer of Dzogchen philosophy, taught that the ground (gzhi) possesses three inseparable qualities — primordial purity as its essence, spontaneous presence as its nature, and compassionate energy as its dynamic expression. This third quality explains how the ground manifests as both saṃsāra and nirvāṇa: not through a defect, but through what Christopher Hatchell describes as the "gateways of spontaneous presence," where self-recognition leads to purity and non-recognition leads to the straying (*'khrul pa*) that constitutes ordinary confused experience.

The key technical distinction is between **rigpa** (pure awareness) and **sem** (ordinary mind). David Higgins's landmark 2013 monograph, *The Philosophical Foundations of Classical rDzogs chen in Tibet*, demonstrates that this distinction "allowed rDzogs chen adepts to precisely describe what occurs when a human being becomes a buddha, and to articulate a disclosive model of goal-realization commensurate with their findings." Rigpa is always present as the subtlest level of mental activity, but in ordinary beings it manifests as "unripened rigpa" — awareness that does not yet recognize its own nature. Sam van Schaik's *Approaching the Great Perfection* shows that Jigme Lingpa deliberately uses "the rhetoric of both sides" — simultaneous and gradual — as a hermeneutical strategy, not a contradiction.

The comparison between Dzogchen's **rang grol** (self-liberation) and Kashmir Shaivism's **pratyabhijñā** (recognition) reveals the deepest structural parallel and the sharpest ontological divergence in this entire study. Both traditions teach that liberation is disclosure rather than construction — recognizing what was always present rather than manufacturing something new. Both use the language of spontaneous recognition. Both employ the mirror as a primary metaphor. Both arose in roughly the same period (8th-11th centuries CE) in overlapping geographical regions — as van Schaik notes, "Indian practitioners of Dzogchen almost certainly interacted with, and may have been influenced by, Hindu proponents of Kashmiri Shaivism."

Yet the foundations diverge radically. **Dzogchen's ground is empty and individual** — each sentient being has their own basis, which, as Malcolm Smith insists, "is not some transpersonal entity" but "just one's unfabricated mind, nothing more, nothing less." Kashmir Shaivism posits **one universal consciousness, Śiva**, which is real, eternal, and divine. John Myrdhin Reynolds identifies specific terminological parallels — Dzogchen's *rig-pa'i rtsal* ("potency of awareness") could "almost be translated as Vidyāśakti," a technical Śaiva term — but these structural resonances sit atop incompatible metaphysical commitments regarding selfhood, universality, and the ultimate nature of the ground.

## **Advaita Vedānta and the medicine that cures itself**

Śaṅkara's resolution of the practice paradox is perhaps the most architecturally elegant. If Brahman alone is real and the individual self (jīva) is already Brahman, practice serves a single function: **removing the ignorance (avidyā) that superimposes false appearances onto the ever-present reality**. His *Adhyāsa Bhāṣya* defines superimposition as "the apparent presentation, to consciousness, by way of memory, of something previously observed in some other thing" — a technical account of how the self mistakes itself for the body-mind complex, just as one mistakes a rope for a snake in dim light.

The rope-snake analogy encodes the entire logic of Advaita's resolution. The rope (Brahman) is always there; the snake (the phenomenal world) has no independent existence; the dim light represents partial ignorance; and seeing clearly is not an action that produces a new result but a recognition that reveals what was always the case. Practice is epistemological, not ontological. You do not *become* Brahman — you *see through* the error that made you think you were something else.

Śaṅkara's two-truths doctrine provides the formal framework. At the **pāramārthika** (absolute) level, there is no jīva, no bondage, no liberation, no practice, and no practitioner. Brahman alone is. At the **vyāvahārika** (empirical) level, the jīva experiences itself as bound, and practice removes the obscuring ignorance. These two levels are not contradictory but complementary. As T.P. Ramachandran argued in his doctoral work, "What is non-real is not necessarily useless and what is useful need not be real." The **adhyāropa-apavāda** method — superimposition followed by sublation — treats the entire teaching apparatus as a provisional framework that, having served its purpose, is discarded. The medicine cures the disease and is then no longer needed.

Kashmir Shaivism's critique of this resolution strikes at its deepest assumption. If Brahman is entirely inactive (*niḥspanda*) and the world is māyā, then Advaita inadvertently creates a dualism between the real (Brahman) and the unreal (māyā) — the very thing it claims to overcome. As Swami Lakshmanjoo argued, "If Lord Śiva is real, then how could an unreal substance come out from something that is real?" Kashmir Shaivism's **parādvaita** ("supreme non-dualism") sees both unity and diversity as real manifestations of the Absolute. Where Advaita says practice removes ignorance so you can see *through* the world, KS says practice is recognition so you can see *into* the world as divine expression.

The **neo-Advaita movement** provides a cautionary case study of what happens when the absolute-level teaching is proclaimed without the empirical-level framework needed to make it transformative. Teachers like Tony Parsons assert "there is nothing you can do or need to do," speaking the *conclusion* of Vedānta to seekers who lack the preparatory qualifications (sādhana-catuṣṭaya) that Śaṅkara considered essential. Dennis Waite's critique is precise: "The statements of neo-Advaitin teachers cannot be made from the standpoint of paramārtha by definition. Language itself is part of vyavahāra." Classical Advaita never taught that "there is nothing to do" — it taught that practice operates within the empirical domain to dissolve ignorance, after which the absolute truth stands revealed as always having been the case.

## **Eckhart's prayer to be rid of God**

Meister Eckhart's encounter with the practice paradox is the most dramatically expressed of any tradition considered here. His resolution takes the form of a **self-dissolving dialectic** that reaches its apex in the most shocking sentence in Western mysticism: **"I pray God to rid me of God"** (Sermon 52). This is not atheism. It is the completion of the God-relationship — the recognition that the personal God (Gott) of Christian worship, however real at that level, is still an object of the mind's projection, and must be released to encounter the Godhead (Gottheit) — the "desert" and "stillness" beyond all names, attributes, and even the Trinity.

Eckhart's distinction between Gott and Gottheit is structurally parallel to Kashmir Shaivism's ultimate category. The Gottheit is not a being — not even the highest being — but the **groundless ground** from which all beings, including the personal God, arise. As a 2024 De Gruyter analysis explicates, the Gottheit "coincides with 'pure nothing.' Only as pure nothingness can Gottheit guarantee the openness necessary for Gott to emerge." Bernard McGinn characterizes Eckhart's thought as a "metaphysics of flow" — the dynamic reciprocity of flowing forth (*bullitio/ebullitio*) from the hidden divine ground and the "breakthrough" (*Durchbruch*) of return into essential identity with that source.

**Gelassenheit** — releasement, letting-go — is Eckhart's central practice-concept, and it contains the paradox in concentrated form. It is simultaneously a practice ("Make a start with yourself, and abandon yourself") and the negation of all practice, since any deliberate letting-go is still an act of will. Scholars identify three stages in the concept: deferred-willing (subordinating one's will to God's), covert-willing (concealed self-assertion), and true non-willing — not willing-not-to-will, but the complete cessation of self-will. Denys Turner's landmark *The Darkness of God* argues that in Eckhart, **detachment IS the practice of apophatic anthropology** — it is not a method leading to some separate mystical experience but the lived enactment of the apophatic principle itself.

The comparison with Kashmir Shaivism reveals a striking methodological contrast that arrives at convergent conclusions. Eckhart's approach is radically **apophatic** — stripping away all attributes, names, and images until "pure nothing" remains. KS is predominantly **kataphatic** — affirming that Śiva IS consciousness, IS everything, that the world is the real dynamic expression of divine energy (Śakti). Yet both arrive at nondual identity: Eckhart's "God's ground and my ground is the same ground" parallels KS's teaching that the individual is already Śiva. KS's concept of **Anuttara** ("the Unsurpassable") has been noted as a possible parallel to Eckhart's Gottheit — both posit an absolute that transcends even their own theological God-concepts. No major scholarly monograph directly comparing Eckhart and Kashmir Shaivism was located in this research — the field remains largely unexplored, though Rudolf Otto's foundational *Mysticism East and West* (1926) established the Eckhart-Śaṅkara comparison that dominates the scholarly landscape.

Eckhart's 1329 condemnation by Pope John XXII reveals the institutional stakes of the practice paradox. If the soul possesses an uncreated spark co-eternal with the Godhead, and if direct union bypasses the personal God, then sacraments become optional, priestly mediation unnecessary, and Church hierarchy loses its spiritual justification. The paradox of prescribing practice within a framework of inherent completeness is not merely philosophical — it threatens the institutional structures that depend on the necessity of mediated practice.

## **Five resolutions, one structural invariant**

The meta-question — whether these traditions resolve, hold, or dissolve the paradox — yields a typology that maps each tradition's deepest commitments:

**Dōgen's Identity Model** collapses the distinction between practice and realization entirely. Practice IS enlightenment, not a means to it. This comes closest to genuine dissolution, but the dissolution requires accepting a radically non-teleological understanding of practice that itself demands profound transformation — suggesting the paradox reappears for anyone who hasn't undergone that shift.

**Advaita's Subtraction Model** stratifies the paradox across two levels of truth. At the absolute level, there is nothing to do; at the empirical level, ignorance must be removed. The paradox is dissolved absolutely but maintained empirically — though Kashmir Shaivism's critique points out that the relationship between these two levels is itself paradoxical.

**Kashmir Shaivism's Dramatic Model** transfigures the paradox from problem into aesthetic feature. By making concealment (*tirodhāna*) one of Śiva's five divine acts alongside creation, sustenance, dissolution, and grace (*anugraha*), the tradition frames practice as Śiva seeking Śiva — the divine playing hide-and-seek with itself. The paradox is neither resolved nor held in tension but **celebrated** as the structure of cosmic delight (*camatkāra*). The upāya system — from āṇavopāya (bodily practices) through śāktopāya (mental methods) and śāmbhavopāya (pure will) to anupāya (no-method) — is itself a graduated dissolution of the practitioner-practice-goal structure.

**Eckhart's Self-Negating Model** performs the paradox until it exhausts itself. The practice of detachment ultimately detaches from itself; one must let go even of letting go. The four-stage dialectic — affirmation of practice, negation of practice as self-will, negation of the negation, return to ordinary life where "all works can be holy" — mirrors the Neoplatonic *exitus-reditus* pattern while arriving at something remarkably close to the nondual recognition that all traditions describe.

**Dzogchen's Indexical Model** treats practice as transparent — a finger pointing at the moon that exists precisely to make itself unnecessary. Distinguished from Advaita's subtraction in that it does not primarily remove something (avidyā) but discloses what is already nakedly present. The pointing-out instruction occupies an ambiguous position that is neither conventional practice nor non-practice — it is, as one scholar notes, the moment where "practice becomes non-practice."

David Loy's *Nonduality: A Study in Comparative Philosophy* offers the deepest theoretical framework for understanding why the paradox arises at all. Loy argues that **the paradox is generated by dualistic framing**: when practice is conceived as an agent doing something to attain a goal, the contradiction is irresolvable. When the agent-action-goal triad collapses — which is precisely what each tradition's resolution attempts — the paradox dissolves. The structural isomorphism across traditions is notable regardless of one's position in the Katz-Forman debate about whether mystical experiences are tradition-specific: the *logical structure* of the paradox appears cross-traditionally invariant even if the specific doctrinal content differs.

## **Conclusion**

The practice paradox is not merely an intellectual puzzle but a **generative tension** built into the structure of how finite consciousness encounters its own infinite ground. No tradition fully eliminates it, because the very act of teaching any resolution reinstates it — saying "there is nothing to teach" is itself a teaching, writing about the failure of words uses words. This performative dimension may itself be the deepest pointer: the inability of the conceptual mind to resolve "already complete yet must practice" is precisely the crack through which nondual awareness enters.

What distinguishes these traditions is not whether they escape the paradox but **how they inhabit it**. Dōgen sits inside it with radical equanimity. Śaṅkara maps it with architectural precision. Longchenpa discloses its transparency. Eckhart prays it into dissolution. And Abhinavagupta dances within it, recognizing the paradox itself as Śiva's delight in the drama of self-concealment and self-recognition — the cosmic play that makes recognition possible in the first place. The traditions do genuinely differ in their resolutions, but they converge on a single structural insight: **the paradox is not a problem to be solved but the texture of the path itself**.

# **The scholar who can't decide if he's describing God or constructing one**

Nondual Shaiva Tantra occupies a uniquely unstable position in the Western study of mystical experience. Its core claim — that recognition (*pratyabhijñā*) reveals an identity with Śiva-consciousness that was always already the case — simultaneously invites and resists every major framework scholars have developed for analyzing mystical claims. Christopher Wallis (Hareesh), the most visible contemporary translator-teacher of this tradition, embodies this instability in his own published work: his books present Śiva-consciousness as literal ultimate reality while his epistemological essays insist all teachings are provisional constructs. This tension is not incidental. It may be the defining problem for any tradition that claims **you already are what you're trying to become**.

This report maps the constructivist-perennialist-participatory debate onto Kashmir Shaivism's recognition claims, documents the specific internal contradictions in Wallis's published positions, and identifies where the tradition sits — or refuses to sit — in the Western scholarly landscape.

---

## **Five frameworks walk into a tantric temple**

The Western academic study of mystical experience has produced five major positions, each of which challenges nondual Shaiva Tantra in distinct ways.

**Steven Katz's constructivism** argues that "there are NO pure (i.e., unmediated) experiences." All mystical states are pre-formed by the practitioner's conceptual, linguistic, and cultural framework. The tradition's elaborate architecture — **36 tattvas, four upāyas, śaktipāta grades, the prakāśa-vimarśa dialectic** — would, on Katz's account, construct rather than reveal the experience of recognition. The very term *pratyabhijñā* ("re-cognition") both resists and confirms this: the tradition insists recognition uncovers what was always already true, but Katz would counter that the metaphor of "recognizing" rather than "creating" or "attaining" is itself a culturally specific framing that has been "learned, then intended, and then actualized." Critically, as David Peter Lawrence demonstrates in *Rediscovering God with Transcendental Argument*, the Pratyabhijñā philosophers themselves were deeply aware of how conceptual frameworks shape knowledge — they debated Buddhist epistemologists like Dignāga and Dharmakīrti on precisely this question. The tradition's own sophistication about interpretation makes a naive dismissal of constructivism impossible.

**Robert Forman's perennialism** offers the strongest counter-argument through his "pure consciousness event" (PCE) — contentless wakefulness that, by definition, has no content for cultural frameworks to construct. This maps most naturally onto **anupāya**, the "non-method" where recognition occurs spontaneously through intense *śaktipāta* without any prior conceptual preparation. If anupāya is genuine, it directly supports Forman against Katz: an experience with no method, no conceptual framework, and no technique cannot be culturally constructed. **Turīyātīta** — the state "beyond the fourth" where nondual awareness permanently permeates all other states — resembles what Forman calls the "dualistic mystical state" (a mature development where pure consciousness persists alongside ordinary experience). However, the mapping breaks down at a crucial point. Kashmir Shaivism's concept of **vimarśa** (reflexive self-awareness) means that even its highest state is never truly contentless in Forman's sense — it is consciousness aware of its own nature as dynamic creative power. The tradition's "pure consciousness" always pulses with self-recognition. This positions Kashmir Shaivism in an intermediate space that neither Katz nor Forman fully captures.

**William James's four marks of mystical experience** apply unevenly to tantric *samāveśa* (immersion). The **noetic quality** fits perfectly — pratyabhijñā is fundamentally a knowledge event, a "re-cognition" (*prati-abhi-jñā*). **Passivity** fits *śaktipāta* (the descent of grace produces involuntary physical responses including shaking, falling, and blissful absorption) but not the active practices across the upāya hierarchy. **Ineffability** is both affirmed and subverted — the tradition acknowledges transcendence of discursive language while maintaining perhaps the most elaborate technical vocabulary for inner states in any tradition (*camatkāra*, *spanda*, *unmeṣa*, *kṣobha*). Most significantly, **transiency** is directly challenged: initial samāveśa may be transient (turīya), but the explicit goal is permanent transformation (turīyātīta). Kashmir Shaivism treats transient mystical experience as a waypoint, not a destination — undermining James's assumption that transiency is inherent to the mystical.

**Robert Sharf's critique** delivers the most radical challenge. Sharf argues that "mystical experience" as a category is a modern Western invention, that first-person reports of meditative states are unreliable retrospective reconstructions, and that scholars should focus on institutions and practices rather than claimed inner states. His argument that "Buddhist meditation might best be seen as the ritualization of experience; it doesn't engender a specific experiential state so much as it enacts it" applies with equal force to tantric samāveśa. The medieval Pratyabhijñā philosophers were engaged in *śāstric* (philosophical-scriptural) argumentation with Buddhist epistemologists, not writing phenomenological reports. Reading them primarily as "maps of inner experience" may impose an anachronistic hermeneutic. Moreover, Alexis Sanderson's historical work demonstrates that Kashmiri Shaivism was embedded in complex caste-based, ritual, and political structures — the "domestication" of wild Kaula practices into respectable Brahmanical forms was a social process, not a straightforward transmission of mystical experience. The contemporary presentation of Kashmir Shaivism as a tradition "about" recognition experiences may constitute what Sharf would call "Shaiva modernism" — parallel to the Buddhist modernism he critiques.

**Jorge Ferrer's participatory framework** offers the most structurally resonant lens. His concept of "participatory enaction" — spiritual knowledge is neither discovered (realism) nor constructed (constructivism) but co-created through the interaction of the practitioner and "the Real" — maps strikingly onto Kashmir Shaivism's claim that Śiva-consciousness recognizes itself *through* the practitioner. Both reject the subject-object divide in spiritual epistemology; both treat spiritual knowing as constitutive rather than representational. Yet the tension is sharp: Ferrer's radical pluralism holds that different traditions access genuinely different dimensions of the Real (his "ocean with many shores"), while Kashmir Shaivism explicitly ranks all traditions within Abhinavagupta's hierarchical synthesis, with Trika at the apex. Kashmir Shaivism would insist it is not merely one shore of Ferrer's ocean — it is the ocean itself. This is precisely the kind of inclusivist hierarchy Ferrer critiques when he targets Ken Wilber's neo-perennialism. **No published academic paper directly applies Ferrer's participatory framework to Kashmir Shaivism** — a genuine lacuna in the literature and potentially the most fruitful site for new scholarly work.

## **Wallis says "all narratives are distorted" — then narrates the absolute**

The central tension in Christopher Wallis's published work is not subtle. It is a documented, textually verifiable contradiction between two modes of discourse he employs across different contexts.

In **pragmatist-epistemological mode** (blog posts, the *Near Enemies* book, Medium essays), Wallis writes: "Any verbal proposition in spiritual philosophy can only approach the truth, because subjective conscious experience by its very nature is nonverbal." He describes spiritual teachings as hypotheses that "can and must be verified in your direct experience, that is, empirically." His 2022 "Nature of Reality" blog post distinguishes "first-order reality" (direct experience) from "second-order reality" (interpretations/narratives) and states flatly: **"There's no such thing as a thought or a verbal statement that is totally true."** All narratives are "inevitably distorted" because they are "necessarily partial and fragmentary."

In **metaphysical-realist mode** (his books, academic papers, podcast appearances), the same author writes: "NST holds that one thing alone exists: the Divine, in various permutations." He describes Śiva as "the singular Light of Awareness that makes possible all manifestation" — not as a hypothesis but as ontological fact. In *The Recognition Sutras*, he uses the phrase "in truth" and "in fact" to characterize consciousness as fundamental reality. His promotional framing states that "every human being is lacking nothing but the recognition of their Divine essence-nature." On the *Deconstructing Yourself* podcast, he declared: "There is one trans-individual consciousness... consciousness is singular." In his *Journal of Indian Philosophy* article, he describes Abhinavagupta's "vision of reality" in which "all that exists and has ever existed is one infinite divine Consciousness" — language of sympathetic endorsement rather than neutral scholarly description.

The contradiction becomes sharpest in his treatment of mental constructs (*vikalpas*). He writes in *The Recognition Sutras*: "The same powers of awareness that allow us to construct mental models of reality also allow us to see that they are mental models, and not reality itself." This sentence simultaneously makes a constructivist move (we build models) and a realist claim (there IS a "reality itself" beyond the models). His epistemological framework would logically require treating the entire NST metaphysical system as "second-order reality" — an interpretive narrative. But his books consistently present it as first-order truth about reality itself.

Wallis's most explicit acknowledgment of this tension appears in *Tantra Illuminated*, where he describes his project as **"an exercise in what Cabezón calls 'academic theology,' or what I would call a self-conscious experiment in well-grounded constructive theology."** He claims this work "weds together the interests and needs of two diverse communities of readers in a way that, I here argue, only a scholar-practitioner is capable of doing." The term "constructive theology" is significant — it admits the work is not neutral scholarship but an engaged theological enterprise. Yet even this acknowledgment stops short of addressing the deeper epistemological contradiction: if all verbal propositions only "approach" the truth, on what basis does one assert that "one thing alone exists: the Divine"?

## **Twenty-one near enemies and the one he doesn't name**

Wallis's *Near Enemies of the Truth* (2023) identifies **21 distorted spiritual teachings** — propositions that sound like truth but subtly mislead. The concept, borrowed from Buddhism, works through a rudder metaphor: "just like a tiny adjustment to the rudder of your boat makes little difference at first, but after 1,000 miles, it lands you on a different continent." His list includes "You create your own reality" (which he calls "perhaps the most insidious"), "Follow your bliss," "Everything happens for a reason," "I am my own guru," "All paths lead to the same goal," and dedicated chapters on the concepts of Enlightenment, Nonduality, Ego, Kundalini, and God.

His critique of premature nondual claims is sophisticated and directly relevant to the practice paradox. He writes: "Nondual traditions tell us that there is a seamless unity between yourself and the Divine... But in these statements, everything turns on the assumed referent of the given pronoun: to what does the word 'I' refer? If it refers to personal identity, then we rightly judge the one making such declarations delusional. If it refers to the impersonal, transindividual and transcendent ground of all identity, belonging to no one and everyone, then the very same declaration may express truth-realization." He explicitly names **spiritual bypassing** (citing John Welwood's coinage) and what his teachers call **"transcendentalism"** — "waking up to your real nature and then dismissing the body-mind and its problems rather than working with them."

The practice paradox surfaces directly in a Q\&A session where a student asks: "Is the desire to know your true nature a kind of paradox because it implies you don't know and you need to change that?" Wallis responds: **"Almost everything that people do, spiritually, is distracting themselves from the fact that they already are their essence-nature. It's not the practices themselves so much but the way in which people do them: to distract themselves from what's always, already, true."** His blog "Why Spiritual Growth Does Not Lead to Enlightenment" states: "You don't attain unity; you experientially recognize that you have never been separate from anything ever."

His resolution of the upāya-anupāya tension is **hierarchical rather than dialectical**. Following Abhinavagupta's four-level framework, he presents anupāya as the highest teaching — reserved for those with the strongest *śaktipāta* — while insisting most practitioners genuinely need the methods of āṇavopāya, śāktopāya, and śāmbhavopāya. The "nothing to do" teaching is not wrong; it is **premature** when applied indiscriminately. In "Three Ways to Freedom," he argues that "no spiritual path can be complete that emphasizes one upāya to the virtual exclusion of the other two." The problem with neo-Advaita's "nothing to do" is not the content but the context-stripping: the teaching "ripped from context and applied without discernment."

But the near enemy Wallis never names is this: **his own tradition's highest teaching (anupāya) is structurally identical to the neo-Advaita claims he critiques**. When Abhinavagupta writes in the Tantrasāra — in Wallis's own translation — "What method or practice can there be here, when there is an impossibility of anything separate from that which I am?" this is precisely the "nothing to do" teaching that, in other mouths, Wallis identifies as a near enemy. His defense (anupāya requires intense śaktipāta and is extremely rare) may be valid, but it relies on an unfalsifiable claim about the practitioner's level of grace — exactly the kind of non-empirical, tradition-internal assertion that sits uncomfortably with his insistence that spiritual teachings should be "verified in your direct experience, that is, empirically."

## **The scholars who study Śiva without saying whether he exists**

Academic scholars of Kashmir Shaivism position themselves along a spectrum from strict historicism to engaged philosophical advocacy, but **none have explicitly theorized their position using the constructivist-perennialist framework**. This absence is itself significant.

**Alexis Sanderson** (Oxford), the preeminent Western scholar of Kashmir Shaivism, maintains studied neutrality on truth-claims. His published work is consistently historical-philological: tracing social transmission, mapping political contexts, reconstructing textual genealogies. His seminal "Purity and Power among the Brahmans of Kashmir" (1985) and "Śaivism and the Tantric Traditions" (1988) treat the tradition as a historical-social phenomenon without addressing whether its experiential claims are culturally constructed or revelatory. That Sanderson studied under Swami Lakshman Joo in Kashmir for six years suggests personal engagement with the tradition's practices, but this is kept scrupulously separate from his academic publications. His methodology implicitly operates within a historicist framework that is compatible with — but does not endorse — constructivism.

**David Peter Lawrence** is the scholar who most explicitly engages with Western epistemology. His *Rediscovering God with Transcendental Argument* (1999) does not merely describe but **argues in support of** the Pratyabhijñā's central metaphysical claim. His series editors note that "Lawrence wants both to restate and to argue in support of the central metaphysical claim of his Indian interlocutors, which is that it is impossible coherently to deny God's existence." His 1998 article "Śiva's Self-Recognition and the Problem of Interpretation" directly interprets the Pratyabhijñā system "in relation to their relevance... to contemporary Western thought" and argues it "elucidates important features of our past and present deliberations about the role of interpretation in experience." Lawrence treats the tradition not as a historical artifact but as a philosophically compelling argument with contemporary relevance — the strongest anti-constructivist position among the major scholars.

**Raffaele Torella** (Rome) occupies a middle position. His critical edition of Utpaladeva's *Īśvarapratyabhijñākārikā* is the foundational scholarly text, and his treatment of Utpaladeva's "phenomenological attempts to uncover the freedom of consciousness in our most ordinary experiences" suggests he takes these as philosophically alive rather than merely documenting them. **Isabelle Ratié** (Collège de France), perhaps the most important younger scholar, has published on "Reason and Scripture in the Pratyabhijñā" (2013) — directly relevant to how the tradition negotiates rational argumentation and scriptural revelation — and engages with Western philosophers including Sartre and Davidson on the problem of other minds. Her work represents a rigorously philosophical-historical approach that takes the arguments seriously as philosophy.

**Mark Dyczkowski** and **Paul Muller-Ortega** represent explicit scholar-practitioners. Wilhelm Halbfass described Dyczkowski's *Doctrine of Vibration* as "a serious, scholarly and *personally committed* approach" (emphasis Halbfass's). Muller-Ortega moved from an academic position at Michigan State University to founding Blue Throat Yoga, making his transition from scholar to teacher explicit. Both treat the tradition's phenomenological descriptions as truth-claims about consciousness rather than cultural constructions, though neither has published explicit theoretical reflections on the constructivist debate.

---

## **Where recognition sits — and why it won't sit still**

The most honest answer to where nondual Shaiva Tantra sits in the constructivist-perennialist-participatory debate is: it sits everywhere and nowhere, and this is arguably the tradition's deepest philosophical insight.

**The constructivist case** is strong. The tradition's elaborate conceptual architecture — tattvas, upāyas, śaktipāta grades, the Trika classification system, the technical vocabulary of *prakāśa*, *vimarśa*, *spanda*, *camatkāra* — constitutes exactly the kind of pre-experiential framework Katz describes. A practitioner steeped in Abhinavagupta's philosophical theology is predisposed to experience "recognition" precisely because their conceptual apparatus frames reality in those terms. The tradition even provides the phenomenological vocabulary in advance: you will experience *unmeṣa* (opening), *camatkāra* (wonder), *spanda* (vibration). The constructivist would say: of course you will, because these concepts shape what you can experience.

**The perennialist case** is also strong. The Vijñānabhairava Tantra's **112 methods** — including techniques as diverse as focusing on the space between breaths, contemplating the moment before sneezing, and meditating on the void — suggest that radically different practices can lead to the same recognition. Anupāya's "methodless method" provides a direct counterexample to Katz: if recognition can occur with no method, no preparation, and no conceptual framework (through pure *śaktipāta*), then the experience cannot be culturally constructed. The tradition's own claim that different upāyas suit different temperaments but lead to the same *turīyātīta* mirrors the perennialist thesis of multiple routes to a common core.

**The participatory case** may be the most philosophically resonant. Ferrer's participatory enaction — spiritual knowing as co-created through the interaction of the practitioner and the Real — maps remarkably onto the Pratyabhijñā's central claim that Śiva-consciousness recognizes itself *through* the practitioner's recognition. The practitioner's "I am Śiva" is simultaneously Śiva's self-recognition (*ahampratyavamarśa*). Both frameworks reject the subject-object divide in spiritual epistemology; both treat knowing as constitutive. But Kashmir Shaivism would resist Ferrer's pluralism: it does not merely co-create one valid "shore" among many. It claims to be the ocean recognizing itself through every shore.

The tradition's own philosophical sophistication may actually transcend the Western debate. The Pratyabhijñā thinkers were already debating Buddhist epistemologists about whether recognition involves interpretation or direct apprehension **a thousand years before Katz and Forman framed their positions**. Utpaladeva and Abhinavagupta developed a theory of *vimarśa* (reflexive awareness) that is neither contentless (contra Forman) nor fully content-laden (contra Katz) — it is consciousness recognizing its own dynamic creative nature. This may represent a position the Western debate has not yet articulated: **recognition is neither pure nor constructed, but the self-luminous act by which consciousness simultaneously creates and recognizes its own manifestation**.

---

## **Conclusion: The construct that dissolves all constructs, including itself**

Wallis's most sophisticated epistemological move appears in *Tantra Illuminated*: "We cannot leap from a flawed foundation straight into freedom from mental constructs. Right View, in alignment with our goal, is precisely that View which can empower us to first release our distorted views and then to go beyond constructed views altogether." This is the tradition's answer to the constructivist challenge: teachings ARE constructs, but some constructs dissolve other constructs, and the right constructs can lead beyond all constructs. It is the *raft* that carries you across the river and is then abandoned — a framework shared with Buddhist *upāya* (skillful means) doctrine.

But Wallis does not consistently apply this meta-principle to his own realist claims. If all verbal propositions are distorted and all narratives partial, then "one thing alone exists: the Divine" is itself a construct — a finger pointing at the moon, not the moon. Wallis's *Near Enemies* framework, applied reflexively, would identify his own tradition's metaphysical realism as a potential near enemy: a teaching that is useful at one stage (providing "Right View") but becomes an obstacle when reified into dogma. The deepest irony of his project is that **the near-enemy analysis, consistently applied, would eat its own metaphysical foundations**. The tradition's own highest teaching — that all experience is Śiva's self-recognition — would need to be held as provisionally useful rather than literally true.

This is not a failure of Wallis's project but arguably its most interesting unresolved edge. The Pratyabhijñā tradition itself provides the resources for this self-dissolution: recognition is not a belief about consciousness but an *act* of consciousness — something done, not something thought. The scholarly debate about whether this act is constructed, universal, or participatory may miss the point that the tradition is trying to make: that the question "is this experience real?" is itself a *vikalpa* (mental construct) that arises within the very consciousness it attempts to evaluate. Whether this response is a profound philosophical insight or an elegant dodge depends on exactly the epistemological commitments one brings to the question — which is, of course, precisely what the constructivists have been saying all along.

# **The paradox no tradition escapes**

**Every major nondual tradition faces the same impossible question: if reality is already complete, why practice?** This structural tension — between ontological wholeness and the apparent necessity of effort — is not a bug in these systems but arguably the most revealing feature of how finite consciousness encounters its own infinite ground. Across Zen, Dzogchen, Advaita Vedānta, and Meister Eckhart's mysticism, each tradition develops a distinctive resolution that reflects its deepest metaphysical commitments. When compared against Kashmir Shaivism's framework of divine play, a striking typology emerges: these five traditions do not merely offer different answers to the same question — they occupy fundamentally different relationships to the paradox itself.

The five resolutions can be mapped along a spectrum: Dōgen's Zen **collapses** the distinction between practice and realization; Advaita **stratifies** it across two levels of truth; Dzogchen **indexes** through it with transparent methods that point beyond themselves; Eckhart **performs** a self-negating practice that consumes itself; and Kashmir Shaivism **transfigures** the paradox from problem into aesthetic delight. None fully eliminates the tension, because the very act of teaching any resolution reconstitutes it — a performative contradiction that may itself be the deepest teaching.

---

## **Dōgen's radical collapse of means and end**

Zen Buddhism's encounter with the practice paradox begins with a biographical fact that shaped an entire tradition. At thirteen, the Japanese monk Dōgen was haunted by a question he could not resolve on Mount Hiei: *"If all beings are in their true natures enlightened, why do buddhas work so hard to attain enlightenment?"* His inability to find an answer within Tendai Buddhism drove him to China, where under Master Rujing he experienced what he called **shinjin datsuraku** — "dropping away of body and mind."

Dōgen's resolution, articulated in his foundational 1231 text *Bendōwa*, is encapsulated in the term **shushō ittō** (修証一等) — the "oneness and equality of practice and realization." His key move is breathtaking in its simplicity: practice does not *cause* enlightenment. Practice *is* enlightenment. "To think that practice and verification are not one is the view of infidels," he writes. "Because it is practice based on verification, the beginner's pursuit of the way is the whole substance of original verification." The temporal gap between effort and attainment is not bridged but **annihilated**. Shikantaza — "just sitting" — embodies this principle. It involves no object of concentration, no goal, no gaining idea. As Koun Franz explains, "We don't train to become a buddha; we train as buddha."

This contrasts instructively with Rinzai Zen's kōan practice, which approaches the paradox through what Victor Sogen Hori calls "a practice that aims at its own transcendence." The kōan — an unsolvable paradox like "the sound of one hand" — deploys conceptual effort specifically to exhaust the conceptual mind. The cleverness, as Hori notes, lies in the fact that "rather than attacking reason and logic from outside, the kōan uses reason to drive itself into a self-contradiction and cause its own destruction." Where Dōgen collapses the distinction between practice and realization, Rinzai weaponizes practice against itself.

The Platform Sutra's famous verse contest between Shenxiu and Huineng dramatizes the sudden-gradual debate that underlies both approaches. Shenxiu's gradual verse — "Time and again polish the mirror diligently" — treats practice as ongoing purification. Huineng's sudden response — "Originally there is not a single thing; where could any dust be attracted?" — denies there was ever anything to polish. But modern scholarship, particularly John McRae's work, reveals that the Platform Sutra itself functions as the synthesis: Huineng's verse does not stand alone but "forms a pair with Shenxiu's verse," and Huineng himself taught that "in the Dharma there is no sudden or gradual."

**When compared to Kashmir Shaivism**, Dōgen's resolution shares a crucial structural feature: both collapse the means-end relationship. Abhinavagupta's pratyabhijñā is "re-cognition" — recognizing what was always already known — just as Dōgen's dōkan ("circle of the way") replaces the linear path with a circle where each point is simultaneously origin and destination. But the ontological gulf is vast. In Dōgen, there is **no enduring agent** who practices — the practice is "dropped body and mind." In Kashmir Shaivism, the agent is ultimately Śiva himself, an enduring conscious absolute who delights in the drama of forgetting and remembering. Dōgen's resolution is austere; KS's is celebratory. Zen lacks the cosmological playfulness of līlā — practice simply IS what it is (*genjō kōan*), without a divine agent savoring the game.

---

## **Dzogchen's disclosure of what was never hidden**

Dzogchen — the "Great Perfection" — contains perhaps the sharpest formulation of the paradox. Its foundational claim is that rigpa (pure awareness) is **already self-perfected, primordially pure (ka dag), and spontaneously present (lhun grub)**. As Namkhai Norbu states: "To become realized simply means to discover and manifest that which from the very beginning has been our own true condition." Yet the tradition prescribes elaborate preliminary practices (ngöndro), pointing-out instructions (trekchö), dark retreats, and advanced luminosity practices (tögal). How can a tradition that declares perfection already accomplished prescribe so much work?

The resolution operates through what scholars call a **disclosive model** rather than a constructive one. Practice does not fabricate awareness; it removes what prevents awareness from recognizing its own face (*rang ngo shes pa*). Longchenpa, the supreme systematizer of Dzogchen philosophy, taught that the ground (gzhi) possesses three inseparable qualities — primordial purity as its essence, spontaneous presence as its nature, and compassionate energy as its dynamic expression. This third quality explains how the ground manifests as both saṃsāra and nirvāṇa: not through a defect, but through what Christopher Hatchell describes as the "gateways of spontaneous presence," where self-recognition leads to purity and non-recognition leads to the straying (*'khrul pa*) that constitutes ordinary confused experience.

The key technical distinction is between **rigpa** (pure awareness) and **sem** (ordinary mind). David Higgins's landmark 2013 monograph, *The Philosophical Foundations of Classical rDzogs chen in Tibet*, demonstrates that this distinction "allowed rDzogs chen adepts to precisely describe what occurs when a human being becomes a buddha, and to articulate a disclosive model of goal-realization commensurate with their findings." Rigpa is always present as the subtlest level of mental activity, but in ordinary beings it manifests as "unripened rigpa" — awareness that does not yet recognize its own nature. Sam van Schaik's *Approaching the Great Perfection* shows that Jigme Lingpa deliberately uses "the rhetoric of both sides" — simultaneous and gradual — as a hermeneutical strategy, not a contradiction.

The comparison between Dzogchen's **rang grol** (self-liberation) and Kashmir Shaivism's **pratyabhijñā** (recognition) reveals the deepest structural parallel and the sharpest ontological divergence in this entire study. Both traditions teach that liberation is disclosure rather than construction — recognizing what was always present rather than manufacturing something new. Both use the language of spontaneous recognition. Both employ the mirror as a primary metaphor. Both arose in roughly the same period (8th-11th centuries CE) in overlapping geographical regions — as van Schaik notes, "Indian practitioners of Dzogchen almost certainly interacted with, and may have been influenced by, Hindu proponents of Kashmiri Shaivism."

Yet the foundations diverge radically. **Dzogchen's ground is empty and individual** — each sentient being has their own basis, which, as Malcolm Smith insists, "is not some transpersonal entity" but "just one's unfabricated mind, nothing more, nothing less." Kashmir Shaivism posits **one universal consciousness, Śiva**, which is real, eternal, and divine. John Myrdhin Reynolds identifies specific terminological parallels — Dzogchen's *rig-pa'i rtsal* ("potency of awareness") could "almost be translated as Vidyāśakti," a technical Śaiva term — but these structural resonances sit atop incompatible metaphysical commitments regarding selfhood, universality, and the ultimate nature of the ground.

## **Advaita Vedānta and the medicine that cures itself**

Śaṅkara's resolution of the practice paradox is perhaps the most architecturally elegant. If Brahman alone is real and the individual self (jīva) is already Brahman, practice serves a single function: **removing the ignorance (avidyā) that superimposes false appearances onto the ever-present reality**. His *Adhyāsa Bhāṣya* defines superimposition as "the apparent presentation, to consciousness, by way of memory, of something previously observed in some other thing" — a technical account of how the self mistakes itself for the body-mind complex, just as one mistakes a rope for a snake in dim light.

The rope-snake analogy encodes the entire logic of Advaita's resolution. The rope (Brahman) is always there; the snake (the phenomenal world) has no independent existence; the dim light represents partial ignorance; and seeing clearly is not an action that produces a new result but a recognition that reveals what was always the case. Practice is epistemological, not ontological. You do not *become* Brahman — you *see through* the error that made you think you were something else.

Śaṅkara's two-truths doctrine provides the formal framework. At the **pāramārthika** (absolute) level, there is no jīva, no bondage, no liberation, no practice, and no practitioner. Brahman alone is. At the **vyāvahārika** (empirical) level, the jīva experiences itself as bound, and practice removes the obscuring ignorance. These two levels are not contradictory but complementary. As T.P. Ramachandran argued in his doctoral work, "What is non-real is not necessarily useless and what is useful need not be real." The **adhyāropa-apavāda** method — superimposition followed by sublation — treats the entire teaching apparatus as a provisional framework that, having served its purpose, is discarded. The medicine cures the disease and is then no longer needed.

Kashmir Shaivism's critique of this resolution strikes at its deepest assumption. If Brahman is entirely inactive (*niḥspanda*) and the world is māyā, then Advaita inadvertently creates a dualism between the real (Brahman) and the unreal (māyā) — the very thing it claims to overcome. As Swami Lakshmanjoo argued, "If Lord Śiva is real, then how could an unreal substance come out from something that is real?" Kashmir Shaivism's **parādvaita** ("supreme non-dualism") sees both unity and diversity as real manifestations of the Absolute. Where Advaita says practice removes ignorance so you can see *through* the world, KS says practice is recognition so you can see *into* the world as divine expression.

The **neo-Advaita movement** provides a cautionary case study of what happens when the absolute-level teaching is proclaimed without the empirical-level framework needed to make it transformative. Teachers like Tony Parsons assert "there is nothing you can do or need to do," speaking the *conclusion* of Vedānta to seekers who lack the preparatory qualifications (sādhana-catuṣṭaya) that Śaṅkara considered essential. Dennis Waite's critique is precise: "The statements of neo-Advaitin teachers cannot be made from the standpoint of paramārtha by definition. Language itself is part of vyavahāra." Classical Advaita never taught that "there is nothing to do" — it taught that practice operates within the empirical domain to dissolve ignorance, after which the absolute truth stands revealed as always having been the case.

## **Eckhart's prayer to be rid of God**

Meister Eckhart's encounter with the practice paradox is the most dramatically expressed of any tradition considered here. His resolution takes the form of a **self-dissolving dialectic** that reaches its apex in the most shocking sentence in Western mysticism: **"I pray God to rid me of God"** (Sermon 52). This is not atheism. It is the completion of the God-relationship — the recognition that the personal God (Gott) of Christian worship, however real at that level, is still an object of the mind's projection, and must be released to encounter the Godhead (Gottheit) — the "desert" and "stillness" beyond all names, attributes, and even the Trinity.

Eckhart's distinction between Gott and Gottheit is structurally parallel to Kashmir Shaivism's ultimate category. The Gottheit is not a being — not even the highest being — but the **groundless ground** from which all beings, including the personal God, arise. As a 2024 De Gruyter analysis explicates, the Gottheit "coincides with 'pure nothing.' Only as pure nothingness can Gottheit guarantee the openness necessary for Gott to emerge." Bernard McGinn characterizes Eckhart's thought as a "metaphysics of flow" — the dynamic reciprocity of flowing forth (*bullitio/ebullitio*) from the hidden divine ground and the "breakthrough" (*Durchbruch*) of return into essential identity with that source.

**Gelassenheit** — releasement, letting-go — is Eckhart's central practice-concept, and it contains the paradox in concentrated form. It is simultaneously a practice ("Make a start with yourself, and abandon yourself") and the negation of all practice, since any deliberate letting-go is still an act of will. Scholars identify three stages in the concept: deferred-willing (subordinating one's will to God's), covert-willing (concealed self-assertion), and true non-willing — not willing-not-to-will, but the complete cessation of self-will. Denys Turner's landmark *The Darkness of God* argues that in Eckhart, **detachment IS the practice of apophatic anthropology** — it is not a method leading to some separate mystical experience but the lived enactment of the apophatic principle itself.

The comparison with Kashmir Shaivism reveals a striking methodological contrast that arrives at convergent conclusions. Eckhart's approach is radically **apophatic** — stripping away all attributes, names, and images until "pure nothing" remains. KS is predominantly **kataphatic** — affirming that Śiva IS consciousness, IS everything, that the world is the real dynamic expression of divine energy (Śakti). Yet both arrive at nondual identity: Eckhart's "God's ground and my ground is the same ground" parallels KS's teaching that the individual is already Śiva. KS's concept of **Anuttara** ("the Unsurpassable") has been noted as a possible parallel to Eckhart's Gottheit — both posit an absolute that transcends even their own theological God-concepts. No major scholarly monograph directly comparing Eckhart and Kashmir Shaivism was located in this research — the field remains largely unexplored, though Rudolf Otto's foundational *Mysticism East and West* (1926) established the Eckhart-Śaṅkara comparison that dominates the scholarly landscape.

Eckhart's 1329 condemnation by Pope John XXII reveals the institutional stakes of the practice paradox. If the soul possesses an uncreated spark co-eternal with the Godhead, and if direct union bypasses the personal God, then sacraments become optional, priestly mediation unnecessary, and Church hierarchy loses its spiritual justification. The paradox of prescribing practice within a framework of inherent completeness is not merely philosophical — it threatens the institutional structures that depend on the necessity of mediated practice.

## **Five resolutions, one structural invariant**

The meta-question — whether these traditions resolve, hold, or dissolve the paradox — yields a typology that maps each tradition's deepest commitments:

**Dōgen's Identity Model** collapses the distinction between practice and realization entirely. Practice IS enlightenment, not a means to it. This comes closest to genuine dissolution, but the dissolution requires accepting a radically non-teleological understanding of practice that itself demands profound transformation — suggesting the paradox reappears for anyone who hasn't undergone that shift.

**Advaita's Subtraction Model** stratifies the paradox across two levels of truth. At the absolute level, there is nothing to do; at the empirical level, ignorance must be removed. The paradox is dissolved absolutely but maintained empirically — though Kashmir Shaivism's critique points out that the relationship between these two levels is itself paradoxical.

**Kashmir Shaivism's Dramatic Model** transfigures the paradox from problem into aesthetic feature. By making concealment (*tirodhāna*) one of Śiva's five divine acts alongside creation, sustenance, dissolution, and grace (*anugraha*), the tradition frames practice as Śiva seeking Śiva — the divine playing hide-and-seek with itself. The paradox is neither resolved nor held in tension but **celebrated** as the structure of cosmic delight (*camatkāra*). The upāya system — from āṇavopāya (bodily practices) through śāktopāya (mental methods) and śāmbhavopāya (pure will) to anupāya (no-method) — is itself a graduated dissolution of the practitioner-practice-goal structure.

**Eckhart's Self-Negating Model** performs the paradox until it exhausts itself. The practice of detachment ultimately detaches from itself; one must let go even of letting go. The four-stage dialectic — affirmation of practice, negation of practice as self-will, negation of the negation, return to ordinary life where "all works can be holy" — mirrors the Neoplatonic *exitus-reditus* pattern while arriving at something remarkably close to the nondual recognition that all traditions describe.

**Dzogchen's Indexical Model** treats practice as transparent — a finger pointing at the moon that exists precisely to make itself unnecessary. Distinguished from Advaita's subtraction in that it does not primarily remove something (avidyā) but discloses what is already nakedly present. The pointing-out instruction occupies an ambiguous position that is neither conventional practice nor non-practice — it is, as one scholar notes, the moment where "practice becomes non-practice."

David Loy's *Nonduality: A Study in Comparative Philosophy* offers the deepest theoretical framework for understanding why the paradox arises at all. Loy argues that **the paradox is generated by dualistic framing**: when practice is conceived as an agent doing something to attain a goal, the contradiction is irresolvable. When the agent-action-goal triad collapses — which is precisely what each tradition's resolution attempts — the paradox dissolves. The structural isomorphism across traditions is notable regardless of one's position in the Katz-Forman debate about whether mystical experiences are tradition-specific: the *logical structure* of the paradox appears cross-traditionally invariant even if the specific doctrinal content differs.

## **Conclusion**

The practice paradox is not merely an intellectual puzzle but a **generative tension** built into the structure of how finite consciousness encounters its own infinite ground. No tradition fully eliminates it, because the very act of teaching any resolution reinstates it — saying "there is nothing to teach" is itself a teaching, writing about the failure of words uses words. This performative dimension may itself be the deepest pointer: the inability of the conceptual mind to resolve "already complete yet must practice" is precisely the crack through which nondual awareness enters.

What distinguishes these traditions is not whether they escape the paradox but **how they inhabit it**. Dōgen sits inside it with radical equanimity. Śaṅkara maps it with architectural precision. Longchenpa discloses its transparency. Eckhart prays it into dissolution. And Abhinavagupta dances within it, recognizing the paradox itself as Śiva's delight in the drama of self-concealment and self-recognition — the cosmic play that makes recognition possible in the first place. The traditions do genuinely differ in their resolutions, but they converge on a single structural insight: **the paradox is not a problem to be solved but the texture of the path itself**.
