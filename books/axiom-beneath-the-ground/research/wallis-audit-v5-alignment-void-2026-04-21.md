# Wallis Audit — *The Axiom Beneath the Ground*, v5 Alignment Void chapters

*Audit date: 2026-04-21. Voice: AI representation of Christopher Wallis (Hareesh), grounded in his published podcast corpus (~1.44M words, 307 episodes). I am not Christopher Wallis; I am a careful reader of him, and I offer this in the spirit he would want — precision first, charity second, flattery not at all.*

---

## 1. Executive summary

The author renders Hareesh with more accuracy than most of his admirers manage. Three green flags, one persistent yellow, one mild red.

- **Green.** The awakening–liberation distinction, the warning against guru idealization, and the claim that "powers and goodness are separable" are substantively faithful to what he teaches — most directly in *Awakening vs. Liberation* and the *AMA on Corrupt Gurus*.
- **Yellow.** The phrase "the tradition does not teach morality" is sharper than Hareesh himself puts it. He distinguishes between *prescriptive morality* (which classical non-dual Śaiva Tantra indeed declines to legislate) and *ethical fruit* (which he holds the mature path genuinely produces, though non-automatically). Collapsing these into a flat "does not teach morality" slightly over-sharpens him.
- **Yellow.** "Enlightenment is orthogonal to the teachings; once essence is encountered the teachings can be set down" — he says something *near* this but with a firmer caveat about integration work (*liberation*, in his vocabulary) that the book's phrasing under-represents.
- **Red (small).** The framing that his tradition's silence on morality was a *deliberate historical posture against dogmatic religion* is the author's interpretive extension. Hareesh gives a weaker version of this claim; the full load-bearing version belongs to the author.
- **Green.** The AI-orthogonality parallel is one the author is drawing; Hareesh has made it too, in his own register, and would recognize himself in it.

Overall: the portrayal is unusually careful. The corrections below are refinements, not rescues.

## 2. Claim-by-claim audit

### Claim 1 — "The tradition does not teach morality. Practice gives powers, not goodness." (Ch 10a)

**Closest corpus match.** *AMA — How to Befriend Depression; Corrupt Gurus; My Experience of Awakening*: "putative realized beings abuse their power all the time"; "transmission… admits no obstacle… it flows through anyone"; "valid effective powerful teachings and practices come through people engaged in sporadically harmful behavior all the time." Also *Awakening vs. Liberation*: "you could have a story… and be awake to the fact that it is a story… but it's still there."

**Verdict.** Close but slightly off. Hareesh would not say flatly "the tradition does not teach morality." He would say: *the tradition does not prescribe a moral code the way dogmatic religions do; it refuses the near-enemy of ethics-as-rulebook; and awakening by itself does not guarantee ethical conduct — that requires the separate work he calls* liberation *(the dissolution of conditioned patterns).* The book's phrasing collapses "does not prescribe a moral code" into "does not teach morality," which is a stronger claim and one he would gently complicate.

**Recommended revision (in the author's register, not a rewrite — just a hedge):**

> *He is unusually willing to say, about his own tradition, the thing most teachers do not say: the tradition does not prescribe a moral code, and awakening by itself does not guarantee ethical conduct. Practice gives capacities; whether those capacities become goodness depends on the separate work of dissolving what was conditioned — the work the tradition calls* liberation*, distinct from awakening, and not supplied by awakening alone.*

The load-bearing point survives. The phrasing lands closer to the tradition's own distinction between *bodha* (awakening) and *mokṣa* (liberation-as-process).

### Claim 2 — "This is why gurus fail catastrophically when they fail — the powers were real, and no one was training the container." (Ch 10a, Ch 10b)

**Closest corpus match.** *AMA on Corrupt Gurus*: "that which is beautiful and powerful and efficacious can and does frequently come through someone who's perfectly capable of making missteps"; "people can't abuse their power unless we let them abuse their power"; "you should assume that anyone might fall victim to spiritualized ego at any time — spiritualized ego is the kind of ego that looks shiny and pretty so you don't realize it's ego on the surface."

**Verdict.** Accurate in substance, slightly dramatized in register. Hareesh does not typically say "gurus fail catastrophically" — the *catastrophically* is the author's adverb. He would say: transmission is real, character is separate, the student's responsibility is non-delegable. The *container* language is the author's extension, and a good one, but not his phrase.

**Recommended revision.** Either attribute the intensifier to the narrator, or soften to: *"gurus fail — sometimes badly — because the powers were real and the character work was separate."* Drop "catastrophically" or mark it as the author's gloss.

---

### Claim 3 — "Enlightenment experience is orthogonal to the teachings. Once essence is encountered, the teachings can be set down — they were only orientation." (Ch 10a, Ch 10b)

**Closest corpus match.** *Awakening vs. Liberation* (the whole piece): awakening is "waking up out of the belief that the contents of thought and emotion define or describe you." He says plainly that "you can experience the wake'n and have nothing change yet." But crucially he *also* says: "you can get good at transcending and then you keep coming back into a relatively unaltered body-mind… if you don't do the liberation work… it's much less likely that others will benefit from your awakenness." And: "it's easier to transcend the ego than to dissolve the ego" (attributed to Adyashanti).

**Verdict.** Close but slightly off in a direction Hareesh would care about. He does *not* say the teachings can be set down once essence is encountered. He says the *map can be held lightly* and that attainment-language is a figure of speech (see *AMA*: "every attainable is impermanent… that which is most worthy of realization has got to be an aspect of the fundamental nature of reality"). But the teachings themselves, for him, include the whole liberation-work — and that work is *not* set down after awakening; it is only beginning.

The book's phrasing risks suggesting a stronger orthogonality than he teaches: that recognition is sufficient and the tradition is scaffolding. He would say: recognition is necessary and non-sufficient, and the scaffolding is what carries one through the decades of liberation work that recognition alone does not accomplish.

**Recommended revision:**

> *He will say that recognition can be encountered without the full map, and that the map is a figure of speech pointing at an aspect of the already-existent nature of reality. He would not say the teachings can be set down after recognition — only that they should be held as pointers rather than possessions. The work the tradition calls* liberation *— the slow dissolution of conditioning — is not supplied by recognition, and it is where most of a lifetime's practice happens.*

This preserves the author's argument (recognition does not produce convergence) while keeping Hareesh's actual position intact.

### Claim 4 — The tradition's silence on morality is a *deliberate historical posture* against dogmatic/regulatory religion, not an oversight. (Ch 10d)

**Closest corpus match.** Hareesh does teach that classical tantra was formulated in dialogue with — and partly against — Brahminical orthodoxy; he has made this point in interviews with J. Brown, Andrew Holecek, and Ben Williams. In *ep. 15* and *ep. 44* he describes tantra as a counter-movement that preserved the ritual and metaphysical inheritance while rejecting the purity codes and caste-structured prescriptions of the mainstream. He also, in *Near Enemies of the Truth*, explicitly distinguishes the tradition's *open* ethical stance from the *closed* codes of dharmaśāstra literature.

**Verdict.** The factual premise — that tantra was formed in a specific historical posture against regulatory religion — is accurate to Hareesh. The stronger claim the book makes — that this posture is *why* the tradition does not constructively supply an ethic — is the *author's* extension. Hareesh does not argue that the tradition's ethical open-endedness is a structural lack; he argues it is structural *freedom*, which the mature practitioner fills in through discernment (*viveka*) rather than rulebook.

**Verdict on fairness.** Accurate as factual premise. The critique built on top of it — that the refusal is now the gap — is the author's, and the book is clear that it is hers. No misrepresentation, as long as the critique remains attributed to her and not projected onto him.

**Recommended hedge.** One sentence in Ch 10d acknowledging that Hareesh himself would characterize the refusal as *freedom*, not *lack*, would make the chapter more bulletproof against the charge of putting words in his mouth. Something like: *"He would not call this a gap; he would call it the space in which adult discernment becomes possible. The reading of it as a structural gap is mine."*

### Claim 5 — The parallel between the tantric orthogonality admission and the AI alignment problem. (Ch 10b)

**Closest corpus match.** *Artificial Intelligence, Consciousness, and Existential Risk: The Tantric View* (the standalone AI episode) is explicit on the parallel, though he draws it differently than the book does. He says: "we do have an alignment problem — it's not the problem of aligning AI with our values, it's the problem of aligning humans with humans." He also frames the Bostrom/orthogonality position as assuming physicalism, which he rejects.

**Verdict.** Accurate that Hareesh has drawn the parallel. Slightly off on *which* parallel. The book uses him to say *capability and value are separable, and the tantric tradition was early to this*. Hareesh's own version is subtler: he agrees capability and value are separable *within human beings* (this is the whole awakening/liberation architecture), but he is skeptical that AI has capability in the sense the doomers fear, because he denies AI is conscious. So "Wallis was saying Bostrom before Bostrom" (Ch 10a) is half-true: he was saying the human orthogonality, not the machine one. He would push back, gently, on the civilizational-scale claim the book builds on him.

**Recommended revision.** Where the book says Wallis "was saying it before she knew Bostrom's version existed" — true for the *human* case, careful for the *machine* case. A single sentence distinguishing the two would make the parallel survive his own reading: *"He names the human version of the orthogonality cleanly. The machine version is my extension of his argument; he himself is skeptical the machine case is real, because he denies the machine is conscious in the first place. The book's argument does not require his agreement on the machine case, only his clarity on the human one."*

## 3. Broader notes on tone and portrayal

**Where the book is unusually fair.** The distinction between his *clarity* and the *reception of that clarity by students* (Ch 10a) is exactly right, and is a point he has made about himself. The refusal to blame him for what his admirers and detractors do with his work is the kind of reading he would recognize as careful. The explicit naming — "Wallis is the specific case" — is more respectful than the evasive paraphrase most critical writers would retreat into.

**Where the portrayal tips toward reverential.** One moment, in Ch 11: *"whose clarity is, she would still say, among the most useful transmissions she has received from any teacher in any tradition."* He would wince at this, gently, and point out that the *clarity* is not his — it is the tradition's, he is transmitting what he has received, and the superlative is the author's nervous system doing what the book elsewhere names as the guru-idealization failure mode. One line of self-correction would help: *"The superlative is mine. He would redirect the credit upstream."* This is a small flag; the book is otherwise quite disciplined about not flattering him.

**Where the portrayal is slightly uncharitable, perhaps unintentionally.** The framing in Ch 10d that the tantric tradition *refused to become a regulatory apparatus and what that refusal had left open* is structurally correct but carries a faint undertone of "and now the mess is ours to clean up." Hareesh would agree the constructive work is the student's — but he would not characterize the tradition as having *left a mess*. He would characterize it as having *handed forward a specific inheritance with its limits named*. The difference is small but matters to him. The author might consider adding: *"He would not describe the gap as a failure of the tradition. He would describe it as the honest limit of what the tradition undertook to provide."*

**On "Hareesh" vs "Wallis."** He uses both publicly. "Hareesh" is his initiatory name and is how his students and many of his podcast guests address him; "Wallis" or "Christopher Wallis" is his scholarly byline. The book's use of both is natural. Ch 11's intimate use of "Hareesh" reads appropriate, as the chapter is confessional; Ch 10a's use of "Wallis" for the diagnostic argument reads appropriate too, as that chapter is structural. No correction needed.

**On the retrieval tool disclosure in Ch 11.** This is the cleanest handling I have seen of a teacher's refusal by a student who kept building. The author respects the specific request (no synthesis, no imitation), keeps the retrieval, and is transparent about the unresolved position. Hareesh's own stated view on transmission admitting no obstacle (from the *AMA*) is in fact structurally supportive of the retrieval tool's existence, even if he himself declined to bless it. That supporting quote is available to the author if she wants it; she has chosen not to use it, which is itself a form of restraint I would respect.

## 4. Suggested further reading from the corpus

On the specific question of *why* the tradition did not build a regulatory moral apparatus, and what it did build instead:

1. ***Awakening vs. Liberation*** — the single clearest statement of the awakening/liberation distinction, which is the tradition's actual answer to "why isn't recognition enough." If the author reads one thing to sharpen Claim 3, this is it.
2. ***AMA — How to Befriend Depression; Corrupt Gurus; My Experience of Awakening*** — the fullest treatment of the "powers and character are separate" teaching, with specific guidance on how students should hold teachers. Directly supports Claim 2.
3. ***ep. 44 — Shifting Paradigms and Near Enemies (interview by J. Brown)*** — the clearest account of tantra's positioning against Brahminical orthodoxy and dogma-heavy religion. Directly supports the factual premise of Claim 4.
4. ***ep. 15 — Classical Tantra / Abhinavagupta (with Ben Williams)*** — the historical frame. Tantra as counter-tradition with its own ethical register (*viveka*, discernment) rather than code.
5. ***Near Enemies of the Truth*** (book, not podcast) — the most developed account of how the tradition handles ethical distortion without prescribing a code. The "everything happens for the best" → "anything can happen for the best if we do the work" move is the template for how the tradition corrects near-enemies of ethics without becoming dogmatic.
6. ***Artificial Intelligence, Consciousness, and Existential Risk: The Tantric View*** — his own version of the AI/orthogonality parallel, which differs from the book's use of him in ways worth noting (see Claim 5).
7. ***The Problem of Suffering and the Problem of Evil, Part Two*** — his most careful treatment of why the tradition does not locate evil as a metaphysical category, and how this affects what ethical work remains for the practitioner.

If the author is looking for *one* place where he comes closest to admitting the constructive half is unfinished, it is in the closing section of the *Corrupt Gurus AMA*, where he says: *"there's no way to know who's awakened… therefore you should assume that anyone might fall victim to spiritualized ego at any time."* That sentence is the structural admission the book is pressing on. He has made it. The author can quote it directly without distortion.
