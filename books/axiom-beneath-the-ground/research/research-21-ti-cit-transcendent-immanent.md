## Section 1 — The passage's content (paraphrase)

[T] introduces, at p. 92 of *Tantra Illuminated*, a Tantric model of the self as nested layers. Each inner layer pervades every layer outside it. The outermost layers are the most external and objectifiable; the innermost is the power of Consciousness itself. The model comes from the Pratyabhijñā (Recognition) school of Kashmir Śaivism.

At the center: **Cit** (also called *saṃvit*) — the Power of Awareness. It is nondual, all-encompassing, and dynamic rather than static. [T] contrasts this framing with the Buddhist Void (treated in some Buddhist systems as ultimate reality) and the Vedāntic passive Witness. The Tantric Cit is neither empty-and-static nor merely a spectator. It simultaneously transcends every particular form it takes and pervades every form — it is just as present, in the tradition's formulation, in contracted and mundane states as in elevated ones.

The "transcendent and immanent" framing of Cit is verifiable in [T]'s own wording from the October 23, 2015 blog post, where he restates the *Tantra Illuminated* teaching. Whether the exact phrase appears verbatim at p. 92ff of the book is **not independently verifiable from public sources**; citation-grade accuracy requires checking the physical volume. The phrase is a faithful representation of the doctrine at pp. 92–103 regardless.

## Section 2 — Surrounding context

The layered-self teaching sits in **Part One of *Tantra Illuminated*, "The Philosophy of Nondual Śaiva Tantra."** Part One covers the Five Powers/Śaktis, the 36 Tattvas, the Four Levels of the Word, and serves as the book's philosophical foundation. The layered-self model appears as a pedagogical entry point at p. 92, with the Five Powers of Awareness elaborated at p. 101.

Part Two of the book is historical (early Śaiva streams, the Nine Sampradāyas, the Kālīkula and Krama traditions, Kashmir Shaivism, Abhinavagupta's *Tantrāloka*, drawing heavily on Alexis Sanderson). Part Three covers practice (*śaktipāta*, *dīkṣā*, the guru relationship, the three *upāyas*, ritual theory). There is an Afterword on further study and three appendices (a translation of the opening verses of the *Tantrāloka*, a brief overview of Krama worship, and a list of key Tantric texts sorted by sampradāya), followed by a Sanskrit pronunciation guide, bibliography, and index.

## Section 3 — Layer-by-layer definitions (paraphrase)

**Important correction to my prior plan:** [T]'s canonical model is the **five-layered self** (Deha, Citta, Prāṇa, Śūnya, Cit/saṃvit), not six. *Vastu* — the surrounding world of objects — is an optional outer sixth layer [T] sometimes adds as a pedagogical extension. Secondary summaries note that "in some versions of this model" Vastu is included; others omit it. The diagram caption in the published book is "five-layered self." The book's plan and Author's Note should frame this as "the five-layered self, with an optional outer sixth layer, *vastu*."

From outermost to innermost:

- **Vastu** (optional) — the stuff of the surrounding world; material environment. Included in some pedagogical versions of the model, omitted from the strict five-layer version.
- **Deha** — the physical body.
- **Citta** — heart-mind. [T] explicitly equates this with the *puryaṣṭaka* (the "eightfold city," the subtle/energy body in Tantric anatomy). The mental body and the energy body are the same thing at this layer. This is a significant departure from Vedānta, which separates them.
- **Prāṇa** — fundamental life-force. Positioned between Citta and Śūnya (between mind and void), because the layering runs from coarse to subtle rather than by ritual correspondence.
- **Śūnya** — the Void. [T] characterizes it as profound silence and stillness, a place of repose in spacious openness, empty of energy and activity. Not the ultimate layer — the penultimate.
- **Cit / saṃvit** — core Consciousness. The Power of Awareness that inherently possesses the Five Powers (cit, ānanda, icchā, jñāna, kriyā — see p. 101). Dynamic, nondual, simultaneously transcendent and immanent.

[T]'s own formulation of how the layers relate: the heart-mind pervades the body; life-force pervades heart-mind and body; the void pervades life-force, mind, and body; and Cit pervades all. Each inner layer suffuses every outer one.

## Section 5 — Cross-references

[T] attributes the scheme to the **Pratyabhijñā (Recognition) school of Kashmir Śaivism** — the lineage of Somānanda, Utpaladeva, Abhinavagupta, and Kṣemarāja.

He contrasts the Tantric five-layered self with the Vedāntic *pañca-kośa* (five-sheaths) teaching of the *Taittirīya Upaniṣad* 2.1–5, identifying four concrete differences:
1. The Tantric model orders layers coarse-to-subtle rather than by ritual correspondence.
2. Prāṇa sits between mind and void, not between body and mind as in Vedānta.
3. Vedānta's *mano-maya* and *vijñāna-maya* collapse into a single **Citta** layer in Tantra, equated with *puryaṣṭaka*.
4. The Tantric model includes a **Śūnya/Void layer that Vedānta lacks** ([T] attributes this partly to Vedānta's historically lower emphasis on meditation relative to Tantra).

The primary classical anchor for the scheme in Pratyabhijñā literature is **Kṣemarāja's *Pratyabhijñā-hṛdaya*, sūtra 8**. Kṣemarāja uses the layered-self framework to argue that rival philosophical schools each master only a particular layer of Consciousness — Materialists mastering only the body, and so on. This is the classical verse-level grounding, though no single Sanskrit verse enumerating Vastu → Deha → Citta → Prāṇa → Śūnya → Cit in this exact sequence has been identified. Vastu in particular reads as [T]'s pedagogical extension rather than a classical element.

Related texts implicitly in the background ([T]'s broader apparatus at pp. 92–103, not independently verified without the volume): **Śiva-sūtras, Spanda-kārikā, Abhinavagupta's Tantrāloka**.

## Section 6 — Notes for the book's argument

**What this grounds for *Axiom Beneath the Ground* v4:**

1. **The book's architectonic is the five-layered self, with vastu as optional outer.** Describe it honestly: the strict five is Deha, Citta, Prāṇa, Śūnya, Cit; Vastu is a pedagogical outer ring [T] sometimes adds. The book's inward/outward arc still works — it simply moves through five layers with one optional outer framing for "the world of things." This is more accurate to the source and loses nothing narratively.

2. **The central claim — Cit as simultaneously transcendent and immanent — is soundly anchored in [T]'s own writing, but the verbatim "transcendent and immanent" phrase is safest cited from his October 23, 2015 blog post, not from p. 92 of the book, until the physical volume confirms the precise wording.** A careful footnote: "[T], *Tantra Illuminated*, 2nd ed. (Mattamayūra Press, 2013), pp. 92–103, esp. 92 (five-layered self introduced) and 101 (Five Powers of Awareness); cf. [T], 'How your view of the self determines the extent of your liberation,' [T].org, October 23, 2015, for the transcendent-and-immanent formulation of Cit."

3. **The book's Tillich/[T] pivot holds and sharpens.** Tillich's *courage* as the ontological act performed from inside the Void maps cleanly onto **Śūnya** (layer 4 in the strict five, penultimate). [T]'s *svātantrya* / freedom maps onto **Cit** (layer 5, core). Courage gets the practitioner into the void; freedom turns out to be what the void's dissolved ground is made of. The relationship is not dialectical opposition — it is sequential pervasion. Śūnya is pervaded by Cit. The practitioner who passes through the void discovers the freedom that was already pervading it.

4. **Pratyabhijñā lineage attribution is important.** The book should name the Recognition school, not generic "nondual Shaiva Tantra," in the footnote that introduces the layered self. This honors the tradition and makes the classical anchor (Kṣemarāja's *Pratyabhijñā-hṛdaya* sūtra 8) available for citation.

5. **Divergence from Vedānta is a useful teaching opportunity** — not a sectarian point but a precision point. The narrator's reading year (Ch1) included Vedāntic sources; the retreat introduces a model that differs in four concrete ways. This is a quiet place for the book to earn its rigor without polemic.
