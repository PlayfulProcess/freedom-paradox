# Research 24b — The Kybalion Ch. XII, free will, and the gradient position

*Targeted refinement of research-24. Focuses on how Kybalion Ch. XII ("Causation") applies the Principle of Polarity to free will and determinism, and how that move is supported across philosophical and clinical traditions.*

## Core finding

**"Not total free will, not none"** is not a New-Age compromise; it is the convergent position of six distinct traditions, each arriving from its own starting point. The Kybalion Ch. XII names, in 1908 language, what William James in the same decade, Chrysippus two millennia earlier, Nāgārjuna, Aristotle, Fischer-Wolf-List, and Linehan all arrive at independently: **degreed agency**.

## Section 1 — Ch. XII passages

The chapter opens asserting the Sixth Hermetic Principle (Cause and Effect), insisting "nothing happens by Chance" and cashing out "chance" as cause unperceived. It pivots via Polarity: "both are but Half-Truths — the opposing poles of Truth" (Ch. XII).

Key passages (≤30 words each, public-domain):
- "may be both Free and yet bound by Necessity, depending upon the … height of Truth from which the matter is examined"
- "The further the creation is from the Centre, the more it is bound; the nearer the Centre it reaches, the nearer Free is it"
- "the majority of people are more or less the slaves of heredity, environment, etc., and manifest very little Freedom"
- "become Movers in the game, instead of Pawns — Causes instead of Effects"
- "The Masters do not escape the Causation of the higher planes, but fall in with the higher laws"
- "Man may use Law to overcome laws"

Four-beat structure: assert universal causation → raise free-will objection → resolve via Polarity into a scale → locate humans at varying heights on that scale.

## Section 2 — William James and classical compatibilism

James coined the vocabulary in "The Dilemma of Determinism" (1884, repr. *The Will to Believe*, 1897): "old-fashioned determinism was what we may call hard determinism" and "nowadays, we have a soft determinism which abhors harsh words … says that its real name is freedom." James rejects both horns for a limited indeterminism: "possibilities may be in excess of actualities."

In *Principles of Psychology* (1890), vol. II, ch. XXVI ("Will"), James locates volition in the "effort of attention to a naturally repugnant idea" — intrinsically graded, since effort varies. James is the proximate intellectual fixture of New Thought; Atkinson's Ch. XII move tracks James's structure precisely.

Classical compatibilism:
- **Harry Frankfurt**, "Freedom of the Will and the Concept of a Person" (*J. Phil.* 1971). Frankfurt cases (1969) detach responsibility from ability to do otherwise.
- **Daniel Dennett**, *Elbow Room* (1984); *Freedom Evolves* (2003). "The free will worth wanting" is evolved naturalistic capacity.
- **P.F. Strawson**, "Freedom and Resentment" (*Proc. Brit. Acad.* 1962). Responsibility is constituted by participant-stance practices.

## Section 3 — Libertarianism and hard determinism (briefly)

- **Robert Kane**, *The Significance of Free Will* (Oxford, 1996). Event-causal libertarianism; "self-forming actions" as the site of indeterminism.
- **Roderick Chisholm**, "Human Freedom and the Self" (1964); **Timothy O'Connor**, *Persons and Causes* (Oxford, 2000). Agent causation.
- **Derk Pereboom**, *Living Without Free Will* (Cambridge, 2001); *Free Will, Agency, and Meaning in Life* (Oxford, 2014). Hard incompatibilism; four-case manipulation argument.
- **Galen Strawson**, "Impossibility of Moral Responsibility" (*Phil. Studies* 75, 1994). The Basic Argument.

These are the poles the "truth in between" claim rejects.

## Section 4 — Gradualist / degrees-of-freedom views (the mainstream)

- **John Martin Fischer & Mark Ravizza**, *Responsibility and Control* (Cambridge, 1998). Semicompatibilism: responsibility requires "guidance control" via moderately **reasons-responsive** mechanism — explicitly scalar.
- **Susan Wolf**, *Freedom Within Reason* (Oxford, 1990). Reason View: freedom admits of degrees.
- **Christian List**, *Why Free Will Is Real* (Harvard, 2019). Emergentist compatibilism.
- **Dana Nelkin**, "Difficulty and Degrees of Moral Praiseworthiness and Blameworthiness" (*Noûs* 50.2, 2016). Scalar praise/blame.
- **Manuel Vargas**, *Building Better Beings* (Oxford, 2013). Revisionist compatibilism; responsibility as cultivated capacity.
- **Alfred Mele**, *Free Will and Luck* (Oxford, 2006). Modest libertarianism; localized indeterminism.

**Binary free-will / no-free-will is a dialectical loser; degreed freedom is the mainstream position.**

## Section 5 — Buddhist middle way on free will

- **SN 12.17 Acela Kassapa Sutta**: the Buddha rejects both *sayaṃkata* (self-caused) and *paraṃkata* (other-caused), teaching by the middle.
- **SN 12.15 Kaccānagotta Sutta**: right view between existence/nonexistence extremes via dependent origination.
- **AN 3.61 Titthāyatana**: three wrong views that collapse agency (pure past-karma, divine fiat, chance) all end in inaction.
- **Nāgārjuna**, *Mūlamadhyamakakārikā* 15 (no *svabhāva*); 8.12 (agent and action mutually establishing); 17 (karma works causally without permanent self or annihilation).

Scholarly split:
- **Peter Harvey**, *Introduction to Buddhist Ethics* (Cambridge, 2000); *JBE* 14 (2007). Moderate compatibilism; middle between "completely rigidly determined" and "totally and unconditionally free."
- **Joanna Macy**, *Mutual Causality in Buddhism and General Systems Theory* (SUNY, 1991). Reciprocal/mutual causality dissolves linear-determinism framing.
- **Mark Siderits**, "Paleo-compatibilism," *Sophia* 47 (2008); *Buddhism as Philosophy* (2007). Two-truths doctrine: conventionally free, ultimately determined, semantically insulated.
- **Riccardo Repetti**, *Buddhism, Meditation, and Free Will* (Routledge, 2019). "Buddhist soft compatibilism"; meditation as cultivation of graded mental freedom.
- **Charles Goodman**, *Consequences of Compassion* (Oxford, 2009). Reads Buddhism closer to hard determinism — but notes this *supports* non-retributivism.

Middle-way synthesis: agency is real and practically indispensable, but not metaphysically substantial.

## Section 6 — Aristotelian and Stoic precursors

- **Aristotle**, *Nicomachean Ethics* III.1–5: *hekousion*, *akousion*, mixed actions. *Prohairesis* discriminates character. Virtue as *hexis prohairetikē* (II.6). **Responsibility is developmental and scalar.**
- **Chrysippus**, preserved in Cicero *De Fato* 39–43 and Aulus Gellius *Attic Nights* VII.2. Antecedent/auxiliary vs. principal/perfect causes. Cylinder analogy: "he who pushed the cylinder gave it the start of its motion, but did not give it its rollability" (*De Fato* 43). **Push is external, roll is internal. Agent is neither unmoved mover nor passive projectile.**

Ch. XII's structure is effectively Chrysippus's cylinder in Hermetic costume.

## Section 7 — DBT and clinical dialectic of agency

**Marsha Linehan** is the clearest clinical embodiment of truth-in-between on agency.

Biosocial theory is backward-deterministic: emotion dysregulation as transactional product of biological vulnerability × invalidating environment. Central assumption: "All behaviors (actions, thoughts, emotions) are caused" (Linehan, 2015).

Yet treatment architecture presupposes forward agency: commitment strategies, skills training, Wise Mind. Signature formulation pairs both:
- "Clients may not have caused all of their own problems, but they have to solve them anyway" (Linehan, 1993, p. 107).
- "Clients are doing the best they can … [and] need to do better, try harder, and/or be more motivated to change" (Linehan, 1993, pp. 106–107).

Radical acceptance acknowledges the causal history; commitment and skills build new causes. Parallels: Hayes's ACT; Deci & Ryan's Self-Determination Theory (autonomy on a continuum, *Am. Psychol.* 55, 2000). Clinically, "not total free will, not none" is the working consensus of empirically-validated third-wave therapies.

## Section 8 — Notes for the book

**Defensible claim:** "Not total free will, not none" is not middling compromise but convergent across six distinct traditions.

Three distinctions to keep the argument clean:
1. **Kybalion's framing is metaphysical** — poles of a single reality, resolved vertically (mental transmutation, rising toward the Centre).
2. **Classical compatibilism is conceptual** — freedom *defined* as acting from one's own reasons; determinism dissolved rather than resolved.
3. **Buddhist middle is ontological** — no independent self; agency is real but dependently-arisen, non-substantial.

All three converge on degreed agency. For a book grounded in Kybalion language, the cleanest move is: state the Ch. XII polarity framing up front, then note the same conclusion is reached by James, Chrysippus, Aristotle, the Buddhist middle way, Fischer/Wolf/List, and Linehan.
