# **Christopher "Hareesh" Wallis: The Scholar Rebuilding Classical Tantra**

Christopher D. Wallis — known by his spiritual name Hareesh — is arguably **the most prominent living Western scholar-practitioner of nondual Shaiva Tantra**. With a PhD from UC Berkeley, an MPhil from Oxford, training under the world's foremost academic authority on Shaivism (Alexis Sanderson), and decades of traditional practice beginning in childhood, he occupies a rare position at the intersection of rigorous Indological scholarship and living spiritual transmission. His flagship book *Tantra Illuminated* has sold over **50,000 copies by word-of-mouth alone**, becoming the standard English-language introduction to classical Tantra. His work centers on a single mission: making the original Sanskrit sources of the nondual Shaiva tradition accessible and alive for contemporary practitioners while correcting widespread distortions of what "Tantra" actually means.

---

## **From ashram child to Oxford Sanskritist**

Wallis entered the world of Indian spirituality at age seven, raised by parents who were devotees of **Baba Muktananda**, founder of the Siddha Yoga lineage. He grew up training at ashrams in **South Fallsburg, New York** and **Ganeshpuri, India**, receiving traditional education in meditation, mantra, kīrtan, karma-yoga, and pedagogical methods. At age nine, his mother's guru gave him the Sanskrit name **Hareesh** (Hareśa), meaning "remover of sorrows" — a name of both Vishnu and Shiva, offered as "an invitation to grow into."

The defining spiritual event of his youth came at **age sixteen**, when he met **Gurumayi Chidvilasananda**, head of the Siddha Yoga tradition. He describes receiving *śaktipāta* — a powerful descent of spiritual energy — that fundamentally transformed him. He subsequently **lived in Gurumayi's ashram for three years**, and regards her as both his *dīkṣā-guru* (initiating teacher) and *mūla-guru* (root teacher). He began teaching "with the permission of my guru."

His academic path was extraordinary in both breadth and rigor. He earned a **B.A. *magna cum laude*** in Religion and Classics from the University of Rochester (2001), was inducted into **Phi Beta Kappa**, then completed an **M.A. with Honors** in South Asian Studies (Sanskrit) at UC Berkeley (2003). A **Beinecke Brothers Scholarship** took him to the **University of Oxford**, where he earned an **M.Phil. with Distinction** in Classical Indian Religions (2005), studying under Alexis Sanderson. He returned to Berkeley for his doctorate, completing his **PhD in 2014** under Robert P. Goldman and Alexander von Rospatt. His dissertation — *"To Enter, to Be Entered, to Merge: The Role of Religious Experience in the Traditions of Tantric Shaivism"* — is a philological study arguing that the technical term *samāveśa* (immersive fusion with divine consciousness) is the key concept distinguishing Tantric Shaivism from mainstream Vedic religion, and that direct spiritual experience was considered indispensable throughout the medieval tradition. His Sanskrit expertise spans Classical, Purānic, Medieval, and Scholastic registers.

Rather than pursuing a conventional academic career, Wallis became a full-time independent scholar-teacher. He currently teaches internationally, leads retreats primarily in **Portugal**, and operates from a base connected to **Boulder, Colorado**. He founded the **Mattamayura Institute** (named after a medieval Shaiva monastic university meaning "hall of the divinely intoxicated peacock") and built **Tantra Illuminated Online** as his primary teaching platform.

## **Seven teachers across scholarly and initiatory traditions**

Wallis's formation reflects an unusual combination of traditional guru-disciple transmission and elite Western academic training. He publicly acknowledges seven primary teachers, each contributing a distinct dimension to his work, while noting that others "wish to remain anonymous."

**Gurumayi Chidvilasananda** provided his foundational initiation and spiritual grounding within the **Siddha Yoga** lineage (Bhagawan Nityananda → Swami Muktananda → Gurumayi). He is listed on the Nityananda Tradition website as a disciple in the Muktananda lineage. However, Wallis has been candid that Siddha Yoga "couldn't teach the practices of Tantra, but only quoted the Tantric scriptures" — their practice instructions were relatively simple, which drove him to seek deeper training.

**Alexis Sanderson**, Emeritus Fellow of All Souls College and former Spalding Professor of Eastern Religions at Oxford, was his academic mentor in Sanskrit and Shaiva-Shakta Tantra. Sanderson spent **six years studying in Kashmir with Swami Lakshman Joo**, the last recognized guru of the Trika lineage, making him a living bridge to the historical tradition. Through Sanderson, Wallis has an indirect scholarly connection to Lakshman Joo and the classical Trika *paramparā*. Wallis studied with Sanderson "formally and informally at Oxford, Leipzig, Kyoto, and Portland."

**Paul Muller-Ortega**, founder of Blue Throat Yoga, author of *The Triadic Heart of Śiva*, and retired professor, taught him Shaiva Tantra and Classical Yoga. Muller-Ortega was himself a devotee of both Maharishi Mahesh Yogi and Gurumayi, but was "really devoted to Abhinavagupta." Wallis describes how both he and Muller-Ortega experienced what felt like "receiving transmission from Abhinavagupta" through deep engagement with his Sanskrit texts — an unconventional but personally significant form of lineage connection.

**Somadeva Vasudeva**, author of *The Yoga of the Mālinīvijayottaratantra*, provided further academic-practitioner training in Shaiva Tantra. **Dharmabodhi Sarasvati** (also known as Kolbjorn Martens), who trained for fifteen years under Paramahamsa Satyananda Saraswati in tantric yoga and kundalini practice, contributed the practical dimension of Tantrik Yoga from his base in northern Thailand. **Marshall Rosenberg** (creator of Nonviolent Communication) and **Adyashanti** (American nondual meditation teacher) round out his acknowledged teachers, representing non-traditional but significant influences.

His teaching lineage thus draws from multiple streams: the **Siddha Yoga** tradition formally, the **Kaula Trika** lineage of Abhinavagupta intellectually and spiritually, the **Krama** tradition (the most radical nondual Tantric lineage), and the **Pratyabhijñā** (Recognition) philosophical school. He advocates replacing the traditional guru model with what he calls the **deshika (spiritual guide) model** — teachers transmit teachings and practice rather than power, and the express purpose of the relationship is "to empower and equip the student to become the guru's equal."

## **Recovering authentic Tantra from its distortions**

Wallis's core philosophical project is the recovery and contemporary presentation of **nondual Shaiva Tantra** (NST) as found in original Sanskrit sources from roughly **800–1100 CE**, the tradition's golden age. He prefers this term over "Kashmir Shaivism," arguing the latter is misleading since the tradition was **pan-Indian**, not limited to the Kashmir region.

His definition of NST centers on four elements: direct experience of a divine reality with both transcendent and immanent aspects (Śiva as pure consciousness, Śakti as the dynamic energy comprising all manifestation); initiation into a guru-disciple relationship and egalitarian spiritual community (*kula*); spiritual practice across four dimensions — contemplation of philosophical teachings (View), meditation rituals, yogic techniques of the subtle body, and **aesthetic cultivation of the senses**; and liberation through recognition rather than acquisition.

The **Pratyabhijñā (Recognition) philosophy** is central to everything he teaches. Drawing from Kṣemarāja's *Pratyabhijñā-hṛdaya*, he argues that liberation comes not through accumulative self-improvement but through *pratyabhijñā* — the sudden recognition of one's own essential nature as divine Consciousness. "You lack nothing but this recognition." He distinguishes this sharply from both Advaita Vedanta's passive witness consciousness and dualistic theistic models, as well as from the modern spiritual-growth paradigm.

His emphasis on **original Sanskrit sources** is methodological as well as philosophical. He argues that nearly all source material for classical Shaiva Tantra exists only in Sanskrit, that most previous translations contained significant errors, and that accurate translation requires both scholarly philological rigor and experiential practice. His blog post "The Real Story on the Chakras" — which received **nearly 200,000 views** — demonstrated this approach by showing how returning to Sanskrit sources overturns popular Western misunderstandings about the chakra system.

Perhaps his most forceful public position involves **distinguishing classical Tantra from "neo-Tantra."** He argues that Western sexual tantra "was invented around 1900 by the American Pierre Bernard" and bears "little to no resemblance" to the classical tradition. Classical Tantra, he insists, is about expanding awareness and breaking through limiting conditioning. While certain classical texts do describe sacred sexual ritual (*kula-yāga*), this was one small element within a vast, comprehensive spiritual system. The word *tantra* itself simply means "a system of essential instruction" or the scripture documenting such a system.

An original conceptual framework he has developed is the **"Near Enemies of the Truth"** — the idea that popular spiritual teachings such as "You create your own reality," "Follow your bliss," "Be in the present moment," and "I am my own guru" are subtle distortions of genuine insights, close enough to seem true but twisted in ways that can cause real suffering. This framework became his third book and represents his most accessible contribution to broader spiritual discourse.

## **Three landmark books and a prolific translation project**

Wallis's published works span three books, peer-reviewed academic publications, and an expansive ongoing body of translations and commentary.

***Tantra Illuminated: The Philosophy, History, and Practice of a Timeless Tradition*** (Mattamayura Press, first published 2012, now in its 3rd edition with a 4th in preparation) is his magnum opus and widely considered **the definitive English-language introduction to classical Tantra**. At 506 pages with illustrations by Ekabhumi Ellik, it covers core philosophy (the five Śaktis, the 36 Tattvas, four levels of the Word), a condensed history of the Shaiva tradition from early beginnings through the nine *sampradāyas*, and practical teachings. Christopher Tompkins called it "the most accurate, comprehensive, and accessible portrayal of the Shaiva tradition by any modern scholar." Translated into **six languages** (Spanish, Portuguese, Romanian, German, French), its **50,000+ copies sold purely by word-of-mouth** is remarkable for a nonfiction work on Indian philosophy.

***The Recognition Sūtras: Illuminating a 1,000-Year-Old Spiritual Masterpiece*** (Mattamayura Press, 2017\) is a translation and extensive commentary on all 20 sūtras of Kṣemarāja's *Pratyabhijñā-hṛdaya*, which Wallis presents as the first fully accurate and explained English translation. The text's opening sūtra — *citiḥ svatantrā viśva-siddhi-hetuḥ* ("Awareness, free and independent, is the cause of the performance of everything") — encapsulates the Recognition school's central insight. The book includes guided meditations and practices, making it both a scholarly translation and a contemplative manual. Sally Kempton praised it as "a reliable and graceful translation."

***Near Enemies of the Truth*** (Wonderwell, 2023\) marks Wallis's entry into mainstream publishing, deliberately written with minimal Sanskrit terminology for a general audience. Each chapter identifies a misleading popular spiritual teaching, explains why it's problematic, reveals the deeper truth it distorts, and includes guided reflections. Endorsed by prominent nondual teacher Rupert Spira, it has been translated into German (as *Toxic Spirituality*) and Spanish.

His **academic publications** include a paper in the *Journal of Indian Philosophy* (2007/2008) on Abhinavagupta's theology of *śaktipāta* and *samāveśa*, including a complete translation of Chapter 11 of the *Tantrasāra*; and a chapter on alchemical metaphors in Abhinavagupta's commentaries in the prestigious **Brill festschrift** *Śaivism and the Tantric Traditions: Essays in Honour of Alexis G.J.S. Sanderson* (2020), alongside leading Indologists like Isabelle Ratié and John Nemec.

As of 2024, Wallis has announced plans for **up to ten additional books**, including translations of Abhinavagupta's *Tantrasāra* and *Tantrāloka*, the *Spanda-kārikā* with Kṣemarāja's commentaries, practice teachings from the Vijñāna-bhairava-tantra, collections of Krama master poetry, and the *Śiva Sūtras*. Much of this work is already appearing in serialized form on his blog, where he maintains **verse-by-verse translation series** on the VBT, Tantrāloka, Tantrasāra, and other texts — effectively publishing in-progress scholarship in real time.

His teaching platform **Tantra Illuminated Online** offers over 25 on-demand courses, 150+ embodiment classes, 50+ guided meditations, weekly Saturday Satsang sessions, and intensive programs including a three-month immersion and a year-long program. He also developed the **Tantra112 app** dedicated to the 112 meditation methods of the Vijñāna-bhairava-tantra.

## **Deep engagement with Abhinavagupta and the Recognition masters**

Wallis's textual scholarship focuses on the philosophical and practical works of the **Pratyabhijñā school** and its greatest synthesizer, **Abhinavagupta** (c. 950–1016 CE), whom he treats not merely as a historical figure but as a living spiritual presence. He and Paul Muller-Ortega both describe experiences of receiving what felt like direct transmission from Abhinavagupta through deep study of his Sanskrit writings — a claim Wallis defends by arguing that "if Abhinavagupta is a Mahāsiddha, he is not bound by time and space."

His work on the **Vijñāna-bhairava-tantra** (VBT) is among his most sustained projects. This text, containing **112 meditation methods** (*yuktis*), is the most celebrated practice text of nondual Śākta-Śaiva Tantra. Wallis has published an ongoing verse-by-verse blog series providing deep-dive translations and commentary, many verses "translated correctly for the first time" by his account. He has also published a concordance comparing eight English, two French, one German, and one Spanish translation, and created the Tantra112 app with detailed audio teachings and guided meditations for each method.

His engagement with the **Krama lineage** — the most esoteric and "left-hand" of the nondual Tantric lineages — represents an area he considers "much more important than previously suspected." He has published blog series on the Twelve Kālīs and the Krama path, with a planned book (*The Way of the Goddess: The Secret Oral Teachings of the Tantric Yoginīs*) devoted to this material. He emphasizes that the tradition was fundamentally "a Śākta-Śaiva tradition and always was" — pointing out that even Abhinavagupta "in his actual practice and worship worshipped only the Goddess" (Parā Devī, Kālī).

His doctoral contribution — arguing that *samāveśa* is the key distinguishing term of Tantric Shaivism — reframes the entire tradition around the centrality of **immersive spiritual experience** rather than ritual procedure or philosophical doctrine alone.

## **Significant influence tempered by substantive debate**

Wallis's influence extends across the contemporary yoga world, academic Indology, and the broader spiritual landscape. His Patreon community numbers **370 paying members**, his Instagram following stands at approximately **15,000**, and he teaches at premier venues including **Omega Institute**. He leads retreats in Portugal, workshops internationally, and pilgrimages to India.

His academic credibility is bolstered by his **inclusion in the Sanderson festschrift** (a marker of acceptance by the field's leading scholars), his peer-reviewed publications, and his training lineage. However, he does not hold a formal university appointment, which limits his standing within traditional academia. His books are published primarily through his own press rather than academic publishers, creating a positioning that sits deliberately between scholarly rigor and practitioner accessibility.

Several substantive controversies mark his public career. The most prominent involved his argument that **Shaivism historically preceded and is distinct from "Hinduism"** as a category. In 2015, Belgian Indologist **Koenraad Elst** published a lengthy critique accusing Wallis of naively borrowing an anti-Hindu academic framework. The exchange became heated, was picked up by Hindu nationalist-aligned publications, and touched the sensitive nerve of decolonization debates in Indian studies.

A more intellectually productive exchange occurred with **Matthew Remski**, a yoga journalist and critic of cultic dynamics. Remski critiqued Wallis's proposed reforms of the guru-disciple relationship as still "isolating power in a single loop while weighting responsibility towards the student" — arguing that structural reforms, not just better education within the tradition, were needed to prevent abuse. Wallis's own background in **Siddha Yoga**, which has been subject to abuse allegations since the 1980s, adds personal stakes to this debate.

An early edition of *Tantra Illuminated* recommended **Swami Shankarananda**, an Australian guru later accused of sexual abuse, prompting criticism from readers about discernment. Some practitioners also find his tone occasionally self-aggrandizing, with one reviewer noting the book "began to feel like a vanity project with how many times Wallis asserted that only he and the select few that had been graced would receive the benefits discussed." Philosophical critic L. Ron Gardner challenged his use of quantum physics analogies to support idealist monism in *The Recognition Sutras*.

## **Conclusion**

Wallis represents something genuinely new in the transmission history of Shaiva Tantra: a Western practitioner with both traditional initiatory credentials and world-class philological training, committed to making the original Sanskrit sources speak to contemporary seekers without diluting them. His insistence on returning to primary texts has produced demonstrably better translations and corrected widespread misconceptions — the chakra article alone reached hundreds of thousands. His "Near Enemies" framework offers an original lens for evaluating spiritual claims. The controversies around him — about Hinduism as a category, about guru-disciple power dynamics, about the limits of scholarly authority in spiritual contexts — are themselves evidence that he operates at genuinely contested boundaries where important questions remain unresolved. With ten books planned and a growing international community, his project of recovering classical Tantra for the contemporary world is still in its middle chapters.
