# Research 24 — The Kybalion: truth in between (broader dossier)

**Provenance caveats up front.** *The Kybalion: A Study of the Hermetic Philosophy of Ancient Egypt and Greece* is a **1908 New Thought book published in Chicago, not an ancient Hermetic text**. Its pharaonic framing is a literary conceit. Authorship, signed "Three Initiates," is now overwhelmingly attributed to **William Walker Atkinson (1862–1932)** alone, based on publishing-network, biographical, and stylometric evidence assembled by Philip Deslippe (2011) and Mitch Horowitz (2009). The scholarly consensus — Deslippe, Horowitz, Nicholas E. Chapel, Wouter Hanegraaff, Florian Ebeling — treats the book as an early-20th-century synthesis of New Thought, Theosophy, and Kingsford/Maitland-style Hermetic paraphrase in Hermetic costume. For a serious nonfiction book, cite the Kybalion as **one modern entry-point** into a dialectical posture, with heavier conceptual weight carried by Aristotle, Nāgārjuna, Heraclitus, Hegel, or Linehan. In the US public domain (pre-1928).

---

## Section 1 — Passages behind "the truth lies in between"

The phrase summarizes, rather than quotes, material distributed across four chapters of the 1908 edition. The **Principle of Polarity**, introduced as an epigraphic maxim at the head of Chapter II ("The Seven Hermetic Principles") and again at Chapter X ("Polarity"), does the core work. Chapter VI ("The Divine Paradox") frames the reconciliation as a warning against half-truths. Chapter XII ("Causation") applies it to free-will/determinism. Chapter XIII ("Gender") extends the logic.

Short quotations (each under 30 words, public-domain, drawn from the 1908 Yogi Publication Society edition via Wikisource / Project Gutenberg):
- Ch. X: "'Opposites' are really only the two extremes of the same thing, with many varying degrees between them."
- Ch. X on Love/Hate: "there are degrees of Hate and degrees of Love, and a middle point in which we use the terms 'Like or Dislike'."
- Ch. VI: "Remember ever the Two Poles of Truth—the Absolute and the Relative. Beware of Half-Truths."
- Ch. XII: "both are but Half-Truths—the opposing poles of Truth."

## Section 2 — Principle of Polarity

Verbatim maxim, at the head of Chapters II and X:

> "Everything is Dual; everything has poles; everything has its pair of opposites; like and unlike are the same; opposites are identical in nature, but different in degree; extremes meet; all truths are but half-truths; all paradoxes may be reconciled."

Framing is monistic and gradualist: opposites are extremes of a single axis with intermediate degrees. Illustrated with thermometer imagery (no point where heat ends and cold begins) and Love/Hate/Like/Dislike.

## Section 3 — "The truth lies in between" is paraphrase, not quotation

Full-text searches of the 1908 edition (Wikisource, Project Gutenberg, YOGeBooks, sacred-texts.com) **do not find the phrase verbatim.** The word "middle" appears only in "middle point" (Ch. X) and "middle-men" (Ch. IV). "The truth lies in between" is a 20th-/21st-century distillation by later Hermeticists — faithful to the spirit but **not a quotation**. The book should cite accordingly.

## Section 4 — Authorship and provenance

- **Publication:** 1908, Yogi Publication Society, Masonic Temple Building, Chicago. LCCN 08018560. 223 pp. US public domain.
- **Atkinson as author:** Illinois attorney turned New Thought publisher. Pseudonyms include Yogi Ramacharaka, Theron Q. Dumont, Magus Incognito. Listed *The Kybalion* among his works in his 1912 *Who's Who in America* entry. Later *The Arcane Teaching* (c.1909) and *The Secret Doctrine of the Rosicrucians* (1918, as Magus Incognito) rework the same seven-principle system.
- **Deslippe scholarship:** Philip Deslippe, *The Kybalion: The Definitive Edition* (Tarcher/Penguin, 2011), 40-page scholarly intro. Archival publisher records + computer-aided stylometric analysis → "Three Initiates" was Atkinson alone. Identifies Atkinson's sources: Kingsford & Maitland, *The Virgin of the World of Hermes Mercurius Trismegistus* (1885); G.R.S. Mead, *Thrice-Greatest Hermes* (1906); Blavatsky, *Secret Doctrine* (1888); Thomas Burgoyne; Florence Huntley.
- **Hermetic-lineage status:** Nicholas E. Chapel, "The Kybalion's New Clothes," *Journal of the Western Mystery Tradition* 3, no. 24 (2013), shows Principle of Vibration derives from David Hartley's 18th-c. associationism; 147-plane cosmology absent from classical Hermetica; anti-theological stance contradicts theocentric *Corpus Hermeticum*. Consistent with Hanegraaff, *Esotericism and the Academy* (Cambridge, 2012) and Ebeling, *Secret History of Hermes Trismegistus* (Cornell, 2007).

## Section 5 — Dialectical family

Any book using the Kybalion as entry-point should mark differences rather than blur them:
- **Hegel's dialectic:** *Aufhebung* (sublation) — to cancel, preserve, and raise up at once (*Science of Logic*). The thesis-antithesis-synthesis formula was popularized by Heinrich Chalybäus (1837); Hegel himself dismissed mechanical triadism as a "lifeless schema." See Mueller, "The Hegel Legend of 'Thesis-Antithesis-Synthesis,'" *J. Hist. Ideas* 19, no. 3 (1958).
- **Buddhist Middle Way:** practical (avoiding indulgence and mortification, SN 56.11); Madhyamaka (Nāgārjuna, *Mūlamadhyamakakārikā* 24.18, 18.8 — tetralemma dissolves all four positions).
- **Aristotle's golden mean:** *Nicomachean Ethics* II.6 (1106b36–1107a2). Agent-relative, not arithmetic. Some actions admit no mean (1107a8–17).
- **Heraclitus:** *palintropos harmoniē* — back-turning attunement. DK B51, B60.
- **Daoist yin–yang:** *Daodejing* 2, 42. Mutually generating correlatives; no higher synthesis.
- **Jung's coincidentia oppositorum:** from Nicholas of Cusa, *De docta ignorantia* (1440). *Mysterium Coniunctionis*, CW vol. 14.
- **Linehan's DBT dialectic:** radical acceptance + commitment to change, "Wise Mind" synthesis.

**Common pitfall:** popular "truth lies in the middle" rhetoric often collapses these into bland moderation. None of the serious traditions permit that.

## Section 6 — Ancient Hermetica vs. the Kybalion

The Kybalion's seven-principle framework **does not appear in any ancient Hermetic text**. Of the seven, three — Mentalism, Correspondence, Gender — have narrow ancient resonance; the other four are 18th–19th-c. imports.

On polarity: *Corpus Hermeticum* I.9, I.15 describe primal Mind and Anthropos as androgynous. *CH* XI.20: bring together opposites of quality (heat/cold, dryness/fluidity) — mystical transcendence of contraries, not spectrum-monism. *Asclepius* 20–21 (and Coptic NHC VI,8): divine androgyny — the one genuine ancient root for the Kybalion's Gender principle. The Emerald Tablet's "as above, so below" is correspondence, not polarity, and is medieval Arabic (8th–9th c. *Sirr al-khalīqa*), not ancient Egyptian — see Kevin van Bladel, *The Arabic Hermes* (Oxford, 2009).

Framing scholarship: Garth Fowden, *The Egyptian Hermes* (Princeton/Cambridge, 1986/1993); Christian H. Bull, *The Tradition of Hermes Trismegistus* (Brill, 2018); Hanegraaff, *Hermetic Spirituality and the Historical Imagination* (Cambridge, 2022).

## Section 7 — Notes for the book

For a book using the Kybalion as **one entry-point among several** into a dialectical posture (absolute/relational, transcendent/immanent, courage/freedom):

**Cite accurately.** The Kybalion is a 1908 New Thought work that popularized a Hermetic-inspired Principle of Polarity. Phrasings like "often summarized — though not verbatim — as 'the truth lies in between'" are defensible.

**Anchor the conceptual argument elsewhere.** The philosophical load is better carried by Nāgārjuna (two-truths, tetralemma), Linehan (acceptance AND change), Aristotle (agent-relative mean), or Hegel (*Aufhebung*). For somatic register: Jung's *coincidentia*, Heraclitus's *palintropos harmoniē*. For practice: Buddhist middle way, Daoist complementarity.

**Flag the shared pitfall.** Holding two truths simultaneously is not splitting the difference. Aristotle's mean is agent-relative and excludes some acts entirely; Nāgārjuna's middle is emptiness; Linehan's dialectic is full acceptance AND full commitment to change; Hegel's sublation preserves what it negates.
