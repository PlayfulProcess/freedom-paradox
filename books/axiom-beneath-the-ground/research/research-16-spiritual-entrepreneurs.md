# **Two spiritual entrepreneurs, two very different business models**

Buddhist minister John B. Freese and tantric scholar Christopher Wallis both operate at the intersection of Eastern spirituality and Western commerce, but their economic realities could not be more different. **Freese runs a modest, multi-hustle operation** combining adjunct teaching at three small institutions with low-fee spiritual counseling and a virtual temple — likely earning under $100K/year total. **Wallis has built a full-scale spiritual media and education business** generating an estimated $300K–$800K+ annually through stacked revenue streams: subscriptions, retreats, self-published bestsellers, an app, and a Portugal-based retreat center. Neither holds a clinical therapy license. Both operate under the clergy/spiritual counseling exemption that exists in virtually every US state — a legal distinction that matters enormously for how each structures their work.

---

## **Freese is not a therapist, and the framing is deliberate**

Rev. Dr. John Brooks Freese (Dhammabodhi) holds a **B.A. in Film from Vassar College** (1995), an **M.Div. in Buddhist Chaplaincy from University of the West**, and a **PhD in Practical Theology from Claremont School of Theology** (2022). None of these are clinical degrees. His doctoral dissertation — "A Mutual Critical Correlation of Buddhist Meditation and Trauma Therapy" — studied the intersection of Buddhist practice and trauma treatment, but studying therapy academically is categorically different from completing the supervised clinical hours required for licensure.

**No clinical license exists for Freese** in any therapist lookup database searched. He does not appear on Psychology Today, GoodTherapy, BetterHelp, or any licensed therapist directory. He holds no LCSW, LMFT, LPC, or psychology license. His bio references "clinical training" — specifically body-centered trauma therapy work with Dr. Stephanie Mines in Boulder during college and dissertation research interviewing psychotherapists — but this does not constitute the **3,000–4,000 supervised clinical hours** required for licensure in any state.

This matters because Freese carefully frames everything he does as **"Buddhist counseling" and "spiritual care"** — never as therapy, psychotherapy, or clinical treatment. Through his virtual temple Mukti Vihara, he and colleague Rev. Corey Swartsel offer Buddhist counseling sessions via Zoom on a **sliding scale of $40–$80 per hour**. The methodology draws on early Buddhist satipatthana teachings, Yogacara consciousness theory, and Acceptance Commitment Therapy as a framework — but the service is explicitly presented as spiritual care, not clinical intervention.

## **The clergy exemption makes this entirely legal**

Virtually every US state includes a clergy exemption in its therapy licensing statutes. The core principle, established in *DeBose v. Bear Valley Church of Christ* (Colorado, 1995): **"So long as the cleric providing pastoral counseling is not held out as a therapeutic counselor, the standards of care applicable to therapeutic counseling cannot be applied to a pastoral counselor."**

The exemption typically requires three conditions: the counselor must be ordained by an established religious organization, the counseling must fall within the scope of ministerial duties, and the counselor must remain accountable to their religious body. Freese satisfies all three — he founded the Dhamma Vinaya Order (a lay Buddhist order with three ordination levels: Upasaka, Dhammacari, and Bodhicari), serves as abbot of Mukti Vihara Temple, and operates within the scope of Buddhist ministerial care.

The phrase **"trauma-informed spiritual care"** is legally distinct from "trauma therapy." A Buddhist minister can be trauma-informed — meaning aware of how trauma manifests and adjusting spiritual care practices accordingly (e.g., not forcing closed-eye meditation for trauma survivors) — without providing clinical trauma treatment like EMDR or prolonged exposure therapy. This is a recognized and growing field within professional chaplaincy, supported by the VA's National Center for PTSD, which specifically distinguishes chaplain-led spiritual care from clinical trauma treatment.

What spiritual counselors **cannot** do: diagnose mental health disorders using DSM criteria, administer psychological assessments, use protected titles (psychologist, licensed counselor), or bill insurance. The 2022 CMS chaplaincy procedure codes (Q9001–Q9003) are tracking codes only — not yet billable for reimbursement.

## **Freese's income is a patchwork of modest streams**

Freese's economic picture resembles many academics and spiritual practitioners: **multiple low-paying positions stacked together**. He teaches at three institutions simultaneously — University of the West, Naropa University, and Sakya Buddha University — which strongly suggests adjunct or part-time status at most or all of them.

**University of the West** is a tiny private Buddhist university in Rosemead, California (47 full-time undergrads), affiliated with the Fo Guang Shan Buddhist order. Full-time faculty salaries likely range **$50,000–$80,000**; adjunct pay would be substantially less. **Naropa University** in Boulder, Colorado pays full-time faculty an average of roughly **$73,000/year** (37% below the Colorado average), with adjunct pay as low as **$2,574–$3,432 per course per semester** — approximately $35,000/year if teaching full-time equivalent load. **Sakya Buddha University** in Santa Ana, California is a small Buddhist institution with BPPE authorization but apparently no regional accreditation; salary data is unavailable but likely modest.

Beyond teaching, his income streams include:

* **Buddhist counseling**: $40–$80/hour via Zoom (volume unknown, but a virtual temple-based practice likely generates limited client flow)  
* **Podcast**: "Down with the Dharma" has 115+ episodes on major platforms — unlikely to generate significant revenue at this scale  
* **Substack**: "The New Order" — may have free and paid tiers, but subscriber numbers appear small  
* **Books**: Published an anthology of Pali Canon discourses through his own Dhamma Vinaya Press, with several more books forthcoming — self-published scholarly Buddhist texts are not high-revenue items  
* **Nonprofit work**: Co-founded the Engaged Buddhist Alliance, teaching Buddhist philosophy in California state prisons — this is mission-driven, not income-generating

**Mukti Vihara Temple** is entirely virtual (online via WordPress), states "our teachings and practices are offered free of charge," and shows no prominent donation infrastructure. No 501(c)(3) registration was found. The Dhamma Vinaya Order that Freese founded confers religious ordination credentials at three levels but is a relatively new and small organization.

A reasonable estimate of Freese's **total annual income is $50,000–$90,000**, composed primarily of teaching compensation across three institutions supplemented by modest counseling fees. This is consistent with the financial reality faced by many scholar-practitioners in small Buddhist institutions.

## **Wallis has built a spiritual media empire**

Christopher Wallis (Hareesh) presents a radically different economic picture. With a **B.A. from Rochester, M.A. and Ph.D. in Sanskrit from UC Berkeley, and an M.Phil. from Oxford**, he left academia entirely and has not held a paid university position since his graduate instructorship at Berkeley ended around 2008\. He is fully independent — and apparently thriving.

His business rests on **six interlocking revenue streams**, each feeding the others:

**1\. Online subscription platform ($100K–$400K+/year estimated)**: Tantra Illuminated Online (learn.tantrailluminated.org) charges **$49/month** for standard access, with premium tiers at **$59 and $69/month** that include charitable donations to scholarship funds. Subscribers get weekly live satsangs, webinar series, 120+ recorded satsangs, 50+ downloadable meditations, 160+ asana classes, and structured courses on tantric philosophy, Sanskrit, and practice. The exact subscriber count is undisclosed, but community size indicators (15,000 Instagram followers, 13,600 YouTube subscribers, 370 Patreon members) suggest hundreds of active paying subscribers.

**2\. Retreats ($100K–$300K+/year estimated)**: Wallis runs multiple retreats annually across Europe and beyond. A **5-night Portugal retreat starts at €950 per person** (at Monte Velho, Algarve). A **10-day Kashmir Valley pilgrimage ranges from $2,800 to $5,300** depending on room type. UK retreats are held at a Grade II Elizabethan estate in Lincolnshire. Critically, **Wallis owns a retreat center in Figueiró dos Vinhos, Portugal** with 24 beds, a yoga shala, and camping for 5 — a significant capital asset that generates revenue from both his own retreats and external group rentals. The center is currently listed as available for sale.

**3\. The Immersion Program ($50K–$100K/year estimated)**: This application-only, 6-month intensive program costs **$2,250 total** ($500 deposit plus five payments of $350). It includes monthly personal sessions with Wallis and a mandatory 4-night retreat. At 20–25 students per cohort, this generates substantial revenue while deepening community commitment.

**4\. Book sales ($30K–$80K/year ongoing)**: *Tantra Illuminated* has sold **50,000+ copies** reportedly by word of mouth alone, is self-published through Wallis's own Mattamayura Press, and has been translated into Spanish, Portuguese, Romanian, German, and French. Self-publishing gives him **40–70% royalty margins** versus the 10–15% a traditional publisher would offer. At an estimated **$8–$12 net per copy**, cumulative revenue from this single title is roughly **$400,000–$600,000** since 2012\. *The Recognition Sutras* (also self-published) and *Near Enemies of the Truth* (published by Sounds True in 2023, with standard trade royalties of \~$2–3/copy) add additional revenue. Audiobook editions exist for all three titles. Seven more books are planned for 2026–2027.

**5\. Tantra 112 app ($10K–$50K/year, early stage)**: Launched in October 2025 through One Twelve Group PTE. LTD. (a Singapore-registered entity), the app offers 112 guided tantric meditation practices. Pricing: **$14.99/month, $89.99/year, or $249.99 lifetime**. It has earned a **5.0-star rating** on Apple (54 ratings) and **4.56 stars** on Google Play (31 ratings, \~1,400 total downloads). At roughly 460 downloads/month and growing, this stream has significant upside as the install base compounds.

**6\. Patreon and other streams ($15K–$30K/year)**: Wallis's Patreon has approximately **370 members starting at $10/month**, generating an estimated **$782–$1,288/month** ($9K–$15K/year). Patrons receive members-only PDF translations from Sanskrit. Additional revenue comes from freelance guest lecturing at yoga teacher trainings worldwide (ongoing since 2008), workshops in cities like London and Lisbon, and a podcast with monetization potential through Acast.

## **Wallis's total revenue picture reveals a scalable model**

Adding these streams together, **Wallis likely generates $300,000–$800,000+ annually** — possibly more if online subscriber counts are on the higher end. The business model is intelligently designed: books create awareness, the free podcast and social media build trust, the subscription platform captures recurring revenue, retreats and the Immersion program create premium experiences and deeper community bonds, and the app extends reach to a mobile-first audience. Each product feeds the funnel for the others.

Several structural advantages amplify this model. Self-publishing through Mattamayura Press captures the full margin on his bestseller rather than surrendering 85–90% to a traditional publisher. Owning a retreat center in Portugal converts what would be venue rental costs into a capital asset (though its listing for sale suggests this may be changing). The Singapore entity for the app likely reflects tax optimization for digital product revenue. And Wallis's **four graduate degrees from elite institutions** — particularly the Oxford and Berkeley credentials — provide scholarly legitimacy that distinguishes him in a crowded spiritual marketplace.

Wallis does **not appear to be independently wealthy** — his business infrastructure suggests someone who has methodically built revenue streams over 15+ years since leaving academia. The absence of any university salary means all income is self-generated, which creates both higher upside and higher risk than Freese's adjunct-salary floor.

## **Conclusion: the economics of spiritual authority**

These two cases illuminate how credentialing, institutional affiliation, and entrepreneurial ambition create vastly different outcomes for scholar-practitioners in American Buddhism and Hinduism-adjacent spirituality.

Freese operates within the traditional Buddhist model: modest institutional salaries, low-cost counseling, free teachings, and mission-driven nonprofit work. His income is limited but his credentials — ordination, three teaching positions, a PhD — provide institutional legitimacy and the legal standing to offer spiritual counseling under the clergy exemption without clinical licensure. The key legal insight is that **his careful use of "Buddhist counseling" rather than "therapy" is not merely branding — it is a legally necessary distinction** that keeps his practice within the scope of ministerial duties rather than regulated mental health practice.

Wallis operates as a fully independent spiritual entrepreneur, having built what amounts to a **one-person media and education company** generating multiples of what a tenured professor would earn. His model depends on audience scale (50,000+ book buyers, 15,000+ social followers) and premium pricing (€950 retreats, $49/month subscriptions) rather than institutional support. The trade-off is complete dependence on personal brand and market demand rather than the stability of academic employment.

Both models are legal. Both are viable. But they represent fundamentally different answers to the question of how scholarship and spiritual practice can sustain a life in 21st-century America.
