# Critic Audit — *The Axiom Beneath the Ground: The Alignment Void*, v5

*Audit date: 2026-04-21. Voice: a composite deconstructive/structural critic — Barthes, Derrida, Butler, Bakhtin, Wood, Shklovsky. Read against itself. The three prior audits ([T], economist, MSW) were each grounded in a tradition the book borrows from, and each read generously from inside the book's frame. This one does not.*

---

## 1. Executive summary

*The Axiom Beneath the Ground* presents itself as a memoir-in-philosophy about alignment across scales. What it is, structurally, is a first-person apologia wearing the mask of close third person, whose argumentative architecture runs on a handful of binary oppositions the book claims to have transcended and in fact depends on to keep its moral energy up. The book is more honest than most contemporary spiritual memoirs, and that honesty is also its principal defensive instrument: the narrator's frequent self-correction ("frame caught; frame released") is deployed in a way that pre-empts criticism by naming the failure-mode smaller than the reader would have. The book's most serious intellectual risk — that the tantric orthogonality thesis and the AI alignment problem are structurally the same problem — is real, is underdeveloped, and is the only claim in the book the other audits could not have caught because each was inside the frame the claim structures.

Three strongest passages: the bath-question from the five-year-old ("Mama, is there a place where I am still even when I'm moving?") which earns its weight without commentary; the porch exchange *"there always is"* (Ch 6), where the prose lets four words do the work four paragraphs would have ruined; and the admission in Ch 10d that *"Mostly no one has come"* to the platform — a sentence that cuts against the book's rhetorical interest and therefore tells the truth.

Three weakest: the Josh Schrei section (Ch 11), which is the book's longest sustained breach of its own stated non-evaluative norm and is, structurally, a settling of an account the book pretends it is merely observing; the "jongo" chapter, which elevates a dance figure to civilizational principle on the strength of childhood memory and then uses the figure to dismiss the "mandala of courses" without any demonstration that the jongo scales; and the Ch 11 Kant section, which imports a four-century-old argument about book piracy to underwrite a contemporary claim about Anthropic's acceptable-use policy, and in so doing flatters the author's own licensing decisions with philosophical cover they have not earned.

One intellectual risk the other audits miss: the book is quietly arguing that the four axes (intelligence, enlightenment, moral seriousness, relational attunement) are structurally independent — a strong empirical and philosophical claim that, if true, dissolves almost every convergence-theory the humanities has produced in two centuries. The book asserts the claim but does not defend it. This is the argument the book should be, and is not yet.

## 2. Speaking position analysis

The book is first-person memoir presented in close third. The narrator's "she" is a pronoun of anonymity, not of literary distance. A genuine third-person close narration produces ironic gaps between narrator and character; this one does not. "She" and the author are the same consciousness, and the book admits it in Ch 11 when the voice switches to "I" without any narrative event motivating the switch — simply because the confessional content of Ch 11 could not be carried by "she." The pronoun is a costume.

What the costume allows: universal-seeming statements of particular experience. When "she" notices that "the compressed interval did, over time, make the person who lived inside it more conceptual and less relational" (Ch 3), the claim reads as anthropological observation. Written in the first person — *I noticed in myself, in my particular apartment, with my particular history* — the same claim reads as autobiography. The third-person disguise smuggles the first-person claim up one rhetorical register.

What "she" is: a Brazilian woman, raised Catholic (pro forma), former Goldman Sachs analyst, MSW student, now a solo software builder who owns a Manhattan apartment with a husband and a five-year-old daughter, who flies to Esalen, retreats to a farmhouse in New England, drives an Acela, takes Vanessa Andreotti's course, pays for [T]'s retreat, goes to a live Josh Schrei event at the Alembic in New York, reads Tillich in the bathtub. Each particular is named; no particular is interrogated as a condition of the thinking.

The class, educational, and professional positions that produce the book's characteristic posture — "the equity-analyst filter," "the Goldman habit of asking who benefits," the ability to "pay for his things," the availability of five days in a barn, the laptop at eleven at night after the daughter is asleep — are presented as neutral biographical facts rather than as the conditions of possibility for the perspective. The book nowhere asks: what would this argument look like from a reader who could not take five days off, who did not have $1,200 for a retreat, who did not have a partner at home with the child, who did not have the disposable literacy to read Tillich for pleasure? This is not a fatal flaw. It is an unexamined one. The most honest sentence in this register is the admission in Ch 10d: *"Mostly no one has come."* That sentence contains the class analysis the rest of the book does not perform on itself.

## 3. Binary oppositions, deconstructed

The book runs on a dozen structural pairs. Three of the load-bearing ones:

**Orthogonality / convergence.** The book's central argumentative move is to collapse the folk theory of convergence (practice → goodness; intelligence → values; attunement → wisdom) and replace it with orthogonality. But the book's *own* rhetorical strategy is convergence-theory at a higher order: three traditions (tantric, economic, Brazilian-artistic) "arriving at the same shape from different vocabularies" (Ch 13) is presented as *weak corroborative evidence* for the shape. This is convergence-theory smuggled back in as meta-framework. The book argues capability and value are orthogonal in individuals and then argues its own thesis is strong because Abhinavagupta, Kant, Hegel, Heidegger, Plotinus, and Kastrup arrived at versions of it. Either multi-tradition convergence is evidence or it is not. If it is not — the orthogonality-thesis position — the book has just undermined its own corroborative frame. If it is — the Ch 11 [T]-parallels position — the folk theory the book set out to destroy is alive and well in the book's own method.

**Honest teacher / failed teacher.** [T] is the honest teacher (clear, revisable, willing-to-be-corrected, declines the AI tool for principled reasons). Schrei is the failed teacher (charisma without lineage, insufficient container, does not take up the question). Osho and a number of unnamed guru-figures are the catastrophic teachers. The binary does a great deal of structural work: it lets the book name specific living figures and sort them. But the sorting criterion is *the teacher's response to the narrator*. [T] corrects her on cutting/piercing and she feels "something being set right"; Schrei does not take up her Grimm-brothers question and he becomes, via three tarot draws, the Devil card. The epistemology of the sorting is that the honest teacher is the one who met the narrator where she wanted to be met. This is the most self-referential ethical criterion available, and the book does not notice it is using it.

**Scaffolding / building (the "cracks" register).** In the "Where the Maps Crack" interlude, every framework is confessed to be a construction, and the mature practitioner is the one who holds map and cracks at once. But the book's own frame — the Linehan three-filter test, the tantric orthogonality thesis, the Bayo/Vanessa composting register, the "jongo" shape — is never presented as having cracks in the way Bailey's Theosophy is (put on the high shelf) or Human Design is (cautiously). The filter is the instrument the book uses to judge other frameworks. The filter itself is never turned on the filter. Linehan's three criteria (useful, fits the data, compassionate) are meta-principles that, if applied to themselves, would require the book to ask: what data is this test fitted to? What does "compassionate" mean when the compassion is performed by the person being audited? What counts as useful, and to whom? The book exempts its own instrument from the scrutiny it subjects every other instrument to. This is the deconstructive reading: *the frame that names every other frame as a construction is itself a construction that refuses the name.*

## 4. Heteroglossia check

Bakhtin's test: does the text contain living voices in genuine conflict, or a single voice wearing masks?

The book contains: the narrator; the teacher (paraphrased, occasionally quoted); the aunt ("all is in perfect balance"); the husband (five or six sentences, all affectionate, all brief); the daughter (two sentences, both archetypal); the silver-haired retreatant; the man-initiated-in-everything; the Black woman at Esalen; the cabocla; Josh Schrei. There are also the archetypal italicized interludes (Kali, Śūnya, the Cracks, the Threshold).

Of these, only two voices are allowed to disagree with the narrator in a way that the narrator does not then absorb: the Black woman at Esalen ("she criticized me. Sharply") and the cabocla ("you should not do it"). Both criticisms are *eventually resolved* in the narrator's favor: the Black woman is reinterpreted as mistaking going-deep for mixing; the cabocla, on second visit, is reported as saying the problem was only "making money with it," and this is then framed as a refinement she gave the narrator. Neither voice gets to stay in disagreement.

The teacher's decline on the AI tool (Ch 10) is the one genuine unresolved voice. The book preserves it. It does not explain it away. It lets the refusal stand and shows the narrator's continued building as a live tension. This is the book's strongest Bakhtinian moment.

The italicized interlude-voices (Kali, Śūnya, the Cracks, the Threshold) are not voices in Bakhtin's sense; they are the narrator's voice in lyrical register. They agree with the narrator, because they *are* the narrator. They perform plurality. The test fails here. A text that includes Kali speaking is not polyvocal if Kali says only what the narrator would have said in prose — and she does.

The husband is the most striking absence. He is present as a phrase ("*Good.* That was the whole exchange. She loved him, again, for the economy"), as an eye ("You're back in the room more"), as a driver to school. He is not present as a contradicting consciousness. A marriage lived for long enough to produce a five-year-old and to be mentioned as central to the book's stakes is rendered in perhaps 500 words total. The other audits missed this because each stayed inside the book's register. The book cannot see the husband because the book is, structurally, a monologue interrupted only by teachers. The husband is neither teacher nor student. He has no role in the book's architecture. He is therefore, in Bakhtin's sense, silenced.

## 5. Genre-defense analysis

The hybrid form (memoir + philosophy + AI commentary) permits a specific defensive move: when a philosophical claim is exposed as under-argued, the text retreats to memoir ("this is what she experienced"); when the memoir feels exposed, the text retreats to philosophy ("this is what the tradition teaches"). Examples:

- Ch 6, the teacher's "nothing would change" answer to the destruction question. As philosophy, this is a strong non-dualist claim that would require unpacking. As reported memoir ("a report of how the tradition thinks"), it is shielded by the specificity of the container: *this was said on a path to one student; it is not portable*. The author then uses the claim's philosophical force while disclaiming its philosophical portability. The note at the end of Ch 6 — "a report of how the tradition thinks" — is the genre-defense made explicit.

- Ch 10e, the claim that most of human cognition was always pattern-completion rather than equation-solving. This is a strong claim in cognitive science. It is presented in the register of a former economist's private observation, which softens the warrant required; a reader who demanded the neuroscientific backing would feel pedantic. The register protects the claim.

- Ch 11, the Dario Amodei endorsement ("I am on Dario's team"). As philosophy, this is a political endorsement that would require argument; as memoir ("a first-rate mind holding two opposed ideas"), it is received wisdom passed through the narrator's temperament. The genre softens the endorsement into intimacy.

- Ch 11, Josh Schrei. When evaluative ("the craft was there... what was less clearly there was the container"), the passage is memoir; when challenged as evaluation, the book can retreat to "I write that as an observation, not a verdict." But the observation does verdict-work throughout the rest of the Schrei section. The "I do not know. The reader will decide" move at the end of the section is precisely the genre-defense: offer the reader the verdict, then refuse responsibility for the verdict on the grounds that memoir is not argument.

The author's CLAUDE.md footnote rule ("academic infrastructure does NOT belong in body prose") is a stylistic commitment that reinforces the genre-defense: by evicting the scholarly apparatus, the prose gains the authority of experience without the accountability of argument. The footnotes are the place the book could be pressed. The body cannot be pressed because the body is memoir.

## 6. What the text represses

What this book cannot look at:

**Money.** The narrator was at Goldman Sachs. The narrator has, since, been "senior analyst-turned-product-person" in a mid-sized company, has paid engineers to build a platform, has sustained a Manhattan apartment and two trips to Esalen and a five-day retreat and the time to write. The word "money" appears, but the question of what the narrator is doing financially *now* is never answered. The book cannot answer it without shifting register into something it does not want to be (career disclosure; class confession). Instead the book gestures: "recursive.eco, small membership layer for families," "the five thousand dollars I paid for the first version" — but never the gross total, never the current runway, never whether this is possible because of accumulated capital from finance years. The composting of modernity is financed by modernity, and the financing is the repressed content.

**Whiteness and Brazilian privilege.** The book tells us the narrator is Brazilian, grew up Catholic, has a Brazilian aunt who is espírita, visited an Umbanda terreiro with her daughter. The book does not tell us the narrator's class position in Brazil. Goldman Sachs equity analysts from Brazil are a small and specific cohort. The Black woman's criticism at Esalen about "mixing" lands on the page and is resolved in 200 words; a sustained reckoning with the narrator's position as a Brazilian of the extractive class engaging Afro-Brazilian traditions would be a different book. The book takes the criticism, tastes it, and routes around it.

**The daughter's interiority.** The daughter appears as: the child at the sandbox, the child asking the bath question, the child screaming at the truck, the child who will one day read this. She is instrumentally deployed. She never has an opinion the book disagrees with. She is the axiom's best witness — and therefore, structurally, she cannot be a person with her own trajectory. The book's most frequent use of her is to ratify the narrator's discoveries (bath question).

**The husband's inner life.** See heteroglossia. The marriage as living difficulty — rather than supportive chorus — is not in the book.

**Teachers she did *not* choose well.** The Theosophy / Arcane School years receive a sentence of composting; they do not receive a narrative. Years spent inside an "esoteric correspondence course" with "racialized cosmologies" is dismissed in a paragraph. The process of being taken in, staying, leaving — the data the book would most benefit from for its guru-idealization chapter — is not given. The book names the failure mode and withholds the specific instance.

**The platform's failure.** Ch 10d says mostly no one has come. That is real. But the book does not look at why with the same seriousness it looks at the composition of the sandbox. A builder who admits the platform is not working and then does not work the failure is performing admission, not inhabiting it.

**Suicide, except as tradition-teaching.** Ch 4a handles contemplation of death with unusual care. Ch 6 handles the teacher's answer ("not a problem in itself… the caution is about method") with surrounding medical-hotline notes. But the narrator's own twenty-year relationship to the question is kept at the generic register ("the question had been there for twenty years; it had been a lodger"). The specificity that makes memoir work is absent in the one place the book most insists on its own honesty.

## 7. Rhetorical-strategy diagnosis

The book performs three rhetorical moves in rotation:

**Confession → authority.** The narrator admits a small failure (misheard the piercing practice; filtered the Harris link defensively; interrupted the friend at lunch). The admission buys credibility. The credibility is then spent on a larger claim adjacent to the admission. The admission in Ch 1 about misreading verse 93 — *she had misheard the practice through the frame of the clinical work* — earns the teacher's authority, which is then deployed through the next three hundred pages. The confession is structurally instrumental.

**Self-correction → immunity.** "The superlative is mine. He would redirect the credit upstream." (Ch 10a) This is an excellent sentence. It names the guru-flattery failure mode. It also pre-empts criticism — a reader who had been about to write "this is guru-flattery" finds the objection already inside the text, disarmed. The move occurs often enough to constitute a pattern: name the objection before the reader can, register the failure in a minor key, keep going. The effect is that the book becomes *argumentatively un-attackable on the axes it names*. It is still attackable on the axes it does not name, which is the work of this audit.

**Specific detail → universal claim.** The mug with the thick base (Ch 9), the Sunday afternoon light (Ch 3), the small yellow shovel (Ch 1) — each specific detail is made to bear a civilizational weight. This is good prose at its best. It is also, at its worst, the rhetorical strategy by which the first person's contingencies become the species' condition. The Sunday afternoon light with the napping child and the blankness-whose-edge-cannot-be-located is presented as Tillich's "third anxiety." The move is stylistically earned and philosophically contraband. The third anxiety has a specific historical description in Tillich; the blank Sunday afternoon of a well-off Manhattan parent is one instance, not a license to inhabit the description generally.

The genuine-warmth/philosophical-claim trade is the book's most consistent engine. The teacher's "there always is" works as philosophy because it is embedded in the remembered warmth of the five-day container (the hug, the path, the mug of tea). Extracted from the warmth, the claim is a commonplace of non-dualist thought. The warmth does philosophical work the argument would not have.

## 8. Complicities

The book critiques modernity and performs modernity (Acela, Substack, Amazon KDP, GitHub commits-as-receipts, Cloudflare R2, YouTube embed). The book critiques convergence-theory and argues convergently (Ch 11's six-thinker parallel: *the fact that they agree is weak corroborative evidence*). The book critiques spiritual-exit and exits the Josh Schrei community ("I decided to leave his group soon after"). The book critiques extractive authorship and names the teacher by pseudonym ("[T]") while quoting him throughout. The book critiques the for-profit engagement-internet while selling on Amazon KDP.

None of these is necessarily a contradiction. Each of them is a position held *inside* a structure the book has partially named. The issue is that the book names some of the contradictions and not others. The ones it names become evidence of honesty; the ones it does not become invisible. The Amazon KDP plan is in the author's project CLAUDE.md but not in the book. The GitHub commits are invoked as receipts ("the commit is public") without considering that GitHub is owned by Microsoft, whose capital the book's ecosystem partly rests on. The "book is pure talk, you can check the commits" line is the kind of modern authentication-move — the blockchain-ish gesture, verifiability-as-virtue — that the book's own frame would critique if someone else were doing it.

The most specific complicity: the retrieval tool built over the teacher's corpus. The book's honest account of this is that the teacher declined, the author revised but did not withdraw, and the asking continues. The book does not name what the tool is made of: the teacher's words, scraped (or licensed) from public lectures, indexed by a vector database, retrieved by a large-language-model query, wrapped in a tarot-card UI, hosted on Cloudflare. Each of those layers is a specific technical and licensing choice. The book spiritualizes the choice ("grammars open; code private") without costing it. A reader looking for what the Kantian personal right the book invokes *concretely requires* — did the author obtain written consent from [T] to train on his corpus? — will not find the answer. The book has chosen the philosophical register for a question that is, at root, legal and relational.

## 9. Close-reading taste check (Wood register)

**Working sentences** (prose doing the work, earning its weight):

1. "Nothing would change, he said. Nothing fundamental. Consciousness does not suffer from the rearrangement of its forms." (Ch 6) — The pause after "Nothing" is the sentence. The repetition as qualification is the tradition's register rendered without commentary.

2. "There always is." (Ch 6, four words held alone) — The book's single best sentence. It does what the surrounding 60,000 words try to say and do not.

3. "Mama, is there a place where I am still even when I'm moving?" (Ch 9) — The five-year-old's phrasing earns its weight precisely because the narrator does not gloss it. The book becomes momentarily porous to a voice not its own.

4. "Mostly no one has come." (Ch 10d) — Short declarative that cuts against the book's rhetorical interest. The admission is philosophical work.

5. "The body knew first. The mind would catch up in its own time." (Ch 2) — Two sentences that earn their parallelism because the preceding lentil-soup scene has shown the thing, not only said it.

6. "She had underlined it in the bathtub." (Ch 1, and recurrently) — A small physical fact that grounds an otherwise abstract reading-year. The bathtub becomes an unshowy epistemological site.

7. "The arrangement is not a solution. The arrangement is the practice." (Ch 1) — The book's thesis in compressed form, done in two beats instead of fifteen.

**Performing sentences** (prose that sounds like work but is not):

1. "She was one of the places the arrangement is happening." (Ch 0) — A sentence that mimics Rilke at low cost; the "arrangement" noun does no specific work and reappears throughout as a filler for "the whole."

2. "The cracks are not where the story is ending. The cracks are where a different story is trying to grow." (Interlude: The Cracks) — This is Akomolafe in summary form, set in italics to borrow authority. The italicization signals "archetypal voice" but the content is summary paraphrase.

3. "Svātantrya is courage in the light. Courage is svātantrya owning itself in the dark." (Ch 6) — Chiasmus as wisdom. Structurally impressive, semantically thin. What does svātantrya "own" in the dark versus the light? The sentence moves the reader past the question through the pleasure of the symmetry.

4. "The desk is not a terreiro. The book is not a ritual. I am writing anyway." (Ch 11) — The posture of the last clause ("anyway") is self-forgiving in the register of memoir while the whole sentence is structured as philosophical concession. Three short clauses doing the work of a shrug.

5. "The sending is the practice. The reply, if it comes, is another instance." (Ch 11) — The tarot-reading sentence. It sounds like acceptance; it performs acceptance; it does not actually relinquish the sending, because the sending is then reported and narrated as the book itself.

6. "Freedom is svātantrya in the light. Courage is what I do when I notice I have been drifting out of the field." (Ch 11) — The Sanskrit loanword does stabilizing work the English word would not. Remove svātantrya and substitute "freedom" throughout and the sentence collapses into platitude. The loanword is the engine.

7. "The book is over. The practice continues. Goodnight." (Ch 13) — The "Goodnight" is the kind of gesture a book makes when it has decided it is literature. It is the move of a writer who wants the ending to feel earned. Whether the preceding chapters earn it is the question the "Goodnight" forecloses.

## 10. Shklovskian defamiliarization check

Where defamiliarization works: the sandbox scene (Ch 1) does let the reader see the ordinary playground newly — the "right size" line and the negotiation of the shovel become a structural observation about adult relational life. The lentil-soup moment works similarly: the bowl is not special, and the not-specialness is the teaching. The dishes in Ch 9 (the mug thicker at the base than at the lip) achieve what Shklovsky asked prose to do: arrest habit.

Where it fails: the unfamiliar is re-familiarized into a "wisdom" register that absorbs the strangeness. The Kali practice in Ch 4 arrives as something that could have remained strange — blue-black, tongue out, standing on a consort — and is quickly re-domesticated: "Kali was not, in the tradition, death dressed up as something else. Kali was the particular face of the one consciousness that could not be approached through the image of the benevolent mother." The gloss catches the strangeness before the body can hold it. The book reaches reflexively for explanation at exactly the moments Shklovsky would want it to withhold.

The ayahuasca voice in the Prelude is the cleanest case. "Neither did I" is an astonishing sentence that could have been left to echo. Instead, the narrator immediately translates: *she did not understand at first. She thought it meant something like a consolation... The voice, patient, meant something else...* The voice is caught and explained within the same paragraph. A Shklovskian read would ask for the sentence to stand alone, unglossed, until the reader had carried it longer. The book does not trust the reader with the strangeness.

## 11. Structural verdict

The book claims to be about alignment across scales. Its structure tells a different story.

Chapter-order count:
- Chapters 1–9 (ten chapters): inward arc, barn, retreat, returning to daily life. Roughly 60% of the text.
- Chapters 10, 10a, 10b, 10c, 10d, 10e, 10f: the argumentative heart, the AI/tantric parallel. Roughly 20%.
- Chapter 11 (Writing Desk): longest single chapter. The "I" returns. The Dario endorsement, the Kant reading, the Josh arc, the tarot reading, the [T] parallels. Roughly 15%.
- Chapters 12, 13, Appendix: composting, right size, Zusya. Roughly 5%.

The architecture the book claims — interior work → constructive ethical work → civilizational scale — is not the architecture the book has. The architecture the book has is: interior work → the narrator's specific teachers and their rankings → the narrator's platform and its specific licensing decisions → a vow in the narrator's voice. The book is, structurally, a self-positioning text: *here is where I stand, relative to these teachers, with these commitments, under these licenses.* The scale-claim is the ceiling; the floor is memoir.

The pronoun pattern confirms this. "She" carries chapters 0–10 and 12–13. "I" carries 11 and most of 10d. The "I" appears precisely when the book needs to do work the "she" cannot do: take a political position on AI, sort Josh from [T], explain the platform's licensing. The architecture of the pronouns reveals what the book is structurally unable to say in the third person — which is most of its ethical content.

**What the book is actually about**, on the structural reading: the narrator's search for a teacher she can trust, her finding one ([T]), and her defense of both the teacher and her own builder-posture against a cohort of adjacent teachers (Schrei, the Arcane School, Bailey, Human Design) and against a cohort of adjacent technical positions (OpenAI, Patel's regulation argument, the doomers). The "alignment across scales" frame is the vocabulary in which this defense is conducted. Alignment is the book's rhetorical umbrella; self-positioning is its operation.

This is not a failure. It is an unacknowledged shape. A book that named itself honestly — *how one specific former analyst found one specific tradition and one specific teacher in a field of plausible alternatives, and what that finding asks of her ethically* — would be a better book than the one that claims to be the philosophy of alignment. The specific is stronger than the universal here, and the book mostly fails to trust that.

## 12. The one revision

If the book could make one move, it should be this: *remove or drastically reduce the "I am on Dario's team" section of Ch 11 and the Josh Schrei section, and fold what remains into a single, shorter meditation on the position-taking itself*.

The reason is not that Dario and Josh are wrong subjects. The reason is that the book's strongest claim — that capability and value are orthogonal and that no single axis supplies the integration — is undermined, at the structural level, by the spectacle of the narrator sorting living figures onto axes. When she declares for Dario and against Josh, she is performing exactly the convergence-theory move the book's orthogonality thesis has spent ten chapters demolishing: *the person I find trustworthy on one axis is trustworthy on the others; the person I found lacking on one axis must be lacking on the others (hence the Devil card)*. The book has, in those two sections, undone its own central argument.

A revision that pulled back from the positioning-as-ethics register and let the orthogonality thesis live at civilizational scale — without the narrator claiming which specific living person stands where — would produce the argument the book gestures at but does not finish.

A harder, related revision: let the husband speak. Let the daughter have one opinion the narrator does not fold into the axiom. Let the Black woman at Esalen not be resolved. Let the cabocla's "you should not do it" remain standing. A book that is honest about the orthogonality thesis would have to be a book in which the narrator's own frame does not, by the final page, absorb every voice it encountered. The failure of absorption would be the proof of the thesis.
