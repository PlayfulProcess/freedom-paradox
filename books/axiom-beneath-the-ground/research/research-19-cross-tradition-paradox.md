# **The paradox no tradition escapes**

**Every major nondual tradition faces the same impossible question: if reality is already complete, why practice?** This structural tension — between ontological wholeness and the apparent necessity of effort — is not a bug in these systems but arguably the most revealing feature of how finite consciousness encounters its own infinite ground. Across Zen, Dzogchen, Advaita Vedānta, and Meister Eckhart's mysticism, each tradition develops a distinctive resolution that reflects its deepest metaphysical commitments. When compared against Kashmir Shaivism's framework of divine play, a striking typology emerges: these five traditions do not merely offer different answers to the same question — they occupy fundamentally different relationships to the paradox itself.

The five resolutions can be mapped along a spectrum: Dōgen's Zen **collapses** the distinction between practice and realization; Advaita **stratifies** it across two levels of truth; Dzogchen **indexes** through it with transparent methods that point beyond themselves; Eckhart **performs** a self-negating practice that consumes itself; and Kashmir Shaivism **transfigures** the paradox from problem into aesthetic delight. None fully eliminates the tension, because the very act of teaching any resolution reconstitutes it — a performative contradiction that may itself be the deepest teaching.

---

## **Dōgen's radical collapse of means and end**

Zen Buddhism's encounter with the practice paradox begins with a biographical fact that shaped an entire tradition. At thirteen, the Japanese monk Dōgen was haunted by a question he could not resolve on Mount Hiei: *"If all beings are in their true natures enlightened, why do buddhas work so hard to attain enlightenment?"* His inability to find an answer within Tendai Buddhism drove him to China, where under Master Rujing he experienced what he called **shinjin datsuraku** — "dropping away of body and mind."

Dōgen's resolution, articulated in his foundational 1231 text *Bendōwa*, is encapsulated in the term **shushō ittō** (修証一等) — the "oneness and equality of practice and realization." His key move is breathtaking in its simplicity: practice does not *cause* enlightenment. Practice *is* enlightenment. "To think that practice and verification are not one is the view of infidels," he writes. "Because it is practice based on verification, the beginner's pursuit of the way is the whole substance of original verification." The temporal gap between effort and attainment is not bridged but **annihilated**. Shikantaza — "just sitting" — embodies this principle. It involves no object of concentration, no goal, no gaining idea. As Koun Franz explains, "We don't train to become a buddha; we train as buddha."

This contrasts instructively with Rinzai Zen's kōan practice, which approaches the paradox through what Victor Sogen Hori calls "a practice that aims at its own transcendence." The kōan — an unsolvable paradox like "the sound of one hand" — deploys conceptual effort specifically to exhaust the conceptual mind. The cleverness, as Hori notes, lies in the fact that "rather than attacking reason and logic from outside, the kōan uses reason to drive itself into a self-contradiction and cause its own destruction." Where Dōgen collapses the distinction between practice and realization, Rinzai weaponizes practice against itself.

The Platform Sutra's famous verse contest between Shenxiu and Huineng dramatizes the sudden-gradual debate that underlies both approaches. Shenxiu's gradual verse — "Time and again polish the mirror diligently" — treats practice as ongoing purification. Huineng's sudden response — "Originally there is not a single thing; where could any dust be attracted?" — denies there was ever anything to polish. But modern scholarship, particularly John McRae's work, reveals that the Platform Sutra itself functions as the synthesis: Huineng's verse does not stand alone but "forms a pair with Shenxiu's verse," and Huineng himself taught that "in the Dharma there is no sudden or gradual."

**When compared to Kashmir Shaivism**, Dōgen's resolution shares a crucial structural feature: both collapse the means-end relationship. Abhinavagupta's pratyabhijñā is "re-cognition" — recognizing what was always already known — just as Dōgen's dōkan ("circle of the way") replaces the linear path with a circle where each point is simultaneously origin and destination. But the ontological gulf is vast. In Dōgen, there is **no enduring agent** who practices — the practice is "dropped body and mind." In Kashmir Shaivism, the agent is ultimately Śiva himself, an enduring conscious absolute who delights in the drama of forgetting and remembering. Dōgen's resolution is austere; KS's is celebratory. Zen lacks the cosmological playfulness of līlā — practice simply IS what it is (*genjō kōan*), without a divine agent savoring the game.

## **Dzogchen's disclosure of what was never hidden**

Dzogchen — the "Great Perfection" — contains perhaps the sharpest formulation of the paradox. Its foundational claim is that rigpa (pure awareness) is **already self-perfected, primordially pure (ka dag), and spontaneously present (lhun grub)**. As Namkhai Norbu states: "To become realized simply means to discover and manifest that which from the very beginning has been our own true condition." Yet the tradition prescribes elaborate preliminary practices (ngöndro), pointing-out instructions (trekchö), dark retreats, and advanced luminosity practices (tögal). How can a tradition that declares perfection already accomplished prescribe so much work?

The resolution operates through what scholars call a **disclosive model** rather than a constructive one. Practice does not fabricate awareness; it removes what prevents awareness from recognizing its own face (*rang ngo shes pa*). Longchenpa, the supreme systematizer of Dzogchen philosophy, taught that the ground (gzhi) possesses three inseparable qualities — primordial purity as its essence, spontaneous presence as its nature, and compassionate energy as its dynamic expression. This third quality explains how the ground manifests as both saṃsāra and nirvāṇa: not through a defect, but through what Christopher Hatchell describes as the "gateways of spontaneous presence," where self-recognition leads to purity and non-recognition leads to the straying (*'khrul pa*) that constitutes ordinary confused experience.

The key technical distinction is between **rigpa** (pure awareness) and **sem** (ordinary mind). David Higgins's landmark 2013 monograph, *The Philosophical Foundations of Classical rDzogs chen in Tibet*, demonstrates that this distinction "allowed rDzogs chen adepts to precisely describe what occurs when a human being becomes a buddha, and to articulate a disclosive model of goal-realization commensurate with their findings." Rigpa is always present as the subtlest level of mental activity, but in ordinary beings it manifests as "unripened rigpa" — awareness that does not yet recognize its own nature. Sam van Schaik's *Approaching the Great Perfection* shows that Jigme Lingpa deliberately uses "the rhetoric of both sides" — simultaneous and gradual — as a hermeneutical strategy, not a contradiction.

The comparison between Dzogchen's **rang grol** (self-liberation) and Kashmir Shaivism's **pratyabhijñā** (recognition) reveals the deepest structural parallel and the sharpest ontological divergence in this entire study. Both traditions teach that liberation is disclosure rather than construction — recognizing what was always present rather than manufacturing something new. Both use the language of spontaneous recognition. Both employ the mirror as a primary metaphor. Both arose in roughly the same period (8th-11th centuries CE) in overlapping geographical regions — as van Schaik notes, "Indian practitioners of Dzogchen almost certainly interacted with, and may have been influenced by, Hindu proponents of Kashmiri Shaivism."

Yet the foundations diverge radically. **Dzogchen's ground is empty and individual** — each sentient being has their own basis, which, as Malcolm Smith insists, "is not some transpersonal entity" but "just one's unfabricated mind, nothing more, nothing less." Kashmir Shaivism posits **one universal consciousness, Śiva**, which is real, eternal, and divine. John Myrdhin Reynolds identifies specific terminological parallels — Dzogchen's *rig-pa'i rtsal* ("potency of awareness") could "almost be translated as Vidyāśakti," a technical Śaiva term — but these structural resonances sit atop incompatible metaphysical commitments regarding selfhood, universality, and the ultimate nature of the ground.

## **Advaita Vedānta and the medicine that cures itself**

Śaṅkara's resolution of the practice paradox is perhaps the most architecturally elegant. If Brahman alone is real and the individual self (jīva) is already Brahman, practice serves a single function: **removing the ignorance (avidyā) that superimposes false appearances onto the ever-present reality**. His *Adhyāsa Bhāṣya* defines superimposition as "the apparent presentation, to consciousness, by way of memory, of something previously observed in some other thing" — a technical account of how the self mistakes itself for the body-mind complex, just as one mistakes a rope for a snake in dim light.

The rope-snake analogy encodes the entire logic of Advaita's resolution. The rope (Brahman) is always there; the snake (the phenomenal world) has no independent existence; the dim light represents partial ignorance; and seeing clearly is not an action that produces a new result but a recognition that reveals what was always the case. Practice is epistemological, not ontological. You do not *become* Brahman — you *see through* the error that made you think you were something else.

Śaṅkara's two-truths doctrine provides the formal framework. At the **pāramārthika** (absolute) level, there is no jīva, no bondage, no liberation, no practice, and no practitioner. Brahman alone is. At the **vyāvahārika** (empirical) level, the jīva experiences itself as bound, and practice removes the obscuring ignorance. These two levels are not contradictory but complementary. As T.P. Ramachandran argued in his doctoral work, "What is non-real is not necessarily useless and what is useful need not be real." The **adhyāropa-apavāda** method — superimposition followed by sublation — treats the entire teaching apparatus as a provisional framework that, having served its purpose, is discarded. The medicine cures the disease and is then no longer needed.

Kashmir Shaivism's critique of this resolution strikes at its deepest assumption. If Brahman is entirely inactive (*niḥspanda*) and the world is māyā, then Advaita inadvertently creates a dualism between the real (Brahman) and the unreal (māyā) — the very thing it claims to overcome. As Swami Lakshmanjoo argued, "If Lord Śiva is real, then how could an unreal substance come out from something that is real?" Kashmir Shaivism's **parādvaita** ("supreme non-dualism") sees both unity and diversity as real manifestations of the Absolute. Where Advaita says practice removes ignorance so you can see *through* the world, KS says practice is recognition so you can see *into* the world as divine expression.

The **neo-Advaita movement** provides a cautionary case study of what happens when the absolute-level teaching is proclaimed without the empirical-level framework needed to make it transformative. Teachers like Tony Parsons assert "there is nothing you can do or need to do," speaking the *conclusion* of Vedānta to seekers who lack the preparatory qualifications (sādhana-catuṣṭaya) that Śaṅkara considered essential. Dennis Waite's critique is precise: "The statements of neo-Advaitin teachers cannot be made from the standpoint of paramārtha by definition. Language itself is part of vyavahāra." Classical Advaita never taught that "there is nothing to do" — it taught that practice operates within the empirical domain to dissolve ignorance, after which the absolute truth stands revealed as always having been the case.

## **Eckhart's prayer to be rid of God**

Meister Eckhart's encounter with the practice paradox is the most dramatically expressed of any tradition considered here. His resolution takes the form of a **self-dissolving dialectic** that reaches its apex in the most shocking sentence in Western mysticism: **"I pray God to rid me of God"** (Sermon 52). This is not atheism. It is the completion of the God-relationship — the recognition that the personal God (Gott) of Christian worship, however real at that level, is still an object of the mind's projection, and must be released to encounter the Godhead (Gottheit) — the "desert" and "stillness" beyond all names, attributes, and even the Trinity.

Eckhart's distinction between Gott and Gottheit is structurally parallel to Kashmir Shaivism's ultimate category. The Gottheit is not a being — not even the highest being — but the **groundless ground** from which all beings, including the personal God, arise. As a 2024 De Gruyter analysis explicates, the Gottheit "coincides with 'pure nothing.' Only as pure nothingness can Gottheit guarantee the openness necessary for Gott to emerge." Bernard McGinn characterizes Eckhart's thought as a "metaphysics of flow" — the dynamic reciprocity of flowing forth (*bullitio/ebullitio*) from the hidden divine ground and the "breakthrough" (*Durchbruch*) of return into essential identity with that source.

**Gelassenheit** — releasement, letting-go — is Eckhart's central practice-concept, and it contains the paradox in concentrated form. It is simultaneously a practice ("Make a start with yourself, and abandon yourself") and the negation of all practice, since any deliberate letting-go is still an act of will. Scholars identify three stages in the concept: deferred-willing (subordinating one's will to God's), covert-willing (concealed self-assertion), and true non-willing — not willing-not-to-will, but the complete cessation of self-will. Denys Turner's landmark *The Darkness of God* argues that in Eckhart, **detachment IS the practice of apophatic anthropology** — it is not a method leading to some separate mystical experience but the lived enactment of the apophatic principle itself.

The comparison with Kashmir Shaivism reveals a striking methodological contrast that arrives at convergent conclusions. Eckhart's approach is radically **apophatic** — stripping away all attributes, names, and images until "pure nothing" remains. KS is predominantly **kataphatic** — affirming that Śiva IS consciousness, IS everything, that the world is the real dynamic expression of divine energy (Śakti). Yet both arrive at nondual identity: Eckhart's "God's ground and my ground is the same ground" parallels KS's teaching that the individual is already Śiva. KS's concept of **Anuttara** ("the Unsurpassable") has been noted as a possible parallel to Eckhart's Gottheit — both posit an absolute that transcends even their own theological God-concepts. No major scholarly monograph directly comparing Eckhart and Kashmir Shaivism was located in this research — the field remains largely unexplored, though Rudolf Otto's foundational *Mysticism East and West* (1926) established the Eckhart-Śaṅkara comparison that dominates the scholarly landscape.

Eckhart's 1329 condemnation by Pope John XXII reveals the institutional stakes of the practice paradox. If the soul possesses an uncreated spark co-eternal with the Godhead, and if direct union bypasses the personal God, then sacraments become optional, priestly mediation unnecessary, and Church hierarchy loses its spiritual justification. The paradox of prescribing practice within a framework of inherent completeness is not merely philosophical — it threatens the institutional structures that depend on the necessity of mediated practice.

## **Five resolutions, one structural invariant**

The meta-question — whether these traditions resolve, hold, or dissolve the paradox — yields a typology that maps each tradition's deepest commitments:

**Dōgen's Identity Model** collapses the distinction between practice and realization entirely. Practice IS enlightenment, not a means to it. This comes closest to genuine dissolution, but the dissolution requires accepting a radically non-teleological understanding of practice that itself demands profound transformation — suggesting the paradox reappears for anyone who hasn't undergone that shift.

**Advaita's Subtraction Model** stratifies the paradox across two levels of truth. At the absolute level, there is nothing to do; at the empirical level, ignorance must be removed. The paradox is dissolved absolutely but maintained empirically — though Kashmir Shaivism's critique points out that the relationship between these two levels is itself paradoxical.

**Kashmir Shaivism's Dramatic Model** transfigures the paradox from problem into aesthetic feature. By making concealment (*tirodhāna*) one of Śiva's five divine acts alongside creation, sustenance, dissolution, and grace (*anugraha*), the tradition frames practice as Śiva seeking Śiva — the divine playing hide-and-seek with itself. The paradox is neither resolved nor held in tension but **celebrated** as the structure of cosmic delight (*camatkāra*). The upāya system — from āṇavopāya (bodily practices) through śāktopāya (mental methods) and śāmbhavopāya (pure will) to anupāya (no-method) — is itself a graduated dissolution of the practitioner-practice-goal structure.

**Eckhart's Self-Negating Model** performs the paradox until it exhausts itself. The practice of detachment ultimately detaches from itself; one must let go even of letting go. The four-stage dialectic — affirmation of practice, negation of practice as self-will, negation of the negation, return to ordinary life where "all works can be holy" — mirrors the Neoplatonic *exitus-reditus* pattern while arriving at something remarkably close to the nondual recognition that all traditions describe.

**Dzogchen's Indexical Model** treats practice as transparent — a finger pointing at the moon that exists precisely to make itself unnecessary. Distinguished from Advaita's subtraction in that it does not primarily remove something (avidyā) but discloses what is already nakedly present. The pointing-out instruction occupies an ambiguous position that is neither conventional practice nor non-practice — it is, as one scholar notes, the moment where "practice becomes non-practice."

David Loy's *Nonduality: A Study in Comparative Philosophy* offers the deepest theoretical framework for understanding why the paradox arises at all. Loy argues that **the paradox is generated by dualistic framing**: when practice is conceived as an agent doing something to attain a goal, the contradiction is irresolvable. When the agent-action-goal triad collapses — which is precisely what each tradition's resolution attempts — the paradox dissolves. The structural isomorphism across traditions is notable regardless of one's position in the Katz-Forman debate about whether mystical experiences are tradition-specific: the *logical structure* of the paradox appears cross-traditionally invariant even if the specific doctrinal content differs.

## **Conclusion**

The practice paradox is not merely an intellectual puzzle but a **generative tension** built into the structure of how finite consciousness encounters its own infinite ground. No tradition fully eliminates it, because the very act of teaching any resolution reinstates it — saying "there is nothing to teach" is itself a teaching, writing about the failure of words uses words. This performative dimension may itself be the deepest pointer: the inability of the conceptual mind to resolve "already complete yet must practice" is precisely the crack through which nondual awareness enters.

What distinguishes these traditions is not whether they escape the paradox but **how they inhabit it**. Dōgen sits inside it with radical equanimity. Śaṅkara maps it with architectural precision. Longchenpa discloses its transparency. Eckhart prays it into dissolution. And Abhinavagupta dances within it, recognizing the paradox itself as Śiva's delight in the drama of self-concealment and self-recognition — the cosmic play that makes recognition possible in the first place. The traditions do genuinely differ in their resolutions, but they converge on a single structural insight: **the paradox is not a problem to be solved but the texture of the path itself**.
