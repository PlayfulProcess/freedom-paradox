## Section 1 — The 2020 position, compressed (from the local 80K Hours episode)

Tristan Harris's long-form case in the 80K Hours interview — the cleanest primary-source statement of his pre-2023 position — has four load-bearing parts.

**1a. The problem is structural, not individual.** Harris frames social media harm as a *systems-change* problem, not a user-habit problem. The frame he uses, citing Donella Meadows's *Twelve Leverage Points*, is that individual-scale interventions (turn off notifications; delete Instagram) cannot solve a problem generated by the platform's core incentive. The incentive is attention-harvesting at scale. The harms are downstream of the incentive (paraphrased from the Center for Humane Technology section of the interview).

**1b. The "climate change of culture" framing.** Harris compares social-media harm to climate change in one specific respect: it is an externality of a growth-based business model. Every dollar of Fed-printed money is an IOU for extraction; every minute of engagement harvested is an IOU for attention-damage. The framing positions the harm at the systemic/cultural level rather than the individual/clinical level. This is why he resists the "is social media addictive, yes or no?" framing — the framing is pitched at the wrong layer.

**1c. Three categories of harm.** Harris's own taxonomy (from the interview's Section 6): (i) *individual* — distraction, overuse, sleep damage; (ii) *social* — polarization, in-group rage, harassment; (iii) *civilizational* — erosion of shared reality, meaning-making captured by engagement algorithms. The book's distinction — individual filter-work vs. collective karma — maps onto Harris's (i) / (ii–iii) split.

**1d. The persuasion apocalypse and negativity bias.** Harris's own verbatim frame (paraphrased at 11 words — "99 positive comments, one negative; attention goes to the one") describes the mechanism by which the attention economy grants *hate a home field advantage*. Harris cites his own background in magic (asymmetry of knowledge) and his study of cults as the epistemological spine for how technology manipulates aspects of cognition users are not aware of.

**Usable quote (under 15 words, attributed, from local transcript):**
- "Hate has a home field advantage." (6 words) — Tristan Harris on attention-economy dynamics in the 80K Hours interview.

**Key moment for the book's Ch03 §VII and the Axiom argument:** the *karma accelerator* thread in v5 Ch03 §VII is not original to the book. Harris had already given the book the structural version of this claim by 2020. The book's contribution is translating it into the Tantric register (*karma* as self-reinforcing residue of action) and naming the *collective karma* layer that individuates the scroll.

## Section 2 — The 2023 pivot to AI

Harris and the Center for Humane Technology broadened their mission from social media to AI starting around 2023 (Wikipedia, "Tristan Harris"). The public vehicle was the March 2023 talk co-presented with Aza Raskin, commonly referred to as *The AI Dilemma*. The Center for Humane Technology's *Your Undivided Attention* podcast absorbed the AI focus through 2023–2026.

**Key claim in the pivot:** the AI wave is repeating social media's first-contact pattern — rapid deployment, competitive pressure, insufficient safety investment, irreversible entanglement with society before governance structures exist. From the CHT August 2024 piece: *You only have one period before technology gets entangled—that's right now* (Tristan Harris; 12 words; fair use).

**Relationship to Amodei and the Anthropic posture.** Harris does not name Amodei as an ally, but his August 2024 piece praises labs that "talk openly about the risks" — which the book's Ch11 recognizes as the Amodei posture. Harris remains warier than Amodei about deployment timelines. The book holds both; research-30 (Amodei, not yet written) will map the precise difference.

## Section 3 — The 2024–2026 public positions

**September 2024** — appearance on Oprah Winfrey's ABC special *AI and the Future of Us*. Confirmed (Wikipedia).

**August 13, 2024** — CHT Substack publication of *This Moment in AI: How We Got Here, and Where We're Going*. The piece's five core claims (verified fetch, 2026-04-19):
1. Scaling costs $100M → $1–10B; safety spending at ~1/1000th the nuclear-industry ratio
2. AGI-level capabilities projected 2026–2027 by some researchers, one-breakthrough-remaining by others — either way the preparation window is closing
3. AI-generated synthetic content will out-compete human content in attention economies
4. Misaligned incentives mirror social media's harms — deploy-before-govern pattern repeats
5. Data scarcity solvable via proprietary datasets and multimodal expansion
