# Methodology — How to represent other voices faithfully
*Internal reference, April 22, 2026.*
*Companion to the Hope is Process essay and future public writing.*

The question this note answers: **"How can we be sure to be representing the other voices right?"**

Short answer: we cannot be fully sure. But we can adopt practices that make the representation auditable, correctable, and falsifiable — which is different from being merely careful or respectful.

---

## The seven practices

### 1. Prefer direct quotation over paraphrase for load-bearing claims

For any claim a reader might use to form an opinion of the source's position, use the source's own words. Cap at ≤30 words per quote for fair-use safety. Put the quote in quotation marks. Attach the URL.

**Bad:** Yudkowsky believes AI will probably kill us all.
**Good:** Yudkowsky has written that, in his assessment, *"[exact quote]"* (2023, [URL]).

This is the practice applied throughout the Hope is Process essay for Harris, Raskin, Patel, Anthropic, Dean Ball, and Andreotti.

### 2. For copyrighted or paywalled sources, paraphrase with attribution AND note what you cannot quote

When a source is behind copyright or you cannot obtain the exact wording, paraphrase clearly and mark the paraphrase. Never invent a quote to fill the gap. This is the rule applied in the book's Chapman & Withers, Tuck & Yang, and Bayo Akomolafe citations.

### 3. State the source's strongest version of their own argument, not the weakest

Before critiquing a view, ensure you are engaging with what the view's proponents themselves consider load-bearing — not with the version easiest to criticize. The Hope is Process essay's treatment of the accelerationist view (Andreessen, Altman) names the strongest version (real benefits, geopolitical stakes, the coordination-failure counterargument) before saying what the view misses. This is intellectual honesty, and it makes the critique harder to dismiss.

### 4. Name what the source has NOT addressed publicly, separately from critiquing what they HAVE

The Amodei comparison document lists four specific claims he has not engaged publicly (Dwarkesh proliferation argument, social-media counterexample, Anthropic-is-racing, household scale). Identifying these gaps is different from accusing him of being wrong about them. It lets readers see where the public record runs out, so they can form their own judgment about whether the source's work, as it stands, carries the weight you are putting on it.

### 5. Track how a source's position has evolved across time

Sources are not static. The Amodei comparison document shows a clear shift from *Loving Grace* (Oct 2024) to *Urgency of Interpretability* (Apr 2025) to the Feb 2026 DoW statements. Representing Amodei by citing only *Loving Grace* would freeze him at his most optimistic. Representing him by citing only the Feb 2026 statement would freeze him at his most defensive. Neither is accurate. The honest representation is the trajectory.

Apply this to: Tristan Harris (the 2023 AI Dilemma framing vs. the 2025 TED talk's sharper "no definition of wisdom in any spiritual tradition that does not involve restraint"); Andreotti (*Hospicing Modernity* 2021 vs. *Outgrowing Modernity* 2025 vs. *Burnout from Humans* 2025); Yudkowsky (the alignment-is-tractable era vs. the late-2025 shift to telling-the-world-plainly).

### 6. Include the source's own response to critique, where it exists

When possible, include how the source has responded to objections similar to the one you are raising. Amodei's *"it's going to be a mess"* hedging in his interviews with Dwarkesh is a response to the exact charge that his published essays are too clean. Including those hedges is fairer than citing only the polished essays.

### 7. Invite correction — and mean it

End with a sentence that names the possibility of being wrong and an email or comment channel for correction. The Hope is Process essay's close (*"If the frames here feel useful, the book goes deeper. If they feel wrong, the book probably compounds the wrongness, and you will have told me something I need to know"*) is this practice. Make it credible by actually replying when someone takes you up on it.

---

## What this methodology does NOT claim

It does not claim the representation is *fully* accurate. No representation is. It claims the representation is **auditable**: a reader who disagrees can check the quotes, the URLs, the trajectory, and the gaps, and form her own view. If the representation turns out to be wrong, the method makes the wrongness findable — which is different from the method being right.

This is the same epistemic frame the book adopts throughout: we do not claim to know what is true; we report what has served, under conditions we can name, and we mark what we cannot verify.
