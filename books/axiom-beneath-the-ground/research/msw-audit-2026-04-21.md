# MSW Audit: *The Axiom Beneath the Ground* (v5)
*Clinical-and-critical social-work reading, 2026-04-21*

---

## 1. Executive Summary

*The Axiom Beneath the Ground* v5 reads, from an MSW vantage, as a philosophical memoir written by a first-year anti-oppression student who is doing her homework visibly — Walters and Bailey arrive directly from a Juneteenth panel she was assigned to watch, Linehan's three-filter is load-bearing for the whole book, Wolfe's *structure-not-event* formulation does real work in Ch10e, and the Arcane School exit in Ch10g is plausibly the cleanest action-anchor in the manuscript. The clinical-and-critical reasoning is, on average, more careful than most trade-press books touching this material. The author is not performing anti-oppression fluency; she is thinking with the material.

The three strongest passages are: (a) the shovel scene in Ch1 as an embodied image of negotiated right-size (relational attunement rendered concretely rather than claimed abstractly); (b) the Linehan-as-theory-of-change move in Ch10d, particularly the footnote bridging Hirschman and Mazzucato — this is a real contribution, not a rehearsal; (c) the Arcane School exit in Ch10g, which models accountable leaving at the level a reader can test against her own memberships.

The three weakest or most at-risk passages are: (a) the Tronick/Feldman/Porges/Linehan cluster in Ch11 (§"The human nervous system is an open circuit") — compressed to the point of distortion, with biosocial theory elided; (b) the Walters/Choctoaw sacred-fire retelling in Ch10a — a second-hand retelling of a living Indigenous teaching used to corroborate the author's structural thesis, CARE principles not visibly applied; (c) the Ch00b Lilac Dance prelude — a trauma-adjacent dream passage published without a consent frame for readers carrying their own body material. Chapman & Withers are nowhere cited despite being the obvious ancestor of Ch10d–g. That omission is the single largest MSW-register gap.

## 2. Claim-by-Claim Audit

### Claim 1 — The "relational attunement" axis of the four orthogonalities (Ch10a)

**What she says.** "She had held relational attunement in highest regard, because of her MSW training and the therapists and mothers she had learned from. Relational attunement is a real skill; people who have it can do things with nervous systems that people who do not have it cannot do." Then: people high on attunement can be low on the other three axes.

**Clinically accurate?** Directionally yes, specifically under-argued. The sentence "do things with nervous systems" is the strongest claim in the paragraph and the one most in need of citation. What the literature actually shows — Feldman's biobehavioral synchrony work (HRV coupling, oxytocin release coordinated across dyads), Tronick's still-face, Schore's right-brain-to-right-brain affect regulation, Porges's polyvagal mapping of ventral-vagal co-regulation — is that attunement is measurable, learnable, and has dose-response effects on infant stress physiology and adult attachment repair. The passage as written would read identically if "attunement" were a trait one either has or does not. That is the error the book is otherwise most careful to avoid.

**Structurally accurate?** The four-axis framing itself is interesting and probably right for the book's argument. But the "relational attunement" axis is not exchangeable with the others in an important way: attunement is the *substrate* that the other three are transmitted through. You cannot transmit recognition (enlightenment), moral seriousness, or even the results of intelligence without co-regulated relational contact. Treating it as a fourth peer-axis flattens what makes it different.

**Trauma-informed concern?** Low. The passage is a cognitive claim, not a body claim.

**Recommended revision.** One paragraph inserted distinguishing attunement-as-trait (a folk reading) from attunement-as-skill (what Feldman et al. measure). One sentence naming that attunement is not just a fourth axis but the medium through which the others travel. One citation — Feldman (2007, *Journal of Child Psychology and Psychiatry*, "Parent-infant synchrony and the construction of shared timing") is the specific paper the book's argument sits on and should name.

### Claim 2 — Still-face / biosocial / biological-hypothesis cluster (Ch11)

**What she says** (Ch11 §"Open circuit"): "The human nervous system is an open circuit… Ed Tronick's still-face experiments, Ruth Feldman's decades of mother-infant biobehavioral synchrony work, Stephen Porges's polyvagal mapping, Marsha Linehan's work on emotion regulation in conditions where the loan failed."

**Clinically accurate?** Mixed. The "open circuit" framing is Coan's (James Coan, UVA, social baseline theory) more than it is Tronick's or Feldman's, and Coan is uncredited. The sequence Tronick→Feldman→Porges→Linehan reads fluently but is doing four different kinds of work. Tronick's still-face (1978) is a disruption paradigm; Feldman measures ongoing synchrony; Porges maps vagal tone and social engagement; Linehan's biosocial theory is specifically a theory of how pervasive invalidation interacts with emotional vulnerability to produce BPD — it is not a general theory of "when the loan failed." Collapsing biosocial theory into the open-circuit story misrepresents what Linehan actually argued.

A smaller point: "sixty years of quiet measurement" — Tronick's still-face is 1978 (so ~48 years); Feldman's program is ~25 years old; Porges's polyvagal papers begin around 1994. "Sixty years" is the Bowlby starting line, not this cluster's. Minor but fixable.

**Structurally accurate?** The CLAUDE.md file explicitly says the still-face experiment belongs to *Fire* Ch1, not Axiom. This passage is duplicating content with less precision than its owner book has. The author's own deduplication rule is being broken.

**Trauma-informed concern?** Low-to-moderate. Readers with attachment trauma will feel something when they read "zero such arcs — a nervous system learns that dysregulation is the condition." The sentence is true and it is also the kind of sentence that wants a one-line consent frame. "If you are recognizing yourself in this, that recognition is the nervous system working, not failing."

**Recommended revision.** Trim to one paragraph. Cite Feldman (2007) and Tronick & Gianino (1986) precisely. Drop Linehan from the list or give her her own sentence that names biosocial theory accurately ("Linehan's biosocial theory — pervasive invalidation interacting with biological emotional vulnerability — is the clinical account of what happens when the co-regulatory loan systematically fails across development"). Add Coan's social baseline theory if the "open circuit" metaphor is kept — that is where the metaphor actually lives. Or: defer to *Fire* Ch1 and cut the passage here.

### Claim 3 — Walters, sacred fire, structure-not-event (Ch10a, Ch10e)

**What she says.** In Ch10a, she retells the Choctaw sacred-fire teaching from Walters's Juneteenth panel — two priests, the sleepy one relighting the fire dishonestly, everyone getting sick — and uses it to triangulate her three-traditions claim. In Ch10e she quotes Walters quoting Wolfe: "Settler colonialism is a structure, not an event."

**Clinically/structurally accurate?** The Wolfe citation is correctly attributed (2006 essay, "Settler Colonialism and the Elimination of the Native," *Journal of Genocide Research*) and the Walters operationalization — Kamloops, Cowessess, the system-versus-individual-racist distinction — is on the record and handled with care. The Ch10e passage is the cleanest structural-register work in the book.

The Ch10a retelling is more fraught. The author retells a specific ceremonial teaching a Choctaw elder shared on a panel with specific framing, and lifts it into a triadic comparison with Śaiva tantra and AI safety. This is the precise move CARE principles (Collective benefit, Authority to control, Responsibility, Ethics) are designed to slow down. Walters is a public scholar who shared the teaching in a professional context, which lowers but does not eliminate the concern. The concern is the *use* — the teaching is being recruited as corroboration for the author's structural thesis. That is an extractive reading of an elder's gift to a profession.

**Trauma-informed concern?** Not to the reader; to the relationship the book is asking for with the teaching.

**Settler-moves-to-innocence?** One shows up. The three-traditions argument — "three independent traditions converge, therefore structural claim" — uses the Choctaw teaching to validate a framework whose primary commitments are Śaiva nondual and AI safety. The Choctoaw teaching is doing labor for the author's argument more than the argument is doing labor for Choctaw sovereignty or futurity. Tuck & Yang (2012) name this pattern as the "settler adoption fantasy" and the "conscientization move to innocence": settler discovers Indigenous wisdom, uses it to reorganize their own worldview, leaves the material conditions of Indigenous life unchanged. The book is not doing the most flagrant version of this, but the structure is present.

**Recommended revision.** Either (a) cite the Juneteenth panel explicitly and link to it so the teaching is returned to its context rather than floating as a free-standing parable; (b) add a paragraph naming what the author is and is not entitled to do with a Choctaw teaching retold on YouTube; or (c) drop the sacred-fire retelling and keep only the Wolfe/Walters structure-not-event, which carries the argument without the appropriation risk. The Wolfe citation should include Walters as the specific panelist who brought Wolfe forward — that is the accurate chain.

### Claim 4 — Chapman & Withers framing (implied Ch10d–g)

**What she says.** Nothing. *A Violent History of Benevolence* is visibly assigned reading (the JPG is in her MSW folder) and its frame — that social work is structurally complicit with settler colonialism, that benevolence and harm are not opposites but often the same gesture, that reform-from-inside has a specific failure mode — is the water Ch10d–g swim in. Chapman and Withers are never named.

**Should it be?** Yes. The Arcane School chapter in particular is Chapman & Withers applied to a personal decision: she is describing the exact mechanism by which a well-intentioned institution can carry harm in its architecture that no amount of personal good faith inside it can compost. That is their book in one sentence. A reader who has done the same SSWO 509 reading will notice the silence.

**Recommended revision.** One named citation in Ch10d or Ch10g: "Chris Chapman and A.J. Withers, in *A Violent History of Benevolence*, name this structural failure — the harm that runs through the benevolent institution's architecture, not in spite of it." That sentence credits the frame, locates the book inside the SSWO 509 reading, and strengthens the argument by naming its ancestor. Right now the book is doing Chapman & Withers' work without their permission (citation-wise) and without the specific sharpening they offer.

### Claim 5 — Linehan's built-alternatives move (Ch10d)

**What she says.** Linehan did not campaign to dismantle psychiatric hospitals; she built DBT. The evidence, accumulated over decades, did the dismantling. This is the model for recursive.eco.

**Clinically accurate?** Partially. The biographical frame is correct — Linehan was hospitalized, received seclusion and ECT, built DBT. The causal claim that "DBT dismantled the old model" is overstated. DBT is the evidence-based treatment of choice for BPD and chronic suicidality; it has *not* dismantled inpatient psychiatric hospitalization, involuntary commitment, or the ECT apparatus, and Linehan herself has used hospital services as part of DBT's crisis protocols. The move is better described as building an alternative that *reduces demand* for the iatrogenic parts of the system for a specific population, not as replacement.

**Clinically useful as a general theory of change?** This is where an MSW reader would push back hardest. DBT was built for a specific population (chronically suicidal women, later BPD broadly) with a specific risk profile, in a specific research-funded context, over forty years, with manualization and fidelity monitoring that is extraordinarily rare outside academic medicine. Generalizing the Linehan move to "build a platform and let the evidence dismantle the old feed-driven systems" is inspirational but it is not the same move at the same scale with the same risk profile. The gap between "Linehan built DBT" and "a contemplative platform will dismantle engagement-optimized consumer tech" is large enough that the reader should see the gap named.

**Recommended revision.** Keep the Linehan story; soften the causal claim from "dismantled" to "reduced the old model's grip on the population DBT served." Add one sentence naming the scale disanalogy — Linehan had a bounded clinical population, forty years, and NIH funding; the author has an open population, a few years, and self-funding. The move is still valid as an orientation. It is not valid as a prediction.

### Claim 6 — The Arcane School exit (Ch10g)

**What she says.** She left after her Week 8 anti-oppression reading gave her language for the racial taxonomy in the cosmology. She wrote to the group. She did not try to build a cleaner version. She kept the adaptive practices (attention, discipline, sitting). She names this as accountable leaving rather than spiritual exit.

**Anti-oppression accuracy?** The diagnosis is correct and under-argued. Theosophy and its derivatives (Alice Bailey's Arcane School, Blavatsky's root-races) carry nineteenth-century racial science — root races, sub-races, hierarchies of consciousness mapped onto somatic and ethnic categories — that is not incidental to the cosmology but load-bearing to it. The book says "racial taxonomy underneath the cosmology was irreducible" and does not show the specific structure. A reader who has not done this reading will not know what she is naming.

**Settler-move-to-innocence check?** She names the risk directly ("accountable love… includes knowing when the love can no longer be the form of accountability") and handles it better than most exits of this shape. The passage that reads almost-but-not-quite as a move-to-innocence is "She left. She wrote to the group to name why. She did not attempt to build a cleaner version." The reader has to trust that the leaving was accountable because the leaving is described from the leaver's perspective. The one addition that would close the gap is whether she stayed in correspondence with anyone from the group, whether the letter she wrote produced any response she honored, and whether any of the harm the cosmology continues to enact — on, for example, students of color who enter Arcane School materials and encounter the root-race architecture — is something her accountability extends to after the exit. The chapter gestures at this ("Her responsibility continues without her being in the room") but does not land it.

**Recommended revision.** One paragraph naming the specific racial-science content — "root races," the mapping of consciousness to ethnic hierarchy, the lineage from Blavatsky to Bailey to the current Arcane School materials — so the reader knows what was there to leave. One sentence on what post-exit accountability looks like in practice (wrote to whom, stayed in touch with whom, what happens to her royalties or platform attention if Arcane School readers find her).

### Claim 7 — The body chapters (Ch00b Lilac Dance; Ch11 on screens)

**What she says (Ch00b).** A dream: aerial silks become a serpent, marks imprinted on her skin, self-congratulation as the trigger. She names Jung's "some dreams take a lifetime to integrate." She publishes the dream.

**Trauma-informed frame?** Thin. The passage is a body-imprint dream and it is offered without a consent line for readers who may have their own imprint material activated. The Jung footnote is the consent gesture the book has, and it is a meta-consent (the *author's* right to carry a dream for decades), not a reader-consent (the reader's right to know what she is about to encounter). The 5 Rhythms / Esalen context later signals body-work literacy; the Ch00b prelude does not show that literacy to a reader who starts at the beginning.

**What she says (Ch11 on screens).** Haidt on adolescents, Christakis on early childhood, the specific claim that "one wrong recommendation click" can take a preschooler from a language-learning cartoon to a sexualized thumbnail.

**Clinically accurate?** Haidt is represented accurately at the level of the public-facing claim (*The Anxious Generation*, screen time and adolescent mental health). Dimitri Christakis's work is cited by name; his specific findings — the associations between early heavy screen exposure and attentional/language outcomes, the dose-response patterns, the distinction between co-viewed and solo viewing — are referenced in gesture but not in specific. The claim about algorithmic drift on YouTube Kids is defensible and has been documented in multiple audits (e.g., Papadamou et al. 2020 on Elsagate-adjacent content), none cited.

**Recommended revision.** Ch00b: one sentence of reader consent at the top of the prelude. "What follows is a dream with somatic content; if your own body carries imprint material, you may want to read this with a trusted person or skip to Ch1." Ch11 screens passage: cite Christakis (2018, *JAMA Pediatrics*) for the specific findings; or be clear that the claim is based on her own parental observation corroborated by the public-facing program.

### Claim 8 — "Love brings accountability" and spiritual-exit critique (Ch10g)

**Clinically useful or sophistry?** Clinically useful. The distinction between spiritual exit (leave the institution, build a cleaner one outside, repeat the failure on a new substrate) and accountable leaving (stay until the structure cannot be reformed from inside, leave without pretending the leaving settles anything, remain in correspondence with what you left) is a real and teachable distinction. It maps onto the clinical distinction between *fleeing* (nervous-system driven exit followed by rapid reattachment) and *grieving exit* (exit that has metabolized what it is leaving). The passage is one of the book's better contributions.

**Is the author doing the work she describes, or is the book itself the spiritual-exit move at a higher scale?** This is the question the chapter invites. The honest answer: she is doing most of it. Receipts (the March 28 commit removing OpenAI, the April 11 licensing split) are cited in Ch10d. The Arcane School letter is cited. The retreat relationship with Wallis is maintained rather than exited. The marriage is named as still being worked on. The platform is being built, not celebrated.

What is less clear is whether *social work itself* — the profession she is entering — is receiving the same treatment or whether the book is using social work as a source of frames without yet being accountable to the profession's scrutiny in return. Ch10g tries to close this gap ("She loves the profession. She has not said so in the book because she had not, until recently, been in the profession long enough to say so without the sentence sounding provisional"). The closing is partial. An MSW reader will still want to know: what happens if the profession reads this book and calls her on something? Is she in a cohort? Is she attending conferences? Is her work being peer-supervised? The book should not resolve those questions, but it should show the reader that they are alive for her.

**Recommended revision.** One paragraph in Ch10g naming her current accountability structures as an MSW student: the specific cohort, the practicum she is heading into, the supervision she will be in. This is not performance; it is the Bailey posture made specific.

### Claim 9 — NASW Code of Ethics alignment

**Code compliance check.** The book describes an unlicensed MSW student building a platform that includes AI-mediated tarot readings, I-Ching spreads, astrology charts, and journaling tools some of which are named as "reflective" and "educational." A reader who knows the NASW Code will be watching three specific standards:

- **§1.04 Competence** — practice within areas of competence. The platform is not presented as therapy, but some of the AI personas (Wallis RAG, the Tattvas deck's AI commentary) are doing work adjacent to clinical interpretation. The book is careful to name them as "study aids" and "structured contemplation" (Ch11), not therapy. That framing holds if users understand it. The risk is that users in distress will not parse the distinction.
- **§1.07 Privacy and confidentiality** — users inputting journal material, draws, reflections into a platform that stores JSONB in Supabase. The book does not discuss the data-handling architecture from an ethics frame. An MSW reviewer will want to know whether informed consent, data retention, and the right to deletion are handled to the standard the Code would require of a clinical record — not because the platform is clinical, but because users may treat it as such.
- **§6.04 Social and political action** — the book's macro register (Ch10d, Ch10e, Ch10g) aligns well with §6.04's call for social-justice engagement.

The NASW 2021 Racial Justice Report is present in spirit (Walters, Juneteenth panel) and absent in specific. The Report's apology-and-accountability framing is a direct ancestor of Bailey's "love brings accountability" closing, and is not cited.

**Recommended revision.** A short author's-note paragraph, either in Ch10g or as a footnote, naming: (a) the platform is not therapy and does not substitute for licensed care; (b) data-handling and user consent standards; (c) her own supervisory relationships as an MSW student. This is both NASW-aligned and, more importantly, honest about what the platform is.

### Claim 10 — Smuggled white-middle-class frame?

**Positionality audit.** The author has done good self-positioning work in Week 4 (ally paper) and the Growth Plan. Her public positionality in the book is: Brazilian by origin, immigrant to the U.S., white-presenting in North American registers, Catholic upbringing, former Goldman analyst, MSW student, mother, married, on OneDrive, in an apartment with a daughter who watches *Mr. Rogers*, who has taken two trips to Esalen, who drove to a retreat in New England alone, who can afford a $5K engineering contract, who can be a Patreon member at the "adept tier."

Where the register does ideological work:

- The "reading year" (Ch1 §III) reads as universalizable — *she read Tillich in the bathtub, she read Linehan, she read Wallis*. What the register smuggles: a reader with full autonomy over her evenings, uninterrupted time for Tillich, the scaffolding (university degrees, English fluency, Brazilian middle-class habitus) to read across traditions. Most social-work clients do not have this reading year. The book's theory of change is built from inside a life that has access to contemplative time the theory does not name.
- The "she drove north with the sun high over the Massachusetts hills" scene (Ch1 §VI) is a class image. It is also a real scene. The risk is not that the scene is class-inflected — it is — but that the surrounding prose does not notice.
- Umbanda is named carefully and with respect. Ayahuasca (via CLAUDE.md Paradevi thread) is in the prelude. The non-Western traditions the author has practiced are not treated as "spiritual tourism," but the book is in English, published through KDP, written for an audience whose primary reference points are Tillich, Haidt, Christakis, and Wallis. The Brazilian and Afro-Brazilian material is secondary in the final frame even though it is primary in the life.

**Settler/whiteness audit.** Less a "white-middle-class" frame than a *trans-Atlantic cosmopolitan frame* — a specific register held by educated immigrants to North America who move across Brazil, New England, California, Catholic-Umbanda-tantra-Theosophy. The register is not universal. The Week 4 paper and the Growth Plan show the author is aware of this. The book itself is less aware. Kimmel's "Open Letter to White Men" (in her MSW reading folder) would sharpen the passage that treats the reading year as the unmarked universal.

**Recommended revision.** One honest paragraph in Ch11 (which is the self-reflexive "writing desk" chapter and the natural home for this move) naming the conditions under which the reading year happened: the class inheritance that made it possible, the specific form of whiteness-adjacent positioning her Brazilian body takes in North American rooms, the distance between her practitioner voice and the voice of someone without her access. The Growth Plan does this well; the book has not yet carried that work across.

## 4. One Direct Recommendation

The single most important MSW-register move before the book goes out is this: **add one paragraph, preferably in Ch10g near the "because I love it" passage, that names her own current location inside the social-work profession — specifically: she is a first-year MSW student in a specific course (SSWO 509), working inside a specific syllabus, under a specific professor's supervision, heading toward a specific practicum, accountable to a specific cohort.**

The book currently asks the reader to trust that the author's love for the profession is earned. Bailey's posture, which she is explicitly adopting, earned its warrant from fifty years of practice. The author is asking for the posture at the start of the practice. That is honest and it requires the location to be named.

Her own NASW ethics reflection paper (per the assignment structure) and her Week 8 *Building Alternatives* paper already do this work — they name where she is, what she is reading, who is supervising. The book has not yet brought that locatedness forward. Doing so would: (a) strengthen §1.04 competence framing for any reader who reads the platform as adjacent to clinical work; (b) model what Chapman & Withers would call "ethical positioning inside a compromised profession" rather than from a vantage above it; (c) close the gap between the Growth Plan's honesty and the book's register.

One paragraph. Specific names for specific structures she is accountable to. Not a credential — a location.

That is the MSW move the book is otherwise ready for.
