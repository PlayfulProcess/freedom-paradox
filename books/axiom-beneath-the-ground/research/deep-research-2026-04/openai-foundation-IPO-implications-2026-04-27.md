The findings below distinguish established legal doctrine, primary documentary evidence, and analyst-grade inference from speculation, and flag what cannot be answered until specific documents become public.*

## I. GOVERNANCE INSTRUMENT SURVIVAL AT IPO

### 1. Survival of Class N Common Stock at listing

There is no public document that yet specifies what happens to the OpenAI Foundation's Class N Common Stock at IPO. Under both NYSE and Nasdaq listing standards, however, a privately-held supervoting/blocking class can survive an IPO and is the standard architecture used to keep a controlling holder in place after listing.

- **NYSE Listed Company Manual §313**: prohibits *disparate reduction* of existing-shareholder voting rights through corporate action; does not prohibit creating an unlisted high-vote class at the time of IPO.
- **Nasdaq Listing Rule 5640 / IM-5640**: dual-class structures permitted at IPO; existing dual-class issuers may raise additional super-voting capital.

Both rules grandfather voting structures in place at listing. The Foundation's Class N can survive in either of two configurations:
- (a) Class N remains unlisted, privately-held supervoting/blocking class (Novo Nordisk, Carlsberg, Hershey model)
- (b) Class N collapsed into structural protections written into the certificate of incorporation (charter-based blocking rights and director-appointment provisions)

The first is the cleaner precedent.

### 2. Survival of §7 prior-written-approval rights

Survival depends on Delaware corporate law mechanics:
- **DGCL §102(b)(4)**: charter provisions requiring supermajority or class votes for specific corporate actions
- **DGCL §141(a)**: modification of board-management default permissible "as may be otherwise provided … in the certificate of incorporation"
- **DGCL §242(b)(2)**: class-vote requirements on charter amendments
- **DGCL §271(a)** + **DGCL §251**: deemed liquidation mechanic; supports class-consent requirements

Controlling doctrine:
- *Williams v. Geier*, 671 A.2d 1368 (Del. 1996) — approved tenured-voting structures
- *In re CBS Corp. Litigation*, C.A. No. 2018-0342-AGB (Del. Ch. May 17, 2018) — charter-based class consent rights enforceable
- *Frederick Hsu Living Trust v. ODN Holding Corp.*, 2017 WL 1437308 (Del. Ch.) — PBC fiduciaries owe normal Revlon/Unocal duties subject to public-benefit balancing under DGCL §365

§7 rights can be preserved post-IPO if codified as Class N voting rights or supermajority approval triggers tied to a Foundation-controlled entity.

### 3. Sole power to appoint and remove every PBC director

- **DGCL §141(d)**: separate classes of stock can be granted exclusive director-election rights
- **DGCL §141(k)**: removal rights can mirror election rights

Mechanism well-established: separate class of stock with sole right to elect (and remove with or without cause) all directors. Preserved at IPO by writing this into the certificate of incorporation and listing only a non-electing class to the public.

**Tata mechanics (verified):** Tata Sons holds controlling stakes in publicly-traded Tata operating companies (TCS ~71.74%, Tata Motors, Tata Steel). Tata Trusts hold ~66% of Tata Sons. Tata Sons' articles grant Tata Trusts the right to nominate one-third of directors and "affirmative voting rights" on specified matters once Trusts hold ≥40% of paid-up capital.

*Tata Consultancy Services Ltd. v. Cyrus Investments Pvt. Ltd.*, Civil Appeal Nos. 440–441/2020 (Sup. Ct. India, March 26, 2021): trust-nominee directors with affirmative voting rights do not perpetrate "oppression" within Sections 241–242 of the Companies Act, 2013. ¶19.30: directors nominated by charitable trusts owe duties combining private and "pure, unadulterated public interest" obligations.

**Bosch mechanics:** Robert Bosch GmbH NOT publicly listed. Robert Bosch Stiftung GmbH owns ~94% of share capital but has *zero* voting rights (transferred 1964–1969). Robert Bosch Industrietreuhand KG holds ~93% of voting rights with ~0.01% economic interest. Bosch family holds ~5% capital, ~7% voting rights. **Structurally incompatible with U.S. public listing without massive restructuring.**

### 4. The 15-year warrant for additional shares (10x trigger)

- Currently disclosed only in OpenAI/CA AG MOU and openai.com/our-structure as triggering "if OpenAI Group's share price increases greater than tenfold after 15 years"
- **S-1 disclosure obligations** (Reg S-K Item 506): Description in "Description of Capital Stock"; dilutive effect quantified in "Capitalization" and "Dilution"; fair-value treatment under ASC 815 / ASC 480
- For 501(c)(3) public charity, no IRS prohibition on holding long-dated warrant on equity of controlled subsidiary; passive investment instrument
- §4943 not applicable (Foundation is public charity, not private foundation)
- Cannot be answered without primary docs: strike price, anti-dilution provisions, exercise mechanism, AG conditions

## II. FOUNDATION DISCLOSURE OBLIGATIONS POST-IPO

### 5. SEC disclosure regime

- **Schedule 13D (not 13G)**: as a controlling shareholder with director-appointment rights, the Foundation is not "passive." Initial 13D due **5 business days** after acquiring >5% (per SEC Release 33-11253, Oct 10, 2023; previously 10 calendar days). Amendments due **2 business days** after material change (≥1% acquisition or disposition). XML format required since Dec 18, 2024.
- **Form 4 / §16(a)**: 7 of 8 Foundation directors also sit on PBC board — each files Forms 3, 4, 5 reporting personal stock transactions within 2 business days. Foundation itself, as >10% beneficial owner, is also §16 reporting person.
- **§16(b) short-swing profits**: insider profit on purchase-and-sale (or sale-and-purchase) within 6 months recoverable by issuer. *Foremost-McKesson v. Provident Securities Co.*, 423 U.S. 232 (1976) — applies mechanically. **Foundation cannot rebalance, harvest, or trade tactically without short-swing exposure.**
- **Form 144 (Rule 144)**: Foundation will be "affiliate" given director-appointment power; faces volume limits (greater of 1% of outstanding or average weekly trading volume over preceding 4 weeks per any 90-day period).

### 6. Form 990 reporting changes

- **Schedule D (Investments)**: pre-IPO Level 3 valuation (independent appraisal) → post-IPO daily mark-to-market disclosure
- **Schedule R (Related Organizations)**: PBC unambiguously a related organization; Parts II and V require disclosure of all transfers, license arrangements, IP transfers, shared services
- **Schedule J (Compensation)**: officer/director compensation from related organization reportable
- **Schedule L**: loans/grants/business transactions between Foundation and disqualified persons more visible post-IPO
- **Form 990-T**: required if ≥$1,000 UBTI; public-company shareholdings generally do not trigger UBIT

### 7. UBIT analysis

Foundation's holding structured to be passive:
- **§512(b)(1)**: dividends, interest, payments with respect to securities loans, annuities all excluded from UBI
- **§512(b)(5)**: capital gains on post-lockup sales excluded
- **§512(b)(13) trap**: interest, rents, royalties, annuities paid by controlled subsidiary (>50% voting/value) to tax-exempt parent get pulled into UBIT to extent exceeding arm's-length amounts. *Currently not implicated by OpenAI structure; but post-IPO restructuring with such flows would create UBIT exposure.*
- **§514 (Debt-Financed Property)**: Foundation acquired stake in recap without debt financing; not implicated
- **PLR 200825050, PLR 200526021**: public charity can hold 100% voting stock of C-corp subsidiary and receive only dividends/interest without UBIT, so long as charity doesn't itself "regularly carry on" the underlying trade or business
- **§4943 excess business holdings**: does NOT apply because Foundation is 501(c)(3) **public charity** (EIN 81-0861541), not private foundation. *This is the structural lynchpin enabling the entire 26%+ holding.*

## III. SELLING PRESSURE AND LOCK-UP

### 8. Standard lock-up periods

Default underwriter lock-up: 180 days. Tech IPOs since 2020 increasingly use early-release triggers (e.g., 25% release if stock trades 20%+ above offer price for 10 of 15 days).

For foundation-controlled IPOs:
- **Novo Nordisk**: Foundation cannot divest A-shares ever, per Foundation Articles of Association. *Strongest possible "lock-up" — permanent and structural.*
- **Carlsberg Foundation**: must "at all times" hold ≥51% of votes per Foundation charter; enforced under Danish Commercial Foundations Act with Danish Business Authority oversight
- **Hershey Trust**: ongoing Pennsylvania AG and Dauphin County Orphans' Court oversight; 2002 episode created precedent that any sale of controlling interest requires AG approval

### 9. AG and watchdog positions on Foundation post-IPO selling

CA AG MOU §3 reserves "The decision whether to relinquish the Class N Common Stock, if ever, [to] the control of the NFP Board" — Foundation board, not PBC or investors, decides any divestment of the control instrument.

No public statements yet from AG Bonta or AG Jennings specifically addressing IPO-stage Foundation selling. **Primary-source gap.**

### 10. Three nonprofit-with-equity patterns

- **Permanent-hold model** (Novo Nordisk, Carlsberg, Bosch, Tata Trusts): Foundation/trust articles prohibit divestment of controlling instrument; sales confined to non-control economic shares. *Dominant model.*
- **Diversification-sale model** (Hershey 2002 attempt): Trust held ~77% of Hershey Foods voting power via Class B; July 2002 announced plan to sell controlling stake; PA AG (Mike Fisher) obtained preliminary injunction in *In re Milton Hershey School Trust*, 807 A.2d 324 (Pa. Commw. 2002); trustees abandoned sale; stock dropped from $78.30 back to $65. Pennsylvania subsequently passed Act 141 of 2002 (codified at 20 Pa.C.S. §7710) requiring AG and Orphans' Court approval before any sale of controlling interest. **Net result: a charitable holder that announces sale of controlling stock of a public company faces enforceable AG intervention.**
- **Mozilla counter-precedent**: Foundation owns Mozilla Corporation since August 2005; explicit commitment to "no IPO, acquisition, or other liquidity event" (Frank Hecker 2005-08-03). Stance held for 20+ years.

## IV. FOUNDATION-CONTROLLED PUBLIC COMPANIES — PRECEDENTS

### 12. Novo Nordisk / Novo Holdings A/S / Novo Nordisk Foundation — THE PRIORITY PRECEDENT

**Ownership chain:** *Novo Nordisk Foundation* (Danish enterprise foundation, "self-governing entity with no owners") → 100% of *Novo Holdings A/S* → A and B shares of *Novo Nordisk A/S* and *Novonesis A/S* (formerly Novozymes).

**A/B share structure:** Share capital DKK 446,500,000. A capital DKK 107,487,200, B capital DKK 339,012,800. **Each A share = 100 votes; each B share = 10 votes** (post-Sept 13, 2023 stock split). A shares NOT LISTED. A shares prohibited from divestment "for as long as the Foundation exists" per Foundation Articles. B shares listed on Nasdaq Copenhagen (NOVO B) and via ADRs on NYSE (NVO).

**Vote vs. economic split:** As of Nov 8, 2024, Novo Holdings A/S held 28.05% of share capital and **77.28%** of votes.

**Three-board governance:** Novo Nordisk Foundation Board → elects members to Novo Holdings A/S Board → Novo Holdings nominates 2 members for election to Novo Nordisk A/S supervisory board. 5 of 8 Novo Nordisk shareholder-elected board members independent under Danish Corporate Governance Recommendations.

**Disclosure:** Form 20-F (CIK 0000353278). Foundation Articles approved by Danish Foundation Authority. Triple-lock: Foundation Articles + Holding-Co charter + state oversight.

**Why this works at $400B+ market cap (down from ~$600B 2024 peak) in regulated pharma:** structure protected at three legal layers — Danish Commercial Foundations Act, Foundation Articles approved by Danish Foundation Authority, Novo Nordisk Articles. Foundation has explicit charter authority to "hinder any divestment by Novo Holdings A/S of A shares" and "maintain a majority of the votes in the form of A shares." **This is the structural template OpenAI Foundation could most plausibly emulate.**

## V. SPECIFIC RISKS POST-IPO

### 17. Activist shareholder risk

With Class N supervoting/blocking rights and sole director-appointment power preserved at IPO, a Carl Icahn-style activist cannot acquire enough public float to challenge governance. CBS-NAI dispute (2018) is the cautionary tale of attempting to dilute non-controlling holders disparately — NYSE §313 prohibition targets that fact pattern, not the existence of supervoting controlling holder. **Architecture is durable** so long as Foundation does not attempt mid-stream to alter public-shareholder rights.

### 18. ESG / index-fund stewardship pressure

BlackRock 2025–2026 Investment Stewardship Guidelines, Vanguard Investment Stewardship Principles, State Street Voting and Engagement Guidelines all formally express concern about unequal voting rights but acknowledge mission-driven control structures as recognized exception. **No documented case where ESG pressure has dislodged a foundation-controlled public-company structure.**

### 19. ISS / Glass Lewis governance ratings

Both providers will assign poor governance scores on "Shareholder Rights" / "Audit & Risk Oversight." Novo Nordisk consistently scores in bottom tier on Shareholder Rights with offsetting strength on Board and Compensation pillars. **Practical effect**: most index funds vote against directors in protest but supervoting structure mechanically defeats protest votes; rating becomes informational rather than dispositive.

### 20. S&P 500 inclusion implications — IMPORTANT REVERSAL

S&P Dow Jones Indices' July 31, 2017 policy excluded multi-class companies from S&P Composite 1500 (S&P 500, MidCap 400, SmallCap 600).

**On April 17, 2023, S&P Dow Jones REVERSED this policy**: companies with multiple share classes again eligible for S&P Composite 1500 inclusion. **OpenAI Group PBC's foundation-control structure is no longer a categorical bar to S&P 500 inclusion.**

CII Executive Director Amy Borrus expressed displeasure ("surprised and disappointed"). FTSE Russell's separate restriction (5% public-vote-power threshold for Russell 3000) remains; whether OpenAI Group meets that depends on post-IPO public-share voting allocation.

### 21. SOX whistleblower / Dodd-Frank implications

- **SOX §806** (18 U.S.C. §1514A): protection for employees reporting fraud or SEC violations; reinstatement, back pay, special damages
- **Dodd-Frank §922** (15 U.S.C. §78u-6): bounty (10–30% of sanctions over $1M); *Digital Realty Trust v. Somers*, 138 S. Ct. 767 (2018) limited retaliation protection to those reporting to SEC
- **SOX §301**: mandatory audit committee whistleblower channels
- **PBC mission article** (DGCL §362(a)(1)) is public commitment whose breach could potentially form basis of §10(b) misrepresentation claim, though untested

**Strengthens mission-protection by giving employees federal legal protection for reporting mission-drift.**

## VII. STRATEGIC QUESTIONS

### 25. Strongest pre-IPO Foundation move for lever preservation

Dominant pattern: structural protection rooted in foundational/charter law that cannot be unilaterally amended, combined with permanent non-divestment of controlling instrument. Translated to OpenAI:

- **Re-paper Class N as a Novo Holdings-style intermediate holding company.** Separate Delaware non-stock corporation (or LLC) wholly owned by Foundation could hold Class N stake; certificate of incorporation of holding entity could prohibit alienation of Class N, mirror Novo Nordisk Foundation Articles' "shall not divest" provision, require AG approval for any structural change. **Triple-lock (Foundation Articles + Holding-Co charter + AG conditions) most closely replicates the Novo template.**
- **Permanent or very long lock-up commitments** (5–10 years) on any Class N or warrant-issued shares; charter prohibition on Class N → Class A conversion
- **Modified dual-class architecture** with Class N retaining supervoting rights and director-appointment, public Class A with economic and limited voting rights — **Carlsberg model**, cleanest from exchange-listing perspective and most precedent-tested
- **Irrevocable trust holding Class N** (Patagonia/Holdfast adapted): adds layer but introduces tax and 501(c)(3)/(c)(4) complications requiring IRS pre-clearance

**Most defensible move**: codify §7 prior-approval rights and director-appointment power directly in OpenAI Group PBC certificate of incorporation as supermajority/class-vote provisions, while Class N share class itself remains unlisted and fully held by Foundation or Foundation-controlled holding entity, subject to charter-level non-divestment language consistent with Foundation's IRS exempt-purpose obligations.

### 26. Worst pre-IPO Foundation moves

- **Selling at IPO to "diversify"**: triggers Hershey-2002 fact pattern; CA AG has parens patriae authority and would face same political incentive Mike Fisher faced
- **Standard 180-day lock-up converting to selling rights**: creates immediate post-IPO sale pressure, public-market valuation gravity well, §16(b) short-swing complications for Foundation directors who are also PBC directors
- **Allowing Class N to convert to common at IPO**: collapses supervoting protection. Forever.
- **Permitting Microsoft-style economic-interest transfers to erode voting control**: undoes "26% economics, 100% control" architecture

### 27. IPO valuation effect on Foundation governance

A $1T+ public valuation creates contradictory pressures:

**Strengthens** the Foundation's bargaining position internally because a 26% stake in a $1T entity = $260B liquid, making the Foundation the world's largest charitable endowment by a wide margin (ahead of Novo Nordisk Foundation at ~$220B and Bill & Melinda Gates Foundation Trust at ~$77B).

**Weakens** to the extent supervoting structure attracts persistent investor opposition; substantial minority of float held by passive index funds whose stewardship policies disfavor structure. Novo Nordisk demonstrates this is survivable.

**Creates dividend-distribution pressure**: at scale, public investors expect cash returns. Non-distribution policy could generate activist or class-action pressure under PBC fiduciary doctrine. *DGCL §365* requires PBC directors to balance pecuniary interests, public-benefit purpose, stakeholder interests; non-distribution policies should be defensible if explicitly tied to mission.

**Activist economics**: at $1T+, even a 1% activist position is $10B — enough to fund prolonged pressure campaigns even if mechanically defeated by Class N votes. Attention/PR risk more than control risk.

**Comparison to non-IPO alternatives**: private capital raise comparable to Microsoft 2025 deal could provide capital without public-market constraints, but at cost of liquidity for early investors and employees. **The IPO is therefore primarily an employee-and-investor liquidity event**; Foundation derives little incremental control benefit and accepts significant new disclosure/governance burden.
