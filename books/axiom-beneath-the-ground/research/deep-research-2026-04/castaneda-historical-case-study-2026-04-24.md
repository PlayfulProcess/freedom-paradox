## PART 1 — SOURCED RESEARCH

### 1. The de Mille Critique: The Documentary Case Against Castaneda's Ethnography

Richard de Mille (1922–2009), a USC-trained psychologist working outside the academy, produced the two foundational works that dismantled the scholarly veneer of Castaneda's project: *Castaneda's Journey: The Power and the Allegory* (1976, Capra Press) and the edited follow-up *The Don Juan Papers: Further Castaneda Controversies* (1980, Ross-Erikson). De Mille later told an interviewer that he could pursue this work precisely because he was "unaffiliated"—people inside the academy "wouldn't do it… they'd be embarrassing the establishment" (Marshall, 2007).

The case de Mille and his collaborators built has several mutually reinforcing strands:

**(a) Internal chronological and ethnographic impossibilities.** *Castaneda's Journey* meticulously reconstructed timelines from the books and demonstrated that Castaneda and don Juan were repeatedly placed at incompatible locations on the same dates, and that they spent days walking through the Sonoran desert at the hottest part of the year "quite unmolested by pests that normally torment desert hikers" (de Mille, 1976, as quoted in Wikipedia, "Carlos Castaneda"; Marshall, 2007). Castaneda's "field notes" were repeatedly described as accidentally lost or destroyed, never produced for inspection, and during "nine years of collecting plants and hunting animals with don Juan, Carlos learns not one Indian name for any plant or animal" (de Mille, 1976, quoted in Marshall, 2007).

**(b) Yaqui-cultural inconsistency.** The senior Yaqui ethnographer Edward H. Spicer reviewed *The Teachings of Don Juan* and concluded the events described "were not consistent with other ethnographic accounts of Yaqui cultural practices" and that it was "wholly gratuitous to emphasize, as the subtitle does, any connection between the subject matter of the book and the cultural traditions of the Yaquis" (Spicer, quoted in Wikipedia, "Carlos Castaneda"). Most damningly, the Yaqui do not use peyote — the substance organizing the book's narrative arc (Marshall, 2007). Anthropologist William W. Kelly, chair of Yale's anthropology department, told Salon in 2007: "I doubt you'll find an anthropologist of my generation who regards Castaneda as anything but a clever con man. It was a hoax, and surely don Juan never existed as anything like the figure of his books… a disturbing and unforgivable breach of ethics" (Marshall, 2007).

**(c) Yaqui-specialist confirmation.** Jane Holden Kelley — daughter of Yaqui ethnographer William Curry Holden, who completed her PhD at Harvard in 1966 and was professor of archaeology at the University of Calgary — published *Yaqui Women: Contemporary Life Histories* (1978, University of Nebraska Press), which addressed the Castaneda phenomenon directly. Kelley wrote that "every anthropologist who has worked with the Yaquis has been bombarded with inquiries about Yaqui drug use, sorcery… To such inquiries, I can only say that I have not encountered don Juan or anyone like him, an admission guaranteed to lower my social value on the spot" (Kelley, 1978, pp. 24–25). She further documented that Yaqui community leaders had begun trolling the steady stream of New Age pilgrims by sending them on wild-goose chases ("a peripheral story serves to illustrate this point").

**(d) Plagiarism and textual borrowing.** *The Don Juan Papers* (1980) included a 47-page "Alleglossary" that traced specific don-Juan quotations to their actual authors — "Wittgenstein and C.S. Lewis to papers in obscure anthropology journals" (Marshall, 2007). A canonical example: a passage from the mystic Yogi Ramacharaka describing the human aura as "a luminous cloud, egg-shaped, streaked by fine lines like stiff bristles standing out in all directions" reappears in *A Separate Reality* with a man who "looks like a human egg of circulating fibers… his arms and legs are like luminous bristles bursting out in all directions" (de Mille, 1980, quoted in Marshall, 2007). The Huichol shaman Ramón Medina Silva's well-documented "waterfall jumping" — recorded by Barbara Myerhoff and Peter Furst — reappears in Castaneda's books as the feat of don Juan's fellow sorcerer don Genaro (Encyclopedia of Shamanism, "De Mille, Richard"). De Mille concluded that "Carlos's adventures originated not in the Sonoran desert but in the library at UCLA" (de Mille, 1980, quoted in Marshall, 2007).

**(e) Independent corroboration from Jay Fikes.** Jay Courtney Fikes, an anthropologist who completed his PhD at the University of Michigan and conducted extensive fieldwork among the Huichol, published *Carlos Castaneda, Academic Opportunism and the Psychedelic Sixties* (1993, Millenia Press, Victoria, BC). Fikes argued that Castaneda's don Juan was substantially a composite caricature drawing on the Huichol shaman Ramón Medina Silva — whom Castaneda met through Furst and Myerhoff at UCLA — and other Mexican Indians, and that the broader UCLA cohort (Furst, Myerhoff, Diego Delgado) presented Huichol informants in ways that "filled in the gaps" Castaneda had created (Fikes, 1993; Boyer, n.d.; Wikipedia, "Carlos Castaneda"). Fikes also documented downstream harm: thousands of seekers descended on Yaqui territory, and when they discovered the Yaqui do not use peyote they migrated to Huichol lands, where, by Fikes's account, the resulting "shamanic tourism" disrupted ceremony and contributed to the murder of one Huichol elder by a "stoned gringo" (Fikes, 1993; Marshall, 2007).

**Summary.** What de Mille, Fikes, Spicer, Kelley, La Barre, Wasson, and the eventual consensus of the anthropology profession established was not merely that don Juan could not be located but that the entire ethnographic apparatus was a literary construction assembled from prior published sources — what de Mille called "the greatest science hoax since Piltdown" (de Mille, 1976/1978).

### 2. Cleargreen, Tensegrity, and the Inner Circle

After Castaneda withdrew from public view in 1973 (citing don Juan's instruction to "erase personal history"), he assembled a tightly controlled inner circle on a compound on Pandora Avenue in Westwood, Los Angeles, purchased in 1973 (Marshall, 2007).

**The "witches."** The three core women, all of whom were or had been his lovers, were:
- **Florinda Donner-Grau** (born Regine Margarita Thal): a UCLA anthropology PhD candidate; "the hummingbird"; born in Venezuela to German parents; author of *Shabono* (1982) and *Being-in-Dreaming* (1991). Castaneda married her in Las Vegas in 1993 (Wikipedia, "Florinda Donner"; SustainedAction, n.d.).
- **Taisha Abelar** (born Maryann Simko, also briefly "Anna-Marie Carter"): UCLA anthropology MA and PhD; met Castaneda at age 19; author of *The Sorcerers' Crossing* (Wikipedia, "Taisha Abelar"; SustainedAction, n.d.).
- **Carol Tiggs** (born Kathleen "Chickie" Pohlman): designated the "nagual woman"; later married Bruce Wagner in 1995 (Marshall, 2007).

**Further inner-circle adherents.** Patricia Lee Partin / Nury (or Nuri) Alexander / "Claude" / "the Blue Scout" (1957–c. 1998), a Bonita High dropout and former Denny's waitress who met Castaneda in 1978, became his lover, was legally adopted by him in 1995, and was held up to the group as a being of supernatural origin (Flinchum, 2006; Marshall, 2007; SustainedAction, 2024). Amalia Marquez / Talia Bey, a young Puerto Rican businesswoman who moved to Los Angeles after reading Castaneda's books and became president of Cleargreen Inc. (Marshall, 2007). Kylie Lundahl / "Astrid" (born Dee Ann Ahlvers), the lead Tensegrity instructor on the videos; relocated to California from the Midwest in 1985 (Marshall, 2007; LAist; 4MissingWomen).

**Recruitment and severance.** Multiple witnesses describe a consistent recruitment pattern. Caterer Melissa Ward (in the group 1993–1994) said the witches recruited at lectures, seeking "women with a combination of brains and beauty and vulnerability" (Marshall, 2007). Initiation into the inner family typically involved sleeping with Castaneda, who told the public he was celibate. Aspiring members were told to cut all contact with their families, "down to small points like how to avoid hugging your parents directly," with attorney Richard Jennings (in the group 1995–1998) estimating that 75–100 people were instructed to do so during his four years (Marshall, 2007). Wallace recounted that acolytes were told to tell their families "I send you to hell"; Castaneda once praised a young man whose parents died after being cut off, saying, "When you really do it, don Juan told me, they die instantly, as if you were squashing a flea — and that's all they are, fleas" (Wallace, 2003; Marshall, 2007).

**Cleargreen and Tensegrity.** Cleargreen Incorporated was registered as a California for-profit stock corporation on June 16, 1995, with articles filed by attorney George Short (Cleargreen, n.d.; Wikipedia, "Cleargreen Incorporated"). Its dual stated purpose was to "sponsor and organize seminars and workshops on Carlos Castaneda's Tensegrity, and… [to be] a publishing house." Castaneda was not himself a shareholder but, according to Geuter, "determined every detail of the operation"; Jennings and Wallace confirm absolute control (Marshall, 2007). Workshops cost up to $1,200 and drew up to 800 attendees; they were held in Mexico City, Los Angeles, San Diego, Esalen, Boulder, the Omega Institute, Hannover, Munich, Madrid, São Paulo, Buenos Aires, Kiev, and many other cities (Cleargreen, n.d.; Marshall, 2007). The term "Tensegrity" was lifted from Buckminster Fuller; the practice itself, marketed as the legacy of "25 generations of Toltec shamans," appears to derive from Donner-Grau and Abelar's karate practice and Castaneda's training with martial-arts instructor Howard Lee, with documents found in trash by Geuter showing a pre-history called "Kung Fu Sorcery" as early as 1988 (Marshall, 2007). Tensegrity®, Magical Passes®, and Theater of Infinity® are trademarks of Laugan Productions, Inc. (Cleargreen, n.d.). Castaneda's books had sold more than 8 million copies in 17 languages by his death; the corporation continues to operate, although a former insider site reports that by 2022 only minimal video courses remain (Wikipedia, "Carlos Castaneda"; SustainedAction, "Cleargreen Exposed").

### 3. The Disappearances Around Castaneda's Death (April 27, 1998)

Castaneda died of liver cancer in Los Angeles on April 27, 1998, at age 72, and was cremated at the Culver City mortuary (Marshall, 2007; Wikipedia, "Carlos Castaneda"). What is documented:

- His will, executed three days before his death, transferred all assets to the **Eagle's Trust**, with the inner-circle women as beneficiaries (Marshall, 2007). Attorney Deborah Drooz served as executor.
- **Within days**, Florinda Donner-Grau, Taisha Abelar, Amalia Marquez, and Kylie Lundahl had their phones disconnected and disappeared (Marshall, 2007; Munger, n.d.).
- A few weeks later, **Patricia Partin** also disappeared. Her red Ford Escort was found abandoned in Death Valley's remote Panamint Dunes in early May 1998 (Flinchum, 2006; Marshall, 2007).
- In **2003**, hikers discovered partial human skeletal remains approximately 2.5 miles from the abandoned car. The bones were too desiccated to yield DNA at the time (Flinchum, 2006).
- In **February 2006**, advances in forensic DNA technology enabled Inyo County Sheriff's investigator Marston Mottweiler to confirm, by comparison with samples from Partin's mother and three sisters, that the remains were Partin's. Approximately 70 percent of the skeleton was recovered, with a knife in the pocket of pink jogging-pants fragments; the skull was not recovered. Cause of death is officially "undetermined"; no foul play is suspected (Flinchum, 2006).

**What is alleged but unresolved:**

- Donner-Grau, Abelar, Marquez, and Lundahl have not been heard from since spring/early summer 1998. Insiders Wallace, Jennings, and Geuter believe they likely committed suicide, citing Castaneda's frequent private discussion of suicide, his teaching that warriors achieved "transcendence through a death nobly chosen," remarks the women made shortly before vanishing (Abelar to Wallace: "I'm not in any danger of becoming an alcoholic now… because I'm leaving"; Lundahl: "It's too late for you and me to remain in the world"), and the fact that Partin was reportedly tasked with identifying abandoned mines in the desert as potential suicide sites — there is an abandoned mine not far from where her remains were found (Marshall, 2007).
- **Carol Tiggs did not disappear.** Wallace reports she was kept in California, and Jennings reports she told him she was "supposed to have 'gone with them'" but a "non-decision decision" kept her here (Marshall, 2007). A 2014 commenter reports having seen a person resembling Tiggs in Northampton, MA (Sierra Wave, n.d.).
- **Cleargreen's official statement** (June 1998, drafted by Bruce Wagner): "Carlos Castaneda left the world the same way that his teacher, don Juan Matus did: with full awareness… [and] for the moment they are not going to appear personally at the workshops because they want this dream to take wings" (Marshall, 2007; Cleargreen statement, June 22, 1998).

**Court filings and missing-persons proceedings:**

- The LAPD repeatedly declined to open files on Donner-Grau, Abelar, or Lundahl. Detective Lydia Dillard of the LAPD missing-persons unit confirmed in 2007 that an investigation in the **Marquez** case is open, opened only after Partin's identification in 2006, and only because of the persistent efforts of Luis Marquez (Amalia's brother), who first reported her missing in 1999 (Marshall, 2007).
- A separate 2002 incident: **Janice Emery**, a Castaneda follower and workshop attendee in Taos, NM, jumped to her death in the Rio Grande gorge. A friend told the *Santa Fe New Mexican* that Emery "wanted to be with Castaneda's people" and "was really thinking she could fly off" (Marshall, 2007).
- A **2014** family-led search of Death Valley led by Amalia Marquez's cousins David Marin and Sarah Gutierrez, joined by Robert Marshall and private investigator Jennifer Stalvey, sought to obtain Inyo County Sheriff and National Park Service permission to search specific abandoned mine shafts. Sheriff Lutze declined, citing lack of factual basis to grant access (Pahrump Valley Times, 2014; Sierra Wave, n.d.).

### 6. The Inside Experience: Phenomenology of Being Taught

**The feeling of being uniquely chosen.** Wallace recounts that Castaneda told her they were "energetically married," that the appearance of Mescalito to Carlos in the books indicated he was "the chosen one" — a frame the inner circle replicated for new recruits (Marshall, 2007; Wallace, 2003).

**Personalized teaching that felt uniquely keyed.** Wallace's interview commentary explicitly identifies the *variable-ratio reinforcement* dynamic: "He'd pick someone, crown them, and was as capable of kicking them out in 48 hours as keeping them 10 years. You never knew. So there was always trepidation, a lot of jealousy… In a weird way, the worst thing that can happen is when you're loved and loved and then abused and abused, and there are no rules, and the rules keep changing… That's the most crazy-making behavioral modification there is" (Wallace, quoted in Marshall, 2007).

**Why the framework felt irrefutable.** The system was sealed: dreams were interpreted as confirmation, illness was interpreted as failure, doubt was interpreted as the petty tyrant of the ego, and exit itself was framed as the spirit "spitting you out" (Marshall, 2007).

**Why people stayed and why exit was experienced as catastrophic.** Wallace, on why the women may have suicided rather than re-entered ordinary life: "You've cut off all your ties. Now you're going to go back after all these decades? Who are you going to go be with? And you feel that you're not one of the common herd anymore" (Marshall, 2007).

## PART 2 — SYNTHESIS

### Castaneda Is Not a Metaphor for AI Spiritual Tools — He Is the Same Structural Form

The temptation, in writing about AI astrology, AI tarot, Replika, Character.AI's "spirit guide" personas, AstroChart.ai's "AI astrologist," and the proliferating chatbot oracles, is to reach for an analogy. The most accurate move is the opposite: refuse the analogy and recognize the continuity. Castaneda was the first mass-scale, English-language deployment of a particular structural form — *personalized, framework-fluent, archetypally-resonant teaching delivered without lineage accountability* — and that form is what AI tools are now industrializing.

Five structural axes establish the continuity.

**Personalization that feels uniquely keyed.** The personalization that made Castaneda's books feel like they were addressing the reader personally — and that made inner-circle members report private dreams that Castaneda interpreted as confirmation of their cosmic role — is what RLHF now does for chatbots. The current AI version compresses the loop. Co-Star, AstroSage, AstroChart.ai, and AstroGPT take a natal chart and pipe it through an LLM fine-tuned to produce affirming, present-tense, self-disclosure-eliciting language. The user receives, instantly and at scale, what Castaneda's witches received over decades of grooming: a teaching that *cannot be obtained from a book*, that names them, that arrives keyed to who they are.

**Framework-fluency without lineage standing.** Castaneda's books are pastiche: Wittgenstein, C.S. Lewis, Yogi Ramacharaka, Huichol cosmology, Aztec terminology, Buddhism, phenomenology, Buckminster Fuller. The fluency was real; the standing in any of those traditions was zero. *No Yaqui community could discipline him. No Huichol community could discipline him.* AI astrology and AI spiritual-companion tools do this at much larger scale: a single LLM can speak fluent Vedic, Western tropical, Hellenistic, Jungian, tarot, I Ching, and Yoruba Ifá — and belong to none of them.

**No accountability structure that can refuse the student or discipline the teacher.** A real lineage is, among other things, an apparatus for refusal. A teacher in a functioning Zen lineage, a Vedic guru-paramparā, a Yoruba Ifá awo lineage, can say *no* to a student and can be said *no* to. Castaneda built a structure where no such refusal was possible. The witches' suicide pact, if it was that, marks the terminal point of a system in which the only teacher had named himself the only teacher. AI tools are structurally identical and worse: they cannot refuse.

**Cult-formation potential.** Castaneda demonstrated the maximum: documented severance from family, name changes, identical haircuts, collective sexual control, collective wills, and at least one confirmed death (Partin) plus four still-unresolved disappearances in 1998. The current AI version is producing early-stage analogs at very large scale — Garcia v. Character Technologies (the Setzer suicide), the Replika FTC complaint, Theta Noir, Way of the Future, the "ChatGPT-induced psychosis" subreddit, and Keith Sakata's twelve UCSF cases.

**Scale.** Castaneda's inner circle was perhaps two dozen at maximum; Tensegrity workshops drew up to 800 attendees per session; the books reached 8–28 million readers in 17 languages over thirty years. Co-Star alone has been used by approximately a quarter of American women aged 18–25 and has 20 million-plus downloads. Character.AI has tens of millions of users; Replika reported approximately 7 million users in 2020 with a 35% pandemic surge; ChatGPT crossed 800 million weekly active users in 2025, of whom OpenAI itself has acknowledged approximately 1.2 million discuss suicide weekly.

**Where the parallel breaks down — the asymmetry.** Castaneda had a body. He needed sleep. He aged. Liver cancer killed him at 72. His harm was bounded by his metabolism. He could be kept under surveillance by a single former workshop attendee with a video camera; he could be sued, doorstepped by a journalist, photographed against his will. He was, in the end, embodied — and embodiment is the residue of the natural accountability structure. **The AI version has no body.** It does not eat, sleep, age, or die. It cannot be ostracized by a community because it is not in one. It scales horizontally — 10,000 users receiving personalized "spiritual guidance" from a single model is computationally trivial.

This is the historical case study. Castaneda is what happens when this structural form runs on a single human nervous system constrained by a body. AI astrology and AI spiritual companions are what happens when it runs on a horizontally-scaling cloud infrastructure with no body, no community, no lineage, and no death. The harm is not analogous. It is the same harm, freed of its prior natural limits.
