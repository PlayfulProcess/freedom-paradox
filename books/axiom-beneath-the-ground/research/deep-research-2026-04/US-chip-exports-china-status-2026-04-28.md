## Bottom Line for the Essay's Claim

The user's claim that **"Trump is selling chips to China for tariff concessions"** is **directionally accurate but requires important nuance** as written. Three points:

1. The U.S. is unambiguously running both a "hawk" policy (export controls remain on the books, with case-by-case licensing) and a "rentier" policy (a U.S. government cut of every China-bound chip sale) **simultaneously**. The structural argument the essay wants to make is well-supported.
2. The deal's official quid pro quo is **revenue capture** (15% on H20, 25% on H200, implemented via a Section 232 import-tariff workaround) — not directly "tariff concessions" as the phrase is normally used.
3. Chip access has nonetheless been **woven into the broader U.S.-China trade negotiation** (alongside tariff truces, rare-earth supply, soybean purchases, and TikTok), so calling it part of a "tariff/trade deal" package is defensible. A tighter formulation would be: *"Trump is selling chips to China in exchange for a U.S. government revenue cut and as part of a broader tariff and rare-earth bargain."*

## 1. Current Status of H20 Exports (April 2026)

The H20 — Nvidia's Hopper-generation chip designed specifically to comply with Biden-era thresholds — has been through a complete policy cycle:

- **April 9, 2025**: Commerce Department's Bureau of Industry and Security (BIS) sent Nvidia an "is-informed" letter requiring licenses for H20 exports to China. Nvidia took a **$4.5 billion charge** on excess inventory and lost an estimated **$2.5 billion** in unshipped Q1 FY26 revenue (per Nvidia's own Q1 FY26 8-K).
- **July 2025**: Trump administration reversed course; BIS began reviewing H20 licenses again.
- **August 11, 2025**: At a White House press conference, Trump announced Nvidia (and AMD, for its MI308) had agreed to remit **15% of revenue** from licensed China sales to the U.S. government in exchange for export approvals. Trump said he had originally asked for a higher share.
- **August 27, 2025 (Q2 FY26 earnings call)**: Nvidia CFO Colette Kress confirmed "USG officials have expressed an expectation that the USG will receive 15%" but stated "to date, the USG has not published a regulation codifying such a requirement." Nvidia made **zero H20 sales** in Q2 FY26 despite a "select number" of customer licenses being granted; its 10-Q flagged constitutional risk under the Export Clause's prohibition on export taxes.
- **As of April 2026**: The 15% H20 arrangement remains operative but has been substantially overtaken by the more important H200 deal (below). Reports from late 2025 indicated Beijing instructed Chinese firms to avoid the H20 in favor of domestic alternatives, blunting demand.

## 2. The H200 Deal — The Major 2025–2026 Development

This is the single most important development the essay must reflect:

- **December 8, 2025**: Trump publicly announced (and Truth Social-confirmed) that the U.S. would allow Nvidia to ship the **H200** — its **second-most-powerful Hopper chip**, *not* a China-specific downgrade — to "approved customers" in China, with **25% of revenues** going to the U.S. government. Trump said he had informed Xi and that "President Xi responded positively." The CNBC report attributes the original Semafor scoop.
- **January 14, 2026**: Trump signed a **Section 232 proclamation imposing a 25% tariff** on a narrow category of advanced AI chips (named in the fact sheet: Nvidia H200, AMD MI325X) when imported into the United States for re-export. This is the legal mechanism: because the Constitution's Export Clause (Art. I, §9) bars export taxes, the administration structured the 25% as an **import tariff on Taiwan-made chips passing through U.S. testing facilities** before reaching China. (Reported by Bloomberg, CNBC, Axios, Irish Times, and TechCrunch on Jan 14–15, 2026.)
- **Conditions** in the BIS rule (revised January 13, 2026): case-by-case licensing; customers must meet security requirements; mandatory third-party U.S. testing of every chip before shipment; **shipments to China capped at 50%** of the same product line shipped to U.S. customers; net effect: not allowed to "reduce global semiconductor production capacity available to U.S. customers."
- **Late February 2026**: BIS issued the first H200 export licenses to Nvidia (reported by Bloomberg's Ed Ludlow; confirmed by Caixin and Nvidia).
- **March 17, 2026**: Jensen Huang at Nvidia's GTC conference confirmed: *"We've been licensed for many customers in China for H200. We have received purchase orders from many customers and we're in the process of restarting our manufacturing."* He estimated the China AI-chip opportunity at ~$50 billion annually. Nvidia simultaneously committed to opening additional H200 production capacity.
- **As of mid-April 2026**: Per Tom's Hardware (April 13, 2026, citing Bloomberg): **Nvidia has not yet shipped a single H200 to China** despite the licenses, due to two compounding bottlenecks: (a) BIS has lost ~20% of its licensing staff and is processing each license individually under Under Secretary Jeffrey Kessler's personal sign-off; (b) Chinese customs authorities reportedly received instructions not to admit H200 shipments — a soft retaliation tactic. The planned **Trump–Xi April 2026 Beijing summit was pushed to mid-May 2026** because of the Iran war drawing administration attention.

## 3. Blackwell / B-Series Status

- **Late August 2025**: Reuters reported Nvidia developing a **B30A** — a single-die Blackwell-Ultra-based China SKU with ~50% of B300 performance, 144 GB HBM3e, retaining NVLink. Trump publicly said he could approve a Blackwell "30–50% off" the top SKU.
- **Nvidia's official position (multiple statements through late 2025)**: A spokesperson told *The Wire China* (Nov 16, 2025): *"There is no product called B30A planned, designed, or produced,"* adding that "degraded export-compliant products cannot succeed in the China market." This was widely read as posturing pressure on the administration to allow a less-degraded Blackwell. The Institute for Progress' November 2025 analysis treated the B30A as a stand-in for "any Blackwell chip ~50% less powerful than the top SKU."
- **As of April 2026**: No Blackwell-class export to China has been formally approved. The H200 (Hopper-generation) remains the most advanced chip approved for China. Commerce Secretary Howard Lutnick told CNBC in late 2025 that the president "will decide how he wants to play it." A B30A or downgraded Blackwell remains under review but not greenlit.

## 4. The Tariff/Trade-Deal Linkage

The chip decisions have been formally and informally bundled with broader U.S.-China trade negotiations:

- **October 30, 2025 (Busan, Korea, sideline to APEC)**: Trump and Xi met for the first time in his second term. Trump rated the meeting "twelve out of ten." The truce package: U.S. cut tariffs on Chinese imports; China agreed to a one-year pause on rare-earth export controls and to resume U.S. soybean purchases; both sides paused planned escalations. The Atlantic Council's experts noted that **chips were discussed but the U.S. publicly held the line on advanced chip controls at Busan** — the H200 reversal came roughly five weeks later.
- **The H20 deal (July–August 2025)** was widely described in contemporaneous reporting (Reuters, FT, The Conversation) as **linked to U.S. negotiations over rare-earth access**, not strictly to tariff levels. The Tax Foundation's tariff tracker treats the 15% as a *de facto* "export tariff" implemented via the Section 232 import-tariff structure to avoid Export Clause challenges.
- **February 2026**: The U.S. Supreme Court struck down portions of Trump's tariff regime (CNBC, Feb 23, 2026), weakening U.S. leverage going into the (then-planned) April Beijing summit and reportedly increasing pressure on the administration to use chip access as a sweetener. Trump responded with a 10–15% global tariff under Section 122 of the Trade Act of 1974.
- **Spring 2026**: U.S.-China talks in Paris reportedly produced the outline of a "Board of Trade" framework. Foreign Policy's April 27, 2026 analysis judged that on advanced chips specifically, "substantive agreement is unlikely" but "clearer signaling and red lines" are possible at the rescheduled summit (now mid-May).

## 5. Saudi Arabia (Humain) and UAE (G42) — The Comparison Case

The "rentier" pole the essay analyzes is alive and accelerating:

- **May 13, 2025 (Riyadh, U.S.–Saudi Investment Forum, during Trump's Gulf trip)**: Nvidia and **Humain** (the PIF-owned Saudi AI venture chaired by MbS) announced an initial **18,000 GB300 Grace Blackwell** supercomputer, with "several hundred thousand" GPUs over five years across up to 500 MW of capacity. AMD signed a parallel deal worth several hundred MW. The Trump administration simultaneously announced it would scrap Biden's tiered "AI diffusion rule" (which had placed Saudi Arabia in Tier 2).
- **November 2025 (Washington)**: Humain expansion announced — **up to 600,000 Nvidia AI systems over three years**, including GB300 and successor platforms. xAI committed to its first non-U.S. data center, a 500+ MW Saudi facility. AWS committed an additional ~150,000 GPUs in a Riyadh AI Zone.
- **Late 2025**: WSJ reported the U.S. approved the sale of **up to 35,000 GB300 servers to G42 (UAE) and Humain combined** following Trump's discussions with MbS and UAE leadership, with security provisions to prevent diversion to China or Huawei.
- **First-shipment status (early 2026)**: Humain has reportedly received its first shipment of GB300 systems. Per the same Tom's Hardware April 13 reporting, however, **Middle East licensing also faces BIS bottlenecks** — Cerebras and Nvidia approvals for Saudi/UAE were conditioned on dollar-for-dollar U.S. investment matching, making each license individually negotiated.
- **Bottom line**: The Gulf pipeline remains fundamentally on track and is dramatically larger in volume than the China H200 channel — Humain alone is contracted for many multiples of what China is being permitted.

## 6. The "Hawk + Rentier Simultaneously" Argument — Verified

As of late April 2026, the United States is demonstrably running both policies at once:

- **Hawk track**: BIS export-license regime remains in effect. Each H200 sale to China requires a case-by-case license personally signed off by an Under Secretary. Blackwell (B100/B200/B300/GB300) remains banned for China. Mandatory U.S. testing of every chip; 50%-of-U.S.-shipments cap; security-vetted end-users only. In November 2025 China itself reportedly told publicly-owned data center firms to source >50% of chips domestically, suggesting the controls are still meaningfully constraining.
- **Rentier track**: The U.S. government takes **15% of H20 China revenue** and **25% of H200 China revenue** (the latter codified via the January 2026 Section 232 proclamation). Trump has publicly hinted that AMD, Intel, and a future Blackwell variant could be brought into the same revenue-sharing structure. This is, as the American Action Forum and multiple constitutional-law commentators have noted, an **unprecedented arrangement** with no clean statutory analog and pending legal-vulnerability questions under the Export Clause.

The same administration is, in parallel, demanding that Gulf states route all advanced chips through U.S.-vetted entities (Humain, G42), build with U.S. cloud partners (AWS, Oracle), match dollar-for-dollar with U.S. investment, and exclude Huawei and Chinese personnel — i.e., it is asking Riyadh and Abu Dhabi to choose the hawk lane while operating both lanes at home.
