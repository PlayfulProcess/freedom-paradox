## Section 1 — Courage as ontological act

Tillich defines courage as **"the self-affirmation of being in spite of the fact of non-being"** — the ontological rather than ethical sense of the term. The compact canonical formulation appears in Chapter V ("Courage and Individualization"), *Courage to Be* Yale ed., p. 152. In the surrounding text Tillich characterizes courage as the act of the individual self that takes the anxiety of non-being into itself, affirming itself either as part of an embracing whole or in its individual selfhood. The definition is prepared in Chapter I ("Being and Courage," pp. 1–31), which traces the ontological reading of courage through Plato, Aquinas, the Stoics, Spinoza, and Nietzsche, and is sharpened against non-being in Chapter II (pp. 32–63). The argument: courage is not one virtue among others but the self-affirmative act by which finite being holds itself against the threat of its own negation.

## Section 2 — Absolute faith and the "God above God"

Chapter VI, "Courage and Transcendence," pp. 155–190, closes the book. The subsection "Absolute faith and the courage to be" begins at p. 171. The final section, "The God above God and the courage to be," spans pp. 186–190.

The decisive formulation: **"Theism in all its forms is transcended in the experience we have called absolute faith. It is the accepting of the acceptance without somebody or something that accepts"** (p. 185 in the Yale ed.; secondary sources variously cite pp. 179 or 185 — `[RESEARCH NEEDED: verify the exact page against a physical copy of the Yale 2000 printing]`).

Surrounding argument (paraphrased): when the God of theism is dissolved in radical doubt and meaninglessness, what remains is not atheism but a faith with no delimitable object — the power of being-itself that accepts and gives the courage to be.

The book's final sentence, p. 190: "The courage to be is rooted in the God who appears when God has disappeared in the anxiety of doubt" (20 words; fair use).

## Section 3 — The three anxieties and their historical periods

Chapter II, "Being, Nonbeing, and Anxiety" (pp. 32–63), lays out the tripartite structure.

**Types of Anxiety** (section beginning p. 40):
- **Fate and death** — ontic, pp. 40–44
- **Emptiness and meaninglessness** — spiritual, pp. 46–51
- **Guilt and condemnation** — moral, pp. 51–54

Each type can be relative or absolute; all three are existential, not pathological.

**Periods of Anxiety** (section pp. 57–63) correlates each type with a historical endpoint:
- Fate/death dominates the end of antiquity
- Guilt/condemnation the end of the Middle Ages (Reformation crisis)
- **Emptiness/meaninglessness the end of the modern period** — Tillich's explicit claim that meaninglessness is the signature anxiety of his own era

The thematic order in Ch. II (ontic → spiritual → moral) differs from the chronological order in "Periods of Anxiety"; cite accordingly. The thesis is restated in Ch. VI as the setting for absolute faith.

## Section 4 — Biographical context

Tillich was ordained in the Evangelical Church of the Prussian Union in 1912 and served as a German army chaplain on the Western Front from **1914 to 1918**, receiving the Iron Cross in 1918. Uncontested across the standard sources (Pauck & Pauck, *Paul Tillich: His Life and Thought*, vol. I, esp. pp. 40–56 covering the war period).

The Paucks characterize Tillich at the start of the war as **"a shy, grown boy, truly a 'dreaming innocent'"** — a Prussian patriot whose worldview did not survive the trenches (`[RESEARCH NEEDED: exact page within Pauck & Pauck vol. I, Ch. 3 war chapter pp. 40–56]`).

**Verdun** is identified as the site of Tillich's most severe trauma. Samuel Shearn, *Pastor Tillich: The Justification of the Doubter* (Oxford UP, 2022), p. 182 and the Cambridge *Journal of Ecclesiastical History* article on Tillich's war sermons (2024) corroborate the Paucks. Ted Farris, Tillich's grandson, writes in *Aeon* (2019): "His traumatic experiences at Verdun and elsewhere on the Western Front led to two nervous breakdowns" (16 words; fair use with attribution).

Tillich's own account in *On the Boundary* (Scribner rev. ed. 1966, pp. 32–37, `[RESEARCH NEEDED: verify against 1966 printing]`) describes the war as the collapse of inherited idealism. He writes that a world war and a political catastrophe were required before he could affirm belief in democratic ideals and social revolution (paraphrase; cf. also "Autobiographical Reflections," in Kegley & Bretall, eds., *The Theology of Paul Tillich*, 1952, pp. 7–8).

The word "shattered" is idiomatic in secondary literature (Farris, *Aeon*; *New World Encyclopedia*); Tillich's own terms are closer to "transformation" and "collapse of the nineteenth-century worldview." The book should use "shattered" advisedly or prefer Tillich's own language.

## Section 5 — Absolute faith as content-less

The most emphatic statement occurs in *Courage to Be* Ch. VI at p. 171: absolute faith is "a faith which has been deprived by doubt of any concrete content, which nevertheless is faith" (16 words, fair use). Tillich continues (pp. 176–177) that this faith has no special content, is undirected, absolute, dissolved by doubt of every determinate object.

In *Dynamics of Faith* (Harper & Row, 1957), Ch. I ("What Faith Is," pp. 1–4), Tillich formally defines faith as **"the state of being ultimately concerned"** (p. 4). The *content* of faith matters infinitely to the believer but not to the formal definition of faith itself. Chapter III ("Symbols of Faith," pp. 41–54, esp. pp. 44–46) supplies the complementary anti-idolatry principle: any finite symbol elevated to ultimacy is idolatrous, because (paraphrased) God transcends God's own name.

## Section 6 — The Protestant Principle

Clearest formulation in *The Protestant Era* (Chicago, 1948), Author's Introduction, **p. viii**: **"Protestantism as a principle is eternal and a permanent criterion of everything temporal"** (13 words, fair use).

The developed statement — the principle as critical negation of every claim to ultimacy — appears in Chapter XI ("The Protestant Principle and the Proletarian Situation," p. 163): the Protestant Principle contains the divine and human protest against any absolute claim made for a relative reality, even one made by a Protestant church.

In *Systematic Theology* vol. III (Chicago, 1963), Part IV, §II.B.4 ("The Conquest of Religion by the Spiritual Presence and the Protestant Principle," beginning p. 245), Tillich embeds the principle in his mature system and argues it must be united with "Catholic substance."

## Section 7 — Notes for the book's argument: the Śūnya → Cit pivot

Two structural mappings are supported by the citations above.

**First:** Tillich's courage as self-affirmation of being in spite of non-being (p. 152) is an act that stands only by taking the abyss of non-being into itself — being without substantial ground. This matches the Tantrik **Śūnya** layer (layer 4 of the five-layered self): the transcendent Void through which identification with any finite identity-content is suspended. Tillich's insistence that courage is *ontological*, not ethical, parallels the Tantrik claim that Śūnya is not a psychological state but a structural feature of the self revealed when identification with the prior sheaths is withdrawn.

**Second:** Tillich's absolute faith — the accepting of the acceptance without somebody or something that accepts (p. 185) — is faith purged of every determinate object (p. 171; *Dynamics of Faith* p. 4). What remains is the "power of being-itself." This structurally mirrors the Tantric recognition of **Cit** (layer 5): Consciousness prior to any object, the self-luminous subject that cannot be objectified because it is the condition for any object whatever.

**The Protestant Principle** (Author's Intro. p. viii; *Protestant Era* p. 163) is the critical engine driving the move from Śūnya to Cit. It enforces the refusal to treat any finite form — including "God" conceived as a being — as the final term. This is the formal counterpart of the Tantric svātantrya's self-negating recognition that no object can be the subject.

**The pivot line, rendered synthetically for the book:** courage from the side of the finite self and svātantrya from the side of totality converge at this hinge — absolute faith as recognition without content, Śūnya opening into Cit.
