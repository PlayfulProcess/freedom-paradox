# Research 03: Tillich's Position Within Existentialism and Theology

**Source:** Deep research compiled by PlayfulProcess
**Serves:** The Axiom Beneath the Ground, Ch 2 (Tillich's Courage), Ch 6 (Tillich-Wallis Dialogue)

---

## The Boundary Figure

Tillich: "The concept of the boundary might be the fitting symbol for the whole of my personal and intellectual development. At almost every point, I have had to stand between alternative possibilities of existence."

He is neither purely philosopher nor purely theologian. His existentialism is neither atheistic nor conventionally theistic. Lived on every boundary: theology/philosophy, church/society, religion/culture, Europe/America, idealism/Marxism.

## Key Relationships

- **Schelling**: deepest philosophical influence. Both dissertations on Schelling. The identity principle (unity of subject and object in the Absolute) → Tillich's ontology of God as Ground of Being.
- **Heidegger**: colleague at Marburg 1924-25. At least thirteen Heideggerian terms in Tillich's work. But Tillich insists the question must "begin and stay with being," objecting to Heidegger's emphasis on nothingness.
- **Kierkegaard**: existential psychology (anxiety, despair, leap of faith) shaped understanding of estrangement.
- **Nietzsche**: agreed theological theism "deserved to be killed" while pointing to God above God.
- **Barth**: great rival. Barth insisted theology from revelation alone; Tillich demanded engagement with philosophy/culture. "The message must be thrown at those in the situation — thrown like a stone" (Tillich's characterization of Barth's method).

## The Method of Correlation

"Theology formulates the questions implied in human existence, and theology formulates the answers implied in divine self-manifestation under the guidance of the questions implied in human existence."

Existentialism provides analysis of human predicament; answers to the questions are religious. "In so doing, existentialism is a natural ally of Christianity."

Avoids both supranaturalism (answers thrown like stones) AND naturalistic liberalism (answers derived from human nature alone).

## God as Being-Itself

"God does not exist. He is being-itself beyond essence and existence. Therefore to argue that God exists is to deny him."

If God were a being alongside other beings, even the supreme being, God would be finite. Instead: Ground of Being, preceding the subject-object dichotomy.

"The name of this infinite and inexhaustible depth and ground of all being is God. That depth is what the word God means. And if that word has not much meaning for you, translate it, and speak of the depths of your life, of the source of your being, of your ultimate concern."

## The God Above God

"Theological theism deprives me of my subjectivity because He is all-powerful and all-knowing... This is the God Nietzsche said had to be killed because nobody can tolerate being made into a mere object of absolute knowledge and absolute control. This is the deepest root of atheism."

"The courage to be is rooted in the God who appears when God has disappeared in the anxiety of doubt."

## Faith Includes Doubt

"Doubt is not the opposite of faith; it is one element of faith."

"Not only he who is in sin but also he who is in doubt is justified through faith... There is faith in every serious doubt, namely, the faith in the truth as such, even if the only truth we can express is our lack of truth."

"Faith includes an element of immediate awareness which gives certainty and an element of uncertainty. To accept this is courage."

## Grace Strikes Us

From "You Are Accepted" sermon:

"Grace strikes us when we are in great pain and restlessness... Sometimes at that moment a wave of light breaks into our darkness, and it is as though a voice were saying: 'You are accepted. You are accepted, accepted by that which is greater than you, and the name of which you do not know. Do not ask for the name now... Simply accept the fact that you are accepted!'"

## The Protestant Principle

Every finite reality that claims unconditional authority must be protested — including Protestantism itself. "Protestantism as a principle is eternal and a permanent criterion of everything temporal."

"History has shown that the most terrible crimes against love have been committed in the name of fanatically defended doctrines."

## Personal Life and the Wound

War chaplain, WWI. Buried closest friend "in the mud of France." Three hospitalizations for nervous breakdowns. His mature theology described as "post-traumatic."

His grandson: "the existential question of our ultimate concern and the existential answer of the Christian message are and always have been predominant in my spiritual life."

## For the Axiom Book

**Tillich IS the axiom position.** He names the ground as chosen, not given. He says "absolute faith" is "without the safety of words and concepts, without a name, a church, a cult, a theology." He refuses to make faith comfortable. He insists that doubt is built into faith, not eliminated by it.

**The Protestant Principle IS a grammar of responsibility.** It says: you may have fire, but you must never worship the flame. Every concrete form must be continuously negated — including the form of the negation. This is the structural equivalent of Wallis's "all models dissolve."

**The convergence:** Both Tillich and Wallis arrive at "all constructs must be transcended." The divergence: Tillich says you transcend into the void with courage. Wallis says you transcend into the full void with recognition.
