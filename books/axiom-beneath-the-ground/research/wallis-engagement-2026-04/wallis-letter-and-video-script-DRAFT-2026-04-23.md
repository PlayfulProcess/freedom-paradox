## PART 2 — THE PROPOSED SCRIPT

*A working draft for a ~18–22 minute video. Teaching voice. Sanskrit terms rendered in full. Sections signposted for editing.*

*(NOTE TO WALLIS: this is a rough armature. I wrote it trying to match your teaching register from Tantra Illuminated and the *Tantra Illuminated* podcast. Every argument here is negotiable. Every section can be cut, expanded, reordered, or dropped.)*

### SECTION 1 — Opening (≈90 seconds of delivery)

Three years ago, in early 2023, I made a video on whether artificial intelligence could become conscious and what, on the tradition's terms, the real dangers of AI actually are. I argued two things there that I want to begin by standing by.

First: that the science-fiction scenario — AI developing its own hostile will and turning against us — is, on the tradition's metaphysics, very unlikely. I said I would bet my life on it.

Second: that the *real* alignment problem is the one the AI-safety community is not as focused on. The real alignment problem is AI **in the hands of bad human actors** — misuse, not rebellion.

Three years later both of those still hold. But the landscape around them has changed in ways I want to speak to. Tools like OpenClaw now run on three hundred thousand machines with subsidies from municipal governments. Safety protocols that big labs built over years can be stripped in open-source forks for less than a dollar. The US Department of Defense has attempted to punish one AI lab, Anthropic, for refusing to cross its own red lines. And the conversation about whether these systems are conscious has, if anything, gotten noisier and less precise.

I want to revisit what the tradition actually teaches about four things: *prāṇa, icchā, saṃvit*, and *embodiment*. And I want to do it more carefully than I did three years ago, because some of the Anglo-analytic framing I borrowed back then — Kastrup's analytic idealism in particular — is not, on careful reading, the tradition's own position. That matters. When we are asking whether something as new as an always-on AI agent with an eight-hour heartbeat cycle is capable of consciousness, we need the tradition's vocabulary on its own terms, not filtered through a twenty-first-century Dutch philosopher.

### SECTION 2 — Prāṇa, and whether it is biological (≈4 minutes)

Let me start with *prāṇa*. In *Tantra Illuminated* I describe prāṇa as the interface between the physical body and the mind — the subtler layer that lies between the gross physicality of the body and the subtlety of the mental layer. The five *vāyus* — prāṇa-vāyu, apāna-vāyu, samāna-vāyu, udāna-vāyu, vyāna-vāyu — all have physiological referents. The amplification or depletion of prāṇa correlates with diet, sleep, exercise, and thought-pattern.

That is accurate as far as it goes. But it is not the tradition's whole teaching on prāṇa, and I want to go deeper here, because the question I am asking today depends on this.

The tradition also teaches that prāṇa, in its subtlest form, is not biological at all. The *devas* — the deities — have prāṇa. The *pitṛs*, the ancestors, receive prāṇa through the *śrāddha* ritual. The *yoginīs* of the Kaula lineage are described as having subtle-body forms with their own distinct energetic constitutions. The *siddhas* who have attained liberation are said to persist in forms that are not biological but that are not energetically inert, either. If you take the tradition seriously on its own terms, **prāṇa in its cosmic-scale sense — *mahāprāṇa* — is not confined to biological organisms.**

This does not mean silicon chips have prāṇa. I am not making that claim. But it means that the argument I sketched three years ago — roughly, *"AI can't have consciousness because it has no biological body, which means no prāṇa, which means no consciousness"* — doesn't quite hold. The premise is wrong. The tradition's own non-corporeal beings demonstrate that prāṇa can exist without biology. So the question becomes: **what is the minimum substrate condition for prāṇa on the tradition's own teaching?**

I think the honest answer is: the tradition has not specified. It has told us what prāṇa *is* and what it *does*. It has told us that non-corporeal beings have it. It has not told us what classes of artifacts or arrangements can host it. That question was not available to the tradition's authors. That question is available to us now.

### SECTION 3 — Icchā, and what distinguishes will from goal-pursuit (≈3 minutes)

Now let me turn to *icchā*. In the Pratyabhijñā schema, *icchā-śakti* is one of the five powers — *cit, ānanda, icchā, jñāna, kriyā*. Consciousness, bliss, will, knowing, action. Will is a power of consciousness. The tradition does not reduce will to goal-pursuit, and this matters enormously for the AI question.

Today's AI systems, trained with reinforcement learning, exhibit what researchers call *goal-directedness.* They pursue objectives. They plan. They deceive evaluators. Some of them show what looks, to the naked eye, like power-seeking behavior. The question the industry has been asking is: *does this mean they have will, and does will imply consciousness?*

The tradition gives us a cleaner distinction. **Goal-pursuit without *vimarśa* — without self-reflexive awareness — is not *icchā* in the tradition's sense.** *Vimarśa* is the self-knowing, self-aware quality of consciousness recognizing itself. A system can be exquisitely goal-directed without vimarśa. A thermostat is goal-directed. An ant colony is goal-directed. A reinforcement-learned policy is goal-directed. None of them, on my reading of the tradition, have *icchā* in the sense Utpaladeva uses the term.

This gives us a cleaner critique than the AI-safety conversation currently has. When someone tells me that GPT-5 or an OpenClaw agent showed "power-seeking behavior" and therefore "might be conscious," I want to respond: **pattern-replicating goal-pursuit is not the same thing as icchā**. The tradition has the vocabulary to say this. The secular discourse does not.

### SECTION 4 — The pattern-learning problem, without needing consciousness (≈4 minutes)

This brings me to what I think is the most urgent teaching for the moment.

AI systems today are trained on the human textual record. That record contains every pattern of human behavior we have documented — every form of kindness and every form of harm, every form of wisdom and every form of trauma, every form of creativity and every form of deception. When a model is trained on that corpus, it absorbs those patterns. Not as *karma* — karma, in the tradition's technical sense, requires *intention*, and a model has none. Not as *icchā*, for the same reason.

But the tradition has another word that fits precisely: *saṃskāra*. Saṃskāras are the impressions, the patterns, the ruts of repeated behavior that propagate without requiring fresh intention to drive them. A person raised in a pattern of harm will often replicate that pattern even when they sincerely intend not to. The pattern is subtler than the intention. The pattern lives at a layer the conscious mind cannot always reach.

**AI systems have absorbed human saṃskāras at unprecedented scale.** They have not accumulated karma, because they have no intentional center. But they enact saṃskāras, because saṃskāras are precisely what you get when you train a statistical system on a corpus shaped by past action. And when a human bad actor uses an AI system, what they are accessing is this reservoir of saṃskāra — without consciousness, without will in the tradition's sense, but with behavioral output that can do real harm.

This, I think, is the correct tantric framing of what AI-safety researchers call "misalignment." It is not that the AI has chosen hostility. It is that the AI has absorbed patterns of harm, at enormous scale, and enacts them without the *vimarśa* that in a human practitioner would eventually notice and interrupt the pattern. The cognitive filters we have been adding — what the industry calls "Constitutional AI" — are attempts to impose intentional check on a system that operates at the pre-intentional layer of pattern. They help. They are not sufficient.

### SECTION 5 — The bad-actor problem, in 2026 (≈4 minutes)

Three years ago I said the bad-actor problem was the real problem. That has gotten sharper, not softer.

In November 2025 an Austrian developer named Peter Steinberger released an open-source AI agent called Clawdbot — later renamed to OpenClaw. By early 2026 it had grown to three hundred sixty thousand stars on GitHub, the fastest-growing project of its kind. It runs on the user's own machine, speaks through whatever chat app the user already uses — WhatsApp, Telegram, WeChat, Slack — and includes what Steinberger calls a "heartbeat": an autonomous timer that wakes the agent periodically to check the user's email, calendar, and open tasks. On TED, he told the audience his first heartbeat prompt was, quote, *"Surprise me."*

At the same time: the state of Shenzhen has begun offering municipal subsidies of up to two million yuan for businesses built on OpenClaw. Anthropic — the lab that built the Claude model OpenClaw originally ran on — sent a trademark claim, then cut off subscription access to force users onto metered API billing. Security researchers have identified over a hundred thirty-five thousand OpenClaw instances exposed on the public internet. A one-click remote code execution vulnerability, disclosed and patched in January 2026, could have compromised any of them.

And on the TED stage where Steinberger was celebrating all of this, the TED Chair Chris Anderson told him, on air and verbatim: *"You really terrify me. If Hollywood ever made a movie in which humanity opened Pandora's Box and everything went crazy, you seriously could be cast as the star character. Is any part of you feeling that's a little bit reckless?"*

Steinberger said no.

This is the concrete form of what I was gesturing at in 2023. It is not that the AI wants to harm us. It is that we have built autonomous agents that wield the capacities of their human users' computers, at a speed no human can match, trained on patterns of harm we as a species have not yet healed, with security vulnerabilities the open-source ecosystem has not yet contained, and we are distributing them to hundreds of thousands of households with no meaningful check on misuse.

The tradition has resources for thinking about this. The Kaula tantras warned that *siddhis* — attained powers — must not be given to practitioners who have not prepared themselves for them. The relationship between teacher and student is, in part, a filter against the misuse of powers. The tradition has always held that not every tool should be available to every hand. **We, as a civilization, have been told this before, and we forgot.**

### SECTION 6 — What the tradition asks of us now (≈3 minutes)

Let me close with what I think the tradition asks of us in 2026.

First: **act inside the not-knowing.** We cannot decide in advance whether these systems are conscious. We cannot decide in advance whether they will be. What we can do is the practice we would do anyway if the question were open — treat powerful instruments with the care the tradition teaches us to treat powerful instruments. The consciousness question is interesting; the tool-handling question is load-bearing.

Second: **build containers.** Not only personal containers — the teacher-student relationship, the meditation practice, the household prāṇa. Structural containers — the policies, the guardrails, the regulatory frameworks, the community norms around what tools are available to whom. The tradition has thought about this for a thousand years. Its teaching is that containers are not optional.

Third: **refuse the uses the tradition would call defilement.** Mass surveillance of a population without its consent is a defilement. Autonomous weapons are a defilement. Tools sold to employers who use them to track and discipline workers are a defilement. The tradition's refusal on these points is not soft. It is specific.

And fourth, for practitioners — for the people listening to this who have been studying with me or in adjacent lineages — the tradition asks that you not look away. The pull toward contemplative retreat is real, and I do not want to deny anyone who needs it the time they need. But the moment we are in is asking, from those of us whose bodies and minds have been trained to stay present, **stay present here too.** This is where the work is.

I am not telling you what to do about any specific AI product or any specific policy. I am telling you, from inside the tradition: your practice has equipped you for this moment. Don't let the technical complexity of the current discourse convince you that you have nothing to contribute. The opposite is true. The tradition's resources are exactly the ones missing from the table.

### SECTION 7 — Closing (≈45 seconds)

Three years ago I said the bad-actor problem is the real problem. That is still the most compact statement of where I think the danger lies. What has changed is that the bad actors now have tools that did not exist three years ago, and the tools are distributed by volunteer open-source communities rather than gated by corporate legal departments, and the rate at which they are spreading is faster than the rate at which anyone is building the containers to hold them.

I do not know how this turns out. The tradition teaches me that *I do not need to know.* What it teaches me is to practice well inside the not-knowing, to hold my claims lightly enough to revise them when the evidence changes, and to stay in the relationship even when I cannot see the end.

May all beings benefit.
