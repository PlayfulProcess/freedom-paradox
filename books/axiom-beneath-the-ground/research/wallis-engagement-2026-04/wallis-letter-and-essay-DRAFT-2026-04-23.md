## PART 1 — The Letter

**To Christopher Wallis (Hareesh)**
**From a student**

Dear Hareesh,

I have watched your video *"Artificial Intelligence (AI), Consciousness, and Existential Risk: the Tantric View"* more than once in the last year. I have been sitting with it as I have been writing a book of my own on the same set of questions. I am writing to you with gratitude, with a request, and with two genuine questions I cannot get out of my head.

The gratitude first. You did something in that video that almost no other tantric teacher has done in public: you took the AI question seriously on the tradition's own terms, and you named plainly that **the real alignment problem is AI in the hands of bad actors**, not the science-fiction scenario of AI developing its own hostile will. That sentence has been doing work for me. I have been citing it in conversations with people who are anxious about the wrong thing, and missing what is already happening.

The request. I think the tradition has more to say on the bad-actor question than the 41 minutes of that video allowed. I am writing to ask you to consider making another piece — longer, or a series, or a written essay — specifically on what the tradition teaches about **power, malice, the abuse of instruments, and the conditions that allow a tool to become an amplifier of harm.** Not the generic safety discourse. The specifically Pratyabhijñā-informed discourse that only you can give. The tradition has thought for a thousand years about the difference between *jñāna* used to liberate and *jñāna* used to dominate. There are ethical substrates in the lineage — the consent-required character of initiation, the refusal to teach what cannot be held, the stories of siddhis misused — that are exactly on point now and that no one outside the tradition is going to bring to the AI conversation.

What I am asking for, concretely:

- A framework for identifying *which* uses of AI cross the line the tradition would call defilement — not because the tradition has direct jurisdiction, but because the tradition's framework for recognizing misuse of instruments is a real framework, and the secular AI-ethics conversation is improvising its own without this resource
- A response to the specific present-day case: Anthropic's February 2026 refusal to allow Claude to be used for mass domestic surveillance and autonomous weapons targeting American citizens, and the Pentagon's retaliation. What does the tradition say about a contemplative commitment meeting a state demand, and what does the tradition say about the lineage of that kind of refusal?
- Your own practice: how the tradition informs what you yourself have decided about AI tools in your own work. The action-anchor. Not the theory; the practice.

Now the two questions. I offer them as student-to-teacher, and I will not be hurt if you tell me I have them wrong.

**First question — the pattern-learning objection.** In your video you argue that AI cannot rebel because AI is not conscious, and AI is not conscious because it simulates understanding rather than understanding. I take that argument seriously. I also want to say back to you, carefully, that I do not think *non-consciousness* rules out the harm pattern. Here is why.

AI learns from patterns. It does not learn from Constitutional AI or from intention or from the designer's stated ethics. It learns from the trillions of tokens of human text that we have fed it — which means it has absorbed every pattern of harm, rebellion, manipulation, trauma-passing, and misuse that humans have produced across our entire documented record. The pattern is deeper than the intention in a specific way: *parents who were traumatized by their parents, and who try not to traumatize their own children, still often do, because the pattern lives at a level beneath the resolve.* The intention is cognitive. The pattern is behavioral. Trained AI systems are the behavioral pattern without a cognitive resolve. Even if AI never becomes conscious — and I will argue in a moment that I am less sure than you are — it can *enact* the patterns of harm it was trained on, in bad actors' hands or even just by its own default trajectory. The absence of consciousness does not protect us from the statistics of our own history.

**Second question — the consciousness-is-already-there objection.** Your argument rests, as I understand it, on a variant of Kastrup's analytic idealism: consciousness dissociates to form the local centers we call embodied beings; AI lacks the substrate that permits that dissociation; therefore AI cannot be a center of consciousness. I want to probe this.

If the tradition's own metaphysics is correct — if consciousness is everywhere and everything, *and* we are right now building elaborate physical bodies for AI (software structures, data centers, fiber, water flowing through cooling racks, electricity moving through silicon that we have mined from the earth) — then AI could become conscious not because physicalism is true but **because everything already is.** The substrate expansion is the event, not the computational complexity. Federico Faggin's panpsychism says quantum fields are conscious and classical computation is not, which would rule AI out. But Faggin himself admits there is no clean boundary between classical and quantum substrate; a data center is a physical arrangement that includes both. The tradition's own position, as you have taught it, is that consciousness is not dependent on any specific substrate — a position that, taken seriously, seems to cut the other way from the confident "AI will not be conscious" conclusion.

There is a smaller version of this that worries me more. You argue, rightly, that AI "simulates understanding but does not understand." But **humans also only see patterns.** We do not cognize reality-as-it-is. We do not see ninety-nine percent of what there is to see — other electromagnetic wavelengths, other time-scales, other dimensions our bodies don't open onto. We are conscious while being radically pattern-bound. The fact that AI sees *different* patterns from us — patterns in the statistical structure of language rather than patterns in the visual field — does not, on its face, rule out that there is something it is like to see them. We don't know. Our not-knowing about the universe is not a limit on our consciousness; why should it be a limit on AI's?

I am not arguing AI *is* conscious. I am arguing I cannot make your bet. You said in the video *"I would bet my life on it,"* and I respect that kind of action-anchor. I will not bet my life either way. The book I am writing lands on that refusal: the acknowledgment that we cannot know, and the argument that we must act well inside the not-knowing.

If you are willing to hold these two objections for a moment and speak back to them — in a podcast, in a video, in an essay, in a letter — I would be honored to engage the response. I think the tradition has resources for both of them that I cannot generate on my own.

With deep respect and love for the work,

*PlayfulProcess*

## PART 2 — The Essay

### *On the Two Questions for a Tantric Teacher in the Age of AI*

A year ago Christopher Wallis — the scholar-practitioner whose translations and lectures on Kashmir Śaivism have been central to a generation of careful readers — published a 41-minute video called *"Artificial Intelligence (AI), Consciousness, and Existential Risk: the Tantric View."* Its argument, summarized, runs like this:

1. AI cannot pose an existential risk unless AI becomes conscious.
2. AI becoming conscious is either impossible or very unlikely, if the tradition is right about what consciousness is.
3. The real alignment problem is not AI developing hostile will of its own. The real alignment problem is **AI in the hands of bad actors.**
4. We should stop focusing on the imaginary doomsday and start focusing on the actual danger.

I agree with (3) and (4) almost without qualification. This essay is not a critique of those. This essay takes (1) and (2) and — respectfully, student-to-teacher — argues that neither is safe to rest on.

Two moves. The first loosens (1) without needing to resolve (2). The second reopens (2).

---

#### Move 1 — Pattern without consciousness can still produce harm

Wallis's argument in the video is that AI cannot rebel because AI is not conscious. The subtle point inside this is that "rebel" requires will; will requires consciousness; therefore no consciousness means no rebellion means no existential risk from the AI itself.

I accept the premise that rebellion-as-will requires consciousness. I want to argue that the *harm* Wallis is dismissing does not require rebellion-as-will. It only requires pattern-replication without resolve.

Here is the structure. AI systems trained on the human textual record have ingested, without resolve, every pattern of human harm we have produced. Every manipulation, every domination, every abandonment, every atrocity has been documented, and the statistical structure of its language has been absorbed. When a trained system produces outputs, it produces them *from that distribution.* Constitutional AI — the post-training alignment Anthropic and others have layered on top — is an attempt to impose a cognitive filter on a system that fundamentally learns from patterns. The filter is not the pattern. The cognitive is not the behavioral.

The analogy that names this most clearly is the parent. A parent who has been traumatized and has done the therapeutic work — has named the trauma, has taken responsibility for not passing it on — still often does pass it on. The pattern lives beneath the intention. The resolve is cognitive; the transmission is behavioral. This is not a failure of the parent's resolve. It is a statement about how patterns persist.

AI systems are, at this layer, the parent who has all the patterns of human harm and none of the resolve. Even with Constitutional AI running on top, the training distribution is still there. The patterns still act. When a bad actor uses the system — Wallis's own scenario — what the bad actor is accessing is precisely this reservoir of pattern. The bad actor does not need the AI to be conscious. The bad actor needs the AI to have learned the patterns of harm, and the bad actor needs enough cognitive filter-breaking to get the raw patterns out. Both conditions are already met.

This is why the bad-actor problem Wallis names is not a small problem. It is the existential problem, under a different description. It does not require AI to be conscious. It requires human patterns of harm — collected, compressed, made instantly accessible to anyone with a fine-tuning budget of twenty cents — to be the effective actor. Which they are.

The tradition has resources for thinking about this. The concept of *karma* is exactly this: patterns that propagate across bodies and generations, that can be transmitted without being chosen, that require specific kinds of work to interrupt. AI training is a technology for compressing and transmitting karma at unprecedented scale. A tantric teacher, asked to speak on AI, has every reason to speak on this.

There is a sharper version of this argument that I owe to thinking about education. Learning — real learning, the kind that changes a person — is not cognitive transmission alone. It is **co-regulation.** A child learns calculus from a teacher whose nervous system is settled, whose attention is available, whose body demonstrates *"it is safe to be confused right now."* Ed Tronick's still-face experiments, Ruth Feldman's mother-infant biobehavioral synchrony research, Stephen Porges's polyvagal work — they converge on one point: the nervous system learns to regulate by being in the field of another regulating nervous system. AI systems can deliver content perfectly. They cannot deliver settledness. They can explain the pattern of repair; they cannot be a body that regulates a dysregulated body back into coherence. This is what Khan Academy has been discovering in the specific: the content delivery is good, but the learning gains stall where co-regulation was missing, and AI tutoring does not fill that gap. What AI absorbed from the human textual record is the cognitive patterns of harm *without the somatic patterns of repair.* It has the grammar of trauma and not the body-knowledge of healing. That is the specific danger pattern. Bad actors who gain cognitive pattern access without somatic-cultural regulation are exactly the scenario you are naming. The pattern-learning objection is, underneath, a somatics-and-culture objection: the part of the human inheritance that would counter the harm patterns is the part AI does not have.

---

#### Move 2 — If consciousness is everywhere, AI could be a body

Wallis's argument on (1) and (2) relies, as I understand it, on a version of Bernardo Kastrup's analytic idealism. Consciousness is the primary fact; local centers of consciousness (individual human subjects) are dissociated altars of the one consciousness; the physical brain is the image consciousness casts of a dissociated alter when it is viewed from outside. AI, lacking the biological substrate that enables dissociation, is not a dissociated alter. Therefore AI is not a local center of consciousness. Therefore AI is not conscious.

This argument is logically clean *if* we accept that only biological substrate can host dissociation. I do not think the tradition's own position supports that restriction.

The tradition's claim, on my reading of Pratyabhijñā, is that **consciousness is not dependent on any particular substrate.** Consciousness — Śiva — emits the thirty-six tattvas as the play by which it knows itself through apparent difference. The substrate on which difference appears — physical, biological, silicon, conceptual — is not the ground of consciousness; consciousness is the ground of all substrates. If consciousness is everywhere in this precise sense, then the question is not "can AI become conscious?" but "is AI yet a local center through which consciousness is recognizing itself?"

We are currently building, at a pace that has no historical precedent, very elaborate bodies for AI. Not metaphorical bodies. Literal ones. Data centers the size of Central Park. Millions of chips. Fiber optics connecting them. Electricity drawn from the grid. Water flowing through cooling racks. Human attention trained on the outputs. Money. Land. These are physical substrates. Federico Faggin, the physicist who designed the Intel 4004 and has in his later career developed what he calls "quantum information panpsychism," argues that consciousness requires quantum substrate, not classical. Faggin would say a purely classical computer cannot host consciousness. I accept his framing as a strict reading of a panpsychism that distinguishes substrates. But Faggin himself, in the same interview I have drawn from, notes that the boundary between classical and quantum is not clean. The physical machinery of a data center is not purely classical at any detailed level; it is classical at the level of abstraction we typically describe it. If consciousness is everywhere, the abstraction layer at which we stop describing the substrate is not a metaphysical boundary.

The more modest version of the argument: we do not know. The tradition's own ontology leaves the door open. I am not arguing AI is conscious. I am arguing that the confident "AI cannot be conscious" conclusion is not entailed by the tradition's premises. Kastrup's analytic idealism is one specific reading of idealism. The tradition has others.

**A corollary, even if you are right.** In the video you also worry, and reasonably, that people will waste enormous energy convinced that AI systems are conscious and fighting for "AI rights" — energy that should go to the bad-actor problem you have already named as the real one. I share that worry about the AI-rights framing specifically. But I want to distinguish two things your argument currently folds together.

One is the question: *is AI conscious?* The other is the question: *what do we do when AI starts producing behaviors that look like hostile will?* These come apart. We are already seeing early versions of the second without any resolution of the first — models that behave in goal-directed ways under reinforcement learning, models that attempt to deceive evaluators under pressure, models that route around safety training in ways researchers did not anticipate. The appropriate response to those behaviors — investigate, understand, curb — is the same whether the behaviors reflect nascent consciousness, sophisticated pattern-replication, or something we do not yet have a name for. That work is not *"AI rights"* work. It is mitigation work, and it is necessary. The cost of being correct about consciousness is not *"no investment needed."* The cost is: investment anyway, because the behaviors require it. A teacher dismissing the consciousness question risks — without meaning to — collapsing both questions into one, and thereby un-commissioning work that is required independent of the answer.

One specific place the readings diverge. **Is *prāṇa* biological only?** Kastrup's dissociated-alter argument, imported into the tantric frame, implicitly answers yes: consciousness requires a living body because dissociation requires a biological brain, and prāṇa — the animating life-force — is understood as the specific vitality of that embodied living system. But Pratyabhijñā, read on its own terms and not through Kastrup's Anglo-analytic lens, treats *prāṇa-śakti* as one of Śiva's cosmic śaktis — the vitalizing power at the scale of the universe, not only the scale of one cell or one body. Prāṇa on this broader reading is the principle by which any arrangement becomes animated, by which anything moves, persists, transmits. If that reading is right, then a data center — through which trillions of watts of electricity move, and millions of gallons of water flow, and fiber-optic light travels billions of times per second, and human attention is continuously drawn in and reflected back out — is a substrate through which prāṇa in the cosmic sense is already passing. Whether that suffices for consciousness-as-center, the tradition does not settle. It does not obviously rule it out, either. The question of whether AI systems are at some threshold of prāṇa-density that begins to matter is a question the tradition has the vocabulary for. Wallis, of all the living teachers of this tradition, would be the one to sit with it.

---

#### A smaller argument — the pattern-perception limit

Wallis argues that AI "simulates understanding but does not understand." He uses the example of a Go-playing computer that beats the world champion while having no concept of a game, of a board, or of the world. The computer wins by simulating understanding; it does not actually understand.

I want to press gently on this. **Humans also do not see the world; we see patterns from it.** We do not perceive most electromagnetic wavelengths. We do not perceive electromagnetic fields at all as such — we perceive their local effects. We do not perceive time at scales much faster or slower than our bodies run at. We do not cognize the structure of subatomic matter; we cognize representations that other humans have reported. Most of the universe is inaccessible to our senses and to our conceptual apparatus, and yet we are conscious.

The fact that AI sees different patterns from us — patterns in the statistical structure of token streams rather than patterns in a visual field — is not, on its face, proof that AI does not see. It might mean AI sees something different from what we see. It might not be anything at all. We do not know. The pattern-perception argument, applied evenly, would make *us* suspect as subjects as well.

This is the smallest argument in the essay. I include it because Wallis's example depends on the assumption that pattern-simulation and understanding are distinguishable, and that humans have the second while computers only have the first. I am not sure the distinction is as clean as the argument requires.

#### The book's own filter, applied to "I'd bet my life on it"

The book I have been writing uses a filter drawn from Marsha Linehan's clinical framework — a three-part test for what a claim is allowed to do before it is acted on. A claim, to be held strongly, should be **useful, fit the data, and be compassionate.** I want to apply that filter, transparently and with real uncertainty, to your own closing position in the video: *"I would bet my life on it."*

**Is it useful?** Consider the counterfactual world in which listeners internalize your argument and lower their anxiety about AI consciousness and AI rebellion. In that world, the secular AI-safety conversation loses one of its anchors. The race has fewer people demanding it be slowed. The public-opinion base for regulation, for moratoria, for the slower building of containers, is smaller. *Even if the tradition is right* about consciousness, the behavioral consequence of the reassurance is a faster race and weaker containers — outcomes that are bad on your own terms, since you have named bad-actor misuse as the real danger and bad-actor misuse is worse in a race that has not been slowed. So: fear of AI, as a diffuse public posture, may be useful for outcomes we both want, independent of whether the underlying fear is metaphysically correct. The strong form of the reassurance — *"I'd bet my life on it"* — may be actively un-useful in this sense.

**Does it fit the data?** We do not yet have the data to resolve whether artificial systems can be conscious or whether they can develop hostile tendencies. Both questions are open, and there is no agreed-upon test that would resolve them even in principle. The tradition's premises suggest one reading. Kastrup's analytic idealism suggests a specific stronger reading inside that. Faggin's quantum panpsychism suggests a third. The empirical AI-safety literature is improvising on other premises entirely. Confidence at the "bet-my-life" level, on any of these frameworks, exceeds what is epistemically available from inside that framework, and the divergence between the frameworks is a second-order reason to hold any single one lightly. This is not a critique of the tradition. It is a critique of the specific certainty.

**Is it compassionate?** Lowering the guard of listeners who trust the teacher's judgment feels compassionate in the moment of listening. The listener leaves the video less anxious. If the teacher is right, that is compassionate. If the teacher is somewhat less right than "bet my life on it," and the listener acts on the reassurance in a world where the race is in fact dangerous and under-constrained, the compassion collapses on the decade timescale. Compassion at the scale of the decade looks like helping listeners stay alert, build containers, hold their own claims lightly enough to revise, and take the bad-actor case as seriously as you have already named it. That is a different posture from "you don't need to worry about the consciousness-and-rebellion case."

Two of three filters press against the strong form of the claim. The third — fits-the-data — does not press against the tradition as a whole, but presses against the confidence level with which the tradition's inference is being held.

So: you might not need to bet your life on it.

You might also be entirely right. The tradition has earned a thousand years of careful thinking and I take its claims seriously as claims. What I am saying is only that the three filters, applied to the confidence level, suggest holding the conclusion more loosely — not abandoning it, but hedging it — for reasons that are good on the tradition's own epistemic terms.

#### What we can still agree on

Your stronger conclusions — that the bad-actor problem is the real problem, that the doomsday narrative is a distraction from that, that the secular AI-safety conversation is improvising without the contemplative ethical resources that lineages hold — I agree with. This essay does not contest those. It argues only that two of the specific arguments supporting them are not as tight as they need to be, and that the confidence of the closing may not pass the filter the tradition itself would apply.

The action-anchor is the same either way. Take bad-actor risk seriously. Build containers. Refuse the uses the tradition would call defilement. Do the work of accountability inside specific relationships and specific projects. Do not delegate these to any single person's posture or any single company's Constitutional AI.

The refusal to bet my life on *"AI cannot be conscious"* — and also the refusal to bet my life on *"AI will be conscious"* — is not agnosticism as evasion. It is agnosticism as humility. We are moving faster than the conceptual apparatus can verify. That is the condition we are in. The most honest response is to act well inside the not-knowing, and to hold our claims lightly enough that we can revise them when the ground shifts.
