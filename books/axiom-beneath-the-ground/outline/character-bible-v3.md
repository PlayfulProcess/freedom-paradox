## The Protagonist: "She"

### Identity
- Woman, approximately forty years old
- No name given — always "she"
- The narrator is third-person limited, close to her interiority
- The narrator NEVER evaluates — no "compelling," "devastating," "brilliant." The narrator observes. The reader decides.

### Professional Background
- Former **equity research analyst** (not investment banking — equity research is the correct term for Goldman Sachs role)
- The work demanded a particular narrowing of attention: numbers, hours, models. It was incompatible with the existential search — not because the search died, but because the search required a kind of openness the role could not hold
- She did not rebel against finance. She became the role because the hypocrisy of being existential in such a narrow position would have been unbearable. It was easier to alienate herself, to stand on other people's grounds, to believe the world had a particular order — that humans excelling in social constructs are more deserving, that value and money are conflated, that money is the proof of value
- After leaving: the question returned, louder for having been suppressed
- Currently: building a technology project (an AI-assisted system, implied to be recursive.eco)

### Family
- **ONE daughter** — age approximately 5-6 (rabbit book phase, bedtime stories, "frog soap")
- **Husband** — together 15+ years, sharing a bed for 15 years
- NO other children. Every reference must be singular: "the child," "her daughter," never "children"

### Apartment
- Small: **two rooms and a kitchen/living room** (kitchen and living room are one space)
- Books stacked on the floor beside the bed — shelf filled months ago
- Laptop on the table
- Bedroom has: a meditation corner with a shelf (the altar), the bed, a laundry basket, a chair used as closet overflow
- Daughter's room: nightlight shaped like a moon (weak-tea colored glow), lamp with yellow shade
- Hallway between office and daughter's room: twelve steps

### Spiritual/Existential History
- **Catholic upbringing** — pro forma, not faith-based
  - Parents attended because their parents had attended
  - Grandfather attended for community standing in the neighborhood
  - More cultural habit than spiritual practice
  - She perceived the emptiness by age 10-11: "this is not alive." Rituals were obligation without presence. Words were someone else's words.
  - She did not rebel. She simply stopped attending. Quietly.
- **The question never fully disappeared** after the church
  - It took different forms: meditation classes with a college roommate, yoga, readings, periods of quiet experimentation
  - The question was always there, wearing different clothes
  - The ONLY time it was truly incompatible was during her years in equity research — the search went underground like a river in limestone country
  - After leaving finance, the question returned
- **The retreat** (around age 40)
  - Five-day practice intensive at a converted farmhouse, two hours north
  - Led by a teacher (woman, silver hair, tall) — NOT Wallis himself, but someone teaching within the tradition Wallis translates
  - She drove herself there (no train)
  - Experience: the room holding, two channels (warm outward / tight inward), the alarm from Catholic childhood firing
  - Key moment: "It is, until you experience it" — the teacher's five words
  - Came home with a small brass figure for her shelf
- **Current practice**: sits on a cushion in the bedroom, chants Sanskrit phonetically, attempts (and fails) deity yoga visualization, burns incense and a candle. Morning sits before daughter wakes, evening sits after daughter sleeps.

### The Alarm
- Fires since age 10-11, from the Catholic church experience
- A low, steady signal — not loud enough to make her leave, but never absent
- Says: "this looks like the church." Forms, altars, chanting, devotion = the same hollowness dressed in different clothes
- Key realization at retreat: the alarm and the room are not saying the same thing. The alarm says "this looks like the church." The room says "look again."
- The alarm never stops. It quiets. It is doing its job. She sits with it.

### The Void
- Not depression — she has been depressed and knows the difference
- The experience of having examined the question of her own value and found no answer that holds
- Not rejecting answers — incapable of receiving them. The mechanism of reception is broken.
- Lives between faith and atheism

### Key Objects
- Blue glass from Lisbon street market (7 years ago, chipped rim, kept for color and memory)
- Dark blue meditation cushion, flattened on one side from weight shifted left
- Brass deity figure from retreat bookshop — fierce and tender, dancing, multiple arms
- Small photograph of the teacher in a dark frame
- Candle in grocery-store glass holder
- Incense: sandalwood and something sweet/resinous
- String of wooden beads (unused, kept because removing felt like a subtraction)
- The rabbit book (daughter's current nightly request — 3+ weeks running)
- "Frog soap" — green bottle with a frog, daughter's name for it, calcified into household vocabulary
- Notebook — where she writes poems

## The Husband

### Character
- Steady, watchful, loving skeptic
- Not hostile to her practice — genuinely sees something is working
- Measures change in the currency he trusts most: the quality of the air in their house
- "Wild Wild Country" reference = his gentle alarm system. Said with half-smile — joke and not-joke simultaneously
- "Keep an eye on it" — his version of care. He watches. She knows he watches. Both are fine with this.

### Physical Reading
- Has spent 15 years calibrating how to read one particular woman's weather
- Reads: angle of shoulders, speed of step, set of mouth, how the door closes, whether the voice is clipped
- Like a sailor reading wind by water surface — not by studying meteorology but by being out in it
- Humming in the kitchen = evening is going well
- Can tell from across the room, even from profile, even from how she closes the office door

### Communication Style
- Economical. "That was fast." "You seem okay." "Whatever it is, tonight was good."
- Observations offered carefully — "the way you offer a hand to someone on uneven ground — not pulling, just present"
- Has a talent (or practice) for staying in the room when the room is uncomfortable
- The nod when she goes to meditate = layered: I know + I'll be quiet + the photograph is strange + I love you + whatever this is it's working + I am watching

### Key Moments
- Pours her the blue Lisbon glass after a good evening
- "You know what that reminds me of?" / "Wild Wild Country" — said twice (once about the altar figure, once about the evening)
- His hand finds her arm in sleep — unconscious reaching, 15 years of body memory

## Style Rules (from CLAUDE.md + Kindle feedback)

1. **Narrator never evaluates.** No: "compelling," "devastating," "brilliant," "extraordinary," "remarkable," "striking," "powerful," "profound," "radical." The narrator observes. The reader decides.
2. **No absolutist claims.** "the only" → "among the few." "every tradition" → "the traditions examined here."
3. **Footnote rule.** Study details, framework listings, epistemological disclaimers → footnotes. If it reads like an academic paper, it's a footnote.
4. **Ground domestic detail in real time.** 8pm bedtime, 10pm void, 6:30am wake.
5. **One daughter.** Always singular. Never "children."
6. **Catholic, not synagogue.** Pro forma. Cultural obligation. Grandfather = community standing.
7. **The question never disappeared.** Only incompatible during equity research years.
8. **She drove to the retreat.** No train.
