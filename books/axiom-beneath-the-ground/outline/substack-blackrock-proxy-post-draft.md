# How to Vote Your BlackRock ETF, and Why You Should

*Draft for PlayfulProcess Substack — 2026-04-21. ~1,100 words. Voice intended to match the Substack's existing *is process* register: first-person, reflective, grounded in specific actions the author is actually taking, not instructional.*

---

I got an email this week inviting me to choose a proxy voting policy for my iShares Core S&P 500 ETF. I almost ignored it. I am glad I did not. This is a short piece on what the thing is, how I am thinking about it, and why I think it might be worth your attention.

---

If you own IVV — one of the largest ETFs in the world, BlackRock's flagship S&P 500 fund — BlackRock votes on your behalf at the shareholder meetings of every company in the fund. Apple, Microsoft, Alphabet, Meta, NVIDIA, all of them. Every year there are ballot items: executive compensation, climate disclosures, board seats, AI-safety proposals, human-rights resolutions. Your fractional ownership has a vote. Until recently, BlackRock simply cast that vote for you, using their in-house *Investment Stewardship* policy.

In 2022 they launched a program called **Voting Choice** for institutional clients. In 2024 and 2025 they extended it to retail investors. If you hold IVV, you now have the option to pick one of eight policies, including BlackRock's own default, and have your proportional shares voted according to that policy.

Eight. The list, briefly:

- **BlackRock Benchmark** — the default; fiduciary, long-term financial performance focus
- **Egan-Jones Wealth-Focused** — explicitly anti-ESG, skeptical of shareholder proposals that "distract from core business"
- **Glass Lewis Benchmark** — corporate-governance best practice
- **Glass Lewis Climate** — strong climate focus, extra scrutiny on systemic emitters
- **Glass Lewis Corporate-Governance Focused** — fiduciary with shareholder-rights emphasis
- **ISS Catholic Faith-based** — aligned with USCCB and the Interfaith Center on Corporate Responsibility
- **ISS Global Board Aligned** — generally votes with management on environmental and social matters
- **ISS Socially Responsible (SRI)** — grounded in UN PRI, UN Global Compact, UNEP FI

If you do nothing, you default to the first one. If you pick, your selection carries forward until you change it.

---

I have been sitting with this decision for a few days.

I am a former equity analyst. I spent most of my twenties watching intelligent people inside intelligent firms construct elegant models that funded extractive things, and I have since learned that whatever it is that makes a person reliably good is not the same as whatever makes a person reliably sharp. I do not trust fiduciary-long-term-returns as a sufficient ethical frame. It is not wrong; it is not enough.

I also went back to graduate school, slowly, to begin a social work degree. Slowly because I am raising a daughter and running a small platform and writing books and cannot hold too many things at once. What the MSW program has been doing, without my having articulated it in advance, is pulling me toward a specific question about ethics. Not *what is right*, in the philosophical sense. Something more operational.

Marsha Linehan, the psychologist who built Dialectical Behavior Therapy, developed a three-filter test for choosing a clinical framework when no existing framework worked: *is it useful, does it fit the data, is it compassionate.* I have borrowed these filters at every scale I can. They do not prove any framework true. They give me a test any framework has to keep passing, and a rationale for abandoning one that stops. Applied to my own actions, the three questions become: *does this action do useful work in the world, does it fit the evidence I have, does it serve without producing cruelty.* I do not always pass my own test. But I try to notice when I am failing.

The BlackRock email is, for me, an unusually clean instance of this test operating at a scale I rarely get to touch. I have, through IVV, a tiny fractional ownership of some of the largest companies on earth. These companies are voting, every year, on things that matter — AI deployment policies, executive pay, climate disclosure, human-rights due diligence. My share of the vote is small but real. If I accept the default, I hand the small real thing to BlackRock's benchmark policy, which is careful but not oriented around my values. If I pick, I signal something, even if the signal is mathematically small.

My choice is **ISS Socially Responsible (SRI)**. It is the broadest of the ethically-oriented options — it includes climate, human rights, labor, indigenous rights — and it is grounded in the UN PRI and UN Global Compact frameworks, which are serious ongoing international efforts rather than single-issue policies. I considered Glass Lewis Climate (narrower, but cleaner) and ISS Catholic Faith-based (nods to my upbringing, though I am not currently practicing). The SRI policy is the closest match to what I would choose if I were constructing the policy from scratch, which I am not, because I am not a policy shop. I will read the full policy document before submitting. The summaries on BlackRock's page are marketing copy; the actual voting guidance is in the PDFs the proxy advisors maintain.

---

If you are reading this and you hold IVV, I am going to gently suggest you do the same. Pick. Even if your choice is different from mine.

The reason is not that one of the policies is obviously right. The reason is that the default — *let BlackRock choose* — is the specific pattern my own work has been trying to name. Power without responsibility. The habit of delegating a small real thing, in the name of efficiency, until nobody who actually holds the thing is attending to what is being done with it. The small acts of delegation accumulate into the larger pattern of our time.

I built [recursive.eco](https://recursive.eco) partly around a version of this problem. The platform is a place where anyone can author a *grammar* — a structured symbolic system, a tarot deck, a philosophical framework, a children's story arc — and share it under CC BY-SA. Recently I moved the platform's code to a private repository while keeping the grammars open. The distinction matters to me. The commons should hold what belongs to everyone; the infrastructure that serves the commons does not need to be donated indiscriminately to every possible use. The platform has been running for a while. A few good people have come. More are welcome. You can start at [recursive.eco](https://recursive.eco), or come read the books I have been writing alongside it at [books.recursive.eco](https://books.recursive.eco), which includes drafts of *The Freedom Paradox*, *Working Architecture*, and the current one, *The Axiom Beneath the Ground: The Alignment Void*.

I do not know if proxy voting will make a material difference at the corporate-governance level. Probably not, by itself. What I know is that accepting the default is itself a choice, and that the small choices I am refusing to make are the places where my stated values would otherwise quietly stop being enacted. The social work training is teaching me to notice this in clinical contexts. The Linehan filters are teaching me to notice it in the frameworks I adopt. BlackRock, in its unexpected way, is teaching me to notice it in how I hold capital.

*Pick. Take the ten minutes. Read one PDF. Submit.*
