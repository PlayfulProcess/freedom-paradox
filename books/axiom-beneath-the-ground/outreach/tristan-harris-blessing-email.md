## Why this email, why now

PlayfulProcess's *Axiom Beneath the Ground* (v4 first-draft just completed on this branch) works through a distinction that matters here: **freedom** is the spiritual question (what is the "I" that acts at all?); **value** is the moral question (to whom do I direct what I have?). Most of the public AI debate is conducted at the value layer — who benefits, who is harmed, who is excluded — without touching the freedom layer, which is the question of *what kind of agent we are becoming by building these systems*.

In Axiom Ch10 the narrator builds a retrieval tool over her teacher's public teachings and he declines to bless it. She revises rather than withdraws. She holds the decline as real and keeps asking. The structure of that scene — *ask the living person, respect the refusal, continue the work* — is the structure this email is trying to honor in a different direction.

Bhagavad Gita frame: Arjuna on the battlefield of Kurukshetra, seeing his relatives arrayed against him, asks Krishna whether this battle is his to fight. Krishna does not say the battle is good. Krishna says the acting is Arjuna's dharma. The *Gita* is the dialogue that follows.

PlayfulProcess's position: the AI work is happening whether she participates or not. Her answer to the question *is this ours to do?* is a careful yes — a yes that honors the warnings, that holds the filter, that respects refusals from people like Harris and from her own teacher, and that keeps building.

She is, as she says, on Dario's team. Not as brand allegiance. As a position on what the dharma asks.

## Email draft

**Subject:** Library screening + a Bhagavad Gita question

Dear Mr. Harris,

I write with a specific request and with enough context that you can decide on the merits.

I'm asking for your blessing to screen one of your documentaries at a small free class I'll be teaching at my local public library — a short course on "vibe coding," the practice of building small software tools in collaboration with an AI model, for people who would not call themselves programmers. Attendance will be in the dozens, not the hundreds. There is no commercial element. The screening would open the course, because the questions your film names are the questions the class has to hold before anyone writes a line of code.

A few things about where I'm coming from, so you can weigh the request.

I was an equity research analyst at Goldman Sachs for the first chapter of my working life. I left finance, began an MSW, and in parallel started building small open-source tools — most recently a public site that makes world-tradition grammars available to families and educators. I have a five-year-old daughter. I have read your work, and watched your public conversations, with care. I take the warnings seriously. I do not think you are wrong.

I should also say, plainly, that I am what one would now call a builder inside the acceleration. Claude is the model I build with; I'm a paying customer and a close reader of Anthropic's public posture. I have come to think — provisionally, revisably — that it is possible to build at scale with sincerity about the risks, and that the possibility is worth testing from inside. I am, in that sense, on Dario's team. I don't say this to stake a position against yours. I say it so you know where the ask is coming from.

The frame I keep returning to is the one you probably know better than I do.

*Arjuna stood on the field at Kurukshetra and asked Krishna whether the battle was his to fight.*

I have been reading, this year, inside a thousand-year-old tradition called nondual Shaiva Tantra — my teacher is Christopher Wallis, who teaches in California — and the *Bhagavad Gita* sits near it as one of the traditional texts the lineage engages. I keep coming back to Arjuna's question because your warnings are, to my ear, the clearest contemporary version of it: *is this ours to do?* You have asked it with more precision, and at more personal cost, than almost anyone in public.

My answer, offered with respect for how unsettled yours may remain, is yes. And I think the yes requires us to build with the awareness your films name — which is why I would like your film to open the course rather than decorate it.

I will screen nothing without your permission. If you decline, I will honor the decline. I have been through a version of this exchange in the last few weeks with my teacher, on a different tool I had built, and I know what it is to receive a refusal with gratitude and make something smaller. If you want to set terms — attribution, context, a specific cut, a facilitator for the discussion, no recording, nothing commercial — I will follow them.

You can decline by not replying. You can decline in a sentence. You can accept in a sentence. I will be grateful for any of the three.

## Practical follow-up — before the email goes out

1. **Which film.** *The Social Dilemma* (2020, dir. Jeff Orlowski, distributed by Netflix) is the most-requested one and the one most libraries can license through their existing institutional subscriptions. *The AI Dilemma* (2023 talk, with Aza Raskin) is easier to obtain but is a recorded lecture, not a documentary. Decide which fits the class.
2. **Public performance rights.** Tristan Harris's blessing is one thing; the distributor's public performance license is another. For *The Social Dilemma*, check whether the library's Netflix or Kanopy institutional subscription covers public screenings at the planned scale. For the 2023 talk, check the Center for Humane Technology's terms. If a license is required, Harris's blessing alone is not sufficient.
3. **Library AV and scheduling.** Confirm the screening room, AV setup, runtime slot, and whether the class session can include the discussion afterward.
4. **Class framing.** The class should present the screening as opening the ethical frame, not as the class's position. The filter (useful / fits the data / compassionate, from Linehan) should be introduced as the tool students use to hold what the film puts forward.
5. **If Harris declines.** The class can still proceed. Alternative framing materials: the Social Dilemma companion guide (Center for Humane Technology, public); a Jonathan Haidt excerpt (*The Anxious Generation*, 2024); a short Anna Lembke clip (*Dopamine Nation*, 2021). A decline is not a blocker; it is a shape the class should respect.
