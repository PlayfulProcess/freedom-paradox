## Ch10 — Ghost as sibling to Nvidia

Current Ch10 §VI is the Nvidia "do as little as possible, as much as necessary" passage, held carefully (principle worth testing, operating record where the test plays out).

Proposed addition as a new §VI-or-VII (drop-in, before the "unresolved" section):

**Ghost, from the other direction.**

A thousand miles away from the chip company, and running at a scale smaller by many orders of magnitude, a man in Singapore named John O'Nolan had been running a company called Ghost since 2013. Ghost was a publishing platform — blogs, newsletters, membership sites — built as an alternative to the ad-funded engagement stack that had consumed most of the adjacent software category. The code was open-source, MIT-licensed, maintained by a small team. The company was organized as a non-profit foundation. Revenue came from Ghost(Pro), a hosted service; the code could be self-hosted freely by anyone who wanted to run it themselves. The platform supported ActivityPub, which meant it federated with other protocols rather than locking users inside its own network. It had been growing quietly for over a decade while larger, VC-funded competitors in the same category had swung between acceleration and collapse and drift.

She had been reading about Ghost in the weeks after the retreat as research for her own building. The more she read, the more the shape of the thing came clear. O'Nolan's posture was the same shape as the chip company's — *do as little as possible, as much as necessary* — approached from the other direction. Nvidia was doing it at the base of the stack; O'Nolan was doing it at the publishing layer. Both were saying: *build the narrow thing; make it open; take revenue from services rather than from attention; let the rest be built by others.* The chip company served a market of hyperscalers, governments, and labs. The publishing company served a market of writers, clergy, scientists, and teachers. The principle traveled.

The ghost model — she had started calling it that, privately — was what she was trying to build at recursive.eco. Not a social network. Not an engagement machine. Not a community monetized through attention. A set of grammars — story patterns, repair patterns, DBT skills as myths, decolonial gestures — published openly, made interactable with large language models at the edge, free at the point of use, supported by a small membership layer for families and teachers who wanted more. Infrastructure instead of attention. Cheap alternatives.

She did not yet know whether the model would work for her. O'Nolan had been at it for thirteen years and the company was profitable and stable; she was six months into her version and most months she did not know whether the next month would hold. She did know that the model was not theoretical. Someone had been running it for a decade, in public, and it was working.

## Ch3 or Ch4 — Harris reversal beat

The narrator's first-read / careful-read reversal on the Harris podcast is a Citta-layer event (heart-mind work; filter loosening; frame caught and corrected). It probably fits Ch3 or Ch4.

Two candidate placements:

### Option A: Ch3 addition (heart-mind / filter chapter)

Add a new section §VII between current §VI ("The Citta Settling") and the chapter close. The walk becomes the frame; the podcast is the specific thing she is chewing on.

Sketch:

She had, on the second morning of the retreat, been sent a link by the retreat's administrator — a link the teacher had asked her to share with the group. It was an interview with a well-known former product-ethics advocate, recorded some weeks before, about the risks of the large-language-model wave. She had listened to a few minutes of it on the walk and had put it away, feeling — in a way she would later want to examine — that she had been handed the link because the teacher wanted the group's AI enthusiasms cooled. She had, in the moment, been slightly indignant about this. She had not said so to anyone.

Walking the perimeter, on the third day, she came back to the link. She put the earbuds in. She listened to the whole interview.

The interview did not say what she had assumed it said. The interviewee — a thoughtful man, clearly — had spent the first third describing the risks of the current engagement-internet model with care, and the remaining two-thirds describing what he called the bright light of 2026: the collapsing cost of building infrastructure, the growing accessibility of alternatives, the opening of a door that had, for most of the engagement-internet era, been closed. His position was not *do not build*. His position was *build differently, cheaply, outside the incentive structure that captured the last wave*.

She stopped at the stone wall. She laughed at herself. She had filtered the link through her own defensiveness and had heard the thing she was defending against rather than the thing the interviewee had said. The teacher had not handed her the link to suppress her. The teacher had handed her the link because the link was accurate.

The filter had caught her in the act. She walked on, less indignant, and more grateful for the slip than she would have thought possible two hours earlier.
