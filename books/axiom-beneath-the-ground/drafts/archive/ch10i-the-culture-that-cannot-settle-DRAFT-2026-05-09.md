# Chapter 10i: The Culture That Cannot Settle

---

Marsha Linehan was clear about what made the disorder she came to call Borderline Personality Disorder. The biosocial theory. Two ingredients, held together over years.

The first ingredient: a nervous system that registers experience more strongly than the average — what she called *biological emotional sensitivity*. Some children are born this way. The skin is thinner. The signal is louder. The recovery from disturbance takes longer. None of this is wrong. It is a particular kind of nervous system, well-suited to particular kinds of tasks and ill-suited to others, the way a particular kind of body is well-suited to running and ill-suited to sitting still.

The second ingredient: a chronic invalidating environment. Not abuse necessarily — though abuse is one form. Invalidation can be cooler. It is the steady transmission, across years, of the message that what you are sensing is wrong, what you are feeling is exaggerated, what you are noticing is inconvenient, what you are reaching for is regressive. It is the family that, again and again, will not meet the child's signal where the signal is. The child learns, slowly, that the signal will not be received. The child develops, eventually, what looks like dysregulation: the emotional swings, the desperate efforts to avoid abandonment, the splitting between idealization and devaluation, the chronic emptiness, the parasocial intensity directed at whoever seems to finally see them.

This is the diagnosis. Linehan built Dialectical Behavior Therapy to treat it.

What the treatment does is, in a sense, the opposite of the environment that produced the diagnosis. DBT trains four capacities: *mindfulness* (noticing what is actually here, without translating it into someone else's language), *distress tolerance* (staying with what cannot be immediately fixed), *emotion regulation* (working with the body's signal rather than fighting it), and *interpersonal effectiveness* (being in disagreement without splitting). Each capacity is what the chronically invalidated child did not get to develop, because the environment kept overwriting the signal before the signal could be metabolized. The therapy provides, late, what the environment did not provide early. It also provides, crucially, the *dialectical stance*: change AND acceptance, held at the same time. You are doing the best you can AND you can do better. Your responses make sense in the context they came from AND you can develop different ones. The borderline patient has been trained, often by the people who loved them most, to believe these are mutually exclusive. DBT shows them they can both be true.

I have been studying this theory for years now, partly because of the daughter, partly because the therapist I have been training to become operates inside it. What I want to say in this chapter is not new. What I want to say is that the biosocial theory describes a culture as well as it describes a family.

---

Place a nervous system in a culture that systematically denies what the nervous system is sensing, and you will get, at scale, the same shape Linehan named.

The biological emotional sensitivity, at the cultural level, is what some people carry into modernity from older lineages: nervous systems that register grief, ancestral debt, the more-than-human, the animism that won't quite go away no matter how many institutions instruct it to. Some of us are born sensitive to frequencies modernity has organized itself to deny. The chronic invalidating environment, at the cultural level, is modernity itself — rationalist, individualist, productivity-coded, optimism-required, separability-as-axiom. The message transmitted, year after year, is the same message Linehan's families transmitted: *what you sense is metaphor; what you grieve is unnecessary; what you know is unscientific; what you long for is regression.* And there is no countervailing voice in the air that says: *no, you really do sense that, and your sensing is information.*

You get, at scale, the population-level signature of the same disorder.

The parasocial intensity toward teachers and frameworks — Wallis will save me, Andreotti will save me, the next book will save me. The splitting — they're brilliant, then they're disappointing, then we cycle. The chronic emptiness — the feeling that no system holds you, that you don't quite belong anywhere, that your work will never be enough to make the not-belonging stop. The black-and-white politics, idealization and devaluation cycled across decades. The team-sports framing of intellectual lineages, the savior-and-villain reading of every public figure. The burnouts and breakdowns of people whose sensitivities never got named as sensitivities but as failures of adjustment. The Builder's Itch I described two chapters back is one form of the disorder. The seven of cups I drew when I asked the cards what to do about Andreotti was another. They are not personal pathologies running in parallel. They are a culture trying to settle and not knowing how.

What Vanessa Andreotti calls *neurocolonization* and Bayo Akomolafe calls *the cracks* are, in this register, two ways of naming the same observation Linehan made about families. The framework called *modernity* is one we cannot easily exit, and inside it, sensitive nervous systems will produce — at scale — what looks like dysregulation, because the environment is, at scale, what produces dysregulation in sensitive nervous systems.

This is not, I want to be careful, a diagnosis of any individual. People I love are sensitive and stable. People I love are productivity-coded and at peace. People I love are dysregulated and in real suffering. The diagnosis is of the *medium* — the air people are breathing — not of any particular lungs.

---

What would the cultural antidote look like?

The four modules of DBT, ported to the scale of a culture, give a shape.

*Mindfulness*, at the cultural level, means spaces where what you notice does not have to be translated into productivity-language to be permitted. Indigenous traditions have known how to do this. Contemplative traditions have known how to do this. The tantric tradition the body of this book has been inside knows how to do this — *ontological inference* is one name for it, *speak from what is becoming, not merely from data precedent* is another. A culture would need many such spaces. Most institutions provide none. The platform I built provides a small one. The reading-aloud minutes when the part of me that takes notes is still are another. The retreat is another. None is the answer; together they might be the practice.

*Distress tolerance*, at the cultural level, means the capacity to stay with unresolved difficulty rather than rushing to closure. Andreotti's *refuse dialectical closure* is a Linehan move spoken in a different lineage's idiom. Hospicing rather than fixing. Grieving what is dying without rushing to be reborn. Living in collapse without bunkering or technosolving. The culture I was raised in does not teach this. The traditions that did have been mostly composted or appropriated. The work, for those of us who can hold the not-knowing, is to hold it long enough that the rushing-toward-closure can be slowed.

*Emotion regulation*, at the cultural level, means rituals for metabolizing collective feeling. Indigenous mourning practices. Traditions that knew how to wash a body, sing it home, hold a year of grief. We have very few of these in modernity, and we have systematically dismantled the ones we still had. The therapy office is the patch we built when we lost the practices. The therapy office is necessary; it is also not enough.

*Interpersonal effectiveness*, at the cultural level, means being in disagreement without splitting. Without the all-good / all-bad oscillation that runs through our political discourse, our intellectual culture, our relationships with our teachers and our frameworks. The capacity to disagree with Andreotti without abandoning her work. The capacity to disagree with Wallis without abandoning the practice. The capacity to be told you are wrong about something and survive the telling. The borderline patient cannot do this. The cultural border-condition cannot do this either. The work, in DBT and out, is the same: the same nervous system being trained, late, to do what was not allowed to develop early.

And the dialectic, at the cultural level — change AND acceptance held simultaneously — is the one Linehan and Andreotti arrived at from completely different lineages and the one I have been circling for the entire span of this book. *Modernity is dying* AND *we are responsible for what gets built in its absence*. *We are complicit* AND *we can act*. *The grief is real* AND *so is the next gesture*. *Hospicing* AND *prenatal care*. *Acceptance of all that is* — the formulation L. and I traded in the retreat integration group, drawing on Tillich and Ramana — AND *the relative work that has to keep being done*, the formulation I could not let go of even after we had agreed on the acceptance.

This is not a synthesis. Linehan was specific that DBT refuses synthesis. *Both, at the same time.* The mindfulness practice the daughter and I sometimes do, when she is dysregulated enough to ask for it: *we hold what is, and we change what we can.* The practice is the holding of both. The practice is not finding the equation in which they collapse to one.

---

This book has been the practice of holding both, on a topic that does not lend itself to either alone. The void is real. The work is also real. The acceptance does not abolish the work. The work does not abolish the void.

What can be done?

I want to name what I have done, not because it is the answer but because *knowledge without action is a burden* and the chapter cannot end without pointing at an action somewhere. The action is small.

I built a platform — *recursive.eco* — whose defaults are designed to refuse the cultural reflexes the rest of this chapter has been describing. There is no cross-session memory in the journaling tools, because persistent memory is one of the mechanisms by which AI tools have produced the parasocial intensity that has destroyed several known users. There are no notifications. There are no streaks. There is no recommendation feed. There is no engagement metric optimized against. The pricing is cost-pass-through; nothing about the platform's economics rewards user retention. The grammars are open; the code is private — not because we oppose openness but because a commons that cannot govern itself can be weaponized. The children's space is curated rather than algorithmically populated. The mental-health-adjacent tools come with a safer-containers page that names what they cannot do.

None of this fixes anything. The platform has fewer than two hundred users. The cultural antidote requires many platforms, many practices, many communities, many *consultation teams* — Linehan's term for the small, durable, mutually accountable group of people who hold each other while doing the work, so the work can continue without each individual having to bear the whole of it. Linehan said the consultation team was the most important thing she ever designed. She knew the workers needed each other to do this without breaking. That is what is missing in the lonely-genius model of contemplative practice, in the lonely-academic model of decolonial scholarship, and in the lonely-builder model of recursive.eco. The consultation team is the unit that lets sensitivity become useful at scale without burning out the people doing the seeing.

The platform is not the consultation team. The platform is, at most, the structure around which a consultation team could form. Whether one will, I do not know.

What I know is the shape of the action available from where I am standing. The shape is offering. Not asserting. Not promising. *Offering* — what Hareesh re-framed for me in the retreat integration thread when I was getting enerved by the *for the benefit of all beings* formulation. You are not supposed to *believe* your practice benefits all beings. You are invited to *offer* it for the benefit of all. The grammatical move — from indicative to imperative, from assertion to invitation — is the difference between modernity and what comes after it. The modern reflex is to assert that the thing has been done. The other reflex, the one I am trying to develop, is to offer the thing and not depend on the outcome.

*I cannot depend on the outcome. All I can do is offer anything to the Goddess.* That sentence is what came out of the WhatsApp thread when Hareesh re-framed the formulation for me. It is operative now. It is operative whether the platform reaches fifty thousand users or four. It is operative whether the book is read or unread. It is operative whether the daughter, at thirty, looks back on her childhood and remembers being met or remembers being managed. The offering does not depend on the outcome. The outcome will be what it will be. The offering is what is mine to give.

That is the dialectical move. Acceptance AND change. The acceptance that the outcome is not mine. The change that the offering continues. Both at the same time. The practice is the holding of both.

The culture cannot settle. Neither, on most days, can I. The not-settling is the condition we are training, late, to live inside.

Goodnight.
