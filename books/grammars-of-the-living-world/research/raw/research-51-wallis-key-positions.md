## Wallis's Core Position: Recognition, Not Belief

From *The Recognition Sutras* (translation and commentary on Kshemaraja's *Pratyabhijñā-hṛdayam*):

The central claim of the Recognition (*pratyabhijñā*) school of Kashmir Shaivism is that liberation is not achieved through gaining something new but through recognizing what was always already the case: that one's own awareness is a direct expression of universal divine Consciousness (*cit-śakti*).

Key distinction Wallis makes: recognition is not a propositional belief. It is more like recognizing your own face in a mirror you didn't know was a mirror. You don't *believe* that's your face; you *see* it. The seeing changes everything.

## Svātantrya-śakti: The Power of Autonomy

From *Tantra Illuminated* and research-14:

Abhinavagupta's *Tantraloka* teaches that *svātantrya-śakti* — the Power of Autonomy — is the one fundamental quality of Consciousness, and it belongs exclusively to the whole, not to individual parts.

"He has only one fundamental quality, which implies all the others. Therefore, the truly correct precept with regard to Śiva is that He is united exclusively with the Power of Autonomy."

The individual is "the whole in contracted form." The same five powers that characterize the whole — awareness, bliss, will, knowledge, and action — exist in the individual but in limited, contracted versions. The path of *pratyabhijñā* is the individual discovering that its apparent freedom was always the freedom of the whole expressing through it.

Wallis's nonduality explicitly includes duality: "Instead of a nondual view that excludes duality and says duality is wrong, this is a nondual view that includes duality, as a valid level of experience, though not an absolute one."

## The Kriya/Karma Distinction

From research-14:

When the Power of Acting (*kriyā-śakti*) operates through the contracted ego (believing itself a separate agent), the result is *karma* — bound, effortful action that creates impressions and binds. When the same power operates through one who has "recognized" identity with the whole, the result is *kriya* — spontaneous, aligned action that does not bind.

"We turn kriya into karma when we resist kriya or attempt to control it."

This maps onto the axiom question: if you practice *in order to* achieve recognition, the motive (controlling the outcome) turns kriya into karma. The practice must be undertaken without the goal of achieving the recognition. But this creates the circularity: you must already hold the axiom (wholeness) in order to practice in the way that reveals it.

## Near Enemies of the Truth

From Wallis's 2023 book *Near Enemies of the Truth*:

Wallis identifies spiritual teachings that are "near enemies" — teachings that sound true and are partially true but, when taken as the whole truth, lead practitioners away from genuine awakening.

Key near enemies relevant to the Tillich dialogue:
- "You create your own reality" — near enemy of the truth that consciousness participates in the creation of experience
- "Become the best version of yourself" — near enemy of the truth that you are already whole
- "Follow your bliss" — near enemy of the truth that joy is a sign of alignment

Each of these, Wallis argues, can become a form of spiritual bypassing — using a partial truth to avoid confronting the full difficulty of the human situation.

**This is directly relevant to the axiom argument.** If "you are already whole" is presented as a truth to be discovered rather than an axiom to be practiced, it becomes a near enemy — a spiritual teaching that bypasses the existential confrontation Tillich insists is unavoidable.

## Wallis on Practice and Foundation

From the letter (research-49), Wallis has written:

"If you practice yoga from the perspective that you are not good enough as you are, or that there is something wrong with you that needs fixing, then your yoga cannot fulfill its ultimate purpose because it is a practice founded on wrong understanding."

This instruction presupposes the very recognition it claims to produce. You must begin by accepting your wholeness in order to realize your wholeness. This is the circularity the Axiom book needs to address.

## The Five Powers

From research-14 and *Tantra Illuminated*:

The five powers of absolute Consciousness:
1. *Cit-śakti* (awareness)
2. *Ānanda-śakti* (bliss)
3. *Icchā-śakti* (will)
4. *Jñāna-śakti* (knowledge)
5. *Kriyā-śakti* (action)

Each exists in the individual in contracted form. Recognition is the discovery that the contracted versions are expressions of the unconstructed whole. The individual is not separate from the whole; the individual IS the whole, experiencing itself through contraction.

## The 112 Dharanas of the Vijñāna-bhairava-tantra

The VBT presents 112 meditation practices — "gateways" — each of which is a method for recognizing the nature of consciousness. They range from breath practices to contemplation of sound to attention to space to practices involving sex, sleep, and death.

For the Axiom book: these are reframed as "practices of the axiom" — not proofs of inherent value but methods for living inside the commitment. Each dharana is a way of enacting the axiom through the body, with the breath, with attention itself.

## Where Wallis Converges with Tillich

Both reject naive theism (God as a being among beings).
Both insist on something beyond the subject-object structure.
Both acknowledge the circularity of the commitment.
Both see the courage/practice as primary, not the doctrine.

## Where Wallis Diverges from Tillich

Tillich: The pointing is all we can do. Absolute faith is "without the safety of words and concepts."
Wallis: The pointing can arrive. Recognition is experientially verifiable.

Tillich: The ground cannot be named without making it an object subject to doubt.
Wallis: Consciousness recognizing itself is structurally different from a subject encountering an object.

Tillich: The theological circle is honest and must be named as a circle.
Wallis: The recognition circle resolves — the circle opens into direct experience.

## The Hinge Question

Can consciousness recognizing itself be distinguished from a self-confirming loop?

Tillich would say: from inside the experience, no. From outside, no (because there is no outside). The circle is all there is. Courage means living inside it.

Wallis would say: the distinction between recognition and construction is experientially available — it has a qualitative character that self-constructed states do not. But he would acknowledge that this claim cannot be verified from outside the practice.

The honest position for the book: this is the question that the book sits with rather than resolves. The book's contribution is to name it clearly — axiom or recognition? — and to show that the answer determines what honest practice looks like.
