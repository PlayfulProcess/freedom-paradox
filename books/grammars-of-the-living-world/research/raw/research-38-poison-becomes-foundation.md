# **Life's deepest pattern: poison becomes foundation**

Every major leap in biological complexity began with a catastrophe. **Mitochondria were invaders. Oxygen was poison. Calcium was toxic waste. Viruses were parasites.** Each became indispensable — the structural material, the energy source, the genetic toolkit, the very engine of the complex life that followed. This is not a marginal pattern but the central grammar of evolutionary innovation: the conversion of enemies into allies, toxins into building blocks, parasites into partners. Lynn Margulis called it symbiogenesis. The geological record calls it the Great Oxidation Event. The human genome calls it 8% retroviral DNA. And right now, in recycling plants and ocean garbage patches, bacteria are beginning to eat plastic.

This report assembles the evidence for what may be the most important counternarrative in biology: that life's creative engine runs not primarily on competition and destruction but on incorporation and metabolic transformation — the literal digestion of crisis into capability. For *Grammars of the Living World*, this material stands as the biological counterpoint to invasion ecology as overshoot, demonstrating that the pattern of invasion-followed-by-integration operates at every scale of life, from organelles to ecosystems.

## **The woman who proved that cells are committees**

In 1967, a young biologist named Lynn Margulis (then Lynn Sagan, recently divorced from astronomer Carl Sagan) submitted a paper to the *Journal of Theoretical Biology* after approximately **15 journals had rejected it** — sometimes without reading it, based on what later commentators described as "not-so-well founded scientific criticisms" mixed with "explicit intellectual and social prejudices." The paper, "On the Origin of Mitosing Cells," proposed something the biological establishment found absurd: that the fundamental organelles of complex cells — mitochondria and chloroplasts — were once free-living bacteria that had been engulfed by, or had invaded, ancestal host cells roughly **2 billion years ago**.

Margulis argued that mitochondria descended from **alpha-proteobacteria** (related to modern *Rickettsiales*) that entered an archaeal host cell — most likely as parasites or predators, not cooperative partners. The initial relationship was exploitative. Chloroplasts, she proposed, were cyanobacteria swallowed in a separate event. The eukaryotic cell — the cell type that constitutes every animal, plant, fungus, and protist on Earth — was not an individual but a **"committee,"** as Harvard's Andrew Knoll later phrased it: a community of formerly independent organisms fused into a new kind of being.

The evidence accumulated relentlessly through the 1970s and 1980s. Mitochondria and chloroplasts have their own **circular DNA** (distinct from nuclear DNA and resembling bacterial chromosomes), **double membranes** (the inner from the original bacterium, the outer from the host's engulfing vacuole), **70S ribosomes** identical in size to bacterial ribosomes, and they replicate by **binary fission** — bacterial cell division. The breakthrough came in 1978 when Robert Schwartz and Margaret Dayhoff published phylogenetic analyses in *Science* confirming that mitochondrial DNA sequences were far more closely related to alpha-proteobacteria than to the nuclear DNA of their own cells. By the early 1980s, the theory was becoming textbook science. In 1983, Margulis was elected to the U.S. National Academy of Sciences. Even Richard Dawkins conceded in 1995: "I greatly admire Lynn Margulis's sheer courage and stamina in sticking by the endosymbiosis theory, and carrying it through from being an unorthodoxy to an orthodoxy."

What Margulis was less credited for — and what matters most for the argument of *Grammars of the Living World* — was her broader philosophical claim. She defined **symbiogenesis** as "the origin of a new organ, metabolic pathway, behavior, tissue, or other feature as a result of long-term hereditary symbiosis." She argued that inherited variation significant for evolution comes not mainly from random mutations but from **"the long-lasting intimacy of strangers"** — the fusion of genomes through symbiotic merger. In *Microcosmos* (1986), co-written with her son Dorion Sagan, she wrote the line that crystallizes the entire counternarrative: **"Life did not take over the globe by combat, but by networking."** The fuller passage is worth quoting: "The view of evolution as chronic bloody competition among individuals and species, a popular distortion of Darwin's notion of 'survival of the fittest,' dissolves before a new view of continual cooperation, strong interaction, and mutual dependence among life forms."

Margulis did not work in isolation. She drew on a suppressed Russian tradition. **Konstantin Mereschkowski** coined the term "symbiogenesis" in 1905, proposing that chloroplasts originated from cyanobacteria. **Boris Kozo-Polyansky** published *A New Principle of Biology: Essay on the Theory of Symbiogenesis* in Moscow in 1924 — the first work to integrate symbiogenesis with Darwinian natural selection. Kozo-Polyansky was ridiculed, never recognized in his lifetime, and never read in the West due to the language barrier. Margulis learned of his work only in 1975, eight years after publishing her own near-identical theory independently. She spent her final years overseeing the first English translation of Kozo-Polyansky's book, published by Harvard University Press in 2010, the year before her death. The Russian lineage — from Kropotkin's observations of cooperation in Siberian ecosystems through Mereschkowski and Kozo-Polyansky to Margulis — represents a continuous counter-tradition emphasizing cooperation in evolution, rooted in ecological observations of harsh environments where survival depends on partnership.

Not all of Margulis's claims held up. Her proposal that spirochete bacteria became the ancestors of eukaryotic flagella and cilia has not been accepted — no DNA or genome has been found associated with the flagellar apparatus. Her broader assertion that symbiogenesis (beyond organelles) is the primary mechanism of speciation across all eukaryotes remains contested. But the core insight — that the eukaryotic cell is a symbiotic merger, that the most consequential innovations in evolutionary history arose from incorporation rather than competition — is now foundational biology.

## **When oxygen was the world's worst pollutant**

The Great Oxidation Event, beginning approximately **2.46 billion years ago** and spanning roughly **400 million years**, was the most catastrophic pollution crisis in Earth's history — and the precondition for every complex organism alive today.

Cyanobacteria evolved oxygenic photosynthesis possibly as early as **2.9–3.4 billion years ago**, hundreds of millions of years before oxygen accumulated in the atmosphere. The delay occurred because the planet's chemistry consumed oxygen faster than biology could produce it. Dissolved ferrous iron in the oceans reacted with O₂ to form insoluble ferric iron, precipitating to the ocean floor in alternating layers visible today as **banded iron formations** — the source of the world's major iron ore deposits in Australia, Canada, and South Africa. Volcanic gases, atmospheric methane from methanogens, and oxidative weathering of continental surfaces all acted as oxygen sinks. Only after these sinks were saturated did free oxygen begin accumulating in the atmosphere.

What followed was mass death on an unprecedented scale. Oxygen generates **reactive oxygen species** — superoxide radicals, hydrogen peroxide, hydroxyl radicals — that destroy the iron-sulfur clusters essential to anaerobic metabolism, oxidize genetic material, aggregate proteins, and disrupt membranes. For the anaerobic organisms that dominated Earth, oxygen was not a resource but a lethal poison. The event is described in the scientific literature as "arguably the largest mass extinction in Earth's history." Survivors retreated to anaerobic refugia — deep subsurface rock, ocean sediments, intestinal tracts — where obligate anaerobes persist to this day. In the human gut, *Bacteroides* species outnumber facultative anaerobes by **four orders of magnitude**, descendants of organisms that fled the oxygen catastrophe billions of years ago.

The transition from oxygen-as-poison to oxygen-as-fuel proceeded through identifiable stages. Early antioxidant enzymes — **superoxide reductases and peroxidases** — evolved to tolerate oxygen without exploiting it. Recent phylogenetic analyses date the origin of ROS-scavenging enzymes to **3.5–4.1 billion years ago**, suggesting oxygen was already a local threat in microenvironments around early photosynthetic organisms long before the GOE. Remarkably, **93% of analyzed strict anaerobes** possess at least one antioxidant enzyme, suggesting that the Last Universal Common Ancestor already had some oxygen-coping capacity. The disproportionating enzymes (superoxide dismutase, catalase) arose later, enabling organisms to survive at higher oxygen concentrations. Facultative anaerobes — organisms that can switch between aerobic and anaerobic metabolism — represent a transitional strategy still common today.

The payoff for adapting to oxygen was enormous. Aerobic respiration yields approximately **36–38 ATP per glucose molecule** (revised modern estimates: \~30–32), compared to just **2 ATP from fermentation** — roughly an **18-fold increase** in energy extraction. This surplus powered the evolution of complex multicellular life. The critical step was **endosymbiosis**: surviving archaea incorporated aerobic alpha-proteobacteria, which became mitochondria. The parasite that could breathe oxygen became the engine of the eukaryotic cell.

The GOE also triggered the **Huronian glaciation** (\~2.45–2.22 billion years ago), the longest ice age in Earth's history, lasting some 300 million years. Cyanobacterial oxygen destroyed atmospheric methane (a potent greenhouse gas), shattering Earth's thermal blanket. Combined with a faint young Sun at only 70–80% of modern brightness, this plunged the planet into Snowball Earth conditions. The organisms that caused the pollution crisis nearly froze the entire planet.

The analogy to current conditions is both illuminating and cautionary. The rate of modern atmospheric change is roughly **one million times faster** than the GOE transition. Pre-industrial CO₂ levels held steady at \~280 ppm for 6,000 years; current levels exceed 420 ppm after just \~200 years. The GOE demonstrates that life can metabolize its way through a pollution crisis — but over hundreds of millions of years, and at the cost of mass extinction of the life forms that caused it.

## **How toxic waste became the skeleton of complex life**

The story of calcium follows the same grammar as oxygen, compressed into a different timescale and material form. Calcium is simultaneously one of the most dangerous ions in biology and the most indispensable structural and signaling molecule in complex life. This paradox is not accidental — it is the paradox of the toxin-to-building-block pattern itself.

Free calcium is lethal to cells because it **precipitates phosphate** at concentrations above \~10 µM. Since life's energy currency (ATP) and genetic encoding (DNA/RNA) are phosphate-based, high intracellular calcium is fundamentally incompatible with phosphate-based biochemistry. Calcium also causes protein aggregation, membrane disruption, chromatin condensation, and activation of degradative enzymes. Every living cell on Earth maintains its internal calcium concentration at approximately **100 nanomolar** — a **10,000 to 20,000-fold gradient** below the 1–2 millimolar concentration outside the cell. This gradient is maintained by energy-expensive molecular pumps: **plasma membrane Ca²⁺-ATPases** that actively extrude calcium, **SERCA pumps** that sequester it in the endoplasmic reticulum, and **sodium-calcium exchangers** that use the sodium gradient to push calcium out.

**Kenneth Simkiss** proposed in 1977 that biomineralization — the formation of mineral structures by organisms — originated as a **detoxification strategy**. Organisms under pressure from rising ocean calcium levels precipitated toxic Ca²⁺ into inert carbonate deposits as waste disposal. The earliest biomineralized structures appear in the geological record around **550–541 million years ago**, with the iconic fossil *Cloudina* — one of the first animals to form a calcium carbonate skeleton. By 535–510 million years ago, calcareous skeletons were forming globally across sponges, cnidarians, and bilaterian phyla.

The Cambrian Explosion's profusion of hard structures — shells, exoskeletons, spines — was built on a biochemical toolkit originally evolved to pump poison out of cells. Biomineralization evolved **independently at least 20 times** across different phyla, suggesting that the same ancestral calcium-management machinery was co-opted repeatedly. A 2019 PNAS study by Gilbert, Porter, and Knoll showed that cnidarians, mollusks, echinoderms, and tunicates all use the same **amorphous calcium carbonate precursor phases** and crystallization mechanisms — convergent solutions from shared detoxification ancestry. The anti-predation pressure of the Cambrian then selected for deployment of these systems into external armor, but the machinery predated the armor by billions of years.

The deepest irony is in calcium signaling. Evolution exploited the very gradient that cells maintain for survival — the 20,000-fold concentration difference across membranes — as a **signaling system**. Brief, controlled calcium "spikes" (from \~100 nM resting to \~1 µM during signaling) decoded by calmodulin and calcium-dependent kinases now control muscle contraction, neurotransmission, hormone secretion, fertilization, gene transcription, immune response, and cell death. Calcium is simultaneously **the most important signaling ion and the primary executioner in cell death** — the same molecule, the same gradient, serving creation and destruction depending on whether the influx is controlled or catastrophic.

## **Bacteria are already eating our waste**

The question of whether life can metabolize industrial pollutants is not theoretical. It is already happening, on timescales that compress the GOE's hundreds of millions of years into decades.

In 2016, Shosuke Yoshida and Kohei Oda published in *Science* the discovery of **Ideonella sakaiensis** 201-F6, a bacterium isolated from PET-contaminated sediment at a plastic bottle recycling facility in Sakai City, Japan. The organism produces two enzymes — **PETase** and **MHETase** — that together break PET plastic into its constituent monomers, terephthalic acid and ethylene glycol, which the bacterium then metabolizes. The wild-type organism degrades a thin PET film in approximately **six weeks**. By 2022, a University of Texas team led by Hal Alper used machine-learning-guided protein engineering to create **FAST-PETase**, which degrades untreated post-consumer PET from 51 different products almost completely **within one week**, some in as little as 24 hours. The French company CARBIOS has built the world's first industrial-scale enzymatic PET recycling plant, expected to process **50,000 tons per year** by late 2025\.

PET is not the only plastic under biological assault. **Pestalotiopsis microspora**, an endophytic fungus discovered in 2011 in the Ecuadorian Amazon by Scott Strobel's Yale lab, degrades polyester polyurethane as its sole carbon source under both aerobic and anaerobic conditions. **Wax worms** (*Galleria mellonella*) digest polyethylene — a 2022 study in *Nature Communications* identified two enzymes in their saliva, belonging to the phenol oxidase family, that oxidize and depolymerize PE at room temperature within hours. **Mealworms** (*Tenebrio molitor*) consume polystyrene, with gut bacteria including *Acinetobacter*, *Serratia*, and *Pseudomonas* species performing the degradation. And in a discovery with profound implications for evolutionary theory, **nylon-eating bacteria** (*Flavobacterium* sp. K172) were found in 1975 near a Japanese nylon factory — just **40 years** after nylon's invention in 1935\. The nylonase enzymes are carried on plasmids and evolved from pre-existing beta-lactamase family enzymes through as few as **two amino acid substitutions**.

The timescale compression is striking. Nylon invented 1935, nylon-eating bacteria found 1975: \~40 years. PET introduced 1941, *Ideonella* found 2016: \~75 years. Polyurethane commercialized 1950s, *Pestalotiopsis* found 2011: \~60 years. These are not truly novel enzymes but **modifications of existing enzymatic scaffolds** — PETase is a modified cutinase (evolved to degrade cutin, a natural plant polyester), nylonase is a modified beta-lactamase. Microbial populations achieve this through enormous population sizes (10⁹–10¹² cells per gram of soil), generation times of minutes to hours, and horizontal gene transfer via plasmids. The contrast with the GOE is instructive: oxygen metabolism required inventing fundamentally new biochemistry over hundreds of millions of years; plastic metabolism requires tweaking existing enzymes over decades.

**PFAS "forever chemicals"** represent the harder problem. The carbon-fluorine bond (\~485 kJ/mol) is among the strongest in organic chemistry, and naturally occurring fluorinated compounds are vanishingly rare — biology has had almost no evolutionary exposure to fluorine chemistry. Yet even here, cracks are appearing. **Acidimicrobium sp. strain A6**, discovered by Peter Jaffé at Princeton, removed up to **60% of PFOA and PFOS** in 100-day incubations. Lawrence Wackett argued in 2024 (*Microbial Biotechnology*) that PFAS persistence reflects **"evolutionary insufficiency, not inherent enzyme inadequacy"** — the C-F bond can be cleaved by enzymes, but evolution has not yet produced efficient multi-step pathways. Given sufficient time and selective pressure, Ernest Gale's 1951 **"Microbial Infallibility Hypothesis"** suggests that if energy can be gained from a compound, microorganisms will eventually evolve to extract it.

Meanwhile, the **Plastisphere** — a term coined in 2013 by Erik Zettler, Tracy Mincer, and Linda Amaral-Zettler — describes the diverse microbial communities colonizing ocean plastic debris. Scanning electron microscopy reveals **pits in plastic surfaces conforming to bacterial cell shapes**, suggesting active polymer hydrolysis. A 2025 KAUST study found bacteria with functional PETase enzymes distributed **across the world's oceans**, carrying a molecular signature ("M5 motif") for PET-degrading capability. Life is not waiting for permission to colonize synthetic substrates.

## **The viral code inside every pregnancy**

Endogenous retroviruses constitute approximately **8% of the human genome** — roughly four times more DNA than all protein-coding genes combined. These are the fossilized remains of retroviral infections that inserted themselves into our ancestors' germline DNA millions of years ago and were passed vertically from parent to offspring ever since. Most are degraded fragments. But some have been **"domesticated"** — co-opted by the host genome for essential functions.

The most dramatic example is **syncytin**. In 2000, Sha Mi and colleagues published in *Nature* the discovery that syncytin-1, the envelope gene of Human Endogenous Retrovirus W (HERV-W), encodes a fusogenic protein essential for forming the **syncytiotrophoblast** — the fused cell layer at the maternal-fetal interface of the human placenta. Without this viral gene, cytotrophoblast cells cannot fuse into the multinucleated tissue that mediates nutrient exchange between mother and fetus. Knockout experiments in mice by Dupressoir et al. (2009) showed that deleting syncytin-A caused embryonic death between days 11.5–13.5 of gestation. As virologist Welkin Johnson put it: "You could argue that without retroviruses you wouldn't have mammals."

The pattern is deeper than a single gene. At least **six independent syncytin capture events** have been identified across mammals — primates, rodents, rabbits, carnivores, ruminants — each from unrelated retroviruses, each convergently providing the same cell-fusion function. Thierry Heidmann's group speculated that the capture of a founding syncytin-like gene was "instrumental in the dramatic transition from egg-laying to placental mammals" roughly 150 million years ago. Another domesticated retroviral gene, **Suppressyn**, protects the placenta from viral infection — a viral turncoat defending against its own kind.

The **ARC gene** extends the pattern into neuroscience. ARC (Activity-Regulated Cytoskeleton-associated protein) is critical for long-term memory consolidation. In 2018, two teams simultaneously discovered that mammalian ARC forms **viral-like capsids** — icosahedral protein shells nearly identical to HIV capsids by cryo-electron microscopy — that package its own mRNA and transfer it between neurons. ARC derives from a Ty3/gypsy retrotransposon domesticated more than **250 million years ago**. Knockout mice lack long-term memory but retain short-term memory. A retroviral capsid protein is literally how your neurons communicate to form lasting memories.

**Wolbachia** demonstrates the parasite-to-partner transition in real time. This intracellular bacterium infects an estimated **20–76% of all arthropod species** and is probably the most prevalent vertically transmitted symbiont on Earth. In most arthropod hosts, Wolbachia is a reproductive parasite — manipulating host reproduction through cytoplasmic incompatibility, male killing, feminization, or induced parthenogenesis. But in the parasitic wasp *Asobara tabida*, Wolbachia has crossed the threshold into obligate mutualism: females cured of Wolbachia with antibiotics **completely fail to produce mature oocytes**. In filarial nematodes (the worms causing river blindness and lymphatic filariasis), Wolbachia is so deeply integrated that antibiotic treatment **kills the worms** — Wolbachia provides essential haem biosynthesis the worms cannot perform alone. The World Mosquito Program has exploited Wolbachia's reproductive manipulation to fight dengue fever: Aedes aegypti mosquitoes transinfected with Wolbachia showed a **77% reduction in dengue incidence** and **86% reduction in hospitalizations** in Yogyakarta, Indonesia. As of January 2026, the program protects over **16.1 million people** across 16 countries.

The human microbiome completes the picture. The revised estimate from Sender, Fuchs, and Milo (2016, Weizmann Institute) puts the ratio of bacterial to human cells at approximately **1:1** (\~3.8 × 10¹³ bacteria vs. \~3.0 × 10¹³ human cells) — correcting the widely cited but never rigorously calculated 10:1 ratio from Thomas Luckey's 1972 back-of-the-envelope estimate. The genetic diversity of the microbiome exceeds the human genome by roughly **100-fold**. Gut bacteria synthesize vitamins, train the immune system, produce approximately **95% of the body's serotonin**, and influence mood, anxiety, and cognition through the gut-brain axis. Germ-free mice show abnormal immune development, altered brain chemistry, and increased anxiety-like behavior.

Coral and zooxanthellae (Symbiodiniaceae), lichens, and mycorrhizal networks extend these partnerships across ecosystems. Corals receive **90–95%** of their energy from photosynthetic dinoflagellate symbionts. Lichens — fungi that, in Trevor Goward's phrase, "have discovered agriculture" — were among the first organisms to colonize dry land \~600 million years ago, chemically weathering bare rock to create soil for all terrestrial life that followed. The "Wood Wide Web" of mycorrhizal networks connecting trees through fungal hyphae, popularized by Suzanne Simard's 1997 *Nature* paper, has been subject to important critique: Karst, Jones, and Hoeksema's 2023 analysis in *Nature Ecology & Evolution* found that **fewer than 20% of well-controlled experiments** showed seedlings performing better when connected to fungal networks, and there is currently **no peer-reviewed evidence** for the claim that "mother trees" preferentially send resources to offspring through common mycorrhizal networks. The mycorrhizal relationship itself is not in dispute — fungi are essential for normal tree growth — but the romantic narrative of intentional resource-sharing requires more rigorous evidence. The continuum from mutualism to parasitism operates even within mycorrhizal relationships, depending on context.

## **What makes a parasite become a partner**

The conditions that drive parasitism-to-mutualism transitions are not mysterious — they are increasingly well-characterized through both theory and experiment. The single most important factor is **transmission mode**. Paul Ewald's 1987 framework established that vertical transmission (parent to offspring) aligns the evolutionary interests of host and symbiont, selecting for reduced virulence and eventually mutualism. Horizontal transmission (between unrelated hosts) does not penalize exploitative strategies. This has been confirmed experimentally: when *Parachlamydia acanthamoebae* was forced to transmit horizontally in amoebae, it upregulated virulence genes. When transmitted vertically, it became benign.

Environmental stress is the second critical driver. The **Stress Gradient Hypothesis**, proposed by Mark Bertness and Ragan Callaway in 1994, predicts that facilitation and cooperation increase under harsh environmental conditions while competition dominates in benign environments. A 2013 meta-analysis of 206 studies across six continents found consistent support: as stress increased, interactions shifted toward mutual support. In microbial experiments, toxic biocides caused interactions between four species to shift from negative to positive. Deep-sea hydrothermal vents, hot springs, and Arctic ecosystems all show elevated cooperation. The Russian naturalist tradition — from Kropotkin through Margulis — was rooted in exactly this observation: in Siberian ecosystems, where conditions are harsh and resources scarce, cooperation is visibly more salient than Malthusian competition.

**Peter Kropotkin's** *Mutual Aid: A Factor of Evolution* (1902) anticipated these findings by a century. His central thesis — that "those who learn to combine so as mutually to support each other" are the truly fit — was dismissed as anarchist ideology by the British and American establishment. Stephen Jay Gould's rehabilitation was succinct: "Kropotkin was no crackpot." His distinction between competition between individuals and collective struggle against harsh environments maps directly onto modern cooperation theory.

**Martin Nowak's** 2006 *Science* paper, "Five Rules for the Evolution of Cooperation," formalized the mechanisms mathematically. Each reduces to a benefit-to-cost ratio exceeding a critical threshold: **kin selection** (Hamilton's r \> c/b), **direct reciprocity** (probability of future encounter w \> c/b), **indirect reciprocity** (probability of knowing reputation q \> c/b), **network reciprocity** (b/c \> k, where k is average number of neighbors), and **group selection** (b/c \> 1 \+ n/m). Nowak argued that cooperation is not an anomaly requiring special explanation but **"the third fundamental principle of evolution"** alongside mutation and natural selection. His 2011 book *SuperCooperators* called it "the master architect of evolution."

The parasitism-mutualism continuum is not a one-way street. Drew, Stevens, and King's 2021 *Nature Reviews Microbiology* analysis documented transitions in **both directions**. Context matters: the same relationship can be mutualistic under stress and parasitic under abundance. Mycorrhizal fungi help trees in nutrient-poor soils but can become carbon parasites in rich soils. **Conditional mutualism** — context-dependent shifting along the continuum — may be the norm rather than the exception. The pattern is probabilistic, not teleological.

## **We have never been individuals**

Scott Gilbert, a developmental biologist who did his master's work under Donna Haraway at Johns Hopkins, delivered a talk at UC Santa Cruz in November 2012 titled "We are all lichens." The accompanying paper, co-authored with Jan Sapp and Alfred Tauber in *The Quarterly Review of Biology*, argued that animals cannot be considered "individuals" by **any** biological criterion — anatomical, physiological, developmental, genetic, immunological, or evolutionary. The paper was dedicated to the memory of Lynn Margulis.

The **holobiont concept** — the organism plus all its associated microbes as a single evolutionary unit — had been formalized by Eugene Rosenberg and Ilana Zilber-Rosenberg at Tel Aviv University in 2008 through their **hologenome theory of evolution**. The theory arose from studying coral bleaching and proposes that the sum of genetic information in host and microbiota (the hologenome) is the unit of natural selection. A key insight: during rapid environmental change, the microbial community can shift composition in a single generation, "buying time" for the slower host genome to evolve through conventional mutation and selection. This is faster than Darwinian adaptation and slower than physiological acclimation — a third temporal scale of biological response.

The philosophical implications are vertiginous. The mealy bug example is perhaps the most extreme: its biosynthetic pathways for essential amino acids require enzymes from the host genome, a primary bacterial endosymbiont (*Tremblaya*), AND a secondary bacterial endosymbiont (*Moranella*) living inside the first. No organism is complete alone. The "individual" is a consortium.

**Anna Tsing's** *The Mushroom at the End of the World* (2015) translated this biological insight into anthropology and political philosophy. Using the matsutake mushroom — the world's most valuable mushroom, which grows only in human-disturbed forests and cannot be cultivated — as her central figure, Tsing explored what she called **"collaborative survival"** in the ruins of capitalism. Her key move was to refuse both progress narratives and despair: "In a global state of precarity, we don't have choices other than looking for life in this ruin." Landscapes are "radical tools for decentering human hubris." Pines, matsutake, and humans "cultivate each other unintentionally" — making each other's world-making possible without anyone designing the system.

**Donna Haraway's** *Staying with the Trouble* (2016) pushed further, proposing **sympoiesis** ("making-with") as the fundamental mode of biological and social existence, contrasted with autopoiesis ("self-making"). "Nothing makes itself; nothing is really autopoietic or self-organizing... Sympoiesis is a word for worlding-with, in company." Chapter 3 explicitly builds on Gilbert's holobiont work and Margulis's symbiogenesis. Haraway rejects both techno-optimism and nihilistic despair, advocating for "staying with the trouble" — living responsibly on a damaged planet. The intellectual lineage flows clearly: Margulis (biological mechanism) → Rosenberg/Zilber-Rosenberg (scientific framework) → Gilbert (biology/philosophy of individuality) → Haraway and Tsing (ethics and politics of multispecies entanglement). Gilbert studied under Haraway. Tsing dedicated her key essay to Haraway. Haraway cites them all.

Connections to non-dual philosophy are not merely metaphorical. Buddhist **pratītyasamutpāda** (dependent origination) — the core ontological principle that all phenomena arise from other pre-existing phenomena and nothing has independent, self-contained existence — maps onto holobiont thinking with startling precision. Cummiskey and Hamilton's 2017 paper in the *Journal of Buddhist Ethics* explicitly connected dependent origination to ecological interdependence, describing species as "dependently originated phenomena constituted by webs of relations, including symbiotic bacteria." Nāgārjuna's emptiness (śūnyatā) — the absence of inherent self-nature — is not nihilism but the recognition that identity is relational, exactly what Gilbert demonstrated for biological organisms. Indigenous relational ontologies, as acknowledged by both Haraway and Tsing, arrive at similar conclusions through entirely different epistemic traditions.

## **The meta-pattern and its moral stakes**

The evidence assembled here reveals a pattern operating across every scale of biological organization and every major transition in the history of life:

* **Oxygen**: toxic waste of cyanobacteria → foundation of aerobic respiration → prerequisite for all complex multicellular life  
* **Calcium**: cellular poison requiring constant active expulsion → structural material for bones, shells, coral reefs → universal intracellular signaling molecule  
* **Alpha-proteobacteria**: parasitic invaders of archaeal cells → mitochondria → the energy engine of all eukaryotic life  
* **Cyanobacteria**: engulfed by eukaryotic cells → chloroplasts → the basis of all photosynthetic eukaryotes  
* **Retroviruses**: genomic parasites → 8% of the human genome → syncytin genes essential for the mammalian placenta → ARC capsids essential for long-term memory  
* **Wolbachia**: reproductive parasite → obligate mutualist essential for oogenesis in wasps and survival of filarial nematodes → tool for fighting dengue

The pattern is not: invasion → destruction. The pattern is: **invasion → crisis → adaptation → incorporation → indispensability**. The grammar is metabolic: life digests its crises, converting threats into raw materials for the next level of complexity. This is not to say the crises are painless — the GOE killed most life on Earth, and the transition took hundreds of millions of years. But it is to say that the overshoot-and-crash narrative, while real, is only half the story. The other half is incorporation and transformation.

Can this pattern apply to industrial pollutants? The evidence is mixed but not negligible. Nylon-eating bacteria evolved in **\~40 years**. PET-degrading organisms in **\~75 years**. Engineered FAST-PETase degrades consumer PET in **24 hours**. Marine bacteria with PETase enzymes are now distributed across the world's oceans. The Plastisphere represents a genuinely novel ecosystem colonizing synthetic substrates. Lawrence Wackett's framing is critical here: PFAS persistence reflects **"evolutionary insufficiency, not inherent enzyme inadequacy."** Given geological time, microbial evolution will likely solve even the carbon-fluorine bond. The question is whether we have geological time.

This is where the ethical stakes become sharp. The evolutionary optimism inherent in the toxin-to-building-block pattern carries a dangerous corollary: if life will eventually metabolize our waste, does present destruction matter? The answer, from every thinker assembled here, is an emphatic no. **Bradshaw's work on heavy metal tolerance** provides the essential corrective: only a minority of species evolve tolerance to toxins; most simply go extinct. Evolutionary rescue is probabilistic, not guaranteed, and depends on large population sizes, standing genetic variation, and environmental change that is moderate rather than catastrophic. The current rate of environmental change — roughly **one million times faster** than the GOE — may exceed evolutionary capacity for most complex organisms.

Haraway's position is the most philosophically precise: sympoiesis — making-with — is not a promise that everything will be fine. It is a description of how life actually works, and an ethical imperative to participate in the making-with rather than accelerate the destruction. Tsing's matsutake mushroom does not prove that capitalism's ruins are acceptable; it proves that life finds ways to persist in ruins, and that those ways are worth attending to. The biological counternarrative does not excuse the crisis. It reframes it within life's demonstrated capacity to metabolize poison — while insisting that the pace and scale of current destruction may overwhelm that capacity for the organisms we care about most, including ourselves.

## **Conclusion: the grammar beneath the grammar**

The deepest grammar of the living world is neither pure competition nor pure cooperation but **metabolic transformation** — the conversion of crisis into capability, toxin into building block, enemy into essential ally. This pattern operated when oxygen killed most life on Earth and then powered all complex life. It operated when cells turned their most dangerous ion into their structural material and their most sensitive signaling molecule. It operated when viral invaders became the genetic toolkit for mammalian pregnancy and neuronal memory. It is operating now, in recycling plants in Japan and on floating plastic in the North Atlantic, as bacteria evolve to eat substances that did not exist a century ago.

For *Grammars of the Living World*, this material offers something specific: **a counternarrative to invasion ecology as overshoot that is neither denial nor false hope**. The biological evidence shows that invasion, toxicity, and parasitism are not endpoints but phases in a longer process of incorporation. This does not mean the invasion phase is benign — the GOE was catastrophic, and the current pollution crisis may be catastrophic for most complex life. But it means that the story does not end with catastrophe. The story ends, if it ends, with the poison becoming the foundation. As Margulis and Sagan wrote: life did not take over the globe by combat, but by networking. The question for our moment is whether the networking can happen fast enough — and whether we will be among the organisms that learn to metabolize the crisis, or among those that do not survive it.
