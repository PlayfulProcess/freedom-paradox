# Trying to Make sense of Colonialism

This is the nerve of the entire book, PlayfulProcess, and it's the place where your Kant insight, the Blackfoot inversion, the decontextualization argument, and the GTDF critique all converge into a single structural claim.

The pattern is precise and repeatable. It has five moves, and it has been running for centuries:

**Move 1: A particular culture develops practices that work within its context.** Prussian Protestantism develops a moral culture of duty, individual conscience, and rational self-governance. This works for Prussians. It reflects their climate, their political arrangements, their theology, their relationship to authority.

**Move 2: A philosopher strips the context and claims the residue is universal.** Kant removes God, removes tradition, removes community, removes the body, and claims what's left — the categorical imperative — is discoverable by "any rational being." But as MacIntyre showed in *After Virtue*, what Kant actually did was take the *content* of his particular moral culture and dress it in the *form* of universal logic. The move from particular to universal is itself the colonial gesture at the level of thought.

**Move 3: The "universal" is enforced through power.** This is where it becomes material. International law, human rights frameworks, development economics, educational systems, psychiatric diagnostics — each one takes a particular European cultural product, declares it universal, and uses institutional power to impose it. Boaventura de Sousa Santos calls this **epistemicide** — the killing of alternative ways of knowing. Not just the killing of people or cultures, but the killing of the *epistemologies* those cultures carried. He argues that the diversity of knowledge systems in the world has been catastrophically reduced, and that this loss is as devastating as biodiversity loss — and structurally connected to it.

**Move 4: Local systems are displaced or destroyed, and the destruction is justified by the "universal" that was asserted.** The Doctrine of Discovery — the actual papal bulls of 1452 and 1493 — declared that non-Christian lands could be claimed because their inhabitants lacked the true faith. This became the legal foundation for colonizing the Americas, Africa, and Oceania. The logic: *we* have the universal truth (Christianity, then Civilization, then Reason, then Development, then Human Rights, then Democracy), *they* have merely local customs. Therefore our imposition is not violence but elevation. Ngũgĩ wa Thiong'o: colonial language imposes colonial myths and displaces indigenous ones — not as a side effect but as the mechanism of control.

**Move 5: When the displaced peoples exhibit symptoms of displacement, the universal framework diagnoses them as deficient.** Indigenous communities with high rates of alcoholism, suicide, and poverty are diagnosed as having individual pathologies — addiction, depression, dysfunction — rather than as exhibiting the predictable consequences of having their grammars destroyed. The diagnostic system itself is a product of the same universalizing move. As Akomolafe would say: psychology creates rather than describes reality. The DSM categories are not discovered in nature. They are the Prussian Protestant moral taxonomy applied to suffering.

The thinkers who have mapped this most rigorously:

**Sylvia Wynter** may be the deepest. Her argument: Western modernity didn't just impose its political or economic system. It imposed its *conception of what it means to be human* — what she calls "Man" — and declared it universal. "Man" is rational, individual, secular, property-owning, self-governing. Anything that doesn't match this template is classified as subhuman, undeveloped, or pathological. Her term is the **overrepresentation of Man** — a particular, local, European invention of the human that has been mistaken for the human itself. This is your Maslow-Blackfoot argument at civilizational scale. Maslow took a communal framework and made it an individual achievement ladder. The West took a culturally specific model of personhood and made it the definition of the species.

**Enrique Dussel** traces the philosophical lineage: the ego cogito (Descartes, 1637) was preceded by the ego conquiro (the "I conquer" that began with Columbus in 1492). The Cartesian subject — the individual rational mind examining its own existence — was not a philosophical discovery made in a vacuum. It was the intellectual expression of a civilization that had already established its right to conquer, extract, and define. Dussel argues you cannot separate modern philosophy from colonial violence. They are the same project.

**Walter Mignolo** extends this with the concept of the **colonial matrix of power** — the interlocking system of knowledge, authority, economy, and subjectivity that constitutes modernity. His key phrase: "there is no modernity without coloniality." They are not sequential (first modernity, then unfortunately colonialism). They are constitutive. The "universal" values of the Enlightenment — reason, freedom, progress — were produced *through* colonial extraction and *require* colonial extraction to sustain themselves.

**Talal Asad** demonstrates this specifically for the category "religion." The very idea that "religion" is a separate domain of human experience — distinguishable from politics, economics, and daily life — is a post-Reformation European construct. When colonial powers arrived in Asia, Africa, and the Americas and asked "what is your religion?" they were imposing a category that didn't exist in those cultures. Hinduism, Buddhism, and Confucianism as "religions" are largely colonial inventions — packaging systems that were actually ways of life, governance structures, cosmologies, and practices into a category that made sense to European administrators. And then the *absence* of a recognizable "religion" became evidence of primitiveness.

**Linda Tuhiwai Smith's** *Decolonizing Methodologies* traces the same pattern through research itself. Western research methods assume the knowing subject is separate from the known object, that knowledge should be extractable and transferable, and that universal claims are superior to local ones. Indigenous communities that have been "researched" for centuries experience research as a form of continued colonization — their knowledge extracted, decontextualized, published in journals they can't access, and used to generate theories that don't serve them.

Now here's where this connects to your book in a way that is structurally important.

Your entire Chapter 1 — "When Stories Lose Their Homes" — is describing Move 4 at the cultural level. Fairy tales extracted from their contexts. The gingerbread house as a starvation metaphor consumed by overfed children. Tomb objects in museums. That chapter already makes the decontextualization argument. But what you haven't yet made explicit is that **decontextualization is the cultural expression of the same gesture that, at the political level, is colonialism.** It's the same move: strip the particular, claim the universal, enforce it through power, diagnose the casualties as deficient.

And your biological hypothesis — bodies in parasympathetic states relate better, relating builds adaptive cultures, cultures need shared grammars to maintain themselves — offers something that the decolonial thinkers largely don't: a non-culturally-specific account of *why* the colonial move is destructive. It's not just morally wrong (which is a claim from within a particular moral framework). It's *biologically* destructive — it severs co-regulation, destroys communal grammars, forces nervous systems into chronic sympathetic activation, and produces the exact population-level dysregulation your research documents. The harm isn't that colonialism violates a universal moral law. The harm is that it disrupts the parasympathetic-to-relational pipeline that every human community needs to function, and it does this while claiming to be *improving* the communities it destroys.

This might be the version of Chapter 8 (The Trap of Solving) that actually works. Not just Andreotti and Akomolafe as provocative thinkers with a caveat about rigor. But a systematic argument: **the problem-solving reflex is the colonial reflex.** Identify a deficiency (in others), design an intervention (from your framework), implement it (through your power), evaluate it (by your metrics). This is the structure of colonial governance, development economics, missionary work, psychiatric treatment, and — your book must honestly acknowledge — contemplative technology platforms. The question Chapter 8 has to hold is whether Recursive.eco can avoid reproducing this structure while using the tools (technology, open source, AI) that are themselves products of it.

That's Rowson's "this move is forced, even if it loses." You can't build outside the system. You can build *knowing* what the system does, and build structures that distribute power rather than concentrate it. CC BY-SA. No algorithm. Community governance. CARE principles for sacred knowledge. Not because these solve the problem but because they make the colonial gesture harder to execute — they're structural antibodies against the universalizing reflex.

The structural critique: every moral system that begins with *the world is broken* implicitly authorizes a class of people who know what *repaired* looks like. Tikkun olam. Original sin. Marx's false consciousness. Haidt's play-based childhood that was lost. Even Andreotti's "modernity needs hospicing" — someone has to be the hospice worker. The brokenness premise is the master key for saviorism because it converts the savior's intervention from an act of power into an act of mercy. You're not dominating. You're *helping.* You're not imposing. You're *repairing.*

Your Linehan filter cuts through this. If a moral claim that presupposes brokenness generates savior dynamics that produce more suffering than they alleviate, it fails on *compassionate.* If the brokenness it diagnoses can't be empirically verified independent of the framework making the diagnosis, it fails on *fits the data.* If it doesn't produce adaptive outcomes at the community level over time, it fails on *useful.*

Here's what the book could look like. Not your current book — or maybe the philosophical core of it, the engine that drives the argument in Chapter 8 and justifies the whole approach:

Take a set of moral claims that have actually structured civilizations. Not just Western ones. Put each one through the three-filter test. Not as a ranking — "this one wins" — but as a diagnostic: where does each one succeed and where does it produce the pathologies it claims to cure?
