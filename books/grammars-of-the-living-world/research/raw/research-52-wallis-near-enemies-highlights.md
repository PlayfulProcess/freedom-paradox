## The Axiom Question: Key Passages

### The final passage — THE MOST IMPORTANT FOR THE BOOK (p. 244)

"Your worth and value is proven by your very existence, and it needs no other proof. Your contribution to the universe lies in simply being yourself. Every human life is a thread in the pattern, and every thread is required to make the pattern."

**Analysis:** This is Wallis stating inherent value as a TRUTH, not an axiom. "Proven by your very existence" — not "chosen as a commitment" or "held as a wager." This is the exact claim the Axiom book challenges. Tillich would say: this is a beautiful statement, but it is precisely the kind of statement that the anxiety of meaninglessness can attack. What "proves" it? The existence itself. But existence is precisely what is in question for someone in the void.

### On truth as efficacy, not correspondence (p. 196)

"Perhaps, in the final analysis, that's really what we mean by truth, whether personal or scientific: propositions and interpretations that are more effective at helping people understand their experience."

**Analysis:** This is Wallis moving toward a pragmatist position — truth as efficacy, not correspondence. This CONVERGES with the Linehan filter (useful, fits the data, compassionate) and with the axiom position. If truth is efficacy, then "your value is proven by your existence" is true *because holding it is more effective than not holding it* — which is exactly the axiom position. Wallis may be closer to Tillich here than his final statement suggests.

### On stopping the search for universal truth (p. 199)

"When we've grokked this, we stop searching for stable truths (or universal truth) in the thought-world, and we evaluate articulable thoughts solely in terms of their efficacy."

**Analysis:** This is explicitly pragmatist. If we evaluate thoughts "solely in terms of their efficacy," then inherent value IS an axiom — held because it works, not because it's provably true. This contradicts "your worth is proven by your very existence" — unless "proven" means "demonstrated by efficacy" rather than "logically established." The tension between these two passages IS the hinge of the book.

### On consciousness contemplating itself (p. 109)

"When consciousness contemplates itself, it catalyzes thoughts and behaviors that otherwise do not occur."

"I believe there is a correlate to this principle of quantum uncertainty on the level of conscious experience... that wiggle room might be leveraged by humans through the faculty of self-reflective awareness (in Sanskrit, vimarśaśakti: the power consciousness has in contemplating itself, or the power that is unique to awareness being aware of itself)."

**Analysis:** This is Wallis's most precise statement on recognition. Vimarśaśakti — the power of self-reflective awareness — is what makes recognition (pratyabhijñā) possible. The claim: when consciousness turns toward itself, something new happens. This is structurally different from a subject encountering an object. The question for the book: is this "something new" the discovery of a pre-existing ground, or the production of a new state through the act of self-reflection?

### On choice and determinism (p. 108)

"The fact that a hypothetical AI supercomputer that was fed all the data of your genome and your life experiences could accurately predict what you will choose, every time, in no way implies that you can circumvent the internal process of making a choice."

**Analysis:** Choice is real even within determinism. This maps onto the axiom argument: the choice to affirm inherent value is real even if the "truth" of inherent value cannot be established from outside the choice. The process of choosing is constitutive, not epiphenomenal.

### On icchā-śakti and the open invitation (p. 111)

"If these potencies become a part of your daily existence, they produce a radically different experience of life — a sense of limitless openness and total harmony with reality. If you can let yourself believe that's possible, and let yourself want it, the whole spiritual path follows from that."

**Analysis:** "If you can let yourself believe that's possible" — this is Wallis acknowledging the circularity. The practice requires prior commitment ("let yourself believe"). The path "follows from that" — from the commitment, not from evidence. This is closer to the axiom position than Wallis's final "proven by your very existence" statement suggests.

## Religion vs Spirituality (Introduction)

"Religion and spirituality: we can say that the former consists of comforting beliefs (and thus, if we're honest, much of what people today call spirituality is actually religion), and the latter consists of the willingness to strip away beliefs."

**Analysis:** Wallis defines spirituality as the willingness to strip away beliefs. This aligns with Tillich's absolute faith — faith "without the safety of words and concepts." But it creates a tension: if spirituality strips away beliefs, then "your value is proven by your existence" must also be stripped away — unless it is not a belief but a recognition. The book's question: is there a difference?

## On discernment increasing with practice (Part One, p. 1)

"The further we go on the spiritual path, the greater and more precise our discernment and understanding must be. This is both because spiritual practice generates power and energy that requires greater discernment to deploy nonharmfully and because the spiritual path seeks to free us from our cultural programming, and without precise discernment and wisdom guiding that process, we can walk perilously close to the edge of madness."

**Analysis:** Power requires responsibility — the same thesis as the Grammars book. Wallis is saying: spiritual practice is fire. Without grammars (discernment, wisdom), it burns.

## On surrender and desire (pp. 240-242)

"It's perfectly possible to accept what is in this moment while simultaneously accepting our desire to change something and acting on that desire."

"Surrendering to what is necessarily includes honoring this activating energy we call desire."

"They are for something rather than against something."

"The fire of hatred burns brightly but briefly in comparison to the fire of love."

"Even if they don't see change happening in response to their actions, the love itself is nourishing. Acting on the basis of love is inherently nourishing."

**Analysis:** This maps onto the gesture — building responsibility infrastructure not because it will succeed but because love is inherently nourishing. "For something rather than against something" is the adaptive frame vs. the moral frame.

## On unconditional love transcending approval (p. 244)

"There are aspects of you that don't necessarily merit approval, but the whole of what you are merits unconditional love, which thankfully transcends the mind-created dichotomy of approval versus disapproval."

**Analysis:** This is Wallis's version of Tillich's "courage to accept acceptance in spite of being unacceptable." The parallel is precise: unacceptable aspects exist, but the whole is accepted/loved. The question: is this unconditional love discovered (Wallis) or chosen (Tillich)?

## On grief vs. wrongness (p. 243)

"The perception of wrongness is not possible in the surrendered state, but plenty of things are worthy of grief. Grief is a natural part of what is."

**Analysis:** The adaptive frame again — not right/wrong but grief-worthy/not. This is the book's moral compass: adaptive/maladaptive, not good/bad.

## On desires that don't require belief (p. 244)

"By contrast, our desires for human connection, belonging, security, and so on are not dependent on a belief."

**Analysis:** Key passage. The desire for connection is pre-belief, pre-axiom. The nervous system reaches for co-regulation before any philosophical commitment is made. This supports the biological hypothesis from Ch 1 of the Grammars book — and it suggests that the axiom of inherent value may be grounded not in philosophy but in biology. The body chooses connection before the mind chooses a framework.

## The Internal Tension in Wallis

The highlights reveal a productive tension within Wallis's own thinking:

**Position A (pragmatist):** "We evaluate articulable thoughts solely in terms of their efficacy." Truth is what works. Inherent value is held because it is effective.

**Position B (realist):** "Your worth and value is proven by your very existence, and it needs no other proof." Inherent value is a fact about reality, not a pragmatic choice.

**Position C (invitational):** "If you can let yourself believe that's possible, and let yourself want it, the whole spiritual path follows from that." The path requires prior commitment — the axiom.

These three positions do not fully cohere. The book's contribution is to name this tension honestly and show that the coherence may not be achievable — and that the practice (the 112 dharanas, the daily renewal of the commitment) is what you do instead of achieving coherence.
