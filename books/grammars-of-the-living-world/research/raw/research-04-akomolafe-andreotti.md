## **1\. Andreotti's *Hospicing Modernity*: The Core Argument**

Vanessa de Oliveira Andreotti (Dean of the Faculty of Education, University of Victoria; former Canada Research Chair in Race, Inequalities and Global Change) published *Hospicing Modernity: Facing Humanity's Wrongs and the Implications for Social Activism* in September 2021\. The book's central metaphor gives it its force: modernity is not a problem to be solved but a dying patient to be accompanied.

### **What Modernity Is**

Andreotti defines modernity as "a ubiquitous story of linear progress, development, evolution, and civilization that is all around us and that informs the ways we think, the ways we imagine things, the ways we hope, the ways we desire. That also informs our neurobiology in many ways: where we source pleasure, what we're afraid of." Modernity is not merely an economic system or a political ideology — it colonizes the unconscious. Its "most important area of domination was the mental universe of the colonised" (echoing Ngũgĩ wa Thiong'o).

### **The Hospice Metaphor**

The book's radical move is refusing to propose an alternative to modernity. Instead, it asks how we can accompany modernity's death — within ourselves and around us — with "compassion, curiosity and dignity." Andreotti writes: "We can soften the crash, but not prevent it: disenchantment and disillusionment will happen, one way or another. But it is your call whether to move toward it now or not, slowly or not, with assistance or not."

This is not passivity. It is a reorientation of agency — from fixing to accompanying, from solving to holding. The book explicitly invites readers to "expand our collective capacity to hold space for difficult and painful things."

### **The Five Modern-Colonial E's**

Andreotti identifies five orientations that modernity produces in its subjects: Entitlements, Exceptionalism, Exaltation, Emancipation, and Enmeshment in "low-intensity struggle activism." The last is particularly relevant for the book: activism that reproduces the very logic it claims to critique — the impulse to identify a problem, propose a solution, implement it, evaluate it, and move on. This is the "trap of solving" that Chapter 7 addresses.

### **The Exercises**

*Hospicing Modernity* is part philosophical treatise, part workbook. It includes numerous "check your bus" exercises — invitations for the reader to examine their own complicity in the structures they critique. This pedagogical method is directly relevant to Recursive.eco's design: the platform holds your question without answering it. The grammar is a contemplative technology for sitting with, not solving.

## **2\. Akomolafe's "Postactivism": When Solutions Become Part of the Crisis**

Bayo Akomolafe (PhD, psychology; Yoruba heritage; founder of The Emergence Network; adjunct at Pacifica Graduate Institute) is a philosopher, psychologist, poet, and public intellectual whose work centers on a single provocative question: **"What if the way we respond to the crisis is part of the crisis?"**

### **The Core Concepts**

**Postactivism** is Akomolafe's central concept. It challenges the binary thinking of traditional activism (us vs. them, oppressor vs. oppressed, problem vs. solution) and questions whether conventional activism perpetuates the same logics that produced the issues it aims to solve. Postactivism "shifts the focus from merely doing (actions and outcomes) to being (ways of being and relating)."

**Onto-fugitivity** describes the dynamic, constantly shifting nature of being. Akomolafe argues that colonialism imposed rigid, hierarchical structures on our understanding of existence. Being is not static but "fugitive" — always escaping categorization. This challenges the conventional identities on which both oppression and resistance are built.

**The "transjective"** is Akomolafe's term for the interstitial space between subject and object. Using the image of a footprint: "the footprint doesn't belong to the mud nor to the foot, but the interstitial space that acquiesced to them to come together." This space — not objective nor subjective but transjective — is where postactivist practice occurs.

### **Psychology Creates Rather Than Describes Reality**

Akomolafe's most relevant claim for the book is that the therapeutic and diagnostic frameworks of Western psychology are not neutral descriptions of reality but active constructions that shape what they claim to observe. He states: "Trauma is already the political project of dissociation from our co-emergence with ecology. The very notion is a colonial imperial settlement ethic — it's about preserving my coddled, isolated, separated mind." And: "A clean bill of health is no less ideological than the Bible."

This critique of psychology-as-world-making maps directly onto the book's critique of the attention economy. Co-Star doesn't describe your astrological reality — it constructs an attention-capturing version of it. Headspace doesn't describe meditation — it constructs a commodified version of it. The question is always: what world does this practice create, and whose interests does that world serve?

### **Yoruba Cosmology as Framework**

Akomolafe draws heavily on Yoruba indigenous cosmologies, particularly the figure of Eshu (the trickster Orisha) as a model for postactivist practice. Eshu doesn't solve problems directly — he introduces chaos, misdirection, and play into situations that appear stuck. The trickster's logic is: when the direct path fails, the indirect path opens. This is the logic of the grammar — not answering your question but reframing the field within which questions arise.

## **3\. Intellectual Lineage: Where They Draw From**

### **Andreotti's Sources**

Andreotti draws from: decolonial theory (Quijano's "coloniality of power," Mignolo's "border thinking"), indigenous cosmologies (particularly Andean traditions from her own Brazilian-Andean background), posthumanism, affect theory, and contemplative practice. Her work with the "Gesturing Towards Decolonial Futures" arts/research collective grounds her theoretical work in participatory practice.

### **Akomolafe's Sources**

Akomolafe draws from: Yoruba cosmology and Ifá, posthumanism (Donna Haraway, Karen Barad), new materialism, Frantz Fanon's post-colonial psychoanalysis, process philosophy, and contemplative traditions. He was trained as a clinical psychologist (Covenant University, Nigeria) before moving into philosophy and public intellectual work.

### **Shared Lineage**

Both draw on: the recognition that modernity's epistemological framework cannot be used to solve problems that the same framework created; indigenous knowledge systems as sources of alternative ontology (not just content but structure); the critique of the autonomous, sovereign individual as a colonial fiction; and the insistence that embodied, relational, situated practice matters more than abstract theory.

## **4\. Critical Reception: Who Finds Them Compelling and Who Doesn't**

### **Positive Reception**

Both thinkers have been celebrated within decolonial, posthumanist, and contemplative activist circles. *Hospicing Modernity* received endorsements from Kyle Whyte (University of Michigan), Dougald Hine (Dark Mountain Project), and Bayo Akomolafe himself, who called it "a book written for us as people who struggle with the everyday manifestations of modern power." Akomolafe has taught at Middlebury, Schumacher College, Simon Fraser, and Pacifica, and was invited as Special Envoy of the International Alliance for Localization.

### **The Scholarly Rigor Question**

The most persistent critique — and the one the book must address honestly — is that both thinkers operate in a register that blurs the line between scholarly argument and poetic/gestural performance. Akomolafe's writing is deliberately non-linear, metaphor-rich, and resistant to the kind of falsifiable claims that academic philosophy demands. Andreotti's *Hospicing Modernity* mixes memoir, philosophy, poetry, drawings, exercises, and songs.

One Goodreads reviewer captured the tension: "I've read this book twice and am still trying to settle my thoughts about it. That's because Hospicing Modernity is an unusual mix of genres and themes... I'm not sure the mix works, but I've been thinking about it for a while, which means it certainly had an impact."

The question for the book is: **does the gestural register weaken or strengthen the argument?** If modernity's deepest reflex is to identify a problem and solve it, then a text that refuses to present its argument in modernity's preferred form (linear, evidence-based, falsifiable) may be performing its argument rather than merely stating it. But this defense can also become circular — any criticism of the work's rigor can be deflected as itself a manifestation of the colonial epistemology being critiqued.

### **What Claims Are Evidenced vs. Poetic**

**Evidenced claims (Andreotti):** The historical record of colonial violence and its epistemological dimensions is well-documented (building on Ngũgĩ, Fanon, Linda Tuhiwai Smith, Quijano). The "five modern-colonial E's" framework has been developed through years of pedagogical practice with NGO workers, teachers, and activists. The exercises have been tested in workshops internationally.

**Evidenced claims (Akomolafe):** The argument that activist frameworks can reproduce the logics they critique has strong support in social movement theory. The Yoruba cosmological material draws on a genuine scholarly tradition (Wande Abimbola, Kola Abimbola, Olupona — all documented in your Ifá research).

**Poetic/gestural claims:** That "modernity is dying" (Andreotti) — this is a metaphorical framing, not an empirically testable claim. That "the way we respond to the crisis is part of the crisis" (Akomolafe) — this is a heuristic, powerful for reorientation but not falsifiable in the scientific sense. That Western psychology "creates" rather than "describes" reality — this is a philosophical position (constructivism) with strong support in some traditions and strong opposition in others.

## **5\. Comparison with More Rigorously Grounded Critiques**

The book's Chapter 7 needs Andreotti and Akomolafe, but it also needs thinkers who make similar arguments with more conventional scholarly grounding, to triangulate the claim.

### **Ngũgĩ wa Thiong'o (b. 1938\)**

*Decolonising the Mind: The Politics of Language in African Literature* (1986) remains the foundational text on epistemological colonialism. Ngũgĩ's argument is concrete and specific: colonialism's "most important area of domination was the mental universe of the colonised, the control through culture, of how people perceived themselves and their relationship to the world." His focus on language as the vehicle of mental colonization — "the bullet was the means of physical subjugation; the language was the means of spiritual subjugation" — is empirically grounded in the history of colonial education policy in Kenya.

Ngũgĩ enacted his argument: he abandoned English in his fiction writing and committed to writing in Gĩkũyũ, was imprisoned without trial by the Kenyan regime, and went into exile. The argument is not merely theoretical — it has a body, a practice, a price. This concreteness is what gives Ngũgĩ's work authority that more abstract decolonial theory sometimes lacks.

**Relevance for the book:** Ngũgĩ shows that decolonizing the mind requires not just critique but alternative practice — actually doing something differently, not just theorizing about doing something differently. This maps directly onto the book's argument: the platform doesn't just critique the attention economy, it provides an alternative.

### **Linda Tuhiwai Smith (b. 1950\)**

*Decolonizing Methodologies: Research and Indigenous Peoples* (1999, 3rd ed. 2021\) is the foundational text on indigenous research methodology. A Māori scholar at the University of Auckland, Tuhiwai Smith builds on Ngũgĩ's thesis to argue that "decolonization is a long-term process involving the cultural, linguistic and psychological divesting of colonial parameters." The Western tradition of knowledge is grounded in positivism — the notion that research is objective and value-free. Tuhiwai Smith introduces Kaupapa Māori as an alternative epistemology: "We have a different epistemological tradition which frames the way we see the world."

**Relevance for the book:** Tuhiwai Smith provides the methodological framework for what ethical engagement with indigenous knowledge systems looks like in practice — exactly the question Recursive.eco faces with initiated traditions like Ifá. Her concept of research accountability to indigenous communities maps onto the CARE Principles the book already uses.

### **Boaventura de Sousa Santos (b. 1940\)**

*Epistemologies of the South: Justice Against Epistemicide* (2014) provides the most rigorous theoretical account of what Santos calls "epistemicide" — the systematic destruction of non-Western ways of knowing. Santos advocates "pluriversal" epistemologies: not replacing Western knowledge with indigenous knowledge, but creating horizontal dialogue between multiple epistemic traditions. His concept of "abyssal thinking" — the division of social reality into two sides, one visible and one invisible — describes the mechanism by which non-Western knowledge is rendered non-existent.

**Relevance for the book:** Santos's "epistemicide" is exactly what Chapter 5 ("Survival") documents — the Qin burning, the destruction of Maya codices, the suppression of Ifá under colonialism. His "pluriversal" framework provides the theoretical basis for a platform that hosts I Ching next to Tarot next to Ifá next to Dhammapada without reducing them to equivalence.

## **6\. How Their Critique Applies (or Doesn't) to Digital Commons**

### **Where It Applies**

Andreotti and Akomolafe's critique applies powerfully to Recursive.eco in several ways:

**The platform cannot solve the crisis of mythic belonging without reproducing the logic that created it.** If modernity's deepest reflex is problem → solution → implementation, then building a platform to "solve" the meaning crisis is already caught in the trap. The book's Chapter 7 argument — that the impulse to solve is part of the problem — draws directly from Akomolafe's question.

**The "low-intensity struggle activism" critique.** Andreotti's fifth "E" (Enmeshment) describes activism that makes people feel good about themselves without actually changing anything. An open-source contemplative platform could easily become this — a way for progressive, educated people to feel they're doing something meaningful while the structural conditions of the attention economy remain unchanged.

**The constructivism of technology.** If psychology creates rather than describes reality (Akomolafe), then a platform creates rather than merely serves contemplative practice. Every design decision — what gets a button, what gets foregrounded, what is measurable — constructs a specific version of contemplative life and suppresses others.

### **Where It Doesn't Fully Apply**

**The recursive public is already the answer to the critique.** Kelty's concept — a community that builds and maintains the very infrastructure through which it makes meaning — is precisely the kind of practice-based, non-solutionist, continuously modifiable approach that both Andreotti and Akomolafe call for. Recursive.eco doesn't claim to have the answer. It creates conditions and sees what the community does with them.

**The "gesture" framing.** The book already uses the term "gesture toward adaptive futures" — borrowed from the same intellectual milieu — to describe what the platform attempts. Not a solution but a wager. Not certainty but courage.

**Some things need to be built.** The deepest tension between Akomolafe/Andreotti and Recursive.eco is that the platform requires building — writing code, designing interfaces, making decisions, deploying infrastructure. Postactivism can become a kind of philosophical paralysis when taken to its extreme: if every response to the crisis is part of the crisis, then no response is possible. The book's answer (drawing on Tillich, Palmer, Wallis) is that inaction is also an action, and that the courage to build something imperfect — treating every version as a draft — is itself a contemplative practice.

## **7\. The Scholarly Rigor Question: What the Book Should Do**

Chapter 7 should present Andreotti and Akomolafe not as authorities but as the most articulate contemporary voices for a position the book takes seriously: that the impulse to solve is itself part of the problem. The chapter should:

1. **State their arguments clearly and sympathetically** — the "trap of solving" thesis, the hospice metaphor, postactivism as an alternative to binary activism.

2. **Triangulate with more conventionally grounded thinkers** — Ngũgĩ on linguistic colonization, Tuhiwai Smith on research methodology, Santos on epistemicide — to show that the decolonial critique is not merely gestural but has empirical and methodological substance.

3. **Acknowledge the circular defense problem honestly.** The book should say, in its own voice, something like: "The risk of this position is that it becomes unfalsifiable — any criticism can be deflected as a manifestation of the colonial logic being critiqued. The reader should hold these arguments as they hold everything else in this book — as questions, not as authorities."

4. **Show how the recursive public addresses the critique practically.** Not by solving the trap of solving, but by building infrastructure that remains modifiable — that treats every version as a draft, that privileges the community's capacity to adapt over the founder's capacity to plan.
