## Core argument

The statement "your value is given simply because you exist" is presented across contemplative, therapeutic, and philosophical traditions as if it were a discoverable truth — something you realize through practice, insight, or grace. The argument here is that it is not a truth. It is an axiom. And the difference matters enormously.

An axiom is a foundational commitment that cannot be derived from anything more basic. It is chosen — or, more precisely, it is what remains when all attempts at proof have been exhausted. You can build coherent systems on top of it, but you cannot dig beneath it and find bedrock. There is no bedrock. There is only the decision to build.

---

## Tillich's position (The Courage to Be)

Paul Tillich argues that the fundamental anxiety of human existence is not death, nor meaninglessness, nor guilt — though all three are real — but the threat of *nonbeing* that underlies them all. Every human being, simply by existing, must confront the possibility that existence itself has no inherent justification. This is not a mood to be overcome. It is the ontological condition.

What Tillich calls "absolute faith" is not belief in God, not confidence in a doctrine, not even trust in experience. It is what remains after every ground has been removed. It is the courage to affirm being *in spite of* the absence of any guaranteed ground for that affirmation.

Key distinction: *the courage to be as oneself* (Nietzschean self-assertion, generating meaning from will alone) vs *the courage to be as a part* (acceptance that one's being is constituted by participation in a larger whole that one did not create and cannot fully comprehend).

Tillich was honest about naming the theological circle: you cannot evaluate a ground of being from outside it, because there is no outside. He did not claim his "absolute faith" was experientially verifiable in a way that would compel assent from someone standing outside the commitment. He said: this is the courage required. Choose or do not choose. But do not pretend the choice is compelled by evidence.

## Kashmir Shaivism's position (Christopher Wallis / Abhinava Gupta)

In *Tantra Illuminated* and the Vijñāna-bhairava-tantra teachings, consciousness (*citi*, *saṃvit*) is presented as the ground of all manifestation. It is simultaneously transcendent and immanent. This is the nondual claim: the ground and its expressions are not ontologically separate.

Within this framework, inherent value does not arise as a separate problem. If everything that exists is a vibration (*spanda*) of consciousness, then nothing that exists is ontologically deficient. To exist is already to be an expression of the free, autonomous (*svātantrya*), inherently valuable ground.

Kashmir Shaivism claims this identity is not a philosophical postulate but a *recognition* (*pratyabhijñā*) — something you *see* when the obscurations fall away. Experientially verifiable.

Christopher Wallis has written: "if you practice yoga from the perspective that you are not good enough as you are, or that there is something wrong with you that needs fixing, then your yoga cannot fulfill its ultimate purpose because it is a practice founded on wrong understanding."

## The circularity challenge

The claim that recognition is experientially verifiable creates a circularity: you need sustained practice to achieve the recognition, but you need enough prior commitment to the framework to sustain the practice. The recognition is only available from inside the axiom. From outside, it looks like a self-confirming loop.

Tillich would call this the "theological circle" and would not see it as a defect. But he was honest about naming it as a circle.

### Four anticipated responses and why they don't close the gap:

**1. "Recognition is not belief. It is direct experience."** The analogy of recognizing your face in a mirror presupposes prior experience of having a face. The recognition presupposes the thing recognized. The experience is real. Its interpretation — as recognition of something always-already-true rather than production of a new state through sustained practice — is exactly what is at issue.

**2. "The question dissolves in nondual awareness."** Kashmir Shaivism's own critique of Patañjali's kaivalya applies here: achieving peace by leaving the question behind rather than integrating it is the transcendentalist move the tradition itself critiques. If the response to "I cannot find the ground of my own value" is "the question dissolves in nondual awareness," then nondual awareness is being used to bypass the existential confrontation — the near-enemy move Christopher Wallis himself warns about.

**3. "Choice is not an illusion."** If choice is real, then the choice not to exist is also real. A tradition that affirms the reality of choice must also affirm the legitimacy of the question: *why choose this?*

**4. "Practice, and the concepts will sort themselves out."** Cannot begin practice in good faith if the practice requires pretending that the ground is solid. The nervous system knows the ground is not solid. The dissonance between the teacher's certainty and the practitioner's experience becomes its own suffering.

## The species-level extension

The question of inherent value extends to the species.

If adaptive/maladaptive is the moral compass (not right/wrong), then humanity as a whole must be evaluated by the same standard. By that standard: the grammars of care — the co-regulatory practices that sustained us, the responsibility structures that constrained our power — are being dismantled faster than we can rebuild them.

The eco-fundamentalist argument: if all things are expressions of consciousness, and one expression is systematically degrading the conditions for all other expressions, does the tradition have resources for addressing this? Not through transcendence — not by rising above the ecological crisis — but through engagement? Through grammars?

If "courage to be as a part" is the frame, and the species is not being as a part but as a devourer, does the axiom still hold?

## Where the traditions converge

**Tillich:** the ground cannot be proven; it can only be affirmed with courage, specifically as a part of a whole you did not create.

**Andreotti:** the grammar of individual inherent value is itself a product of colonial separability; the question dissolves when you stop abstracting yourself from the relational field.

**Akomolafe:** the question of whether you have a right to exist is malformed; flee the grammar, not the world.

**Jyotish:** you are a pattern of participation in a cosmos that does not ask its nodes for credentials.

**Linehan's filters:** hold what is useful, what fits the data, and what is compassionate — and accept that these filters cannot justify themselves.

**The Grammars work:** the bedtime story is the oldest technology for building a nervous system's capacity to face the dark — love is the mechanism, not the decoration. The container is disappearing. Humanity has the power of gods and the responsibility structures of adolescents.

**Kashmir Shaivism:** consciousness is the ground, and you are not other than it. All models dissolve. Practice with pure motive.

---

## The self-undermining of the Linehan filters

*Useful for what?* Usefulness presupposes a purpose, which presupposes that something matters, which presupposes the value-axiom.

*Fits what data?* Data-fitting presupposes that truth-tracking is important, which presupposes that there is something worth being accurate about.

*Compassionate toward whom?* Compassion presupposes that beings are worth caring for — the axiom stated in emotional rather than ontological terms.

The filters cannot bootstrap themselves. They depend on a prior commitment to existence-as-worth-living-in that they cannot generate. Every methodology stands on something it cannot justify.

---

## The request

Can Kashmir Shaivism present the recognition of consciousness-as-ground as a commitment worth making, without claiming it is a truth that eliminates the need for commitment?

If so, then it offers something unique: a *practice infrastructure* for the courage to be as a part. The 112 gateways of the Vijñāna-bhairava-tantra are not proofs of inherent value. They are *practices of the axiom*. Ways of living inside the commitment, with the body, with the breath, with attention itself.

Not a tradition that resolves the question. A tradition that gives somewhere to practice the courage of not resolving it — while still building grammars, still telling stories to children, still trying to be a responsible part of a species that has not yet proven it deserves the fire it stole.
