## The Contradiction IS in the Sutras

PlayfulProcess asked: is the contradiction (truth vs axiom) in the sutras themselves, or only in Wallis's interpretation?

**Answer: it is in the sutras.**

The VBT closing verses contain both positions simultaneously:

### Position A: The practices lead to a state (axiom/pragmatist)
- Verse 139: "I have taught 112 concise teachings on how to become 'waveless'." — The practices produce a state. This is technique leading to outcome.
- Verse 148: "the aspect of Bhairava called 'nourished fullness' will arise and develop day by day" — Progressive, developmental, something that "arises" through practice.

### Position B: There is nothing to achieve (recognition/realist)
- Verse 146: "meditative visualization is a mind that has become motionless, free of forms, and supportless, NOT imagining a deity with a body, eyes, face" — Strip away all content.
- Verse 147: "Pūjā is likewise NOT the offering of flowers and so on. A mind made firm, that through careful attention dissolves into the thought-free ultimate void" — True worship is dissolution of all constructs.
- Verse 152: "The essence of one's real being is simply Freedom, Joy, and Awareness" — Already present. Not achieved.
- Verse 153: "The transcendent-yet-immanent Divinity who is worshipped... those substances themselves, and the worshipper: all this is One. How, then, can we use the term 'worship'?" — The very concept of achieving something is dissolved.

### The Goddess names the contradiction (verse 142c-144b)

The Goddess HERSELF asks the question: if this teaching is the true form of the Supreme, then who worships? What would be visualized? Who receives offerings? She is asking: if everything is already One, what is the point of practice?

This is EXACTLY the Tillich-Wallis tension stated in 9th-century Sanskrit. The Goddess is asking: is the ground already here (in which case practice is meaningless) or must it be achieved (in which case the nondual claim is false)?

### Bhairava's answer: radical reinterpretation, not resolution

Bhairava does NOT resolve the contradiction. He REINTERPRETS the practices:
- Japa (mantra repetition) becomes "ever greater meditative absorption into the supreme state"
- The mantra becomes "the spontaneous resonance of self-awareness"
- Visualization becomes "a mind that has become motionless"
- Worship becomes "a mind made firm, that dissolves into the thought-free ultimate void"

Each practice is kept but emptied of its conventional content. The form is preserved. The content is transformed. The CONTAINER remains. The thing-to-be-found is dissolved.

**This is PlayfulProcess's insight stated in the sutra itself: "there is nothing to be found" — but the practices of not-finding are essential.**

### Wallis's commentary confirms the tension

Wallis notes: "the teaching of the VBT omits much that is typical of classical Tantra, such as deity yoga and ritual worship. Does this text, then, completely undermine the mainstream practices of the tradition? Not exactly—those practices are not thrown out but radically re-interpreted in a nondual mode."

And: "Only God can worship God. It's impossible to venerate the deeper nature without knowing it, and the only true knowing of it is experiential immersion in it."

This is the circle: you must already know what you are seeking in order to seek it properly. The practice presupposes the recognition it aims to achieve. The sutra HOLDS this without resolving it.

## Connection to the Axiom Book

The VBT closing verses demonstrate that the axiom-vs-truth tension is not a modern philosophical problem imposed on an ancient text. It is BUILT INTO the text. The Goddess asks the question. Bhairava's answer is not "here is the truth" but "here is how to practice."

The 112 dharanas are practices of the axiom — not because the sutra says "this is an axiom" but because the sutra demonstrates the structural impossibility of saying "this is the truth" while simultaneously dissolving every construct that would make truth-claims possible.

"Liberation is complete when we are no longer waiting to be liberated" (Wallis, Tantra Illuminated p. 80) — but the 112 practices are for those who ARE still waiting. The practices are the bridge between axiom and recognition. And the bridge is the practice itself — the container, the community, the 1-1 relationship between teacher and student.

This is why oral traditions exist. The text can hold the contradiction but cannot resolve it. The resolution (if it comes) happens in the field between two nervous systems — the guru and the shishya, the parent and the child, the therapist and the client. The grammar is the container that holds the axiom long enough for recognition to arrive — or not.
