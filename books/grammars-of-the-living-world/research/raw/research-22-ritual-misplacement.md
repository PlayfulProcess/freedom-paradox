# Ritual Misplacement: Screens and the Dissolution of Shared Story

---

## **The argument**

The progression from fire to television to personal screens represents a progressive dissolution of what we might call the **ritual container** for shared story. Each step maintains the *form* of gathering while destroying the *substance* of communal meaning-making. The gingerbread house reappears: the platform offers the appearance of intimacy and nourishment while the extraction mechanisms are invisible.

---

## **On Dimitri Christakis and joint attention**

Dimitri Christakis at Seattle Children's Hospital / University of Washington School of Medicine is the central figure. He's arguably the most important researcher on early childhood screen exposure in the world. His work directly supports the ritual misplacement argument from multiple angles:

**The joint attention finding.** Christakis emphasizes that "joint attention" — shared awareness between caregiver and child, looking at the same thing together — happens hundreds of times a day in infancy, even when parents aren't aware of it. This is the developmental bedrock. His lab demonstrated that playing with highly engaging tablet apps was associated with fewer responses to joint attention prompts among toddlers, and less acknowledgment of behavioral requests among male toddlers. The more engaging the app, the harder it was to get the child's attention back — **the device literally competes with the parent for the child's gaze.**

**The pace problem.** Christakis found that every hour of TV kids watch before age three increases their likelihood of attention problems by 10 percent, driven by hyperpaced programming that conditions their minds to a faster pace than actual reality. This is ritual misplacement: content designed to maximize engagement operates at a tempo that is structurally incompatible with the developmental needs of the child.

**The co-watching research (nuanced).** Christakis says that if you allow screen time, you should co-view it, because "it's equally important that you can leverage those messages when the program is over, when you can revisit the show with them and reinforce the messages." A longitudinal study on joint media engagement found that children who watched video content and played video games together with siblings developed more inhibitory control over a year compared to those who did it alone. But here's the complicated part: **co-viewing of video content with parents was actually associated with a decrease in cognitive flexibility**, as opposed to watching alone. The research suggests there are optimal formats of joint engagement, and simply being physically present while a child watches isn't enough — which speaks to the quality of gathering, not just the fact of it.

**Background media.** Background TV decreases play quality and complexity, and also decreases parent-child interaction quality — parents are slower to respond to bids for attention and respond in a more passive manner.

## **The hearth progression**

The progression you're intuiting — fire → TV → personal screens — has real scholarly backing.

McLuhan declared that television had replaced the hearth as the focus for family gatherings, and **Cecelia Tichi's *Electronic Hearth*** (1991) explored the TV as environment — mapping the "teleconsciousness" that begins with "a continuous reprioritizing of attention in the habitat of the 'always on' TV." Neil Postman extended this, arguing that the screen is "so saturated with our memories of profane events, so deeply associated with the commercial and entertainment worlds that it is difficult for it to be recreated as a frame for sacred events."

But here's what makes your argument sharper than either McLuhan's or Postman's: they were both writing about a **shared** screen. The family still gathered around the TV. Gone are the days of the family gathered around a single television set — today, fragmented viewing is the norm.

**Tristan Harris's framing** captures why: he describes the shift from an "extractive attention economy that treats humans as resources to mine attention out of" toward what he calls "attention farming." At a 2019 presentation, he coined the phrase "human downgrading" to describe an interconnected system of mutually reinforcing harms — addiction, distraction, isolation, polarization — that weakens human capacity in order to capture human attention.

A recent study from Bengkulu, Indonesia — using McLuhan and Postman's media ecology framework — found exactly what you're describing: **digital media generates "simultaneous, dynamic, and often ambiguous communication patterns, where family members are frequently physically present but psychologically detached"** — what they call "absent presence." Teenagers in these families spent more time on entertainment activities like watching short videos or playing online games, while communication with parents became shallower — limited to practical reminders about schedules and schoolwork rather than deep conversations about values, emotions, or aspirations.

## **The argument you're building (and its strength)**

Here's the synthesis:

**The progression is: fire → TV → personal screens**, and each step represents a degradation of what we might call the **ritual container** for shared story.

Around the fire, a community received the same story at the same time from a live teller embedded in the same material conditions. This is Campbell's "living mythology" — myth cannot be exported, it belongs to a place. The TV era preserved *some* of this: the family still gathered, still shared a narrative, still had a common symbolic vocabulary. (Everyone watched the same three channels; you could reference Walter Cronkite or *The Wizard of Oz* and be understood.) But the medium was already compromised — Postman's point about the screen being saturated with profane associations, the inability to make it sacred.

In the personal screen era, even that degraded gathering dissolves. Each family member is consuming their own algorithmically curated narrative stream, optimized not for meaning or development or shared understanding but for **engagement** — which, as Harris identifies, means optimized for attention extraction. The family in the same room, each on a different device, is the spatial image of mythic belonging's impossibility. **You can't have a shared story if no one is receiving the same story.**

And this directly connects to polarization: before the advent of talk radio, cable news, and the Internet, citizens received a relatively consistent news package — this fostered a common collective intelligence where people were exposed to all types of news from varying perspectives. Today there are so many places to consume news that personal selectivity is always present, leading to media fragmentation — the idea that each person will likely consume different fragments of news content. This isn't just a political problem; it's a **mythic problem.** A polis needs shared stories the way a family needs shared stories. Without them, there's no commons, no shared symbolic ground from which to negotiate meaning.

Your Egyptian artifact analogy works beautifully here too. Engagement-driven content, like a tomb object in a museum, has been extracted from one context (the culture that produced the story) and placed in another (an algorithmic feed) where its original function — to nourish, to initiate, to create belonging — is structurally impossible. The content might look the same, but the **ritual container** has been replaced by an extraction apparatus. The gingerbread house, again.
