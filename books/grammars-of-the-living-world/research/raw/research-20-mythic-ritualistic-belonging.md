# Mythic and Ritualistic Belonging

# **When stories lose their homes: mythic displacement from Pooh to the pharaohs**

**Stories, like sacred objects, can become dysfunctional when extracted from the contexts that gave them meaning.** This insight — that context is constitutive of meaning, not merely decorative — emerges from tracing a thread across six seemingly disparate topics: the trauma behind Winnie the Pooh, a satirical medical paper that escaped its frame, fairy tales rooted in medieval famine, the paradox of telling starvation stories in an age of abundance, the scholarly concept of "mythic belonging," and the cultural metaphor of the pharaoh's curse. Each case illuminates the same fundamental problem: what happens when narratives or artifacts are severed from the lived experience that produced them.

---

## **A bear born from the trenches of the Somme**

A.A. Milne's Hundred Acre Wood is one of literature's gentlest places. Its creation story is not. Milne served as a signals officer with the 4th Battalion, Royal Warwickshire Regiment during the Battle of the Somme in 1916. His best friend, Ernest Pusch, was killed by a shell "just as he was settling down to his tea." On August 12, 1916, Milne's platoon attacked and lost approximately **60 men killed and over 100 wounded**. He contracted trench fever and was eventually invalided home. In his 1939 autobiography *It's Too Late Now*, he wrote that recalling the war made him "almost physically sick" and described it as "that nightmare of mental and moral degradation." His poem "From a Full Heart" expresses what we would now recognize as PTSD through absurdist imagery: "I'm tired of the noise and the turmoil of battle / I'm even upset by the lowing of cattle / And the clang of the bluebells is death to my liver."

The pastoral world he created for his son was, in this light, a counter-world — predictable, gentle, and safe where the real one had been none of these. Christopher Robin Milne was born in 1920, and his stuffed animals — a Harrods teddy bear renamed "Winnie" after a real Canadian black bear at the London Zoo, plus Eeyore, Piglet, Kanga, and Tigger — became the cast. The bear's namesake, Winnipeg, had been purchased by Canadian veterinary officer **Harry Colebourn** from a hunter at White River, Ontario, for $20 in August 1914, served as a regimental mascot, and was donated to the London Zoo in 1919, where Christopher Robin visited her regularly. The settings were drawn directly from **Ashdown Forest** in Sussex, near the family's country home at Cotchford Farm. E.H. Shepard sketched the actual landscapes; Christopher Robin later confirmed that "Pooh's Forest and Ashdown Forest are identical."

The four children's books — *When We Were Very Young* (1924), *Winnie-the-Pooh* (1926), *Now We Are Six* (1927), and *The House at Pooh Corner* (1928) — made Christopher Robin, at just six years old, the face of a global publicity campaign. The consequences were severe. At boarding school he was relentlessly bullied; classmates chanted his father's poems and yelled "Where's your teddy bear?" By thirteen he was taking boxing lessons. In his 1974 memoir *The Enchanted Places*, he wrote with pained precision: "It seemed to me almost that my father had got to where he was by climbing upon my infant shoulders, that he had filched from me my good name and had left me with the empty fame of being his son." The relationship between father and son deteriorated beyond repair. Christopher's mother, Daphne, reportedly refused to see him on her deathbed.

Ann Thwaite's **Whitbread Prize-winning biography** *A.A. Milne: His Life* (1990) reveals that Milne resented being known primarily as a children's author. The children's books became, in Thwaite's account, "an almost intolerable burden" for both father and son — a story about innocence that destroyed the innocence it depicted. The original stuffed animals now sit in a climate-controlled glass case at the **New York Public Library**, donated by Christopher Robin to his father's American publisher in 1947, a gesture of deliberate separation. Here, already, is a small parable of decontextualization: objects of intimate childhood play, preserved as public artifacts, their meaning fundamentally altered by the display case.

## **The satirical diagnosis that became "fact"**

Against this biographical backdrop, the popular claim that each Pooh character "represents" a mental disorder takes on a different character. The claim traces primarily to a **December 2000 article** in the *Canadian Medical Association Journal* by Sarah E. Shea and colleagues at Dalhousie University, titled "Pathology in the Hundred Acre Wood: a neurodevelopmental perspective on A.A. Milne." Published in the CMAJ's Holiday Review issue — the Canadian equivalent of the *BMJ*'s Christmas issue, which traditionally features humorous or lighthearted articles — the paper assigned DSM-IV diagnoses with deliberate absurdity. Pooh received ADHD (inattentive type) and OCD; Piglet got Generalized Anxiety Disorder; Eeyore was diagnosed with chronic dysthymia; Tigger received ADHD (hyperactive-impulsive type); Owl was deemed dyslexic. The authors gave themselves Pooh-style names in the byline ("Sarah-the-Shea"), joked about "Shaken Bear Syndrome," and composed a parody poem about taking pills.

The satirical intent was explicit. Lead author Shea told BBC News that the study was meant "to poke fun at her profession," and a 2001 CMAJ follow-up called it "a spoof written for the Holiday issue." The article won a gold prize in the Canadian Business Press's Kenneth R. Wilson Memorial Awards and was nominated for an **Ig Nobel Award**. Yet it generated over 60 letters to the editor and international press coverage — because the satire was mistaken for, or willfully converted into, earnest diagnosis.

**The internet version of the theory has distorted the original beyond recognition.** The widely repeated claim that Christopher Robin "represents schizophrenia" does not appear in the 2000 paper; the authors explicitly stated he "has not exhibited any diagnosable condition as yet." The mappings of Roo to autism and Kanga to social anxiety are likewise internet elaborations, not findings from the paper. Snopes rates the broader claim — that Milne created the characters to represent mental disorders — as essentially misleading, comparing it to the unfounded theory that Gilligan's Island characters represent the seven deadly sins.

The deeper precedent is **Frederick Crews' *The Pooh Perplex*** (1963), a brilliant satire in which the UC Berkeley English professor wrote twelve essays under fictitious pseudonyms, each applying a different school of literary criticism — Freudian, Marxist, Aristotelian, Christian — to Winnie-the-Pooh. The *New York Times* called it "the most brilliant volume of parodies since the publication of Max Beerbohm's *A Christmas Garland*." Crews' central point — that any text can be made to support any theoretical framework — applies directly to the later diagnostic readings. His 2001 sequel, *Postmodern Pooh*, extended the satire to deconstruction, feminism, and queer theory.

The scholarly critique of retrofitting clinical diagnoses onto fictional characters is consistent across disciplines. It is **anachronistic** (DSM-IV criteria appeared in 1994; Milne died in 1956; the term "learning disability" wasn't coined until 1963). It is **reductive**, turning complex literary characters into diagnostic checklists. And it is **presentist**, imposing contemporary clinical categories on works that predate them. The legitimate pedagogical use of fictional characters in psychology education — as accessible illustrations of diagnostic criteria — is a different matter from claiming the characters were designed to embody disorders.

This case itself enacts the theme of contextual displacement. A Holiday-issue satire, rooted in a specific tradition of medical humor, was extracted from that context and transplanted onto the internet, where it lost its framing, its ironic tone, and its self-aware absurdity — and became "fact."

## **Hansel and Gretel: a fairy tale born from real hunger**

The scholarly consensus on "Hansel and Gretel" is that it reflects, at minimum, the material conditions of European famine — even if the precise historical referent remains debated. First published on December 20, 1812, as tale #15 in the Grimms' *Kinder- und Hausmärchen*, the story underwent revealing revisions across seven editions. In the **1812 first edition**, both parents agree to abandon the children, and the woman is unambiguously "the mother." By the **1857 final edition**, she has become "the stepmother," the father has been made more sympathetic, and religious consolation ("God will not forsake us") has been inserted. Jack Zipes argues the Grimms made this change because they "held motherhood sacred" — the revision reflects shifting bourgeois sensibilities, not the tale's original structure.

The connections to historical famine are substantial. **The Great Famine of 1315–1317**, triggered by the Little Ice Age, devastated approximately 30 million people across 400,000 square miles of Europe, killing up to 25% of the population in some areas. Chronicles from the period document both child abandonment and survival cannibalism. An Irish chronicler recorded that people "extracted bodies of the dead from cemeteries and dug out the flesh from the skulls and ate it, and women ate their children out of hunger." Jack Zipes dates the tale's emergence to the Late Middle Ages in Germany (1250–1500), and a 2024 analysis from the **Cambridge Group for the History of Population and Social Structure** observed that tales of hunger and famine "abound in the folklore of most European societies" but are "curiously absent from English fairy tales" — because England's last national famine occurred in the 1590s, centuries before such tales were being systematically collected on the continent. The absence of famine stories in English folklore, matched by their prevalence in German and French traditions, powerfully supports the historical-material interpretation.

**John Boswell's** *The Kindness of Strangers* (1988) documents that child abandonment was "a common and morally acceptable practice from antiquity until the Renaissance," providing the demographic reality behind the tale's premise. **Robert Darnton**, in "Peasants Tell Tales" (*The Great Cat Massacre*, 1984), explicitly attacked psychoanalytic readings and argued that fairy tales should be read as historical documents: "Parents, unable to feed another child, were forced to abandon newborns." He interpreted the tales in terms of "the omnipresence of hunger, the hazards of life on the road, the burden of children in poor households."

This brings the scholarly debate to its central fault line. **Bruno Bettelheim's** *The Uses of Enchantment* (1976) reads Hansel and Gretel as a drama of psychological development — the gingerbread house as regression to the Freudian oral stage, the witch as "the destructive aspects of orality," the children's journey as maturation from dependency to autonomy. The historicist camp — Darnton, Zipes, **Marina Warner** — objects that this "leeches the history out of fairy tales," in Warner's phrase, giving them "a falsely archetypal and universal character." Warner's *From the Beast to the Blonde* (1994) insists on returning tales to the social, economic, and legal conditions that produced them — "a context in which small children really were abandoned in the forest during times of famine." **Maria Tatar** offers a middle ground in *The Hard Facts of the Grimms' Fairy Tales* (1987), attending to both material contexts and psychological resonance.

The witch fattening Hansel for consumption is itself a rich site of interpretive conflict. Zipes reads her as a figure for aristocratic hoarding — "the hatred which the peasantry felt for the aristocracy as hoarders and oppressors." The gingerbread house evokes the medieval **Land of Cockaigne** (*Schlaraffenland*), a mythical land of plenty that functions as an ironic trap: apparent abundance leads to the consumer being consumed. Bettelheim reads it as oral regression. Both readings are internally coherent; the question is which context the interpreter privileges.

## **Starvation stories in an age of overconsumption**

Here the research reaches its most provocative gap. **No single scholar has published a dedicated study theorizing the "symbolic dissonance" of telling famine-origin stories to children in affluent, obesity-prone societies.** Yet the intellectual infrastructure exists across multiple disciplines, waiting to be assembled.

Jack Zipes, drawing on the Frankfurt School (Ernst Bloch, Herbert Marcuse, Walter Benjamin), provides the most systematic treatment of how fairy tales function differently under consumer capitalism versus subsistence economies. In *Breaking the Magic Spell* (1979/2002), he argues that tales originally served "to endow with meaning the daily lives of members of a tribe" and were "violated" when bourgeois society co-opted them to manage desire within consumer capitalism. In *Happily Ever After* (1997), he demonstrates how Disney reconfigured fairy tales as vehicles for consumer socialization, and in *Relentless Progress* (2009), he warns that tales "may lose their power if they are too diluted by commercialism and merchandising." The fairy tale, in Zipes' analysis, has been progressively stripped of its original material urgency — the scarcity that gave it stakes — and repurposed as entertainment commodity.

Warner's historicist method reveals the same problem from a different angle. Her characterization of fairy tales as "coded stratagems for survival" implies that when survival is no longer at stake, the codes become opaque. She notes the contemporary difficulty: "The most difficult task has now become how to make a story of child abandonment and cannibal witches bearable for small children at Christmas." The very discomfort signals the mismatch between the tale's originating conditions and its current reception context.

The **KQED documentation of Hansel and Gretel adaptations** traces the progressive defanging of the tale: from children abandoned by their biological mother (1812) to abandoned by a stepmother (1857) to children who voluntarily leave home (1951 TV version) to a "stranger danger" parable (1983 TV version). Each adaptation reflects affluent society's increasing inability to engage with the tale's actual subject — the economic desperation that drives parents to abandon children.

This constitutes what might be called **mythic mismatch**: a narrative structure calibrated for scarcity being applied in a context of abundance, where the dominant pathologies are overconsumption, obesity, and excess rather than starvation. The gingerbread house, originally a deadly trap that weaponized the fantasy of plenty against starving children, now reads differently to children who have never known hunger and for whom unlimited sweets are an everyday reality rather than a fatal temptation. The tale's moral architecture — that apparent abundance conceals danger — persists, but its referent has shifted from literal famine to something more diffuse and harder to name.

## **Mythic belonging and the loss of living stories**

The concept of "mythic belonging" — that stories need to emerge from a culture's actual lived experience to be psychologically effective — does not yet have a single established scholarly term, but it threads through multiple disciplines under different names.

**Joseph Campbell** most directly addressed mythic loss, warning that "when a civilisation loses its mythology, the life goes out of it." His four functions of mythology — metaphysical, cosmological, sociological, and pedagogical — provide a framework for understanding what collapses when myths lose their cultural grounding. The **Joseph Campbell Foundation** states the principle with striking clarity: "In a living mythology, the factual world that supplies the images of myth is the factual world here present. **Myth cannot be exported into another land or another time.**" This is a strong claim about mythic belonging: myths are rooted in specific environmental, historical, and cultural conditions, and transplanting them elsewhere changes their nature.

**Mircea Eliade** theorized the process through his concept of **desacralization**. In *The Sacred and the Profane* (1957), he argues that modern "nonreligious man" lives in a "desacralized cosmos" but "never succeeds in completely doing away with religious behavior." The sacred persists in degraded, camouflaged forms — cinema, for instance, "takes over and employs countless mythical motifs." The absence of functioning sacred narrative creates what Eliade calls **existential anxiety**, "stemming from the absence of things yearned for."

**James Hillman**, founder of Archetypal Psychology, approached the problem differently. In *Re-Visioning Psychology* (1975), nominated for the Pulitzer Prize, he argued for situating psychology within "the culture of imagination, metaphor, and Western mythology." Hillman acknowledged that depth psychology is "historically European in origin and that Greek mythology and its gods and goddesses are uniquely influential in that particular cultural context" — an admission of cultural specificity that cuts against Jungian universalism. His work on *anima mundi* addressed the psychological consequences of modern culture's disconnection from mythic imagination, arguing that modern culture's "obsession with endless growth, consumption, and technological domination" can itself be understood through archetypal myths like the Titans.

In folklore studies, **Alan Dundes** developed the framework of "texture, text, and context" — insisting that the context (the specific social situation in which a tale is employed) is essential to interpretation. His central thesis that folklore is "autobiographical ethnography, a people's own description of themselves" directly supports the claim that myths lose interpretive validity when separated from their originating community. A 2017 conference at Kanagawa University, published as *Re-Orienting the Fairy Tale*, examined how Hawaiian oral traditions "have been exploited and adapted to further colonialist interests" — mythic displacement as an instrument of colonialism.

The postcolonial dimension is essential. **Ngũgĩ wa Thiong'o**, in *Decolonising the Mind* (1986), argues that "colonial alienation" — alienation from one's self, identity, and heritage via linguistic oppression — is "imperialism's greatest threat." When indigenous languages are suppressed, the mythic traditions encoded in them are severed. Colonial language imposes colonial myths and worldviews, displacing indigenous ones. This is mythic displacement as political violence, not mere cultural drift.

**Indigenous scholars** offer the most rigorous models of story-as-belonging. **Gregory A. Cajete** (Tewa, Santa Clara Pueblo) argues that indigenous oral traditions "have always been integrated with drawing, arts, and practical education" — they are not extractable content but embedded practice. Indigenous communities maintain that stories have **owners, seasons, occasions, and authorized tellers**. The Library of Congress's American Folklife Center notes that "many of the stories included are subject to restrictions based on seasonality, occasion, topic, teller, audience, or other criteria." **Linda Tuhiwai Smith's** *Decolonizing Methodologies* (1999) critiques how Western research assumes individual ownership of knowledge, "enabling exploitative practices that can co-opt and distort Indigenous ways of knowing."

The distinction from cultural appropriation is important. Cultural appropriation discourse focuses on **power dynamics** — a dominant culture taking from a marginalized one. Mythic displacement is a broader phenomenon that includes a culture's relationship to its **own** inherited stories. A modern German telling "Hansel and Gretel" to a well-fed child is not appropriating another culture's story; the tale belongs to the German tradition. But the tale may still be "displaced" — operating in a context so different from its origin that its original psychological and social functions no longer activate. This is mythic mismatch without appropriation, contextual loss without theft.

## **The pharaoh's curse as a metaphor for displacement**

The literal "curse of the pharaohs" associated with Tutankhamun's 1922 tomb discovery was, as Egyptologist Donald B. Redford stated, "unadulterated claptrap" — no curse was inscribed in Tut's tomb. Lord Carnarvon died of an infected mosquito bite, and the press, fueled by novelist Marie Corelli's letter to the *New York Times*, constructed the narrative. But the **metaphorical reading** of the curse — as an expression of anxiety about contextual violation — resonates across museum studies, postcolonial theory, and the broader question of mythic displacement.

**Walter Benjamin's concept of "aura"** provides the theoretical foundation. In "The Work of Art in the Age of Mechanical Reproduction" (1935/1936), Benjamin argued that artworks possess a unique "presence in time and space" rooted in their ritual function. Works of art begin as "ceremonial objects destined to serve in a cult"; the shift from **cult value** to **exhibition value** is precisely what happens when sacred objects enter museums. An Egyptian tomb object has cult value in its funerary context — it serves a ritual function within a cosmological system. In the British Museum, exhibition value predominates. The aura — the object's living embeddedness in practice — is destroyed.

**James Clifford's** "Museums as Contact Zones" (*Routes*, 1997) reframed museums as "social spaces where cultures meet, clash and grapple with each other, often in contexts of highly asymmetrical relations of power." **Tony Bennett's** *The Birth of the Museum* (1995) developed the concept of the "exhibitionary complex," showing how museums served as instruments of the nation-state, positioning non-Western objects as evidence of "earlier, primitive stages" in a narrative of civilizational progress. **Eilean Hooper-Greenhill** proposed the "post-museum" that would acknowledge objects carry multiple, culturally-situated meanings — examining cases like a Maori meeting house stranded in Surrey and a Lakota Ghost Dance Shirt in Glasgow. **Carol Duncan's** *Civilizing Rituals* (1995) made the paradoxical observation that **museums are themselves ritual sites** — visitors undergo "a transformative experience comparable to those that religions promote" — while simultaneously stripping sacred objects of their original ritual power and substituting a secular one.

The **NAGPRA framework** (Native American Graves Protection and Repatriation Act, 1990) and **Dan Hicks'** *The Brutish Museums* (2020) — on the Benin Bronzes looted during the 1897 British naval attack on Benin City — represent the political dimension. Hicks identified **161+ institutions** holding looted Benin objects and argued that "the corporate-militaristic pillage behind Europe's encyclopedic collections is not a simple matter of possession, but a systematic extension of warfare across time." **Amy Lonetree** (Ho-Chunk), in *Decolonizing Museums* (2012), insists that "objects in museums are **living entities**" that "embody layers of meaning" — decontextualization is not merely an intellectual problem but one of ongoing grief and severed relationship.

The concept of **epistemic violence** — Gayatri Chakravorty Spivak's term for the "remotely orchestrated project to constitute the colonial subject as Other" — applies directly to museum practice. Objects are stripped of their indigenous knowledge systems and reframed within Western epistemological categories: sacred objects become "art," ritual instruments become "artifacts," living cultural items become "specimens." **Helena Wangefelt Ström** proposes three models for how museums affect sacredness: the museum-as-destroyer (the object gains a new identity as museum object), the hybrid model (the object carries multiple simultaneous identities), and the use-based model (the distinction between worship and cultural use is decisive).

The analogy to mythic narratives is powerful. Benjamin's own essay "The Storyteller" (1936) mourned the decline of oral storytelling and "the communicability of experience" — another form of aura-loss through decontextualization. An oral story told in community has aura; a printed, mass-distributed version does not. By extension, a myth told within its living cultural context — embedded in ritual practice, community identity, seasonal cycles — possesses something that the same narrative anthologized in a "world mythology" textbook lacks. **Northrop Frye** theorized this explicitly in "Myth, Fiction, and Displacement" (*Daedalus*, 1961), arguing that myths undergo transformations that attenuate their power when displaced into new literary contexts.

## **Conclusion: context as constitutive, not decorative**

The thread connecting these six topics is a single principle: **context is part of what things mean, not merely where they happen to be**. A satirical medical paper loses its irony on the internet. A famine story loses its urgency in affluent societies. A sacred object loses its ritual power in a glass case. A culture's mythic tradition loses its psychological effectiveness when the lived conditions that generated it no longer obtain.

What emerges from this research is not a single scholarly theory but a **convergence across disciplines** — museum studies, postcolonial theory, folklore studies, depth psychology, and literary criticism — all arriving at the same insight from different directions. Benjamin calls it aura. Campbell calls it living mythology. Eliade calls it the sacred. Ngũgĩ calls it cultural identity. Indigenous scholars call it story ownership. Dundes calls it context. The Shea et al. paper's journey from satire to "fact" demonstrates the principle in miniature: meaning is fragile, context-dependent, and easily lost in transit.

The genuine scholarly gap identified here — the **specific paradox of applying scarcity-born narratives to abundance cultures** — represents an opportunity. All the intellectual infrastructure exists: Zipes' Marxist analysis of fairy tales under consumer capitalism, Warner's historicist recovery of material context, Campbell and Eliade's theories of mythic loss, Hillman's depth psychology of cultural disconnection, and postcolonial frameworks for understanding narrative displacement. What has not yet been written is the synthesis that brings these together around the specific question of what happens to the psychological architecture of a starvation story when starvation is no longer the problem — when the gingerbread house is no longer a lethal fantasy but a description of the average child's Tuesday. That the "curse" may not be supernatural but structural — an artifact of displacement itself — is perhaps the most useful insight to carry forward.
