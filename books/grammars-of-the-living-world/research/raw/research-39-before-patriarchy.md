# **Was there a time before patriarchy?**

**Patriarchy is historical, not inevitable — but the story is far more complex than any golden age narrative allows.** The archaeological, genetic, and anthropological evidence now converges on a striking conclusion: for most of the roughly 300,000 years of *Homo sapiens* existence, human societies organized themselves without systematic male dominance, and some Neolithic communities were demonstrably egalitarian and matrilineal. Yet the specific narrative Riane Eisler popularized — a peaceful goddess-worshipping partnership civilization overthrown by patriarchal warriors — is both partially vindicated and substantially complicated by three decades of new evidence. The truth is more interesting than either the dominator myth or the partnership counter-myth: humans have always possessed a far wider palette of social possibilities than any binary framework captures. For a book about the crisis of mythic belonging and the possibility of alternative social grammars, this is powerful material — not because it confirms a lost paradise, but because it proves that **the grammar of domination was written, not given**, and can therefore be rewritten.

---

## **Eisler's chalice and blade: the argument that changed the conversation**

Riane Eisler's *The Chalice and the Blade* (1987) proposed that all human societies fall on a spectrum between two organizing models: the **partnership model** (symbolized by the chalice — egalitarian, life-giving, with "hierarchies of actualization" where power empowers) and the **dominator model** (the blade — authoritarian, violent, ranking man over woman, race over race). She coined the term **"gylany"** — from Greek *gyne* and *andros* linked by *ly* — to describe partnership societies, deliberately avoiding "matriarchy," which she considered another face of domination.

Drawing heavily on archaeologist Marija Gimbutas and excavator James Mellaart, Eisler argued that Neolithic civilizations across southeastern Europe and the Mediterranean (roughly **7000–3500 BCE**) organized around partnership principles: matrifocal kinship, goddess veneration, relative gender equality, low militarism. These were then "truncated" by waves of Indo-European pastoral warriors from the Pontic-Caspian steppe — Gimbutas's "Kurgan" peoples — who imposed patriarchal hierarchy through conquest between roughly 4400 and 2800 BCE. Her key theoretical contribution was **Cultural Transformation Theory**: the shift from partnership to domination was a *historical event*, not an expression of human nature.

The book sold over **500,000 copies**, was translated into **27 languages**, reached its **57th U.S. printing** by 2017, and drew extravagant praise. Ashley Montagu called it "the most important book since Darwin's *Origin of Species*." It influenced fields from sociology to economics, inspired a *MacGyver* episode, and is widely credited as a source for Dan Brown's *The Da Vinci Code*. Chinese scholars at the Academy of Social Sciences applied Eisler's framework to Chinese prehistory and published *The Chalice and the Blade in Chinese Culture* (1995). Writers from bell hooks to Terence McKenna adopted her terminology. Eisler went on to develop "caring economics" in *The Real Wealth of Nations* (2007) and, with anthropologist Douglas Fry, integrated neuroscience and cross-cultural data in *Nurturing Our Humanity* (2019, Oxford University Press), arguing that humans evolved capacities for empathy and cooperation that domination systems actively suppress.

The book's limitations, however, are real. Historian Elizabeth Fox-Genovese dismissed it as "antimale science fiction" in *The New York Times*. More damagingly, **feminist archaeologists** — scholars who shared Eisler's goals — delivered the sharpest critiques. Margaret Conkey and Ruth Tringham at UC Berkeley criticized both Gimbutas and "her followers (e.g., Eisler 1987)" for "feminist essentialisms" and for deploying archaeological data without adequate interpretive caution. Eisler's symbolic contrast between the nurturing chalice and destructive blade, despite her disavowals, perpetuates the very gender binary she seeks to transcend. As one careful reviewer noted, she "makes far-reaching statements about social structure essentially on the basis of a limited set of paintings, buildings, and figurines," treating the speculations of contested archaeologists as proven fact.

## **Gimbutas vindicated — but only halfway**

Marija Gimbutas (1921–1994), the Lithuanian-born UCLA archaeologist whose work provided the empirical foundation for Eisler's argument, is one of the most fascinating figures in twentieth-century scholarship. Fluent in numerous languages, trained across archaeology, ethnology, folklore, and linguistics, she created an interdisciplinary field she called **archaeomythology**. Her career arcs from universal respect to marginalization to posthumous partial rehabilitation — a trajectory shaped by both the merits and overreach of her claims and by the gendered dynamics of academic power.

Her **Kurgan hypothesis**, first presented in 1956, proposed that Proto-Indo-European speakers originated in the Pontic-Caspian steppe and expanded into Europe through successive waves, bringing patriarchal warrior culture, horse domestication, and sky-god religion. She identified three major waves of expansion between roughly **4400 and 2800 BCE**. The cultures they displaced, which she called **"Old Europe"** — including the Vinča culture of Serbia, the Cucuteni-Trypillia culture of Romania and Ukraine, and Minoan Crete — she described as peaceful, agrarian, egalitarian, matrifocal, and organized around goddess veneration.

The Kurgan hypothesis has been **spectacularly confirmed** by ancient DNA evidence since 2015\. Three landmark studies — Haak et al. and Allentoft et al. (both *Nature*, 2015\) — documented massive population movement from the steppe, with Corded Ware people in Germany tracing roughly **75% of their ancestry** to the Yamnaya. Colin Renfrew, who had been Gimbutas's most prominent rival for decades with his competing Anatolian hypothesis, delivered the inaugural Marija Gimbutas Memorial Lecture at UCLA in 2018 and conceded: **"Marija's Kurgan hypothesis is magnificently vindicated."** David Anthony, another former critic, stated she had been "proved right but for the wrong reasons" — the core migration pattern was correct even if specific details needed refinement.

But what was vindicated was the **migration**, not the broader cultural narrative. Gimbutas's interpretation of thousands of Neolithic female figurines as evidence of a unified goddess religion remains deeply contested. Her critics — including Peter Ucko (1968), Ruth Tringham, Margaret Conkey, and especially Lynn Meskell — argued that she over-interpreted ambiguous material, selected evidence to fit her narrative, presented conclusions as objective fact when the archaeological record demands interpretive humility, and treated diverse cultures spanning millennia and thousands of kilometers as a single coherent civilization. As Tringham put it: "Feminist archaeological research is based on a celebration of the ambiguity of the archaeological record... Gimbutas, however, has mystified the process of interpretation." David Anthony further challenged the "peaceful Old Europe" claim, noting that fortified sites, weapons, and evidence of warfare existed in Neolithic Europe well before any Kurgan contact.

It is crucial to note what Gimbutas actually argued. **She explicitly rejected the term "matriarchy"** after her 1974 book, adopting instead "matristic" and "matrifocal," and also used Eisler's "gylanic." As a dedicated scholarly study confirms: "At no time during her life and academic career did Gimbutas ever characterize the egalitarian societies of Old Europe as matriarchal, nor did she ever suggest that males were dominated by females." Her vision was of societies with matrilineal descent, matrilocal residence, and roughly equal status between the sexes — not a mirror-image of patriarchy. That this distinction has been so persistently misrepresented — by both her critics and some of her admirers — reflects the depth of cultural resistance to imagining alternatives to domination.

## **Çatalhöyük: what the ground actually tells us**

The most important test case for the partnership thesis is **Çatalhöyük** in central Turkey — a Neolithic settlement of **3,000–8,000 people** occupied from roughly 7400 to 6000 BCE, with densely packed mudbrick houses accessed through roof openings, no streets, no palaces, no temples, no fortifications. Ian Hodder of Stanford directed a 25-year international excavation there (1993–2018) involving **160 researchers across 36 subdisciplines**, making it one of the most thoroughly studied archaeological sites in the world.

The findings are genuinely remarkable. Hodder described the society as **"aggressively egalitarian"** — no houses with distinctive features indicating royalty or priestly elite, no administrative buildings, no significant wealth differentiation. Isotope analysis found **no statistical variation between male and female bone chemistry**, indicating identical diets. Both sexes showed identical carbon deposits in their ribs from indoor smoke. Bone wear patterns demonstrated similar daily activities. Naomi Hamilton searched extensively for gendered burial patterns — orientation, placement, grave goods — and **found none**. Houses were uniform in size and layout. All religious symbolism was integrated into domestic practice, not separated into temples or shrines.

However, Hodder's team also **overturned the goddess-worship narrative**. Of approximately **2,000 figurines** recovered (ten times Mellaart's count), the majority depicted animals, and **fewer than 5% depicted women**. Many were found in refuse deposits, not ritual contexts. The famous "Seated Woman" figurine that Mellaart identified as a Mother Goddess was reinterpreted by Meskell and colleagues as possibly representing "older women who have achieved status" — venerated elders, not divine beings. A figurine found with a fleshy front and skeletal back suggested concerns with the cycle of life and death, not fertility worship. Hodder stated plainly in 2009: "There is very little evidence of a mother goddess and very little evidence of some sort of female-based matriarchy."

Then came a stunning development. A **2025 study published in *Science*** (Yüncü et al.), analyzing 131 paleogenomes from the site, found that within-building genetic connections were **predominantly maternal** across all periods. There was no indication of patrilocal mobility — contrasting with most Neolithic European sites. Female subadults received up to **five times more grave goods** than males. The researchers described Çatalhöyük as the **"oldest known example of a female-centered social structure supported by DNA evidence"** — a matrilineal society sustained for over a thousand years.

The picture that emerges is both a vindication and a complication. Gimbutas's broader vision of an egalitarian, female-centered Neolithic society finds real support at Çatalhöyük. But the specific mechanism she proposed — goddess worship as the organizing principle — does not. **The egalitarianism was real; the goddess cult was not supported by the evidence.** This is a crucial distinction for anyone building on this research: the absence of systematic male dominance can be demonstrated archaeologically, but the positive content of these societies' worldviews remains genuinely ambiguous.

## **The strongest critiques: romanticism, binaries, and the limits of inference**

The most rigorous challenge to the pre-patriarchal thesis came from Cynthia Eller's *The Myth of Matriarchal Prehistory* (2000). Eller, a professor of religious studies at Claremont Graduate University, argued the narrative is an "ennobling lie" — a modern myth serving political purposes but lacking evidentiary rigor. Her strongest points are genuinely difficult to dismiss. She observed that **goddess worship does not guarantee female social power** — Hindu goddesses, the Virgin Mary, and Japanese Shinto deities coexist with deeply patriarchal societies. She noted the interpretive leap from artifact to social organization is enormous when no texts exist. And she argued that grounding feminism in prehistoric claims that may be disproven is strategically dangerous.

Eller's book was itself challenged by Joan Marler and Max Dashu, who argued she constructed straw-man arguments, conflated rigorous scholarship with popular Goddess-movement claims, and misrepresented Gimbutas (who never argued for "matriarchy"). These defenses have merit — Eller's critique works best against the weakest popular versions of the thesis rather than its most sophisticated scholarly formulations.

**Feminist critiques from the left** are equally important. The partnership/dominator binary has been challenged as replicating the very binary thinking Eisler associates with domination. Queer archaeologists point out that the model assumes heterosexual reproductive pairs as the basic social unit. Postcolonial scholars note that Eisler extrapolates primarily from European evidence to universal claims. Meskell argued the goddess narrative promotes an essentialist view of women reduced to biology and reproduction — paradoxically mirroring patriarchal stereotypes. The **archaeology of gender movement** that developed through the 1990s, led by scholars like Joan Gero, Margaret Conkey, and Alison Wylie, shifted the field from asking "Were there goddesses?" to asking "How did gender systems operate in specific past societies?" — emphasizing complexity, locality, and intersectionality over grand narrative.

Perhaps the most honest position comes from scholars who accept partial findings while rejecting the overarching story. **Lucy Goodison and Christine Morris**, editing *Ancient Goddesses* (1998), concluded with "a revelation. Not of a single, fundamental pattern universally repeating itself, but of a picture of staggering diversity." Gerda Lerner's *The Creation of Patriarchy* (1986) demonstrated that patriarchy was historically constructed through specific mechanisms — controlling women's reproductive capacity, denying education, excluding from public life — without positing a utopian matriarchal golden age. This may be the most intellectually defensible position: patriarchy has a history and therefore is not destiny, but the world it replaced was more varied, ambiguous, and locally specific than any single counter-narrative allows.

## **Ancient DNA rewrites the invasion story — with troubling complexity**

The genetic evidence has transformed this debate more than any other development. Beyond confirming the Yamnaya migration, the data reveals something disturbing about its character. A landmark 2017 PNAS study by Goldberg, Günther, and Jakobsson found that the steppe migration was **massively male-biased** — approximately **10 migrating males for every migrating female**. In Iberia, Olalde et al. (2019) documented roughly 40% autosomal ancestry replacement but nearly **100% Y-chromosome replacement** — local male lineages essentially extinguished within about 200 years after 2200 BCE. David Reich drew a direct comparison: "People in Colombia have almost no local Y-chromosomes. They're almost all European. And they have almost no European mitochondrial DNAs. They're almost all Native American. And that's the result of exploitation and social inequality."

This is powerfully suggestive but not conclusive. Iñigo Olalde cautioned against assuming direct violence: "It would be a mistake to jump to the conclusion that Iberian men were killed or forcibly displaced." A 2024 *Nature Communications* study (Rivollat et al.) showed that patrilineal kinship systems with group fission can produce identical genetic patterns without warfare. Early plague strains (*Yersinia pestis*) detected from 5,000–6,000 years ago may have devastated Neolithic farming populations before steppe arrival. Multiple mechanisms likely operated: disease, technological advantages (horses, wheels, metallurgy), elite dominance, differential fertility over centuries, and yes, probably some violence — but not necessarily the dramatic "invasion" Gimbutas envisioned.

The political weaponization of this research demands careful handling. White supremacist groups have seized on Yamnaya findings to celebrate "Aryan warrior ancestry." Hindu nationalists have pressured Indian researchers to deny steppe migration into South Asia, despite genetic evidence clearly showing **6.5–50.2% steppe-related admixture** among modern South Asians, concentrated among Indo-Aryan speakers and upper castes. Responsible scholars now avoid the term "Aryan" entirely, speaking instead of "steppe ancestry" or "Bronze Age population movement." As Reich emphasizes, ancient DNA reveals that **all populations are mixed** — undercut­ting every claim to racial purity from any direction.

## **Beyond the binary: Graeber, Wengrow, and the wider palette**

The most productive complication of Eisler's framework comes from David Graeber and David Wengrow's *The Dawn of Everything* (2021), which argues that prehistoric humans were not stuck in any single social form but **experimented fluidly** with hierarchy, egalitarianism, and arrangements in between — sometimes shifting seasonally. They reject both the Hobbesian narrative (life was brutal until the state saved us) and the Rousseauian narrative (life was innocent until agriculture ruined us), calling both "the Myth of the Stupid Savage" — the condescending assumption that early humans were incapable of conscious political deliberation.

Their evidence is formidable: monumental architecture built by non-state societies (Göbekli Tepe, Poverty Point); egalitarian cities of thousands without centralized authority (Ukrainian mega-sites, Teotihuacan, Mohenjo-daro); seasonal political oscillation between authoritarian and communal modes documented among Plains peoples; the deliberate abandonment of the hierarchical city of Cahokia, whose residents reorganized into tribal republics. They propose three fundamental freedoms that prehistoric societies maintained: **the freedom to disobey, the freedom to relocate, and the freedom to create new social arrangements.**

For "Grammars of the Living World," Graeber and Wengrow's framework matters enormously. Where Eisler offers a binary (partnership vs. domination), they offer **a spectrum of conscious political experimentation**. They would likely view the partnership/dominator distinction as yet another oversimplification, though they share Eisler's conviction that domination was not inevitable. Their work suggests the question is not "Was there a partnership era or wasn't there?" but rather: **humans have always possessed the capacity to organize themselves in radically different ways, and the foreclosure of that capacity is itself the catastrophe.**

James Scott's *Against the Grain* (2017) powerfully complements this view. Scott demonstrates that early states were built on cereal grains — not because grains are nutritionally superior, but because they are **visible, measurable, storable, and taxable**. The state "sees" wheat; it cannot "see" tubers underground. The 4,000-year gap between first cultivation and first states suggests people farmed for millennia without hierarchy. State "collapse" was often liberation — disaggregation into decentralized communities. Crucially, Scott links patriarchal family formation directly to state domestication logic: "First fire, then plants, livestock, subjects of the state, captives, and finally women in the patriarchal family — all of which can be viewed as a way of gaining control over reproduction."

## **Living proof that alternatives exist**

The strongest evidence that non-patriarchal social organization is viable comes not from the contested past but from the living present. The **Minangkabau of Sumatra** — roughly 4–8 million people, the world's largest matrilineal society — organize property, clan names, and family identity through the female line. Women own land, houses, and livestock. Decision-making is by consensus. Remarkably, this system coexists with Islam and survived two centuries of European colonialism. Anthropologist Peggy Reeves Sanday, after decades of fieldwork, describes a society where genders are "interlinked rather than ranked," with mutual agreement as "the ultimate sovereign."

The **Mosuo of China** (\~30,000 people near Lake Lugu) practice "walking marriages" — no formal marriage institution, with women choosing partners freely and men returning to their mothers' households. Senior women head households and manage finances. The system has persisted for at least seven centuries, surviving even the Cultural Revolution's attempts to abolish it. The **Khasi of Meghalaya, India** (over a million people) trace lineage through mothers, with the youngest daughter inheriting wealth and property.

Approximately **160 matrilineal communities exist worldwide**. None features systematic female domination of men. They are better described as featuring **complementary roles with women at the economic and kinship center** — precisely the pattern Gimbutas described as "matristic" rather than "matriarchal." Their existence demolishes the claim that patriarchy is universal or biologically inevitable.

Christopher Boehm's research on hunter-gatherer societies reveals another mechanism: **reverse dominance hierarchies**, where the band collectively dominates any individual who attempts to dominate others, through ridicule, ostracism, and in extreme cases execution. Egalitarianism, Boehm shows, is not the absence of hierarchy but an **active political achievement** requiring constant maintenance. Sarah Blaffer Hrdy's work on **cooperative breeding** — humans' unique practice among great apes of raising offspring through networks of "alloparents" — suggests the foundational mode of human social organization was cooperative and maternal-centered, making domination a deviation from rather than the basis of our evolutionary heritage.

## **What this means for alternative social grammars**

The evidence, taken whole, supports a set of conclusions with different confidence levels. **High confidence**: patriarchy is not a biological inevitability but a historical development with identifiable (if debated) origins, primarily in the Bronze Age. The Neolithic-to-Bronze-Age transition involved massive population movements from the Pontic-Caspian steppe that reshaped European and South Asian societies genetically, linguistically, and culturally. Some Neolithic societies, including Çatalhöyük, were demonstrably egalitarian and matrilineal. Non-patriarchal societies exist today at scales from thousands to millions. **Moderate confidence**: the steppe expansion was male-biased and involved some form of elite dominance or violence. Pre-steppe European societies were generally less hierarchical than their Bronze Age successors. **Lower confidence**: a unified goddess religion organized Neolithic societies. Old Europe constituted a coherent "partnership civilization." The transition was a single dramatic "truncation" rather than a complex, multi-causal, region-specific process.

For "Grammars of the Living World," several threads connect. If symbiogenesis shows cooperation is as fundamental as competition in evolution, and if archaeological evidence shows partnership is as old as domination in human social organization, then the dominator model is not destiny. The grammar of domination was composed — through specific historical mechanisms of state formation, grain agriculture, patrilineal kinship, and the control of reproduction — and what was composed can be decomposed.

But the Graeber and Wengrow critique is essential. The question may not be "partnership vs. domination" as a binary but a **much wider palette of social possibilities** that humans have always experimented with. The real tragedy is not the fall from a particular paradise but **the loss of social imagination itself** — the foreclosure of the human capacity to consciously choose and redesign social arrangements. This connects to the book's interest in wisdom traditions: many encode non-dominator cosmologies not because they remember a specific pre-patriarchal era, but because they preserve the deeper insight that social reality is constructed and therefore transformable.

The honest answer to "Was there a time before patriarchy?" is: **yes, but it was not a single thing.** It was many things — matrilineal villages, egalitarian towns, seasonal confederacies, reverse-dominance bands, cooperative breeding networks, and social forms we probably cannot yet imagine from their material remains. The inherited cultural stories are indeed calibrated for a world that no longer exists. But the archaeological and genetic record reveals something even more radical than a lost partnership era: it reveals that humans have always been capable of building worlds that do not yet exist.
