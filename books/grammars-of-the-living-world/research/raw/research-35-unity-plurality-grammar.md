## **I. THE PERENNIAL PHILOSOPHY TRAP: Is Finding Unity Across Traditions Itself a Colonial Move?**

### **The Perennialist Claim**

The perennial philosophy — from Marsilio Ficino and Pico della Mirandola through Aldous Huxley, René Guénon, and the Traditionalist School (Nasr, Schuon) — holds that beneath the surface diversity of the world's religious and spiritual traditions lies a common metaphysical truth: a single primordial Tradition expressed differently across cultures. In Huxley's formulation, this involves recognizing (1) a divine Ground of all being, (2) that human beings can know this Ground directly, (3) that this knowledge is the final purpose of existence, and (4) that there is a universal Law or Way that leads to it.

The appeal is obvious: if all traditions point to the same non-separable reality, then separability is exposed as the illusion it is, and the resources for its overcoming are found everywhere. The perennial philosopher looks at Kashmir Shaivism, Kabbalah, Neoplatonism, Indigenous cosmologies, and process philosophy and says: "See? They all agree. Separation is not ultimate."

### **The Critique: Five Lines of Attack**

**1\. Epistemic violence through homogenization.** The most fundamental critique, from both academic and decolonial perspectives, is that perennialism flattens real differences between traditions to produce an appearance of unity. Steven Katz's constructivist argument holds that mystical experiences are shaped by the frameworks within which they occur — a Sufi's *fanā* is not a Buddhist's *śūnyatā* is not a Kabbalist's *devekut*, even if they share family resemblances. By declaring them "really the same," perennialism performs a kind of epistemic violence: it erases what is distinctive, particular, and irreducible in each tradition.

**2\. The colonial structure of the universal gaze.** When a Western scholar surveys the world's traditions from a position of presumed neutrality and announces their underlying unity, the move replicates the very logic of separability it claims to overcome. The surveyor stands outside all traditions — in the "view from nowhere" that is the hallmark of Enlightenment epistemology — and reduces them to data for a meta-theory. This is precisely Ferreira da Silva's "self-determining Subject" who requires "no-bodies" as objects of knowledge. The perennial philosopher becomes, despite intentions, a colonial cartographer of consciousness.

**3\. Who benefits from "unity"?** Zoe Todd's critique of the ontological turn applies here with full force: the "breathless realisation" that Indigenous peoples have always known what Western philosophy is now discovering operates as a new form of extraction — intellectual this time rather than material. Indigenous knowledge is mined for confirmation of a theory produced elsewhere, stripped of its relational, place-based, and ceremonial context, and repackaged as "evidence" for a universal thesis. The Indigenous thinker's decades of embodied practice become a footnote in a Western philosopher's argument.

**4\. Traditionalism's reactionary shadow.** The Traditionalist School (Guénon, Schuon, Nasr) has been critiqued for its deep conservatism — its valorization of "orthodox" traditional forms, its hostility to modernity, and its nostalgia for a lost primordial unity. More troublingly, as recent scholarship has shown, the concept of the "pluriverse" itself has been appropriated by the European New Right (Alain de Benoist, Alexander Dugin) to justify ethnopluralism — the idea that different peoples should remain in their "own" civilizational spheres. The line from "each tradition expresses a universal truth" to "each people should stay in its own tradition" is disturbingly short.

**5\. The "no true Scotsman" problem.** Craig Martin argues that perennialism involves unfalsifiable claims that resemble the "no true Scotsman" fallacy: when traditions demonstrably contradict each other (Buddhist anātman vs. Hindu ātman, for example), the perennialist simply declares that the "esoteric" meaning resolves the contradiction. The exoteric level is dismissed as superficial, and the perennialist's preferred interpretation becomes the arbiter of what each tradition "really" means.

### **The Counter-Argument: What Survives the Critique**

Yet the perennialist intuition doesn't die easily, and for good reason. Several scholars have attempted more nuanced positions:

**Jorge Ferrer's participatory pluralism:** Ferrer doesn't deny common ground between traditions but insists the similarities have been overstated. He speaks of "a common spiritual dynamism underlying the plurality of religious insights and ultimates" — but the "ultimates" themselves are genuinely different. Different traditions don't climb the same mountain by different routes; they cultivate different spiritual realities. This is pluralism without perennialism: real commonality at the level of practice (contemplative techniques, ethical cultivation) without metaphysical unity at the level of destination.

**Steve Taylor's "soft perennialism":** Taylor argues for a "perennial phenomenology" that focuses on shared features of spiritual experience (ego-dissolution, expanded awareness, heightened interconnection) without making ontological claims about a single Divine Ground. The phenomenological overlap is real and significant — people across cultures do report remarkably similar states — but this doesn't license a metaphysical conclusion.

**David Bentley Hart's syncretism over perennialism:** The Orthodox theologian Hart argues for syncretism (the creative mixing of traditions looking *forward*) rather than perennialism (the search for a lost original unity looking *backward*). Syncretism respects the "complex and sometimes incommensurable ways different religions understand divinity" while remaining open to cross-pollination.

**William James's "pluralist mysticism":** James coined the term "multiverse" and suggested that spiritual practice opens access to genuinely different dimensions of reality. "Variety, not uniformity, is more likely to be the key to progress." This is perhaps the most radical alternative: what if the traditions are not different perspectives on the same reality, but different real relationships with a reality so excessive that no single perspective could exhaust it?

## **II. CAN NON-DUAL TRADITIONS AND DECOLONIAL THEORY TALK TO EACH OTHER?**

### **The Obvious Convergence**

At a structural level, non-dual traditions (Kashmir Shaivism, Advaita, certain Buddhist schools, Indigenous relational ontologies) and decolonial theory (da Silva, Andreotti, Wynter, Akomolafe) share an enemy: **the logic of separability**. Both diagnose the fundamental error of modern Western thought as the reification of boundaries between entities that are, in reality, constitutively entangled.

Kashmir Shaivism's teaching that all apparent multiplicity is the self-contraction of a single consciousness, and da Silva's argument that separability is the epistemic architecture enabling racial violence, appear to be making the same point from different directions. Andreotti's identification of separability as the "foundation of the house modernity built" could be a paraphrase of Kṣemarāja's teaching that āṇava mala (the contraction of individuation) is the root of suffering.

### **The Tension: Three Critical Fault Lines**

**Fault Line 1: Is the diagnosis ontological or political?**

Non-dual traditions typically frame separability as an ontological error — a misperception of the nature of reality that can be corrected through practice, recognition, or grace. The disease is ignorance (*avidyā*, *ajñāna*); the cure is knowledge (*jñāna*, *pratyabhijñā*).

Decolonial theory frames separability as a political and material structure — maintained by institutions, economies, and embodied habits of being. Wynter's genres of the human are not perceptual errors; they are sociogenically enforced through "semantically-neurochemically opiate-rewarded" identification systems. You can't meditate your way out of coloniality.

This is not an irreconcilable difference, but it requires careful navigation. The strongest position holds both: separability is *both* an ontological misperception *and* a politically enforced structure, and addressing only one dimension leaves the other intact. Recognition without redistribution is spiritual bypassing. Redistribution without recognition is rearranging deck chairs.

**Fault Line 2: Whose non-duality?**

When Western or Western-trained scholars deploy non-dual frameworks to address colonial questions, the risk is a new form of what Todd calls epistemic appropriation. A Kashmiri Shaiva framework is not Indigenous; a process-philosophical framework is not Andean. The temptation to synthesize them into a meta-theory of non-separability replicates exactly the colonial move of rendering all knowledge commensurable under a single rubric.

The antidote is what Santos calls **intercultural translation** rather than synthesis: creating mutual intelligibility between traditions without reducing one to another. Kashmir Shaivism and Andean cosmovision can illuminate each other without being declared "really the same." The conversation enriches both without flattening either.

**Fault Line 3: The politics of the "ontological turn"**

Zoe Todd's critique and Vanessa Watts's warning (that translating Indigenous cosmology through Euro-Western processes "necessitates a distinction between place and thought" that colonizes the interpretation) apply with full force to any project that attempts to connect non-dual traditions with decolonial theory.

Jeremy Sorgen, writing from UC Berkeley, puts it bluntly: scholars who study Indigenous ontologies "would better serve the concepts and values they promote if they spent less time doing conceptual analysis and more time working in partnership with Indigenous groups." The ontological turn "is actually quite conservative when evaluated from the standpoint of affecting human-land relations" — it changes minds without changing material relations.

### **The Productive Possibility: Ontological Pluralism**

The concept of **ontological pluralism** — the recognition that there are genuinely different ways of being, not just different perspectives on a single being — may offer a path through these tensions. Drawing on Viveiros de Castro's multinaturalism (many natures, one culture) rather than multiculturalism (one nature, many cultures), ontological pluralism holds that:

* Different traditions don't merely *interpret* reality differently; they *enact* different realities.  
* These different realities are not mere social constructions but have genuine ontological weight.  
* They cannot be reduced to a common substrate (against perennialism) but they can enter into productive relations with each other (against incommensurability).  
* The political question is not which ontology is "true" but which ontological arrangements permit flourishing and which produce violence.

This connects directly to Mignolo's pluriversality — "a world where many worlds fit" — and to Escobar's "designs for the pluriverse." The pluriverse is not a perennial unity rediscovered; it is a political achievement of coexistence among genuinely different ways of being.

## **III. WHAT YOUR THESIS REVEALS: The Grammar Behind the Grammars**

### **The Recursive Structure**

Your thesis — that the disease of separability IS coloniality — has a recursive structure that the research illuminates:

1. **Separability is not uniquely human** (Stream 1): It operates cosmically in Kashmir Shaivism, divinely in Kabbalah, ontologically in Neoplatonism. Even Indigenous traditions, which resist the framework, describe differentiation within relation.

2. **Separability was weaponized as coloniality** (Stream 2): What was a cosmic principle (or creative mechanism, or enacted phenomenon) was seized, reified, and installed as the dominant ontological regime through colonialism. Da Silva's triad (separability, determinacy, sequentiality) is the epistemic toolkit; Wynter's Man1/Man2 is the sociogenic enforcement; Andreotti's "house" is the structural expression.

3. **The way out is neither recovery nor creation but composting** (Stream 2-3): The desert metaphor holds because the disease is not merely cognitive — it is embodied, neurochemical, institutional. The generation shaped by separability-as-coloniality must undergo transformation, not merely re-education.

4. **The meta-question — whether finding unity across traditions is itself colonial — is the test case for the thesis** (Stream 3): If you approach the cross-traditional evidence perennially (declaring all traditions "really" teach the same non-separability), you have performed separability's signature move: extracted diverse knowledges from their living contexts and subsumed them under a universal theory. If you approach it pluriversally (recognizing that each tradition enacts its own mode of non-separability, and that these modes can illuminate each other without being collapsed), you have demonstrated what "grammar" means in your framework: a living, placed, relational structure of meaning-making.

### **The Contribution of "Grammars of the Living World"**

Your book's title names the alternative to both perennialism and relativism: **grammars** (plural) of the **living** (not abstract) **world** (singular but plurally inhabited). This is not a perennial philosophy (one grammar for all); it is not a relativism (incommensurable grammars with no shared world); it is a **grammatology of relation** — different living structures of meaning-making that inhabit a shared world without claiming identical access to it.

The concept of **ritual misplacement** — which you've developed as a lens on media, technology, and cultural fragmentation — becomes, in this context, a diagnosis of how separability operates at the level of practice: the rituals that once embedded persons in relational webs have been displaced into commodified simulacra (social media as pseudo-ritual, self-help as pseudo-ceremony). The disease of separability is not just ontological or political — it is **liturgical**. The grammar has been broken at the level of practice, not just thought.

### **The 112 Gateways as Counter-Grammar**

Your contemplative card deck based on the Vijñāna-bhairava-tantra is, in this light, not merely a spiritual practice tool but a **decolonial gesture**: it offers 112 gateways into non-separable experience drawn from a tradition (non-dual Śaiva Tantra) that explicitly teaches that separability is Śiva's own cosmic play — not a colonial imposition but a divine creative act that can be recognized and, in recognition, liberated. The deck doesn't recover a pre-colonial paradise or create a new system; it provides **practices** for metabolizing the desert — for composting separability in the body, not just the mind.
