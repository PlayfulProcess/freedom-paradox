# Cosmic Topology

# **Cosmic topology and the third way beyond binaries**

**The universe itself models non-binary thinking.** Across mathematics, theology, psychology, and political theory, the same structural insight recurs: the whole possesses properties its parts cannot, opposites share a single nature, and genuine freedom lies not in choosing one pole but in recognizing the field that generates both. This research compiles the philosophical and intellectual foundations for an essay arguing that "cosmic topology" — both literal and metaphorical — reveals the poverty of binary thinking and the power of a third way.

---

## **Russell's paradox proves the whole transcends its parts**

The mathematical foundation for this essay begins with a devastating logical discovery. In 1901, Bertrand Russell showed that the "set of all sets" — the *conjunto de todos os conjuntos* — cannot exist without contradiction. The argument is elegant: consider the set R of all sets that are not members of themselves. Is R a member of itself? If yes, then by definition it shouldn't be; if no, then by definition it should be. The binary question "member or not?" has **no valid answer** at the level of totality. The very framing breaks down.

This is not an isolated quirk. Cantor's paradox (1896) showed the set of all sets would need to be both smaller than and equal to its own power set. The Burali-Forti paradox (1897) showed the set of all ordinal numbers generates an ordinal greater than any it contains. Each paradox demonstrates the same principle: **the whole has fundamentally different properties from its elements**. A collection of teaspoons is not itself a teaspoon, but the collection of *all* sets tries to be a set — and collapses into contradiction.

The mathematical resolution reinforces the philosophical point. Zermelo-Fraenkel set theory (ZFC) resolved the paradox by denying the universal set exists as a set at all — it is a "proper class," an entity of a different logical type than its members. Russell's own type theory made it grammatically impossible to even ask whether a set contains itself. Both solutions impose **hierarchy**: you cannot flatten all levels of reality into a single undifferentiated plane. The whole belongs to a different category than its parts.

The connection to emergence is direct. If the totality cannot be treated as "just another element," then reductionism — the attempt to explain wholes purely in terms of parts — faces a structural limit embedded in logic itself. Recent work at UCSD (Li et al., 2025, published in *Neural Computation*) formalizes this using category theory: their framework "ties the potential for emergent effects of a system to its network topology and local structures," encoding emergence as mathematical cohomologies. The principle is not merely philosophical but formally demonstrable.

## **Cosmic topology shows local flatness tells us nothing about global shape**

The literal "cosmic topology" of the essay's title provides the most vivid scientific illustration. **Cosmological topology** — the term coined in 1995 by Marc Lachièze-Rey and Jean-Pierre Luminet — studies the global shape of the universe. Its central revelation: Einstein's general relativity determines local geometry (curvature) but leaves global topology (overall shape) entirely undetermined. Local properties do not dictate global ones.

The Planck mission's final results (2018) measured the universe's curvature parameter at **ΩK = 0.0007 ± 0.0019** — consistent with perfect flatness. But a flat universe could be infinite (like an endless plane) or finite (like a three-dimensional torus, where space wraps around on itself). The flatness you measure in your neighborhood tells you nothing about whether the universe is finite or infinite, simply connected or multiply connected. **The parts cannot determine the nature of the whole.**

The leading candidates for cosmic topology include the **Poincaré dodecahedral space** — proposed by Luminet and colleagues in 2003 — a positively curved, finite space whose volume is 120 times smaller than the hypersphere of equal curvature radius. Colloquially, it's "soccer-ball shaped." In such a universe, light wraps around space creating "topological mirages" — multiple images of the same cosmic sources, like a hall of mirrors. The pattern of hot and cold spots in the cosmic microwave background acts like Chladni patterns on a drumhead: the shape of the "drum" determines the pattern. The method of detection is itself topological — you can only infer the whole from characteristic signatures that no local measurement provides.

This challenges naive binary thinking directly. The question "Is the universe finite or infinite?" may itself be ill-posed without specifying the topology. A flat torus is finite yet has no boundary — combining properties that seem contradictory from a binary perspective. The essay's cosmological metaphor is precise: reality at every scale exhibits properties that cannot be captured by either/or.

### **How a humanities thinker can access mathematical topology**

The most recommended entry point is Jeffrey R. Weeks's *The Shape of Space* (3rd edition, 2020) — written by a MacArthur Fellow who covers geometry, topology, manifolds, and cosmological topology without heavy formalism. Reviewers call it "by far the clearest and best introduction to topology I have ever seen." Sidney Morris's *Topology Without Tears* is free at topologywithouttears.net and praised by students in economics and humanities worldwide. For visual learners, **3Blue1Brown** (Grant Sanderson) on YouTube offers stunning visualizations of topological concepts. Jean-Pierre Luminet's popular book *The Wraparound Universe* (2008) covers cosmic topology accessibly. For deeper study, Bert Mendelson's *Introduction to Topology* (Dover, ~$7) and James Munkres's *Topology* (2nd edition) are the standard progression.

## **Wallis: freedom belongs to the whole, not the individual**

Christopher Wallis (Hareesh), who holds an MPhil from Oxford and PhD from Berkeley in Sanskrit, provides the metaphysical architecture for the essay's argument through Nondual Shaiva Tantra. His core teaching, drawn directly from Abhinavagupta's *Tantraloka*, is that **svātantrya-śakti (the Power of Autonomy) is the one fundamental quality of Consciousness — and it belongs exclusively to the whole, not to individual parts**.

From Wallis's translation of Abhinavagupta: "He has only one fundamental quality, which implies all the others. Therefore, the truly correct precept with regard to Śiva is that He is united exclusively with the Power of Autonomy. The fact that He is said to have many powers follows from His inseparable union with that one Power." And from Wallis's own teaching: "The tradition itself declares that individual agency is a fiction, that there is only one agent, one performer of all actions: God, universal divine Consciousness... there is a single patterned flow of energy or 'intelligence' that underlies everything, and its flow always eventually proves to be irresistible."

This does not mean the individual is an illusion — it means the individual is **the whole in contracted form**. Sūtra 4 of the *Pratyabhijñā-hṛdayam* teaches that the individual consists of "the universe in contracted form" (*vishvamaya sankuchita*). The same five powers that characterize the whole — awareness (*cit*), bliss (*ānanda*), will (*icchā*), knowledge (*jñāna*), and action (*kriyā*) — exist in the individual but in limited, contracted versions. The path of *pratyabhijñā* ("recognition") is the individual discovering that its apparent freedom was always the freedom of the whole expressing through it.

Wallis's nonduality explicitly **includes duality** rather than negating it. He describes the tradition as *Parameshwar Advaya-Vāda* — "the way of the supreme nonduality of divinity." From his translation of Abhinavagupta: "Yet we cannot in truth say that difference does not exist, for this shining manifestation includes everything (even apparent duality)." And from a podcast interview: "Instead of a nondual view that excludes duality and says duality is wrong, this is a nondual view that includes duality, as a valid level of experience, though not an absolute one." This is the structural logic the essay needs: the third way does not eliminate the two poles but reveals them as expressions of a unity that encompasses both.

## **Kriya and karma map the difference between whole-action and part-action**

The kriya/karma distinction operationalizes Wallis's metaphysics. In Nondual Shaiva Tantra, **kriyā-śakti** is one of the five powers of absolute Consciousness — "the Power of Acting," the capacity to assume any form. When this power operates through the contracted individual self (the ego believing itself to be a separate agent), the result is **karma** — bound, effortful action that creates impressions (*saṃskāras*) and binds awareness to the past. When the same power operates through an individual who has "recognized" their identity with the whole, the result is **kriya** — spontaneous, frictionless, divinely-aligned action that does not bind.

As one traditional source puts it: "We turn kriya into karma when we resist kriya or attempt to control it." Wallis describes the post-recognition state: "You are simply letting Life do what it ultimately wants to do through you. Eventually, the teachings must dissolve into direct experience of the divine flow. Now there is no more struggle, no debate, and, curiously, no more illusion of choice — for the liberated being, there is only one thing to do in every moment." And: "What the awakened one experiences is that God/Life/Consciousness has enthusiastically chosen to become the whole of the present moment, and consistently chooses to flow towards the action that is of greatest benefit to all beings."

The distinction matters for the essay because it provides a **practical criterion** for the third way: action arising from identification with one pole (ego-driven, agenda-driven) is karma; action arising from recognition of the whole is kriya. This maps directly onto Parker Palmer's framework and Tim Urban's vertical dimension.

## **Palmer's "letting your life speak" is kriya by another name**

Parker Palmer's *Let Your Life Speak* (1999) reaches the same structural insight from within the Quaker tradition. His core thesis: "Before you tell your life what you intend to do with it, listen for what it intends to do with you." Vocation is not a goal to be achieved but "a calling that I hear." Palmer warns that "the willful pursuit of vocation is an act of violence toward ourselves — violence in the name of a vision that, however lofty, is forced on the self from without rather than grown from within."

The essay's strongest connection comes through Palmer's concept of **"functional atheism"** — which he defines as "the belief that ultimate responsibility for everything rests with us." This is not about religious belief; it's the unexamined conviction that "if anything decent is going to happen here, I am the one who needs to make it happen — a conviction held even by people who talk a good game about God." Functional atheism produces workaholism, burnout, and broken relationships. It explains why groups can tolerate only 15 seconds of silence: "if we are not making noise, we believe, nothing good is happening."

**Functional atheism is karma dressed in Western clothes.** The ego believes it is the sole agent and must control outcomes. Palmer's alternative — listening to what life wants to do through you, acting from "a place of organic reality within me" — is kriya in Quaker language. Palmer writes: "When the gift I give to the other is integral to my own nature, when it comes from a place of organic reality within me, it will renew itself — and me — even as I give it away." This is action that does not deplete because it arises from alignment with the whole rather than from the contracted self.

Palmer's own depression narrative reinforces the argument. He describes having lived "at altitude" — ego, intellect, and imposed ethical "oughts" lifting him off the ground of his being. His therapist's reframe was transformative: "You seem to look upon depression as the hand of an enemy trying to crush you. Do you think you could see it instead as the hand of a friend, pressing you down to ground on which it is safe to stand?" Palmer connects this to Tillich's theology of God as the ground of being: "I had always imagined God to be in the same general direction as everything else I valued: up. I had to be forced underground before I could understand that the way to God is not up but down."

## **Tillich's "God above God" dissolves the theism-atheism binary**

Paul Tillich's *The Courage to Be* (1952) provides the essay's theological backbone. Tillich defines courage as "the self-affirmation of being in spite of the fact of nonbeing" — not the elimination of anxiety but its incorporation. He identifies three existential anxieties that cannot be eliminated because they "belong to existence itself": the anxiety of **fate and death** (dominant in antiquity), **guilt and condemnation** (dominant in the late Middle Ages), and **emptiness and meaninglessness** (dominant in modernity).

The critical move for the essay is Tillich's analysis of what happens when we flee anxiety. Unable to face doubt, people "construct a citadel of certainty, from which they fend off all threat of doubt on the basis of an absolutized authority. This absolutized authority may be either a personal revelation, a social or religious institution, or a fanatical leader of a movement." Binary certainties — rigid belief versus nihilistic despair — are neurotic defenses against anxiety that belongs to the structure of existence itself.

Tillich's climactic concept, **"the God above God,"** transcends the theism/atheism binary outright. He critiques "theological theism" — God conceived as "a being beside others and as such a part of the whole of reality" — as the very thing that generates justified atheism: "This is the God Nietzsche said had to be killed because nobody can tolerate being made into a mere object of absolute knowledge and absolute control." The God above God is **being-itself** — the ground, power, and abyss of being that transcends the subject-object structure entirely. Tillich's famous final line: "The courage to be is rooted in the God who appears when God has disappeared in the anxiety of doubt."

This is not agnosticism or fence-sitting. It is recognizing that both theism and atheism operate within a binary that the ground of being transcends. As Tillich wrote: "It is as atheistic to affirm the existence of God as it is to deny it. God is being itself, not a being." The "third way" here is not a compromise between believing and not believing — it is the recognition of a depth dimension that makes both positions possible.

## **The Trinity models how three transcends two**

The doctrine of the Trinity functions as a philosophical structure for holding opposites together through a third principle. It resolved two competing theological imperatives: Jewish monotheism's absolute distinction between God and creation, and Greek philosophy's need for mediating beings to bridge the ontological gap. By incorporating the Logos (Christ) *within* the Godhead itself, the Trinity created something that is neither pure monotheism nor polytheism but a **third category** — three persons sharing one essence (*homoousion*), distinct yet indivisible.

The technical term **perichoresis** (mutual indwelling) describes how each person of the Trinity fully indwells the other two while maintaining distinct identity. Augustine captured it: "Each are in each, and all in each, and each in all, and all are one." This provides a model of **identity-in-difference** — distinction without separation, unity without fusion.

Hegel was explicit that the Trinity was the foundational structure of his entire philosophy. As Peter Benson explains: "The original triad, from which all the others can be derived, is formed by the three persons of one God. To get a grasp on Hegel's dialectic, it is far more helpful to think in terms of Father, Son and Spirit, than to worry about the quasi-logical terms Thesis, Antithesis, Synthesis." The Holy Spirit, crucially, is not a "third thing" but **the relation between** Father and Son — the living bond of love. The third principle is not a synthesis-as-combination but a focus on the dynamic relationship that generates and unites the first two.

Tillich himself recognized this: "One's need for a balance between the concrete and the absolute drives him toward trinitarian structures." His own theology embodies it — the Abyss (mystery, nonbeing), the Logos (form, meaning), and Spirit (their dynamic union). Parallel trinitarian structures appear across traditions: the Hindu Trimurti (Brahma-Vishnu-Shiva as creation-preservation-destruction), the three gunas (*sattva-rajas-tamas*), and Kashmir Shaivism's Trika ("the threefold" — Shiva, Shakti, and Nara). All function to reconcile apparent opposites through a third principle that reveals their underlying unity.

## **Urban's ladder reframes politics from horizontal to vertical**

Tim Urban's *What's Our Problem?* (2023) applies the same structural logic to political discourse. His central framework — the **Ladder** — proposes that political thinking should be evaluated not on a horizontal left-right axis ("what you think") but on a vertical axis ("how you think"). The Ladder has four rungs: the **Scientist** (follows evidence wherever it leads), the **Sports Fan** (respects rules of rational discourse but has a preferred team), the **Attorney** (starts from conclusions and argues backward), and the **Zealot** (complete certainty, immune to evidence).

The top two rungs constitute "high-rung thinking" — truth-seeking, hypothesis-testing, willingness to update beliefs. The bottom two constitute "low-rung thinking" — tribal, motivated reasoning, treating ideas as sacred rather than testable. Urban's key insight: **"The core conflict that is happening is not left versus right, but low-rung thinking trying to overpower high-rung thinking."** You can be a low-rung moderate or a high-rung extremist. Being centrist doesn't automatically mean reasonable.

At the group level, high-rung cultures produce **Idea Labs** (safe for people, dangerous for ideas — viewpoint diversity encouraged) while low-rung cultures produce **Echo Chambers** (safe for sacred ideas, dangerous for dissent). The emergent entities from these cultures are equally different: Idea Labs generate "Genies" (collective intelligence greater than its parts), while Echo Chambers generate "Golems" ("the ultimate Zealot — a giant that's totally certain of itself, totally unable to learn or change its mind, and worse at thinking than the average human").

For the essay, Urban's framework offers a concrete, contemporary illustration of the vertical third way. The real divide isn't between left and right but between those committed to truth-seeking discourse and those engaged in tribal power games. "For the politically homeless people, a lot of the home they're looking for is actually not in the center" — it's on the upper rungs, alongside high-rung thinkers of any political persuasion. The quality of democratic engagement matters more than ideological content.

## **Leloup's hierarchy matches levels of problem to levels of treatment**

Jean-Yves Leloup (b. 1950), the French Orthodox theologian with a PhD in transpersonal psychology, provides the essay's framework for understanding why different problems require different levels of intervention. Drawing from the patristic Christian trichotomy of body-soul-spirit (*corps-âme-esprit*) and from the ancient Therapeutae of Alexandria described by Philo, Leloup proposes that therapeutic modalities form a hierarchy:

**CBT** addresses behavior and cognition — the surface level, the body's conditioning patterns. **Psychoanalysis** (Freud) addresses the psyche — unconscious patterns, repressed trauma, the deeper architecture beneath behavior. **Psychosynthesis** (Assagioli) addresses the spirit — the transpersonal dimension of meaning, purpose, and connection to what transcends the individual.

This is not a hierarchy of superiority but of **depth and scope**. Each level is appropriate for its corresponding level of problem. A behavioral symptom may respond to CBT. A recurring unconscious pattern may require psychoanalytic depth work. But an existential or spiritual crisis — Palmer's depression, Tillich's anxiety of meaninglessness — requires a framework that acknowledges the transpersonal dimension. The error is treating all problems at a single level, which is itself a form of binary thinking: either everything is behavioral (reductive materialism) or everything is spiritual (spiritual bypass).

Leloup's broader work, including *Prendre soin de l'être* (Care of Being) and *Guérir l'esprit* (Healing the Spirit), consistently argues that modern therapeutic approaches are incomplete without addressing the spiritual dimension. He coined the term **"normose"** — pathological normality — for harmful habits accepted by social consensus, suggesting that the deepest pathologies are those an entire culture shares and therefore cannot see.

## **Psychosynthesis gives the "soul" a map**

Roberto Assagioli (1888–1974), the Italian psychiatrist who brought Freud's ideas to Italy before moving decisively beyond them, founded psychosynthesis in the 1920s. His famous metaphor: "Freud said, 'I am interested only in the basement of the human being.' Psychosynthesis is interested in the whole building. We try to build an elevator which will allow a person access to every level of his personality."

Assagioli's **egg diagram** maps the psyche in three layers: the **lower unconscious** (Freud's territory — drives, repressed material, primitive urges), the **middle unconscious** (preconscious, ordinary mental life, the field of self-actualization), and the **higher unconscious or superconscious** (spiritual energies, creative inspiration, higher values, states of illumination). At the center of awareness sits the conscious "I"; above the egg, connected by a dotted line, sits the **Transpersonal Self** — "outside time and above it, existing in the dimension of the Eternal."

The crucial distinction from Freud: psychosynthesis holds that "the needs for meaning, for higher values, for a spiritual life, are as real as biological or social needs." Spiritual drives are not sublimated sexuality. The "repression of the sublime" — suppressing higher aspirations — can be as psychologically damaging as repressing sexuality. Assagioli explicitly stated: "Psychosynthesis presupposes psychoanalysis, or rather, includes it as a first and necessary stage." This mirrors the non-dual logic of Wallis: the higher includes the lower without negating it.

The Transpersonal Self is described as an "ontological reality" whose relationship to the personal "I" is like the sun to a planet. The personal self is a "reflection or projection" of the Higher Self. This parallels the Recognition tradition's teaching that the individual is the whole in contracted form. Palmer's "true self" maps onto Assagioli's Transpersonal Self; Palmer's "functional atheism" maps onto the personality disconnected from its transpersonal source.

## **Seven traditions converge: opposites share a single nature**

The deepest philosophical support for the essay's argument comes from a principle that appears across at least seven major traditions: **opposites can only be opposites because they share the same fundamental nature**. Hot and cold are both temperatures. Light and dark are both degrees of luminosity. Up and down are both directions on the same road. Without the continuum, the poles cannot exist.

**Heraclitus** (c. 535–475 BCE) articulated this first in the West: "The road up and the road down are one and the same." His doctrine of the unity of opposites holds that the **Logos** — the rational pattern governing the cosmos — is precisely this hidden unity. "God is day night, winter summer, war peace, satiety hunger" — not eight things but one principle holding four pairs together. "Listening not to me but to the Logos, it is wise to agree that all things are one."

**Nicholas of Cusa** (1401–1464) gave this the Latin name **coincidentia oppositorum** — the coincidence of opposites. God, as the Absolute Infinite, is the place where all opposites coincide: "Himself the Absolute Ground, in which all otherness is unity, and all diversity is identity." His mathematical illustration is precise: a circle and a polygon coincide only at infinity, as the polygon's sides approach infinity. Opposites that appear distinct in the finite realm coincide in the Infinite. And crucially: God is *beyond* even the coincidence of opposites — transcending the unity itself.

**Carl Jung** made this the central principle of his entire psychology. He adopted Heraclitus's term **enantiodromia** — "running contrariwise" — for the tendency of extreme one-sidedness to flip into its opposite. His magnum opus, *Mysterium Coniunctionis*, explored the **coniunctio oppositorum** (union of opposites) through alchemical symbolism. "The self, as a symbol of wholeness, is a coincidentia oppositorum, and therefore contains light and darkness simultaneously." Individuation — becoming whole — means integrating conscious and unconscious, persona and shadow, anima and animus.

**Tantric philosophy** teaches that Shiva (consciousness) and Shakti (energy) "are never apart: we never find energy without consciousness, nor consciousness without energy." **Taoism** encodes this in the taijitu symbol — the dot of white within black and black within white showing that each pole contains the seed of its opposite. **Hegel's dialectic** formalizes how thesis and antithesis generate synthesis through *Aufhebung* (sublation) — simultaneously canceling, preserving, and elevating. Even **astrology** illustrates the principle structurally: "It's never really been 12 signs. Instead, there have always been 6 pairs of signs, 6 axes" — Taurus and Scorpio both address security from opposite poles of the same continuum.

The convergence is extraordinary. Every tradition names the same insight differently — Logos, God, the Self, Brahman, the Tao, Absolute Spirit, the Axis — but the structure is identical: **the whole is not one pole or the other but the field on which both poles arise**.

## **The essay's architecture in synthesis**

The argument threads together as follows. Russell's paradox establishes the logical principle: the whole has different properties than its parts and cannot be reduced to them. Cosmic topology demonstrates this scientifically: local flatness tells you nothing about global shape. Wallis translates this into metaphysics: svātantrya (freedom) belongs to Consciousness as a whole, not to any contracted individual part. The kriya/karma distinction operationalizes it practically: action from the whole flows freely; action from a part trying to control creates binding. Palmer independently discovers the same truth in Quaker key — functional atheism (karma) versus letting your life speak (kriya). Tillich provides the theological framework: fleeing into binary certainties is neurotic; genuine courage means accepting anxiety and discovering "the God above God" who transcends theism and atheism alike. The Trinity models how three transcends two — not by eliminating the poles but by revealing their mutual indwelling (perichoresis). Urban applies it to politics: the real divide is vertical (how you think) not horizontal (what you think). Leloup and Assagioli map it onto therapeutic depth: different levels of problem require different levels of intervention, and no single level captures the whole. And seven philosophical traditions converge on the foundational insight: opposites share a single nature, and the whole is the field that generates and encompasses both poles.

The "third way" this essay argues for is not a tepid centrism or fence-sitting compromise. It is the recognition — in the precise technical sense of *pratyabhijñā* — that both poles of any binary arise from and exist within a deeper unity. To identify exclusively with one pole is to be partial. To shuttle endlessly between poles is to be trapped. To recognize the field itself is to discover that freedom was never a property of the choosing part but of the whole that expresses through all apparent choices.
