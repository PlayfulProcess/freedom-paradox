## The Question

Before the book makes any MacIntyre-flavored claim about Enlightenment moral philosophy, this needs pressure-testing. The honest version requires taking Kant seriously — praising what works, identifying precisely where it breaks down, and refusing both dismissal and uncritical defense.

---

## The Strongest Defense of Kant's Universalism

The categorical imperative isn't "be dutiful in a Prussian way." It's a formal test: can you will your maxim as a universal law without contradiction?

- "I will lie whenever convenient" self-destructs when universalized — if everyone lies, the concept of truth on which lying depends collapses.
- "I will steal" self-destructs — if everyone steals, property (the precondition of stealing) ceases to exist.
- This isn't cultural. It's logical. A Blackfoot elder and a Prussian professor would both recognize that a universalized lie destroys the conditions for lying.

### Key Defenders

**Christine Korsgaard** — probably Kant's most formidable living defender. Argues the categorical imperative isn't smuggling in content. It's a constraint on any moral content — a meta-rule about consistency. MacIntyre confuses Kant's *examples* (culturally flavored) with his *principle* (formal). The principle says: whatever you do, you must be able to will that everyone does it. That's not Prussian. That's the structure of rational agency itself.

**Onora O'Neill** — extends this: the categorical imperative is essentially a test against deception and coercion. Any maxim requiring others to be deceived or coerced can't be universalized because it treats others as means rather than ends. This generates a surprisingly robust ethics — anti-colonial, anti-extractive, anti-manipulative — from purely formal premises.

---

## Where It Breaks Down — Decisively

### The emptiness problem (Hegel's original objection)

The formal test tells you which maxims are self-contradictory when universalized. It doesn't tell you which maxims to test.

- "I will always prioritize my child over strangers" — can be universalized without contradiction.
- "I will treat all children equally regardless of biological relation" — also universalizable without contradiction.
- Both pass the formal test. Kant's machinery can't choose between them.

The choice comes from somewhere else — from a culture that values family loyalty or one that values impartial justice. The formal test is genuinely universal. But it's empty. It eliminates some options (lying, stealing, coercing) but can't select among the remaining ones.

**Nobody has fully answered Hegel's objection.** The categorical imperative is like a spell-checker. It can catch errors. It can't write the essay.

### The deeper problem: the model of agency

Even the capacity for the formal test isn't culturally neutral. Kant assumes a very specific model of rationality — individual, disembodied, deliberative, propositional. The rational agent sits alone, examines maxims, tests them for logical consistency. This is a particular topology of mind — the bounded individual reasoning in isolation. It's the Cartesian subject applied to ethics.

Many cultures don't locate moral reasoning in the individual at all:

- **Ifá's divination** puts the moral question to the Odu — to a communal symbolic system that includes ancestors, orisha, and the Babaláwo's training. The moral agent isn't an individual testing maxims. It's a relational field generating wisdom.
- **Quaker business method** locates moral authority in the gathered meeting's collective discernment, not in any individual's reasoning.
- **Ubuntu** says a person becomes rational *through* community — rationality isn't prior to relationship but produced by it.

Kant's formal test may be logically universal. But the subject who performs the test — the isolated rational individual — is the culturally specific part. And that's what gets universalized through colonial power.

**The move:** Not "don't lie" (which is indeed cross-cultural) but "the way to determine what's right is for an individual to reason alone." That's what displaces the elder council, the divination system, the gathered meeting, the ancestor consultation. Not by declaring them wrong but by declaring them pre-rational — lower on a developmental scale that culminates in the autonomous Kantian subject.

---

## The Honest Answer for the Book

Kant's formal principle — don't universalize self-contradictory maxims — likely does hold as a genuine logical constraint. MacIntyre overshoots when he implies the categorical imperative is entirely Prussian content. It's not. "Don't lie, don't coerce, don't treat people as mere instruments" is genuinely cross-cultural and logically derivable.

But the formal principle is thin. It eliminates some bad options and selects among none of the remaining ones. And more importantly, the model of moral agency that Kant packages with the principle — individual, disembodied, deliberative — is the culturally specific payload. **The universal logic is the Trojan horse. Inside it is a very particular model of who counts as a moral reasoner and how moral reasoning should be conducted.** And that model, when enforced through colonial power, displaces every communal, embodied, relational moral practice it encounters — not by refuting them but by defining them as primitive.

## Through the Book's Three-Filter Test (Ch 8)

| Filter | Formal level | Agency level |
|---|---|---|
| **Useful?** | Yes — a genuine logical check against self-contradictory maxims | Partially — but empty without communal content to fill it |
| **Fits the data?** | Partially — "don't lie, don't coerce" is cross-cultural | Fails — the isolated rational individual is culturally specific, not universal |
| **Compassionate?** | Fails in edge cases (never lie to the murderer) | Fails systemically — authorizes displacement of communal moral reasoning with individual moral reasoning. This is epistemicide at the level of *how we decide what's right* |

## Editorial Principle

The book's three-filter test can acknowledge what's genuinely universal in Kant (the formal constraint) while identifying precisely where the colonial reflex hides (the model of agency). That's more honest than either dismissing Kant entirely or defending him uncritically.

**This demonstrates what the filter is for** — not sorting systems into good and bad but identifying where each one's adaptive function ends and its extractive function begins.

The book praises those who tried to solve the problem of universal ethics. It acknowledges they may get it right at the formal level. The critique is not "they were wrong" but "they were incomplete" — they built freedom without building the responsibility structures to hold it. The categorical imperative is a constraint. Grammars are the content. You need both.
