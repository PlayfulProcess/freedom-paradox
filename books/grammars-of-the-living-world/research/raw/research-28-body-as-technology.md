# **The body as technology: how embodied practices encode relational selfhood**

Bodies that move together literally synchronize — their brainwaves couple, their heartbeats align, their endorphin systems co-activate, and the neurological boundary between self and other temporarily dissolves. This is not metaphor. A convergence of hyperscanning neuroscience, polyvagal theory, endorphin research, and cross-cultural ethnography now confirms what indigenous traditions have practiced for millennia: **rhythmic, collective, embodied practice is a fundamental technology of human consciousness and social cohesion**, not merely cultural expression. The implications for contemplative technology design are profound and paradoxical — the most important thing a digital platform can do may be to point people away from screens and toward each other's physical presence.

This report covers the neuroscience of synchrony and embodied selfhood, indigenous and traditional practices that never separated body from community, the clinical evidence base for somatic therapies, and what all of this means for designing contemplative technology platforms. The through-line is a distinction between two topologies of the human being — the individual-bounded-self (dominant in Western modernity) and the relational-embedded-self (the default mode of most human cultures across history) — and how embodied practice is the primary medium through which the relational topology is activated and sustained.

## **Moving together rewires the boundary between self and other**

The neuroscience case begins with mirror neurons, discovered by Rizzolatti and Gallese in macaque premotor cortex in 1992\. About **40% of visually responsive neurons** in the relevant brain area fire both when an individual performs a motor act and when they observe another performing the same act. Gallese's concept of "embodied simulation" proposes that this mirror mechanism provides the neural substrate for shared intersubjectivity — action understanding achieved without cognitive mediation, through direct bodily resonance. The same motor neuron that fires when you grasp a peanut fires when you watch someone else do so. This means the nervous system is architecturally relational, not self-contained.

Stephen Porges' Polyvagal Theory, introduced in 1994–95, extends this picture. His model identifies three hierarchical autonomic circuits: the evolutionarily newest ventral vagal complex (supporting social engagement), the sympathetic system (fight/flight), and the ancient dorsal vagal complex (freeze/shutdown). The ventral vagal pathway coordinates facial expression, vocalization, listening, and heart rate into what Porges calls the **"Social Engagement System"** — a neural infrastructure that allows nervous systems to broadcast and receive cues of safety, enabling continuous autonomic co-regulation between individuals. His concept of "neuroception" describes how safety and threat are detected pre-cognitively, through embodied evaluation rather than conscious reasoning. It bears noting that Polyvagal Theory faces significant scientific critique — Grossman's 2023 analysis in *Biological Psychology* challenged its phylogenetic claims, and a 2025 paper gathered 38+ researchers questioning its neuroanatomical foundations — yet its clinical utility as a heuristic for understanding nervous system states remains widely valued by trauma therapists.

The synchrony research fills in the mechanism. Scott Wiltermuth and Chip Heath's 2009 experiments in *Psychological Science* found that people who walked in synchrony felt significantly more connected (effect size d \= 0.96) and cooperated more in economic games than those who walked asynchronously. Crucially, positive emotions were not required — the bonding effect operated through structural synchrony, not mood enhancement. Bronwyn Tarr's 2015 study with **264 participants** in Brazil demonstrated that both synchrony and physical exertion independently raised pain thresholds (a proxy for endorphin release) and in-group bonding, with the combination producing the greatest effects. Her 2016 "silent disco" study with wireless headphones confirmed that only those dancing in full synchrony showed elevated endorphin release.

Robin Dunbar's endorphin hypothesis ties this together. Since primate social bonding depends on one-on-one grooming (which activates the endorphin system but is too time-costly for human-sized groups), humans evolved synchronized movement, singing, dancing, and laughter as behaviors that activate the same neurochemical bonding system in **multiple individuals simultaneously**. This is the evolutionary logic behind why keeping together in time creates social cohesion — it is a scaled-up grooming mechanism, operating through the brain's opioid system.

Hyperscanning studies now provide direct neural evidence. Brain-to-brain synchronization has been documented during conversation, cooperative tasks, musical performance, and classroom engagement. Pérez, Carreiras, and Duñabeitia's 2017 study found brainwave synchronization between speakers and listeners beyond what shared auditory input could explain. Most strikingly, Xygalatas and colleagues measured heart-rate dynamics during a Spanish fire-walking ritual, finding **strong alignment between fire-walkers' and spectators' heart rates** — the empathetic response extending through a network effect from related to unrelated observers. This was the first quantitative measurement of what Durkheim called collective effervescence.

## **Two topologies of the body encode two topologies of selfhood**

The research above supports a fundamental distinction. In one mode — body-as-instrument-of-individual-will — the body is a bounded tool to be optimized: the gym, the competitive race, the solo performance. This engages predominantly sympathetic activation, self-monitoring, and the Western default model of the autonomous individual. In the other mode — body-as-medium-of-collective-participation — the body becomes a permeable node in a collective field: circle dance, call-and-response singing, communal labor. This engages ventral vagal activation, mirror neuron coupling, endorphin release through synchrony, and what William McNeill called **"muscular bonding"** — the felt dissolution of self-other boundaries.

The geometry of circle dance versus solo performance encodes this distinction physically. In circle dance there is no audience; all are participants, and the spatial arrangement itself encodes equality and mutual visibility. In solo performance, one body is elevated for observation by others, maintaining the performer/audience distinction. These are not merely cultural metaphors — they activate different physiological systems.

McNeill articulated this in his 1995 book *Keeping Together in Time*, drawing on his own experience of close-order drill during World War II: "A strange sense of personal enlargement; a sort of swelling out, becoming bigger than life, thanks to participation in a collective ritual." His historical survey spans paleolithic origins through Greek civilization, early Christianity, Islamic prayer, early modern military drill, and Japanese factory exercises. His conclusion is stark: **"Human beings desperately need to belong to communities... and moving rhythmically while giving voice together is the surest, most speedy, and efficacious way of creating and sustaining such communities that our species has ever hit upon."**

Durkheim named this in 1912 as "collective effervescence" — "a sort of electricity formed by their collecting which quickly transports them to an extraordinary degree of exaltation." Modern validation comes from Liebst's 2019 multilevel regression showing spatial density of gatherings predicts emotional effervescence, and from Pizarro's 2022 meta-analytic review finding CE strongly associated with self-transcendent emotions. Victor Turner's complementary concept of "communitas" — the spontaneous, egalitarian, liminal state arising through bodily ritual — described the subjective experience: "something magical... the feeling of endless power" when social masks are dropped and participants encounter each other as equals.

The cross-cultural psychology of Markus and Kitayama (1991) provides the theoretical frame. Their foundational paper in *Psychological Review* documented two fundamentally different self-construals: the **independent self** (unique, autonomous, firm boundaries — characteristic of Western cultures) and the **interdependent self** (defined through relationships, permeable boundaries — characteristic of many Asian, African, and Southern European cultures). Alan Watts captured this in his 1966 book *The Book* with characteristic directness: "The prevalent sensation of oneself as a separate ego enclosed in a bag of skin is a hallucination which accords neither with Western science nor with the experimental philosophy-religions of the East." Ubuntu philosophy — *Umuntu ngumuntu ngabantu*, "a person is a person through other persons" — represents the most fully developed philosophical articulation of the relational self, locating personhood not as innate status but as an ongoing process of becoming through community.

## **Indigenous practices that never severed body from community**

Every indigenous embodied practice examined in this research is structurally communal — not individual practice done in proximity to others, but practice whose very architecture requires collective participation.

**West African polyrhythmic drumming** may be the most sophisticated example. In a polyrhythmic ensemble, one drummer plays a three-beat pattern, another four-beat, another six-beat — creating interlocking layers where no single rhythm is complete without the others. As John Miller Chernoff documented in *African Rhythm and African Sensibility* (1979), this creates structural interdependence: each player must hold awareness of the other parts simultaneously, producing what amounts to a web of relational attention. Children learn cross-beats (three against two) through games, developing what scholars call "a two-dimensional attitude to rhythm" — the ability to hold one's own pattern while tracking the whole. The drum is described as "the sign of life: its beat is the heartbeat of the community."

The San/Bushman healing dance of the Kalahari — potentially the longest continuous religious tradition in humanity, estimated at **70,000–100,000 years old** — demonstrates the communal structure at its most radical. Women sit in a tight circle, providing complex rhythmic clapping and polyphonic singing. Men dance around them in a circular rut, feet pounding in counterpoint, wearing dry cocoon rattles. As the intensity builds, healers enter \!kia (trance), during which they perceive and remove sickness and mend social conflicts. Richard Katz's *Boiling Energy* (1982) documented a crucial feature: by adulthood, approximately half of men and a third of women have become healers. This is emphatically not a specialist practice — the entire community participates, and without the women's singing, healing cannot occur. The practice simultaneously treats individual illness and repairs social fabric.

Malidoma Patrice Somé (1956–2021), a Dagara man from Burkina Faso who held doctorates from the Sorbonne and Brandeis, described his people's grief rituals as embodied communal processing of loss. In Dagara villages, grief rituals happen weekly — involving singing, drumming, wailing, and keening held within the containment of song and ritual structure. "Communal grieving offers something we cannot get when we grieve by ourselves," wrote his wife Sobonfu. Somé's central critique of the West was that its pathologies — depression, addiction, alienation — stem from the loss of ritual and community: "A community that doesn't have a ritual cannot exist."

Capoeira emerged as embodied resistance during more than 300 years of slavery in Brazil. Enslaved Africans encoded martial techniques in dance, preserving combat knowledge, cultural identity, and spiritual practice through the body when all other forms of transmission were forbidden. The roda (circle) is described as "a metaphor for the circle of life," where those forming the circle are as essential as the two players inside — setting energy through clapping, singing, and instruments. Robert Farris Thompson, in his landmark *Flash of the Spirit* (1983), traced how five African civilizations transmitted aesthetic and philosophical knowledge through the body across the Middle Passage — demonstrating that **embodied knowledge proved indestructible** even when material culture, language, and social structures were deliberately stripped away. UNESCO recognized Roda de Capoeira as Intangible Heritage of Humanity in 2014\.

The Sufi traditions offer a different architecture. Mevlevi whirling (sema), inspired by Rumi's ecstatic response to the rhythmic hammering of a goldbeater, involves semazens whirling counter-clockwise with right palm upward (receiving divine grace) and left palm downward (channeling it to the world). The whirlers trained to spin at the same speed, maintaining identical positions to facilitate selflessness. Group dhikr — rhythmic repetition of divine names with coordinated breathing and swaying — creates intense shared states that Sufi practitioners describe through the concept of fana (annihilation of self) leading to baqa (eternal existence with God). Individual transformation occurs within, and depends upon, the communal container.

Mark Singleton's *Yoga Body* (2010) documents what happens when the communal dimension is stripped away. Traditional yoga as described in Patanjali's Yoga Sutras prioritized ethical disciplines (yamas, niyamas), meditation, and spiritual transformation within a guru-student relationship; postures received minimal attention, with only twelve listed poses, all seated. Modern postural yoga emerged in 1920s India under the influence of European gymnastics and bodybuilding, and was further individualized in Western adoption — becoming predominantly solo, postural, exercise-focused, and severed from its communal and ethical context.

The Quaker tradition provides a remarkable Western example of embodied practice being lost. Early Friends literally quaked, trembled, and shook during worship — their name derives directly from this bodily practice. Douglas Gwyn describes early Quaker worship as "strongly emotional, filled with dread, punctuated with inchoate sounds of sobbing, groaning, sighing and impromptu singing." By the close of the 17th century, quaking fell out of favor; the Shakers split off in 1747, preserving the ecstatic elements mainstream Quakers abandoned. Modern Quaker worship is primarily silent and still — a vivid case study in the systematic suppression of embodied practice within Western Christianity.

## **The body's return: from Reich to the evidence base for somatic therapies**

Wilhelm Reich (1897–1957) was the first psychotherapist to systematically work with the body. A protégé of Freud and deputy director of his Vienna Ambulatorium, Reich broke with psychoanalysis over the body's role, introducing the concept of **"character armor"** — chronic muscular tensions that serve as psychological defenses. A child who suppresses crying clenches the jaw, tightens the stomach, holds breath; repeated, these patterns become structural. Reich's later work on "orgone energy" moved into pseudoscience, and his fate was grim: the Nazis burned his books in 1933, he fled to America, and in 1956 the FDA ordered six tons of his books, journals, and papers burned at the Gansevoort incinerator in New York. He died in Lewisburg Federal Penitentiary in 1957\. No established journal carried an obituary.

Yet his core insight — that psychological defenses are encoded in the body — generated an entire lineage. Alexander Lowen developed Bioenergetic Analysis in the 1950s, mapping character structures to bodily patterns. The Feldenkrais Method (Moshe Feldenkrais, 1904–1984) took a different path, positioning itself as somatic education rather than therapy, emphasizing neuroplasticity and minimal-effort exploratory movement.

Peter Levine's Somatic Experiencing (SE), developed from the 1970s onward, represents the lineage's most clinically developed form. His core insight — that trauma is a physiological response, not merely psychological, occurring when fight/flight responses are interrupted and survival energy remains undischarged — was inspired by observing that wild animals face constant threat but discharge freeze-state energy through spontaneous trembling. The first known RCT (Brom et al., 2017\) tested SE with 63 PTSD participants, finding large effect sizes (Cohen's d \= **0.94–1.26**) for PTSD severity reduction. A 2021 scoping review found "preliminary evidence" across 16 qualifying studies, though it called for stronger clinical trials.

Bessel van der Kolk's *The Body Keeps the Score* (2014) — now 376 weeks on the New York Times bestseller list, translated into 43 languages — became the popular synthesis of this work. His 2014 RCT of trauma-sensitive yoga for chronic PTSD (N \= 60 women) found that **52% of the yoga group no longer met PTSD criteria** versus 21% of controls, with large effect sizes (d \= 1.07). A broader meta-analysis of 20 RCTs (N \= 954\) on yoga for PTSD found a standardized mean difference of −0.51. Van der Kolk's broader argument — that body-based practices succeed where talk therapy alone fails — has been influential despite significant scientific criticism; Scheeringa's 2024 analysis found "multiple instances where cited evidence was incomplete, selectively presented, or erroneously presented."

Dance/Movement Therapy has the deepest institutional roots. Marian Chace began working at St. Elizabeths Hospital in 1942, becoming the first dance therapist formally employed by the federal government. She worked with schizophrenic patients and PTSD veterans in circles, using empathic attunement to reflect patients' nonverbal expressions through her own movement. She founded the American Dance Therapy Association in **1966**. Koch and colleagues' 2019 meta-analysis of 41 controlled studies (N \= 2,374) found DMT effective for decreasing depression and anxiety and increasing quality of life, with effect sizes of 0.27–0.45. Research production has accelerated from 1.3 studies per year (1996–2012) to 6.8 per year (2012–2018).

EMDR, with 38+ RCTs, stands as the most extensively studied body-involving trauma therapy. Meta-analyses consistently show effectiveness equivalent to trauma-focused CBT, though the mechanism debate remains unresolved — whether bilateral stimulation contributes beyond the exposure component is still contested, with most meta-analyses finding eye movements make "little or no difference" to outcomes, though Lee and Cuijpers' 2013 analysis found a moderate effect in clinical contexts.

The breathwork lineage connects modern practice to ancient traditions. Stanislav Grof developed Holotropic Breathwork in the mid-1970s as a non-drug alternative to his earlier LSD research; scientific evidence remains anecdotal. The Wim Hof Method has stronger research backing — Kox et al.'s 2014 study in *PNAS* demonstrated that trained practitioners could voluntarily influence their innate immune response — though a 2024 systematic review rated overall study quality as "very low." Both modalities share mechanisms with pranayama traditions, operating through controlled manipulation of blood CO₂ levels.

## **The systematic erasure of ecstatic practice in the West**

Barbara Ehrenreich's *Dancing in the Streets: A History of Collective Joy* (2006) documents what amounts to a centuries-long campaign against embodied collective practice. Early Christian services were reportedly "noisy and charismatic, involving the consumption of wine, listening to music, and dancing." The Roman Catholic Church progressively drove festivities out of churches into streets during the 12th and 13th centuries. Protestant reformers then **criminalized carnival**. The Calvinist work ethic and emerging market capitalism rendered festivity suspect — unproductive, disorderly, threatening to hierarchy.

The suppression produced eruptions. "Dance manias" swept Europe — hundreds suddenly and uncontrollably dancing until collapse — possibly representing "a collective, contagious impulse to recapture earlier forms of ecstatic dance." In 17th-century England, an epidemic of melancholy engulfed the educated classes (Bunyan, Donne, Johnson, Cromwell). Ehrenreich connects this directly to the loss of collective ecstatic practice. Colonialism then exported the suppression globally. As she writes: European colonizers learned "something centrally important about themselves: that the essence of the Western mind... was its ability to resist the contagious rhythm of the drums, to wall itself up in a fortress of ego and rationality against the seductive wildness of the world."

Descartes' *Cogito ergo sum* provided the philosophical architecture — locating selfhood in thought, severing mind from body. This was not merely abstract philosophy; it underwrote a cultural infrastructure that devalued embodied, ecstatic, and collective modes of knowing. The modern return of the body — through somatic therapies, yoga's mainstreaming, ecstatic dance movements, and neuroscientific validation of embodied cognition — represents a counter-movement against four centuries of suppression.

## **The paradox at the heart of contemplative technology**

The most challenging implication of this research for contemplative technology design is a fundamental limitation: **the core mechanisms of embodied practice — autonomic co-regulation, entrainment, endorphin release through synchrony, collective effervescence — require physical co-presence.** Mirror neuron activation is diminished through video; Shimada and Hiraki (2006) found greater sensorimotor activation in response to live observation versus recordings. A 2017 analysis in *Frontiers in Psychology* concluded that "the exaptation of the neural architecture supporting communication operates less efficiently and effectively in the presence of temporal, spatial, and social disruptions" characteristic of video-mediated interaction. Vine's 2023 ethnographic study concluded directly: **"Physical co-presence is imperative if we are to realise a sense of social togetherness."**

COVID-19 provided the natural experiment. When embodied practices moved online, a study of 209 meditation teachers and 318 practitioners found teachers perceived online programs "somewhat less effective" (Cohen's d \= 0.46). Practitioners appreciated accessibility and reported improvements — likely because they had more time — but consistently identified the loss of community as the primary deficit. Meditation app use surged nearly **3,000%** globally during the pandemic, while research showed increased loneliness and decreased mental well-being across populations. The lesson was clear: digital delivery increases accessibility but cannot replicate the communal dimension.

Randall Collins' interaction ritual theory provides the framework for understanding why. Ritual requires group assembly, barriers to outsiders, mutual focus of attention, and shared mood — producing group solidarity, emotional energy, and sacred symbols. Collins argues that "bodies moving into the same place starts off the ritual process" because there is a "minimal excitement of being within a mass of human bodies." Durkheim himself stated the principle: "All forces, even the most spiritual, are worn away over time if nothing comes along to restore the energy they lose." Collective effervescence must be periodically restored through physical assembly — it is not a one-time event but requires ongoing embodied renewal.

Yet digital technology has genuine capabilities. It can schedule and coordinate in-person practice groups. It can educate through video instruction and written guides. It can connect people to local communities — Meetup.com hosts thousands of meditation groups worldwide. It can track seasonal rhythms and create accountability structures. Insight Timer originally functioned as a "social network to meet other Buddhists in their community." The hybrid model — digital coordination layer supporting embodied practice — is consistently recommended in post-COVID research.

## **Ritual misplacement and the consumption engine**

Byung-Chul Han's analysis in *The Disappearance of Rituals* identifies the core dynamic of what might be called "ritual misplacement." He distinguishes "community without communication" (ritual togetherness, where shared presence suffices) from "communication without community" (digital connection, where constant messaging fails to create belonging). When legitimate ritual containers dissolve, the impulses they served — for belonging, meaning, collective effervescence, transcendence — do not disappear. They migrate.

Randall Collins' interaction ritual chain theory shows the mechanism: people seek "emotional energy" through rituals, and when legitimate sources are unavailable, they seek substitutes. Consumer culture actively mimics ritual structures — brand communities as congregations, shopping as pilgrimage, social media posting as testimony and confession. A 2022 Oxford Academic typology identified social media rituals of consumption (unboxing), sensation (ASMR), transformation (decluttering), pledging (anti-haul), and disclosure — each representing genuine ritual impulses channeled through commercial platforms. As Han writes: "Today we consume not only things themselves but also the emotions bound up with things. You cannot consume things endlessly, but emotions you can." The substitutes fail to satisfy, creating compulsive loops — the architectural logic of addiction operating at the scale of culture.

Malidoma Somé saw this from the outside with devastating clarity: "The hurt that a person feels in the midst of this modern culture should be taken as a language spoken by the body. Our soul communicates things to us that the body translates as need, or want, or absence. So we enter into ritual in order to respond to the call of the soul." Without ritual, humans live in nostalgia — or in compulsive consumption, which is nostalgia's commercial form.

## **What a contemplative platform should actually do**

The design implication is paradoxical but clear. A contemplative technology platform's most important function may be helping people find and sustain local embodied practice communities — serving as matchmaker for in-person practice rather than substitute for it. The concept of "digital threshold, embodied sanctuary" captures this: technology as doorway to non-digital practice, coordination layer rather than the practice itself.

No major platform currently occupies this design space with full intentionality. Headspace and Calm are primarily individual and subscription-based, criticized for commodifying mindfulness and detaching it from communal roots. Insight Timer's original design came closest — showing who was meditating nearby and facilitating local connections — but has drifted toward the individualistic self-optimization model. Meetup provides matchmaking without contemplative framing. Mindful Leader explicitly frames group meditation as "reciprocal-care" rather than self-care. But none is designed around the honest acknowledgment that the most transformative dimensions of contemplative practice require physical co-presence, and that technology's role is to facilitate the conditions under which bodies can come together.

The research surveyed here suggests such a platform would need to support several functions: connecting people to local practice groups by tradition, geography, and seasonal rhythm; providing education about the full range of embodied practices (not just seated meditation); honoring indigenous and traditional contexts rather than extracting techniques; tracking the natural calendars that traditional practices are embedded in; and, crucially, being honest about its own limitations. The platform that acknowledges "you need to put down this screen and go be with other people" is, paradoxically, the most trustworthy platform — and the one most aligned with what the science and the traditions converge upon.

## **Conclusion: the body remembers what the culture forgot**

The convergence documented here — from Rizzolatti's mirror neurons to the San healing dance, from Dunbar's endorphin hypothesis to Dagara grief ritual, from hyperscanning studies to Quaker quaking — points to a single conclusion. The human nervous system is not a self-contained unit but a relational organ, continuously calibrated by the nervous systems of others through proximity, rhythm, and shared movement. The "individual-bounded-self" is not a neurological given but a culturally constructed topology — one that the Western philosophical tradition (Descartes), economic system (capitalism's productivity ethic), and religious heritage (Protestant suspicion of the body) conspired to make seem natural and inevitable.

What indigenous and traditional practices preserve — and what somatic therapies are rediscovering — is that **the relational-embedded-self is accessed through the body in motion with other bodies**. No amount of cognitive understanding substitutes for the direct experience of autonomic co-regulation, endorphin release through synchrony, and the felt dissolution of self-other boundaries that McNeill called muscular bonding and Durkheim called collective effervescence. The body remembers what the culture forgot. The question for contemplative technology is whether it will deepen the forgetting — by offering yet another screen-mediated simulation of connection — or serve as a threshold through which people rediscover the irreplaceable power of physical co-presence. The research is unambiguous about which direction to go.
