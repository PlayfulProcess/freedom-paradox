# **The ancient mirror: comfort-seeking and overshoot in deep human time**

The claim that destructive attachment to comfort is uniquely modern is almost certainly false. **The same behavioral and ecological pattern driving today's environmental overshoot — enter a resource-rich system, consume faster than it regenerates, simplify ecosystems, crash or adapt — appears everywhere humans migrated for at least 50,000 years.** The Pleistocene megafauna extinctions, the depopulation of Pacific island birds, the collapse of Madagascar's giant lemurs, and the obliteration of New Zealand's moa all share a causal structure with the modern crisis: an intelligent, cooperative, fire-wielding generalist enters an ecosystem without co-evolved checks and triggers catastrophic simplification. What fossil fuels changed was scale and speed, not the underlying dynamic.

Yet this is not a story of humans as uniquely evil. It is an ecological one. Indigenous fire management across California and Australia demonstrates that humans can become biodiversity-enhancing ecological actors — but only after millennia of accumulated relational knowledge. The modern crisis may be less the emergence of something new than the suppression of that hard-won wisdom combined with the unprecedented amplification of an ancient tendency.

## **Fire and extinction at the La Brea tar pits**

The most detailed case study linking human activity to Pleistocene megafauna extinction in California comes from a landmark 2023 *Science* cover article by F. Robin O'Keefe, Emily Lindsey, and colleagues at the La Brea Tar Pits. The team radiocarbon-dated **172 specimens** from seven extinct and one extant species spanning 15,600 to 10,000 years ago. Their findings reconfigured the extinction timeline for Southern California and pointed firmly toward a fire-mediated synergy between human arrival and climate stress.

Seven megafauna species — saber-toothed cats, dire wolves, American lions, Columbian mammoths, ancient bison, ground sloths, and western camels — **disappeared by 12,900 years ago, before the Younger Dryas cooling even began**. Sloths and camels vanished first, around 13,600 years ago. Then herbivore populations crashed precipitously around 13,200 years ago, with carnivores following. The extinction coincided precisely with a **300-year spike in charcoal** preserved in Lake Elsinore sediment cores, roughly 80 miles from the tar pits — the signature of catastrophic, landscape-scale fires without precedent in the prior record.

The proposed mechanism is a synergistic cascade. Climate warming of approximately **5.6°C** during the Bølling-Allerød period dried Southern California's juniper-oak woodlands into fire-prone tinder. But the region had endured similar warm-dry periods before without ecological collapse. The difference was humans — a novel ignition source practicing landscape-scale burning for hunting, clearing, and plant management. Fire transformed woodland into chaparral scrub permanently, destroying megafaunal habitat and food sources. As Lindsey put it: "The difference at the end of the last ice age is humans are here." Time-series VARX modeling confirmed that **human population growth was the key catalyst** of this ecological state shift.

The La Brea study carries a haunting coda. O'Keefe's team noted that conditions triggering the end-Pleistocene state shift in Southern California "are recurring today across the western United States." Southern California has warmed more than 2°C in the past century — an order of magnitude faster than the Bølling-Allerød warming that preceded the original collapse.

## **The overkill debate has shifted toward synergy**

Paul Martin's blitzkrieg overkill hypothesis — that human hunters rapidly exterminated naïve megafauna across the Americas within roughly a thousand years — dominated the debate for decades after its formal articulation in the 1960s. The evidence for it remains formidable at the global scale. A 2014 analysis by Sandom and colleagues in *Proceedings of the Royal Society B* found that **extinction severity was "strongly tied to hominin palaeobiogeography, with at most a weak, Eurasia-specific link to climate change."** A 2024 review by Svenning and colleagues in *Cambridge Prisms: Extinction* surveyed the entire field and concluded there is "little support for any major influence of climate" and "strong and increasing support for human pressures as the key driver." The pattern is starkest when viewed globally: Africa, where megafauna co-evolved with hominins over millions of years, retained most of its large animals. Every continent humans entered as newcomers suffered catastrophic megafauna loss.

Direct evidence of human dietary reliance on megafauna was dramatically strengthened by a 2024 *Science Advances* paper analyzing isotopes from the Anzick-1 Clovis burial in Montana (\~12,800 years ago). The child's mother derived roughly **40% of her protein from mammoth and 96% from megafauna overall** — her isotopic profile was virtually identical to the scimitar cat, an obligate mammoth specialist.

Yet the North American picture resists simple overkill. Archaeologists Donald Grayson and David Meltzer have argued for decades that only about 15 kill sites across the entire continent link humans to extinct megafauna — remarkably sparse evidence for a "blitzkrieg." A 2021 *Nature Communications* study by Stewart and colleagues found greater concordance between megafaunal declines and climate change than with human population growth for North America specifically. The emerging consensus, particularly after the La Brea study, favors **multi-causal synergy**: climate change pushed ecosystems toward vulnerability, human arrival introduced both direct predation and novel fire, and the combination — but neither factor alone — triggered irreversible ecological state shifts. Jacquelyn Gill's foundational 2009 *Science* paper added a critical feedback loop: as megaherbivores declined, ungrazed vegetation accumulated as fuel, intensifying fires, further degrading habitat in a self-reinforcing cascade.

The Younger Dryas impact hypothesis — the proposal that a cometary airburst around 12,850 years ago triggered the cooling event, hemisphere-spanning wildfires, and extinctions — remains **rejected by the majority of the scientific community**. Proposed by Richard Firestone and colleagues in 2007, the hypothesis has been plagued by replication failures, the absence of a crater, and shifting descriptions of the impactor. Most critically, the La Brea data shows California megafauna were extinct before the Younger Dryas began. The hypothesis retains vocal proponents, including James Kennett at UC Santa Barbara, but a 2025 *Skeptical Inquirer* assessment characterized it as a "failed hypothesis."

## **The same pattern, continent after continent**

The California case is one node in a global pattern that repeats with eerie consistency wherever *Homo sapiens* arrived as a newcomer.

**Australia (\~50,000–45,000 years ago)**: More than **85% of mammals, birds, and reptiles weighing over 100 pounds** vanished within roughly 4,000 years of human arrival. A 2017 *Nature Communications* study by van der Kaars and Gifford Miller used a 150,000-year marine sediment core to show megafaunal population collapse between 45,000 and 43,100 years ago — with "no temporal association between climatically driven environmental changes and megafaunal extinction." Miller's "imperceptible overkill" model demonstrated that even killing one juvenile mammal per person per decade could drive extinction within centuries. The megafauna had survived far worse climate fluctuations over the preceding 100,000 years; what they had never faced was a tool-using, fire-wielding social predator.

**New Zealand (\~700 years ago)**: The moa extinction is the **clearest case of human-caused megafauna extinction on record**. Polynesian settlers arrived around 1280 CE. Nine moa species were extinct within 100–200 years. Ancient DNA from 281 individual moas showed "no loss of genetic diversity" before human arrival — populations were stable and even expanding. A founding population of perhaps 400 humans, growing to no more than 2,000, accomplished the extinction. A 2025 modeling study in *Science of the Total Environment* concluded that extinction "could only have been avoided if Polynesian colonists maintained unrealistically expansive no-take zones covering at least half of New Zealand's land area."

**Pacific Islands (\~3,500–900 years ago)**: David Steadman's landmark 1995 *Science* paper estimated Polynesian expansion eliminated over **2,000 bird species — a 20% worldwide reduction in bird diversity**. A 2013 Bayesian analysis refined this to roughly 1,300 species, with flightless birds 33 times more likely to vanish. Pimm and colleagues noted in 2006 that European exploration "merely continued to extinguish species at rates similar to those caused by the earlier Polynesian expansion."

**Madagascar (\~2,500–1,000 years ago)**: Giant lemurs, elephant birds, pygmy hippos, and giant tortoises survived multiple severe climate episodes over tens of thousands of years. They did not survive human arrival. A 2020 *Science Advances* speleothem study showed that "millennial-scale drying trends and megadroughts occurred repeatedly" and "were more severe and longer than their late Holocene counterparts" — yet megafauna survived them all. Recovery of pre-human mammalian diversity on Madagascar would take an estimated **2.9 million years**.

The pattern is unmistakable. Each time *Homo sapiens* entered an ecosystem where prey had not co-evolved with hominin predators, the same sequence unfolded: exploitation of naïve fauna, population expansion, resource depletion, ecological simplification. The timeline compressed as human technology improved — millennia in Australia, centuries in New Zealand, decades for modern industrial extinctions — but the causal architecture remained constant.

## **Humans as invasive species is an ecological claim, not a moral one**

The ecological parallel is both precise and well-documented in the scholarly literature. Curtis Marean of Arizona State University, writing in *Scientific American* in 2015, framed the human out-of-Africa dispersal explicitly as an invasion event: "Everywhere *H. sapiens* went, massive ecological changes followed. The archaic humans they encountered went extinct, as did vast numbers of animal species. It was, without a doubt, **the most consequential migration event in the history of our planet.**"

Humans fit the technical criteria of invasive species dynamics with uncomfortable exactness. The classic invasive pattern described by Strayer and colleagues in a 2017 *Ecology Letters* review — lag phase, exponential growth, overshoot of carrying capacity, resource depletion, ecosystem simplification, crash or equilibrium — maps onto human expansion at every scale. The key mechanism is **ecological release**: when an organism escapes its co-evolved checks (predators, pathogens, competitors), population constraints dissolve. Marc Cadotte of the University of Toronto puts it plainly: "They've been able to escape their evolutionary controls... we are very good at doing that."

William Catton's 1980 book *Overshoot: The Ecological Basis of Revolutionary Change* provided the foundational framework. Catton introduced the concept of "ghost acreages" — the phantom carrying capacity created by fossil fuels, which allowed industrial civilization to temporarily behave as though Earth were vastly larger than it is. His concept of "Homo Colossus" — the technology-amplified human whose ecological footprint vastly exceeds biological needs — bridges the Pleistocene hunter and the modern consumer. Both are the same organism; one simply has access to concentrated ancient sunlight.

William Rees extended this analysis in a 2023 *World* paper, arguing that "humanity is exhibiting the characteristic dynamics of a one-off population boom-bust cycle." He identifies three innate predispositions driving overshoot — the capacity to reproduce exponentially, the tendency to expand geographically, and the impulse to consume all available resources — shared with all species but uniquely potent in humans due to cultural amplification. Rees and colleagues published a companion paper in *Science Progress* (2023) framing overshoot as rooted in "previously highly adaptive, but now self-defeating, impulses" to acquire, consume, hoard, and discount future consequences.

The St. Matthew Island reindeer remain the textbook parable. Twenty-nine animals introduced in 1944 grew to roughly 6,000 by 1963, then crashed to 42 by 1966 after exhausting their lichen base. The pattern is the same whether the organism is a reindeer, a zebra mussel, or a species that can write poetry about itself.

Edward Abbey captured the structural identity in a single sentence: **"Growth for the sake of growth is the ideology of the cancer cell."** The ecological economists — Herman Daly, Kenneth Boulding, Nicholas Georgescu-Roegen, and the *Limits to Growth* team — formalized what Abbey intuited. Donella Meadows and colleagues projected in 1972 that unchecked exponential growth on a finite planet would produce overshoot and collapse. A 2020 analysis by Gaya Herrington, published through Yale's *Journal of Industrial Ecology*, found current empirical data "broadly consistent with the 1972 projections" and that economic growth could peak and decline by around 2040\.

Crucially, this framing is not misanthropic. Meredith Root-Bernstein's 2019 *Ecology and Evolution* paper carefully placed *Homo sapiens* within the large omnivore guild, examining "its possible status as an invasive species" while also noting the potential to "leverage human learning capacities to decouple" destructive patterns. Arne Næss's deep ecology holds that "the human species is a part of the Earth and not separate from it." The point is recognition, not condemnation: humans are ecological actors subject to the same biophysical constraints as every other organism. Understanding this is the precondition for changing it.

## **The comfort trap runs deeper than modernity**

The key thinkers converge on a single insight: the behavioral disposition driving overshoot is evolutionary, not cultural.

Tim Flannery's *The Future Eaters* (1994) remains the most direct articulation. Flannery identified three waves of "future eating" across Australasia — Aboriginal arrival 60,000 years ago, Polynesian migration 800–3,500 years ago, European colonization 250 years ago — each following the same arc: "Human populations soar, then drop as food sources become extinct or soil is exhausted." His central finding was that **lack of coevolution** determines destructiveness. "Who destroys the future? Those who have not shared a past." In Africa, where prey evolved alongside hominins for millions of years, megafauna survived. Everywhere else, it did not.

Yuval Noah Harari's *Sapiens* (2011) extended the pattern to agriculture with his concept of the **"luxury trap"**: "One of history's few iron laws is that luxuries tend to become necessities and to spawn new obligations." Each marginal improvement in food security — a slightly larger wheat field, a slightly more reliable water source — expanded population to consume the surplus, locking humans into harder labor with no return path. Harari called the Agricultural Revolution "history's biggest fraud" and described *Homo sapiens* as "an ecological serial killer."

Elizabeth Kolbert's Pulitzer-winning *The Sixth Extinction* (2014) drew the explicit continuity: "The megafauna extinctions occurred in pulses around the globe that coincide with the sequence of human settlement... Though it might be nice to imagine there once was a time when man lived in harmony with nature, it's not clear that he ever really did." Kolbert proposed that the same quality enabling our survival — a kind of "mad ambition," an intense drive to explore and dominate — is inseparable from our destructive capacity.

Braje and Erlandson formalized the continuity thesis in a 2013 *Anthropocene* paper arguing for "an essentially continuous cascade" from Late Pleistocene to Holocene to Anthropocene, criticizing the view that "preindustrial societies had only local and transitory environmental impacts" as "mistaken." Boivin and colleagues in a 2016 *PNAS* paper on niche construction reached the same conclusion with archaeological evidence.

Niche construction theory provides the evolutionary mechanism. Odling-Smee, Laland, and Feldman's foundational 2003 work established that organisms do not merely adapt to environments — they modify them, creating "ecological inheritance" that alters selection pressures. Humans are the paradigmatic niche constructors. Erle Ellis crystallized the implication: "The key to understanding the Anthropocene is to recognize that humans have an extraordinary capacity to construct their ecological niche, at social and spatial scales that are steadily increasing: from the use of fire in small hunter-gatherer communities, to the global supply chains of the industrial world today." The drive to reduce risk, increase comfort, and buffer against uncertainty is not a modern cultural invention. It is what niche-constructing organisms do.

## **The counter-argument deserves its due**

Scholars in the Capitalocene tradition argue that framing overshoot as an inherent human trait dangerously naturalizes what is actually a historically specific, politically contingent system. Jason Moore (*Anthropocene or Capitalocene?*, 2016\) insists the ecological crisis is a product of capitalism's drive to appropriate "Cheap Nature" — food, labor, energy, raw materials — beginning not with steam power but with 16th-century colonialism. Andreas Malm (*Fossil Capital*, 2016\) argues the rise of fossil fuels was "not a collective decision of mankind nor the inevitable result of its nature, but the outcome of specific dynamics of capitalist production."

The quantitative case for modern uniqueness is genuinely powerful. The Great Acceleration — Will Steffen's term for the post-1950 explosion in every measurable index of human impact — has no precedent in Earth system history. Atmospheric CO₂ alteration, ocean acidification, synthetic chemicals, nitrogen cycle disruption, and radioactive fallout are entirely novel. *Homo sapiens* took **250,000 years to reach one billion** (in 1820\) and just 200 years to reach eight billion. Fossil energy use increased **1,300-fold** in that period. Three-quarters of human-contributed atmospheric CO₂ accumulated after World War II.

Gluckman and colleagues made a useful distinction in a 2019 *Trends in Ecology & Evolution* paper between niche *construction* (adaptive, equilibrium-maintaining) and niche *modification* (cumulative, progressive, irrespective of adaptive need). They argued modern humans are niche modifiers — "continually changing their environment irrespective of adaptive need or pressures" — and that the pace of change "may exceed our ability to develop innovations to deal with their potential ill effects."

Murray Bookchin's social ecology offers a further corrective: the problem is not "humans" categorically but specific hierarchical and growth-addicted structures. Blaming human nature risks fatalism; identifying systemic drivers preserves the possibility of change.

The honest synthesis holds both. **The behavioral disposition toward overshoot is evolutionarily deep, but its actualization at planetary scale required historically specific conditions** — fossil fuels, industrial technology, capitalist growth imperatives — that are not reducible to "human nature." The pattern is ancient; the magnitude is not.

## **Indigenous fire ecology complicates the destroyer narrative without erasing it**

The strongest challenge to a reductive "humans as destroyers" framing comes from indigenous fire ecology, which demonstrates that humans can be — and for millennia were — biodiversity-enhancing ecological actors.

M. Kat Anderson's *Tending the Wild* (2005) showed that California's landscapes were not pristine wilderness but actively managed gardens. "What Muir was really seeing when he admired the grand vistas of Yosemite and the gold and purple flowers carpeting the Central Valley were the fertile gardens of the Sierra Miwok and Valley Yokuts Indians, modified and made productive by centuries of harvesting, tilling, sowing, pruning, and burning." Indigenous management enhanced biodiversity — fire, pruning, and selective harvesting created more diverse ecosystems than unmanaged wilderness.

Bill Gammage's *The Biggest Estate on Earth* (2011) demonstrated that pre-1788 Australia was managed on a continental scale through precise fire regimes. Early European observers repeatedly described the landscape as looking "like a park" or "a gentleman's estate." Aboriginal people maintained carefully designed associations of grass, shelter plants, and food plants integrated across neighbors and sustained over centuries. Bliege Bird and colleagues confirmed in a 2008 *PNAS* study that contemporary Martu Aboriginal burning creates greater habitat diversity than lightning-fire landscapes.

Ron Goode of the North Fork Mono Tribe and Frank K. Lake of the USFS have documented how cultural burning differs fundamentally from agency prescribed burns: smaller in scale, holistic in purpose, preceded by extensive preparation and sustained by intergenerational ecological knowledge. California law as of January 2022 affirmed the right to cultural burns, reducing liability barriers.

The critical distinction is temporal. The initial contact phase — when humans first entered Australia, or the Americas, or New Zealand — produced ecological catastrophe. But over millennia, some societies developed sophisticated fire-based management that enhanced rather than degraded biodiversity. Aboriginal Australians arrived and contributed to megafaunal extinction around 50,000 years ago, then spent the next 49,000 years developing one of the most sophisticated landscape management systems on Earth. Reviewers of Gammage note his "frustrating habit of skipping over arguments against his thesis — in particular arguments that Aboriginal custodianship may not have been as perfectly sustainable as he paints — **no megafauna extinctions in this book\!**"

Both truths must be held. Humans are capable of devastating ecological impact upon entering new systems AND of developing deeply sustainable relationships with ecosystems over time. The modern crisis may represent not the emergence of something unprecedented but the **suppression of accumulated ecological wisdom** — through colonization, fire suppression, and the loss of traditional ecological knowledge — combined with the amplification of the ancient overshoot tendency through industrial technology. The Australian savanna burning program, which generates $50 million AUD annually in carbon credits by applying traditional Aboriginal fire knowledge, suggests what recovery of that wisdom might look like at scale.

## **Conclusion: the mirror and the choice**

The evidence assembled here supports a specific, well-grounded claim: the behavioral and ecological pattern driving modern environmental overshoot is not a product of the Industrial Revolution, capitalism, or any particular cultural formation. It is at minimum 50,000 years old, visible in the charcoal spikes of Lake Elsinore, the moa bone middens of New Zealand, the vanished giant lemurs of Madagascar. Humans exhibit the dynamics of any invasive species entering a system without co-evolved constraints — boom, simplification, overshoot. Niche construction theory, invasion ecology, and the global archaeological record converge on this point.

Yet the same record reveals something the simple invasive-species framing misses. Humans are the only organism known to have repeatedly overshot and then — sometimes, over long enough timescales — **developed cultural practices that brought them back into sustainable relationship with damaged ecosystems**. Aboriginal fire management, Polynesian marine tenure systems, and indigenous California land stewardship represent not the absence of the overshoot pattern but its slow, hard-won correction through accumulated ecological knowledge.

The deepest insight may be that modernity did not invent the comfort trap. It industrialized it while simultaneously destroying the cultural systems — the grammars of the living world — that had learned, over millennia, to contain it. The ancient mirror shows us what we are: ecological actors capable of both devastation and repair, depending on whether we recognize or deny the pattern. The question is not whether we are part of nature. We never stopped being part of it. The question is whether we can learn, again, fast enough.
