## PlayfulProcess's Breakthrough Insight (March 31, 2026)

> "He contradicts himself probably to be compassionate, useful and to fit the experiential data of the individual. Maybe this is why oral traditions were important and 1-1 containers like Umbanda. If the data for subjectivity can only be I, there can be no body of work that is non-oral that can fully claim there is a 'get there' thing. We need ultimately to surrender, but not to find something — precisely because there is nothing to be found."

**This is the thesis of the Axiom book stated in one paragraph.**

The contradiction in Wallis is not a flaw — it is the structural consequence of trying to write down what can only be transmitted in relationship. The oral tradition (Griô, Babaláwo, guru-shishya) is the container that holds the contradiction without resolving it. The book (any book, including Wallis's, including ours) necessarily collapses the contradiction into propositions — and propositions demand coherence that the living transmission does not.

This connects everything:
- The bedtime story works because of the container (Ch 1 of Species That Tells Stories)
- Grammars are responsibility structures because they are practiced in community (Grammars book)
- The axiom cannot be written down as a truth because it is enacted in relationship (Axiom book)
- The 1-1 container (Umbanda, therapy, guru-student, parent-child) is where the axiom lives — not in the text but in the field between two nervous systems

**"There is nothing to be found" is not nihilism. It is the recognition that what we seek is the seeking itself — the practice, the relationship, the container. The grammar IS the practice of the axiom. The axiom IS the grammar.**

## Key Highlights from Tantra Illuminated

### On narratives and truth (p. 48-49)

"All narratives are false in the sense that they are necessarily distortions of reality and true in the sense that they bear some relationship to reality."

"A religion, then, is a metanarrative. When you engage with a spiritual tradition such as Śaiva Tantra, you are inquiring into whether its narratives about reality are sufficiently compelling and effective to warrant overwriting some of your narratives with some of its."

"A more uplifting interpretation of reality is a necessary step on the way to complete transcendence of all narratives."

"An approximation in language (i.e., a narrative) is useful insofar as it ultimately leads you beyond language to a desired experience of reality, which is itself a wordless, immediate state of personal revelation."

**Analysis:** Wallis is explicitly pragmatist here — narratives are evaluated by effectiveness, not correspondence. And the goal is to go beyond ALL narratives. This is the axiom position: you hold the narrative (inherent value) because it works, and the holding is a step toward transcending the need for any narrative at all.

### The foundational instruction — and its circularity (p. 56)

"If you undertake the practice of yoga with the right View of yourself, that you already are a perfect and whole expression of the Divine and that you are doing yoga to realize and then fully express what is already true, then you have empowered your practice to take you all the way."

**Analysis:** THE circularity passage. You must begin by holding the View (axiom) in order to realize the View (truth). The instruction is: hold the axiom first, and the axiom will become recognition. But this means the recognition is PRODUCED by the axiom-holding, not discovered independently of it. Tillich would say: yes, exactly — and the courage to hold the axiom without the guarantee of recognition is the courage to be.

### On consciousness contemplating itself (p. 64-65)

"Self-aware being realizes that all other beings are reflections of herself, as she is also a reflection of them, and that all are reflections of the one Self."

"Capacity for contemplation upon itself, collapsing subject and object, and thus reintegrating itself with the Whole, which it actually never left."

**Analysis:** Vimarśa — self-reflection — collapses subject and object. This is what Wallis would say distinguishes recognition from belief: it is not a subject encountering an object (which Tillich rightly says can always be doubted) but consciousness turning toward itself (which has no external standpoint from which to doubt). The question: is the collapse of subject-object a discovery or an effect of the practice?

### On play and purposelessness (p. 65, 76)

"The act of creation is called a divine play or game (krīḍā), in the sense that this activity is fundamentally a free and joyous act of self-expression, done entirely for its own sake."

"This antiteleological stance is called the doctrine of divine play."

**Analysis:** No purpose. No goal. The universe exists for its own sake. This directly challenges the Linehan filter's "useful for what?" — because krīḍā says the question itself is misconceived. There is nothing to be useful FOR. Being is its own justification. This is either the deepest answer to the axiom question or the most sophisticated bypass of it.

### On evil and good as constructs (p. 85)

"'Good' and 'evil' are conceptual structures that people overlay onto reality; they are not inherent in reality itself."

"What separates those we wish to call 'evil' from those we wish to call 'good' is their relative degree of ignorance."

**Analysis:** The adaptive frame from the Grammars book — adaptive/maladaptive, not good/evil. Wallis arrives at the same place from within the tradition that the book arrives at from game theory.

### On liberation and spiritual bypassing (p. 80)

"Liberation is complete when we are no longer waiting to be liberated. But that is not a teaching that is useful for beginners, since it can undermine their beneficial dedication to practice and encourage spiritual bypassing."

**Analysis:** Wallis NAMES the bypassing risk of his own highest teaching. This is the honesty the letter asks for. He knows that "you are already free" can be used to avoid the work. The question: is naming the risk enough, or does the structural circularity persist even after naming?

### On the Indian logical system (p. 74)

"The standard argument in the Indian system of logic is not deduction, which seeks to establish irrefutable certainty, but inference. Unlike in Western philosophy, in the Indian system you never decide that you know for sure, and so you never completely close yourself to unguessed possibilities."

**Analysis:** Indian epistemology is structurally axiom-friendly. It doesn't seek certainty. It seeks effectiveness. This is closer to the Linehan wager than to Kant's categorical imperative.

### On discipline and freedom (p. 113)

"It is in submitting ourselves to a discipline that we become truly free and capable of real self-expression."

**Analysis:** The grammar as enabler, not constraint. Freedom within structure. The coral reef, not the monoculture.

### On suffering as feedback (p. 92)

"If a way of looking at a situation causes you to suffer and brings you into division and conflict, then that's all the evidence you need to mark that perspective as misaligned and begin to release it."

**Analysis:** Pragmatist test again — suffering as evidence of misalignment. Not "is this true?" but "does this cause suffering?" This is the Linehan filter stated in tantric terms.

### On kriyā vs karma (p. 113)

"Karmic actions (defined as volitional actions motivated by an expected result) bind the individual soul and restrict her freedom, whereas actions that partake of kriyā-śakti are, like all the śaktis, absolutely free and thus not part of the usual karmic system."

**Analysis:** If you practice to achieve recognition (expected result), the practice is karma and binds. If you practice as free expression of what you already are (kriyā), the practice liberates. But this requires already holding the View that you are already free — the circularity again.

### On the three supports of knowledge (p. 73)

"Experiential knowing of reality is based on these three supports: sound and careful reflection on your experience (sat-tarka), the guidance of a great teacher (sad-guru) who is skilled in meditative enquiry and has attained its fruit, the wisdom of the scriptures (sad-āgama)."

**Analysis:** Three supports — none of which is individual reasoning alone. This is the communal counter to Kant's isolated moral agent. Knowledge requires community (teacher), tradition (scripture), AND personal experience. All three. This is the grammar structure: you cannot know alone.

### On Svātantrya-śakti (p. 106)

"'Meta-power' that encompasses the other five: Svātantrya-śakti, the Power of Freedom. This power is 'meta' because it provides the context for the operation of the other five."

**Analysis:** Freedom is not one power among others. It is the context in which all powers operate. This maps onto Tillich's "power of being-itself" — not a being but the ground of being. Svātantrya and being-itself may be the same referent in different languages.

### On bliss as feedback (p. 83)

"Ānanda is a very important feedback mechanism for consciousness. If you believe you have given up your struggle and accepted what is and you do not experience ānanda (joy, relief, profound contentment, or similar) as a result, then that means you have actually surrendered to your dominant story about reality, rather than to reality itself."

**Analysis:** Bliss as empirical test — not the goal but the feedback. If the practice produces ānanda, it is working. If not, you have surrendered to a narrative, not to reality. This is testable. This is the Linehan filter in tantric language.

## The Emerging Synthesis

Wallis is more honest than his critics (and than his own most dogmatic statements) suggest. Across these two books, his actual position is:

1. **All narratives are distortions** — including the narrative that consciousness is the ground
2. **Narratives are evaluated by effectiveness** — pragmatist, not realist
3. **The highest teaching can be used to bypass** — he names this explicitly
4. **The right View must be held before recognition** — the axiom precedes the discovery
5. **Bliss is the feedback mechanism** — testable, not dogmatic
6. **Indian logic never claims certainty** — inference, not deduction
7. **Knowledge requires community** — teacher + scripture + experience (the grammar)
8. **Freedom (svātantrya) is the context, not a content** — maps onto Tillich's being-itself

When PlayfulProcess says "there is nothing to be found," this is not nihilism but the recognition that what Wallis calls recognition and what Tillich calls absolute faith are both descriptions of the same non-event: the dropping away of the search, which can only happen in relationship, in community, in the container that holds the contradiction without resolving it.

**The oral tradition — the 1-1 container — is why the axiom cannot be a book. The book can only point. The pointing arrives (or doesn't) in the field between two nervous systems. This is why the bedtime story is the oldest technology on earth.**
