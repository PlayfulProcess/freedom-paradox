## **I. THE ARCHITECTURE OF SEPARABILITY: Five Thinkers on the Ontological Core of Coloniality**

### **A. Denise Ferreira da Silva: The Triad That Built the Modern World**

The most precise articulation of separability as the foundational logic of modernity comes from Denise Ferreira da Silva, who identifies **separability, determinacy, and sequentiality** as the three ontological pillars sustaining modern thought. Her argument, developed across *Toward a Global Idea of Race* (2007), "On Difference Without Separability" (2016), and *Unpayable Debt* (2022), is not that separability is one feature of colonialism among others, but that it is the epistemic architecture without which racial and colonial violence becomes unintelligible.

**Separability** assumes that entities exist as discrete, bounded units that can be isolated, measured, and compared. **Determinacy** assumes that these entities are governed by knowable laws that can predict their behavior. **Sequentiality** assumes that events unfold in linear time, allowing narratives of progress, development, and civilization. Together, these three pillars produce what da Silva calls the "Ordered World" — the modern episteme in which the self-determining Subject (implicitly white, European, male) requires as its logical condition "a category of no-bodies who are separate from itself, determinable by inexorable laws, and developmentally or sequentially more primitive than itself."

Da Silva's radical move is to insist that these are not merely *epistemological* errors that can be corrected within the existing framework. They are the framework. Any attempt to "solve" racism or colonialism using the tools of determinacy and sequentiality — identifying causes, proposing solutions, measuring progress — necessarily reproduces the logic of separability it seeks to overcome. Her alternative draws on quantum physics' concept of **nonlocality**: a "World as Plenum" in which "everything in the universe co-exists as a singular expression of everything else in the universe." She names this praxis **Black feminist poethics** — not a theory but an imaging of "existence without separability, determinacy, and sequentiality."

**Key text:** "On Difference Without Separability" (2016), written for the 32nd São Paulo Biennial.

### **B. Vanessa Andreotti and the GTDF: The House That Separability Built**

Vanessa Andreotti's *Hospicing Modernity* (2021) translates da Silva's philosophical architecture into a pedagogical and somatic framework through the metaphor of **the house modernity built**. The house has a specific structure:

* **Foundation:** Separability — "the separation between humans and what we call 'nature.' That separation then generates other types of separation, creating hierarchies between humans, and between humans and other species." The GTDF collective defines this as "the trauma of separability (the perceived separation of humans from the land-metabolism, the cosmos, each other, and their own intrinsic value)."  
* **Two carrying walls:** (1) Universal Reason / the Enlightenment — "a totalising, universalising form of rationality that wants to reduce being to knowing"; (2) The Nation-State — "primarily created to protect capital."  
* **Roof:** Financial shareholder capitalism (distinct from industrial capitalism).

The house is cracking. Ecological, economic, and mental health crises are symptoms of structural failure. Andreotti's decisive contribution is the **hospice metaphor**: rather than trying to fix, reform, or replace the house, the task is to offer it a death with dignity while simultaneously "midwifing" forms of co-existence that are "still undefined, and are potentially (but not necessarily) wiser." The metaphor came from Indigenous collaborators who "perceive colonialism, the bedrock of modernity, as a debilitating disease." Fighting a disease, Andreotti emphasizes, requires a different approach than fighting a machine.

The GTDF identifies **three denials** that must be confronted: the denial of systemic violence, the denial of planetary limits, and the denial of entanglement. They also map **five modern-colonial desires** — entitlements, exceptionalism, exaltation, emancipation, and enmeshment — that keep people invested in the house even as it collapses. The pedagogical work involves "composting the shit" — confronting and metabolizing the difficult emotions, unresolved traumas, and unsustainable practices embedded in modern subjectivity.

Crucially, Andreotti distinguishes between **desires** and **yearnings**: desires are modernity's programming (for comfort, certainty, control); yearnings are deeper callings toward reconnection that modernity has numbed but not extinguished. "I think we are deeply entangled still with the Earth and with each other, and we have now deactivated the capacity to feel each other's pains."

**Key texts:** *Hospicing Modernity* (2021); "Calibrating Our Vital Compass" (GTDF collective, 2021).

### **C. Sylvia Wynter: Genres of the Human and the Sociogenic Principle**

Sylvia Wynter provides the deepest genealogy of how separability became installed as the dominant ontological regime through what she calls the **overrepresentation of Man**. Her argument, developed across four decades of scholarship, traces two sequential "genres of the human" that Western modernity produced:

* **Man1 (homo politicus):** Emerging from the Renaissance and Enlightenment, this genre redefined the human in terms of reason, science, and empiricism. Rooted in Descartes and Locke, Man1 was "implicitly white, male, bourgeois, and European." It "enabled and justified transatlantic slavery, colonialism, and racial hierarchy by grounding difference in nature and reason."  
* **Man2 (homo oeconomicus):** Developing through 19th-20th century biocentric and evolutionary thinking, Man2 frames the human through Darwinian biology and economic behavior — "the entrepreneurial, self-maximizing subject" of neoliberalism. This genre "preserves racial and class exclusions by linking human worth to economic productivity and scientific rationality."

Both Man1 and Man2 present themselves as *the* human, when they are in fact specific, culturally produced genres. This overrepresentation is the mechanism by which separability becomes invisible — it is experienced not as a cultural choice but as the nature of reality itself.

Wynter's alternative draws on Frantz Fanon's concept of **sociogeny**: human being is not merely biological (phylogeny) or individually developmental (ontogeny) but constituted through stories, myths, and symbolic systems. We are "hybridly human" — both *bios* and *mythoi*. Each culture produces its genre of the human through "origin myths and cosmogonically charted narratives" that neurochemically reward identification with one's kind. This is why the colonial project was not merely political or economic but **ontological** — it installed a new story of what it means to be human, with its own internal reward system.

Wynter's call is not for "inclusion" within Man2 but for a genuinely **new genre of the human** — what she frames as a "third event" following Fanon, in which "sociogeny" becomes the basis for reimagining being-human as praxis, as both biological and narrative, natural and cultural. This is creation, not recovery.

**Key texts:** "Unsettling the Coloniality of Being/Power/Truth/Freedom" (2003); *On Being Human as Praxis* (McKittrick, ed., 2015).

### **D. Bayo Akomolafe: Fugitivity, Tricksterism, and the Cracks**

Bayo Akomolafe's work represents the most radical departure from solutionism within the decolonial conversation. His signature provocation — **"the times are urgent, let us slow down"** — is not a call to passivity but a posthumanist recognition that "the way we think about the crisis of note is often that crisis." His concept of **postactivism** names the recognition that "the ways we care for ourselves and our causes and our worlds could actually be incarcerated" — care can become carceral, justice can reproduce the prison.

Drawing deeply on **Yoruba cosmology**, particularly the trickster-deity Èsù (the crossroads figure), Akomolafe reframes the question of separability through **ontofugitivity** — not fleeing *from* something to something better, but the ontological practice of refusing capture by any fixed position. "In pursuing justice, we're reinforcing the system we're trying to escape. In trying to climb out of the pits that we've dug for ourselves, the pits become resilient."

Akomolafe does not propose a cure for the disease of separability. He proposes **fugitive spaces** where shape-shifting becomes possible: "We need trickster approaches, we need ways of dancing away, or dancing to, fugitive spaces; dancing to sanctuaries where we can shape-shift." His concept of **brokenness as redemption** — inspired by the Hindu goddess Akhilandeshvari, "the Goddess who is never not broken" — directly challenges the idea that the response to separability is wholeness or reunion: "I'm wondering about how brokenness can redeem us from the incarceration of wholeness."

This has profound implications for the recovery/creation question: Akomolafe suggests that the binary itself is a trap. The fugitive doesn't recover a lost homeland or build a new one — the fugitive *runs*, and in running, discovers "subterranean worlds of new political imaginaries and possibilities." Rest itself becomes marronage — "the process of extricating oneself from slavery."

**Key texts:** *These Wilds Beyond Our Fences* (2017); "We Will Dance with Mountains" (ongoing course/event).

### **E. Quijano, Mignolo, and the Colonial Matrix of Power**

Aníbal Quijano's **coloniality of power** provides the structural foundation for the entire field. His argument: colonialism didn't end with political independence. It installed a durable pattern of power — the *colonial matrix of power* — that persists as **coloniality**: the ongoing structuring of knowledge, being, and economy according to racial hierarchies established during European colonial expansion. The classification of the world's population by race was not incidental to capitalism but constitutive of it.

Walter Mignolo extends this with **coloniality of being** and the concept of the **colonial wound** — the existential damage inflicted on colonized peoples and, differently, on colonizers themselves. His key contribution is the concept of **delinking**: the epistemological and ontological practice of detaching from the colonial matrix of power. Delinking is not anti-modern (which still positions itself relative to modernity) but *transmodern* — it operates from the borders, from the experiences and knowledges that modernity rendered invisible.

Mignolo's concept of **pluriversality** — "a world in which many worlds fit" (originally a Zapatista formulation) — directly addresses the recovery/creation question. Pluriversality is neither recovery of a pre-colonial past nor creation of a single new system. It is the coexistence of multiple ontological horizons: "pluriversality cannot be designed and universally managed; it just happens." This is a spontaneous bottom-up emergence from delinking, not a project of reconstruction.

**Key texts:** Quijano, "Coloniality of Power" (2000); Mignolo, "Delinking" (2007); Mignolo and Walsh, *On Decoloniality* (2018).

## **II. EPISTEMICIDE: What Was Burned, What Survived, and the Contamination Question**

### **A. The Concept of Epistemicide**

Boaventura de Sousa Santos coined the term **epistemicide** to name "the suppression, or rather the violent destruction, of non-Western local knowledge" under colonialism. His argument: global social justice is impossible without global **cognitive justice** — the recognition that Western modernity "profoundly devalued and marginalized the knowledge and wisdom that had been in existence in the global South." Santos identifies five "monocultures" that waste human experience: the monoculture of knowledge (scientism), of linear time (progress), of naturalization of differences (hierarchy), of the dominant scale (universalism), and of capitalist productivity.

Santos proposes four tools for working against epistemicide: a **sociology of absences** (making visible what has been rendered nonexistent), a **sociology of emergences** (identifying seeds of future possibilities in present realities), an **ecology of knowledges** (recognizing epistemic diversity without hierarchy), and **intercultural translation** (creating mutual intelligibility among different knowledge systems without reducing one to another).

### **B. What Was Destroyed**

The historical record of epistemicide is staggering in scope:

* **Mesoamerican codices:** Spanish missionaries systematically burned Aztec and Mayan manuscripts. Diego de Landa's auto-da-fé at Maní (1562) destroyed thousands of Mayan texts; fewer than four pre-Columbian Maya codices survive.  
* **Nalanda University:** The destruction of this great Buddhist center of learning (c. 1200 CE, by Bakhtiyar Khilji's forces) eliminated libraries containing centuries of accumulated knowledge across multiple traditions.  
* **African oral knowledge systems:** The Middle Passage disrupted but did not destroy African epistemologies. The enslaved carried embodied knowledge — ritual, musical, medicinal, cosmological — that proved remarkably resilient.  
* **European witch trials:** The persecution of an estimated 40,000-60,000 people (overwhelmingly women) between the 15th and 18th centuries represented an epistemicide of embodied, plant-based, and somatic knowledge traditions within Europe itself — what Silvia Federici documents as foundational to the emergence of capitalism.  
* **Australian Aboriginal knowledge:** Missions, stolen generations, and forced assimilation policies systematically attacked the transmission of Dreaming knowledge, songlines, and ceremonial practices across many communities.  
* **Colonial disruption of South Asian traditions:** The British colonial encounter reframed Tantric, yogic, and other knowledge systems through Orientalist lenses, privileging textual over embodied traditions and creating the conditions for the "neo-Hindu" reformulations that often internalized colonial shame.  
* **Cordoba's library:** At the height of Al-Andalus, Cordoba's library held approximately 500,000 volumes — at a time when major European libraries held 5,000-10,000. The Spanish burned it, along with libraries elsewhere in Islamic Spain.

### **C. What Survived and How**

The survival stories are as remarkable as the destruction:

* **Ifá across the Atlantic:** Yoruba cosmological knowledge survived the Middle Passage through embodied practice, reconstituting as Candomblé (Brazil), Santería/Lucumí (Cuba), Vodou (Haiti), and related traditions. The survival strategy was syncretism — encoding Orishas within Catholic saint imagery — but this was creative adaptation, not mere concealment.  
* **Andean cosmovision:** Despite centuries of Spanish colonial suppression, ayllu organization and ayni (reciprocity) persisted as living practices, not merely as cultural memory. Marisol de la Cadena's *Earth Beings* documents that earth-beings (tirakuna) remain active relational participants in Andean life.  
* **Aboriginal songlines:** Despite devastating colonial impact, songlines and Dreaming knowledge continue to be transmitted in many Aboriginal communities. The very structure of this knowledge — encoded in landscape, song, and ceremony rather than in separable texts — made it both more vulnerable to disruption and more resistant to total destruction.  
* **Kabbalistic traditions through diaspora:** Kabbalah survived through communities in exile — from the expulsion from Spain (1492) to the development of Lurianic Kabbalah in Safed, to Hasidic traditions in Eastern Europe. Diaspora itself became a generative condition for the tradition.  
* **Tantric lineage survival:** Despite Mughal-era disruption and British colonial mischaracterization, Tantric lineages survived through guru-disciple transmission, particularly in Kashmir, Bengal, and South India. The current revival of non-dual Śaiva Tantra scholarship (Wallis, Dyczkowski, Sanderson) draws on manuscripts and lineages that persisted underground.

### **D. The Contamination Question**

Can these surviving traditions be "recovered" in pure form? Several perspectives bear on this:

**Homi Bhabha's hybridity:** Colonial encounter produces a "Third Space" that is neither the colonizer's nor the colonized's original culture but something genuinely new. All post-colonial knowledge is necessarily hybrid. The quest for pre-colonial purity is itself a colonial fantasy — the myth of the "noble savage" or the "authentic primitive."

**Gloria Anzaldúa's nepantla:** The concept of nepantla (Nahuatl for "in-between") names a liminal space of transformation. Anzaldúa frames this not as contamination but as creative ground — the place where new consciousness is forged precisely through the discomfort of inhabiting multiple worlds simultaneously.

**Zoe Todd's critique:** The "breathless realisations" by Western scholars that non-human entities might be persons or that matter might be vibrant "is itself perpetuating the exploitation of Indigenous peoples." The "ontological turn" in Western philosophy risks a new form of epistemicide — extracting Indigenous insights, stripping them of their relational context, and repackaging them as "theory."

**The synthesis position:** Surviving traditions are neither pristine repositories of pre-colonial wisdom nor hopelessly contaminated by colonial encounter. They are living systems that have been changed by the encounter while retaining something that exceeds colonial categorization. The question is not whether they are "pure" but whether they offer genuine resources for thinking and living beyond separability.

## **III. RECOVERY VS. CREATION: The Desert Metaphor and Postmodernity as Liminal Passage**

### **A. The Biblical Desert**

The forty years in the wilderness (Numbers, Deuteronomy) is one of the most potent liminal metaphors in the Western tradition. The Israelites who left Egypt could not enter the Promised Land — the generation shaped by slavery had to die in the desert. Even Moses, the liberator, was forbidden entry. He could see the destination from Mount Nebo but could not cross over.

Theological interpretations vary significantly:

* **Punishment:** The wilderness generation's lack of faith (the golden calf, the rebellion of Korah, the refusal to enter Canaan after the spies' report) earned divine punishment.  
* **Purification:** The desert stripped away the slave mentality. You cannot build a free society with people who have internalized the master's worldview. The desert is a purgation.  
* **Transformation:** The desert was where the covenant was forged — Sinai is in the wilderness. The Torah was received not in the Promised Land but in the liminal space between worlds. The desert is not merely transitional but generative — it is where the new identity is constituted.

The third reading is the most relevant to your thesis: **the desert is where the new grammar is received**. The old grammar (Egypt, slavery, separability as the dominant ontology) must die, but the new grammar (Torah, covenant, relational being) cannot be received within the old structure. The in-between space — structureless, dangerous, disorienting — is the necessary condition for revelation.

### **B. Victor Turner's Liminality**

Turner's concept of **liminality** provides the anthropological framework for this metaphor. Drawing on Van Gennep's rites of passage (separation → transition → incorporation), Turner showed that the liminal phase — the threshold between structures — is characterized by ambiguity, potentiality, and the dissolution of normal social distinctions. In liminality, participants are "betwixt and between" — "neither here nor there," stripped of their normal identities and social positions.

Turner's decisive insight: liminality generates **communitas** — a mode of social relation characterized by egalitarianism, spontaneity, and heightened fellow-feeling, radically different from structured hierarchy. Communitas is inherently unstable and must eventually give way to a new structure — but it is the creative matrix from which new structures can emerge.

Turner himself suggested that "history itself seems to have its discernible liminal periods" between "relatively stabilized configurations of social relations and cultural values." He added: "Ours may well be one of them." This was written in the late 1970s. The resonance has only deepened.

### **C. Postmodernity as Desert**

Your thesis — that postmodernity is the desert, the necessary period of wandering between the death of one mythos and the birth of another — finds support across several traditions:

**Lyotard's "incredulity toward metanarratives"** names the collapse of the grand stories (Christianity, Marxism, Enlightenment Progress) that organized Western meaning-making. This is the epistemological equivalent of leaving Egypt — the old structure has been abandoned — but Lyotard offers no promised land. Postmodernity, in his framing, is *permanent* desert.

**Charles Taylor's "secular age"** describes the disenchantment of the modern world but preserves the possibility of re-enchantment — not as a return to pre-modern belief but as a new "fullness" adequate to the conditions of secular modernity. Taylor's is a cautious hope that the desert might end.

**Gianni Vattimo's "weak thought" (pensiero debole)** proposes that the weakening of metaphysical claims is itself a spiritual event — a kind of kenosis (divine self-emptying) at the civilizational level. The death of strong ontological claims creates space for a more humble, relational mode of being.

**Thomas Berry's "Great Work"** frames the current transition as a shift from the Cenozoic Era to the "Ecozoic" — a new relationship between humanity and Earth that requires leaving behind industrial civilization's assumptions. Berry explicitly uses the language of a civilizational passage.

**Joanna Macy's "Great Turning"** posits a transition from an industrial growth society to a life-sustaining civilization, involving three simultaneous dimensions: holding actions (slowing damage), structural alternatives (new institutions), and shift in consciousness.

**Richard Rohr's liminal space:** The Franciscan theologian explicitly applies Turner's liminality to the spiritual life and, by extension, to civilizational transition. "We are betwixt and between the familiar and the completely unknown. There alone is our old world left behind, while we are not yet sure of the new existence."

### **D. What the Decolonial Thinkers Say**

Each of the five thinkers anchoring this research takes a distinctive position on the recovery/creation question:

**Wynter:** Decisively on the side of **creation**. Her call for a new "genre of the human" is explicitly not a return to pre-colonial genres. The "third event" she invokes (after the Christian genre and Man1/Man2) is something that has not yet existed. Sociogeny — the capacity to constitute ourselves through stories — means we can author new genres. But this authoring requires first recognizing that Man2 is a genre, not the human.

**Andreotti:** The hospice metaphor implies something more complex than either recovery or creation. Hospicing means accompanying modernity's death with dignity, "composting" its undigested material, and being present to whatever emerges. She explicitly warns against "trying to build something from the undigested lessons of the old." The GTDF speaks of "midwifing" — but what is being born is "still undefined." Andreotti's position: we are not yet ready to build. We are in the composting phase. This is the desert.

**Akomolafe:** Rejects the binary altogether. His fugitivity is neither recovery nor creation but **escape** — from the very frame that poses the question. "Maybe just leaving the plantation is enough. Then we might find the magic in other worlds and other becomings." The trickster (Èsù) operates at crossroads, in-between spaces, refusing the choice between return and advance.

**Mignolo:** His concept of pluriversality suggests **neither** recovery of one pre-colonial ontology nor creation of a new universal. Instead, pluriversality is the *coexistence* of many living ontological traditions — "a world where many worlds fit." This is a practical cosmopolitanism from below, emerging spontaneously from delinking rather than from design.

**Escobar:** His *Designs for the Pluriverse* frames the task as **transition** — designing "differently to repair, heal, and care in a way that does not destroy the many worlds dominated and destroyed by Western civilisation." This is closer to the recovery side insofar as it privileges existing living traditions (particularly Latin American Indigenous and Afro-descended communities), but it is oriented toward creating new conditions for their flourishing, not restoring a lost past.

**Da Silva:** Her "World as Plenum" is a radical ontological reimagining that is neither recovery nor creation in conventional terms. It is an **abolition** of the Ordered World — "releasing existence from knowledge" — that draws on quantum physics, Leibniz, and Black feminist thought to image a mode of being that has never been dominant but has always been present as a suppressed possibility.

### **E. The Deepest Insight: The Desert IS the Composting**

The convergence across these thinkers suggests that your desert metaphor is not merely apt but structurally necessary. If the disease is separability, and separability is not merely an idea but an embodied, neurochemically reinforced, institutionally maintained way of being (Wynter's "sociogeny"), then it cannot be overcome by *thinking differently*. It requires a passage through dissolution — what Andreotti calls composting, what Turner calls liminality, what the Israelites called the wilderness.

The forty years is not punitive. It is the time required for a new grammar to be received in bodies that have been freed from the old one. Postmodernity's "incredulity toward metanarratives" is the stripping away of Egypt's stories. The meaning-crisis, the sense of rootlessness, the collapse of institutional authority — these are not failures. They are the desert doing its work.

But the desert is also dangerous. Not everyone who enters it arrives. The golden calf is always available — the regression to a simulacrum of the old certainty (nationalism, fundamentalism, techno-utopianism). Andreotti's warning about building "from the undigested lessons of the old" applies: if modernity's shit is not composted, the new structure will be built on toxic soil.

The question is not recovery *or* creation but **what kind of composting is adequate to what wants to emerge**. And the honest answer, from nearly all these thinkers, is: we don't yet know. We are in the desert. The grammar hasn't been fully received. The task is to develop the stamina to stay there.
