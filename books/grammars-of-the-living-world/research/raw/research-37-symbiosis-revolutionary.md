# Simbiosis

You're identifying something that fundamentally changes the argument's architecture, PlayfulProcess. The research as it stands has an implicit frame: decontextualization \= loss, purity \= power. But Haiti blows that apart.

The Bois Caïman ceremony in August 1791 — Boukman and Cécile Fatiman leading a Vodou ritual that launched the only successful slave revolution in history — was performed in a tradition that was *already* multiply decontextualized. Vodou itself was Fon cosmology mixed with Yoruba Orisha practice mixed with Kongo spiritual technology mixed with Catholic imagery, forged in the holds of slave ships and on plantations where no single tradition survived intact. None of it was "pure." All of it was alive. And it generated enough spiritual force to defeat Napoleon's army.

This isn't an exception to the decontextualization critique — it's the *other half* of the story the research is missing. Under conditions of extreme injustice, traditions don't need to be contextually intact to be powerful. They need to be *responsive to the actual situation*. The enslaved didn't have the luxury of practicing Ifá in its full Ile-Ife form. They took what they could carry in their bodies — rhythm, invocation, cosmological structure, the knowledge that the gods are real and can be called — and they made something new that was adequate to the emergency they were in.

This pattern repeats. Brazilian quilombos sustained resistance communities through Candomblé — another "contaminated" syncretic tradition. The US Civil Rights Movement drew simultaneously on Black church tradition, Gandhian nonviolence (itself a synthesis of Jainism, Tolstoy, and Hindu philosophy), and constitutional law — none of these in their "original" context. Liberation theology fused Catholic sacramental practice with Marxist structural analysis in ways that horrified both the Vatican and orthodox Marxists. Each case: extreme injustice as the crucible in which traditions *had* to cross-pollinate because no single tradition alone was adequate to the crisis.

This is actually what Fanon argued in *The Wretched of the Earth* — that colonized peoples don't recover their pre-colonial culture; they create genuinely new culture *through the act of liberation itself*. The struggle is generative, not merely restorative. Wynter builds directly on this: her "third event" is creation, not recovery.

What this means for the book's argument is significant. The chapter structure currently moves from crisis (Part I) through grammars (Part II) to the trap of solving (Part III) to the gesture (Part IV). But the Haiti case — and the broader pattern of revolutionary syncretism — suggests a missing register. The argument isn't just "decontextualization destroys meaning" (Chapter 1\) and "grammars survive through compactness and openness" (Chapter 5). It's also that *under conditions of sufficient urgency, decontextualization becomes creative rather than destructive*. The traditions call out their own gods not because safety protocols have been followed but because the house is on fire.

This connects to something Akomolafe is getting at with fugitivity — the escaped slave doesn't carefully preserve tradition; the escaped slave *runs*, and in running, something new and powerful emerges. And it connects to your own position with Recursive.eco: you're not building from a place of academic completeness but from urgency. The attention economy is doing real damage to children right now. The grammars need to be available now, even if the CARE Principles haven't been perfectly resolved, even if the perennialism question hasn't been fully answered.

The gap in the research is essentially this: **when does the mixing become medicine rather than extraction?** The provisional answer from the cases: when it arises from genuine need rather than intellectual curiosity, when it serves liberation rather than consumption, when the practitioners have skin in the game rather than observer distance, and when the traditions themselves are treated as living allies rather than inert resources. Boukman wasn't "appropriating" Fon and Kongo practices — he was calling on every spiritual force available because his people were enslaved and the situation demanded everything.

This should probably become a section within Chapter 6 (Tarot's Double Life) or Chapter 7 (The Trap of Solving) — the recognition that the openness/capture dialectic has a third term: *emergency creativity*. The same structural openness that enabled colonial capture and enables decolonial reinvention also enables revolutionary synthesis under conditions of extremity. The question for Recursive.eco isn't whether mixing traditions is legitimate — Haiti answered that — but whether the current crisis is urgent enough to justify it. Your book's answer, I think, is yes.
