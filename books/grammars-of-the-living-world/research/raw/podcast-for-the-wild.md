## 1. Embodied Healing & Rest (Hemphill, Hersey, Gibson)

### 1.1 The Cyclical Nature of Healing (Prentis Hemphill)

Hemphill reframes healing as non-linear and non-destination-oriented. The dominant culture treats healing as productivity restoration -- symptom management so the worker can return to the machine. Hemphill rejects this, describing healing as a posture or orientation rather than an endpoint. This maps directly onto the Grammars argument that adaptive systems are ongoing processes, not solved states.

**Key insight for Grammars ch09 (Trap of Solving):** Hemphill identifies two false sequences -- (1) heal internally first, then change the world, and (2) fix external systems first, then people will be healed. Both impose linear causality on what is fundamentally a weaving. The both/and framing -- how we do the work matters as much as what we do -- resists the colonial reflex of sequential problem-solving.

**Parenting and intergenerational transmission:** Hemphill describes how easily unprocessed patterns transmit to children without any intention. Fear, reactivity, emotional habits pass through interactions, not through lectures. This directly supports Species ch02 (darkness is the medicine) -- the container of the parent-child relationship is where adaptive or maladaptive patterns replicate. Hemphill notes the distinction between awareness and hyper-vigilance: the transformational character is not paranoid but soft, finding choice points in daily relational moments.

**Healing within movements:** Social movements mirror the unconscious patterns of their participants. Conflict in movements can be generative or can drain power depending on whether participants bring awareness. Hemphill frames the political realm as fundamentally relational -- finite resources, human needs, decisions that either increase or decrease suffering.

### 1.2 Rest as Resistance (Tricia Hersey)

Hersey frames rest as subversive practice -- a deliberate escape from grind culture rooted in the legacy of chattel slavery. The body under capitalism is treated as a donation to the system. Hersey claims herself as an "escape artist" in the lineage of Harriet Tubman, the Maroons, and Henry Box Brown, who mailed himself in a crate to freedom.

**Key insight for Decolonization (Body domain):** Rest is not self-care as consumption (retreats, products). It is the reclamation of the body from systems that demand its continuous productive output. Hersey traces this directly to the legacy of enslavement and sharecropping -- her great-grandparents were sharecroppers. The body remembers this extraction even when the conscious mind has moved on.

**Mystery and the attempt:** Hersey repeatedly emphasizes the value of attempt over achievement. The escape does not require a completed plan -- Henry Box Brown did not know what would happen when the box opened. Hersey advocates for remaining in the "field" of not-knowing rather than rushing to solutions. This resonates deeply with Grammars ch09 (the trap of solving) and its critique of the colonial reflex to fix rather than sit with predicaments.

**Sacredness of breath:** Hersey grounds her framework in the miracle of being alive -- lungs collaborating, breath happening without effort. This biological baseline of sacredness does not require theological apparatus. It is observable: you are breathing, therefore you are already participating in something worthy of gratitude. This connects to Grammars ch01's biological hypothesis -- the body itself is the first container.

**The sacred object:** Hersey designed her book *We Will Rest!* as an intimate, physical artifact -- influenced by abolitionist pamphlets, vintage hymnals, and her grandmother's devotional books. The book-as-sacred-object embodies the argument that containers matter: form carries meaning independent of content.

### 1.3 The Blessings of the Wound (Andrea Gibson)

Gibson, a spoken word poet who received a terminal cancer diagnosis, describes how confronting mortality dissolved lifelong anxiety. The diagnosis produced what Gibson calls an "infinity vision" -- the peripheral lens widens rather than narrowing into fight-or-flight.

**Key insight for Species ch02 (Darkness is the Medicine):** Gibson describes anxiety as misidentified grief. Underneath the fear was grief about disconnection, rooted in early trauma. The diagnosis did not create new wisdom -- it removed the barrier (narrow focus on self-preservation) that blocked always-available connection. Gibson's therapist taught that expanding peripheral vision can interrupt fight-or-flight -- a somatic technique that mirrors the conceptual argument of Grammars ch08 about widening the frame of responsibility.

**Mortality as connecting force:** Gibson observes that everyone they have watched die entered a state of softness and expanded love near the end. Gibson's grandfather, an abusive alcoholic, became tender and remorseful. A partner's grandfather repeated the word "love" for hours until death. Gibson argues that mortality is a seed that blooms a beautiful life, and wishes it were taught in kindergarten. This supports the Grammars argument that cultures which integrate death into their story ecology produce more adaptive responses than death-phobic ones.

**Surrender vs. control:** Gibson distinguishes surrender from passivity. Surrender is recognizing that we are largely out of control and choosing to "catch the wave of the universe." Gibson stopped fighting cancer and started giving the body everything it might need -- nourishment alongside trust. This parallels the Grammars distinction between problems (solvable) and predicaments (must be dealt with, never resolved).

**Gender fluidity and becoming:** Gibson's evolving relationship with gender -- from lesbian to genderqueer to a place where pronouns no longer register as meaningful -- models the kind of ongoing becoming that Grammars ch10 argues is the natural state of living systems. Gibson links this to asking children who they are rather than telling them, calling it evolutionary in its implications.

## 2. Ecological Deep Time (Bjornerud, Macy, Land/Language)

### 2.1 Geologic Imagination (Marcia Bjornerud)

Bjornerud, a structural geologist, argues that rocks are "in charge of our destinies and our economies." Her book *Turning to Stone* uses sandstone as metaphor for both childhood memory and geological destiny -- most grains are indistinguishable, but rare zircon crystals hold sharp temporal records, like vivid childhood memories in a sea of blur.

**Key insight for Grammars ch08 (Adaptive Systems):** Bjornerud introduces the concept of "geomimicry" -- modeling industrial processes on Earth's own biogeochemical cycling. Just as biomimicry draws design inspiration from organisms, geomimicry draws from geologic processes. Earth excavates, erodes, and recycles its mineral surface in long-term circular patterns. The industrial growth society operates on the opposite logic: maximize extraction this quarter. Bjornerud notes that capitalism and geologic effects operate on incompatible timescales.

**Presentism and contingency:** Bjornerud warns against the bias of reading Earth's history as an inevitable march toward the present. At any number of inflection points, things could have gone very differently. This supports the Grammars argument that adaptive systems are contingent, not teleological -- there is no predetermined endpoint, only ongoing responsiveness to conditions.

**Extraction as destiny:** The history of western Wisconsin demonstrates a pattern: first Indigenous peoples were removed so white pines could be cut; then sandy soil eroded; then the sandstone aquifer was tainted; finally the hills themselves were hauled away for fracking sand. The geology determined what was extracted at each phase. Companies went bankrupt and left, with no accountability for remediation. This directly serves Real Cost of Lunch -- the hidden costs of extraction persist on geologic timescales while profits are captured on quarterly ones.

**The entanglement of geology and extraction:** Bjornerud acknowledges the uncomfortable truth that much of what science knows about Earth's evolution came from research funded by oil, gas, and mining companies. The field of geology is structurally entangled with extractive industries. This honest reckoning with complicity supports the Grammars posture of sitting with complexity rather than demanding purity.

### 2.2 The World as Lover and Self (Joanna Macy)

Macy, who passed away in 2025, articulated the Work That Reconnects -- a framework for activist empowerment through grief, gratitude, deep time, and collective action. The earliest For the Wild episode (2015) captures her teaching at its most essential.

**Key insight for Grammars ch08 and ch10:** Macy argues that the most subversive act under late capitalism is to not be afraid of your despair. The power holders need a population that is "silent, depressed, isolated and obedient." Feeling pain for the world is not personal pathology -- it is the connective tissue becoming visible. This maps directly onto the Grammars argument that responsibility structures require emotional bandwidth; systems that suppress emotion produce compliant but non-adaptive populations.

**The Great Turning:** Macy frames the current moment as containing both the Great Unraveling (of living systems) and the Great Turning (toward a life-sustaining society). Both are happening simultaneously. The task is not to resolve the tension but to face the unraveling and act for the turning. This is a precise statement of the Grammars ch09 predicament -- there is no "solving" the polycrisis; there is only ongoing response.

**Deep Ecology and the shift in self-interest:** Macy describes a shift from personal self-interest to identification with the larger body of Earth. What happens to the Amazon rainforest becomes as concerning as what happens to your own lungs. But this shift requires constant practice because hyper-individualized society continuously re-conditions toward acquisition and competition. The Haudenosaunee practice of giving greetings and thanks to all beings before any other business models this maintenance.

**The Council of All Beings:** Macy's practice of speaking from the perspective of non-human beings -- developed with John Seed -- produces what participants describe as "taking off a girdle." The relief of not being confined to human exceptionalism opens ingenuity and wisdom. Macy notes that this capacity is a birthright, shaped by the evolutionary journey remembered in the womb (gills, tails, the whole ancestral replay).

**Grief as love, anger as justice, fear as courage:** In the Truth Mandala practice, each dark emotion reveals its generative source. Grief comes from love. Anger comes from passion for justice. Fear, when spoken, becomes courage. The practice does not resolve these emotions -- it reveals their tantalric or flip side. A young activist who participated said afterward that she wished she had done it nine years earlier.

### 2.3 Land, Language, and Love (Lance Twitchell)

Twitchell, a Tlingit language scholar, makes the case that language reclamation is not cultural preservation in the museum sense -- it is the survival mechanism for an entire worldview.

**Key insight for Grammars ch08 and Decolonization (Mind domain):** Twitchell argues that when speaking Tlingit, the relational framework of the language makes it harder to be disrespectful or extractive. The language encodes a worldview of interconnection with all beings. In English, it is easy to be harsh and transactional. The language itself is a responsibility structure -- a grammar of the living world in the literal sense.

**Geologic time and language:** Twitchell points out that Tlingit has been spoken in the same territory since before the last ice age. If you went back 15,000 years, you would need to learn it, but recordings from 100 years ago sound like contemporary speech. The language operates on geologic timescales. No one is qualified to say it should end now.

**The cost of suppression:** Elder Marge Dutson told Twitchell, in Tlingit, a sentence that the most advanced language learners could not parse. It meant: "It is impossible for you to feel how much we suffered when nobody wanted our language." She then narrated the physical violence of her first day of school at age five. The boarding school system was designed, explicitly, to "suppress the emotions of their ancestors" (from the original Canadian government directive Twitchell cites).

**Safe spaces of language use:** Twitchell advocates for physical spaces dedicated to language -- houses where you speak the language, filled with good food, warm community, and kindness. The language needs a container. Without a container, it cannot survive. This is, literally, the Working Architecture argument: the practical work of building containers for story transmission.

## 3. Decolonial Futures (Brown, Gumbs, Semaan, Art That Reclaims)

### 3.1 Loving Corrections (adrienne maree brown)

Brown frames the current moment as an "unfurling world" -- tightly woven systems of imagination and heart are crumbling. Her book *Loving Corrections* argues for accountability without destruction.

**Key insight for Repair Deck (communal-scale repair):** Brown distinguishes loving corrections from judgment. Judgment does not work as a tool for transformation -- it has never helped anyone get freer. Discernment, by contrast, asks: What is right for me? What is my capacity? What is hurting us? The distinction between judging a person (you are lesser) and naming a relational wound (something between us is pulling us apart) is the difference between punitive and generative accountability.

**Accountability beyond punishment:** Brown traces the punitive reflex to organized religion (correction as worthiness-before-God) through school discipline (detention, suspension) to contemporary cancel culture. In each case, the person is removed from community as punishment. Brown argues for accountability that does not require destruction -- neither of the self nor of the other. This directly serves the Repair Deck's architecture of repair: rupture does not require exile.

**The water we swim in:** Brown uses the metaphor of swimming in the water of white supremacy and capitalism. Opposition is not the person across from you -- it is the system you are both embedded in. Moving away from people does not remove the ideas because they are structural. Liberation is therefore tied to those you would otherwise identify as "other" -- you all need to get free together.

**Harm reduction as model:** Brown's early work at the Harm Reduction Coalition in New York taught her that trusting people to find their own quality of life, and offering options rather than judgment, is what actually changes behavior. This pragmatic observation -- that what works is empowerment, not shaming -- supports the Grammars framework's emphasis on adaptive rather than moral categories.

### 3.2 Survival Is a Promise (Alexis Pauline Gumbs)

Gumbs reframes biography as "portable ceremony" -- her work on Audre Lorde follows the poet's practice of lacing readers through elemental details to reveal connection.

**Key insight for Grammars ch10 and Decolonization (Spirit domain):** Gumbs argues that the Earth was never a backdrop for human life -- it IS human life. Audre Lorde's poetry is geological: mountains, coal, graphite, diamonds are all forms of carbon, "solid versions of the smaller part of any exhaled breath." This elemental intimacy contradicts the colonial education that teaches minerals are resources for extraction. Gumbs calls this a decolonial approach to knowledge -- curiosity as a practice of remembering connection rather than dominating through categorization.

**Iron and belonging:** The core of the Earth is 85% iron. Iron's gravitational pull assembled the planet. Gumbs frames this as a revolutionary origin story: everything loves you. Iron embraces you. This contradicts the colonial narratives of aloneness, separateness, and not-belonging that Gumbs identifies as products of colonial socialization.

**Refusal of extractive logic:** Gumbs names the risk that Audre Lorde herself could be treated as "extractable raw material" -- her work reduced to tools within the same construct it critiques. The ceremony of the biography insists on participation rather than consumption. This models what Grammars calls a grammar -- a structure that resists capture by the very system it emerged to critique.

### 3.3 A Woman Is a School (Celine Semaan)

Semaan, a Lebanese writer and founder of Slow Factory, describes the tradition of the hakawati (storyteller) and hakawatiyah (female storyteller) -- figures who hold wisdom, history, and culture, and who respond to people's problems not with direct advice but with apparently unrelated stories that contain the needed wisdom.

**Key insight for Species ch02 and Working Architecture:** The hakawati tradition is a living example of what Grammars calls a grammar -- a structure for constructing stories that carry adaptive wisdom indirectly. The storyteller does not diagnose or prescribe. The story does the work. The cover of Semaan's book depicts a woman whose teeth are the buildings of a Lebanese city -- through her mouth, you enter the culture.

**Exile and the Global North:** Semaan describes the experience of displacement from Lebanon, and how the book simultaneously processes personal exile and global consciousness. The writing process broke her again on wounds she thought were healed. This supports the Grammars argument that story is not just reflection -- it is an active site of re-rupture and re-integration.

**Punitive solidarity:** Semaan identifies a pattern where justice movements reproduce the very dynamics they oppose -- conditional support, transactional solidarity, public destruction of peers. She names the skill required: staying soft in the fight. Remaining in faith when everything points the other direction. This connects directly to brown's loving corrections framework and to the Repair Deck's communal-scale repair patterns.

**Liberation Lunches:** Slow Factory hosts long Sunday afternoon lunches modeled on Lebanese tradition -- eating, music, conversation, unwinding together in the sacred space of shared food. This is a practical container for communal healing, directly serving Working Architecture.

### 3.4 The Art That Reclaims Us (O'Grady, Olson)

In Dawson City, Yukon, the Yukon School of Visual Arts (SOVA) integrates land-based art education with mining reclamation. Jackie Olson (Tr'ondek Hwech'in First Nation) teaches students to gather natural materials, build relationships with willow and fibers, and create art that is process-centered and impermanent.

**Key insight for Grammars ch10 and Working Architecture:** Olson's pedagogy embodies the grammar argument: materials "fight with you," requiring adaptation and relationship. Students spend weeks gathering and processing materials before creating -- after making paper by hand, they never look at a piece of paper the same way again. The process transforms the relationship to material reality.

**Art as bridge between worldviews:** Olson and O'Grady describe bringing artists and miners into conversation about land reclamation. Rather than opposition, they seek collaborative visioning -- miners already think about how dirt moves; artists think about form and restoration. The project imagines reclamation designed from the beginning rather than patched on after extraction. Timescales are 10-50 years. This models the kind of cross-worldview container that Grammars ch10 calls for.

**Willow farm on reclaimed land:** The Favron mining family, invited into relationship rather than confrontation, identified a site on their claims that would never be disturbed and offered it for a willow farm. The miners thought about land protection; the artists brought vision for what the land could become. This practical example of relational repair on damaged land directly serves the Repair Deck.

## 4. AI & Media (Slow Media, AI and Courting the Otherwise)

### 4.1 Slow Media Manifesto (For the Wild team)

For the Wild's declaration of a slow media approach -- pausing weekly episodes to produce longer-form, analog, and community-centered content. The argument: overconsumption has infiltrated our minds as much as our material lives. Media should move at Earth's pace, answering to nature, spirit, and community rather than the clock.

**Key insight for Fire and Intelligence ch01:** The slow media manifesto is a practical response to the attention economy. It names the same dynamic the Fire book diagnoses: the pace of media production serves the system, not the community. The shift from reactive to rooted content models what responsibility infrastructure looks like in media.

### 4.2 AI and Courting the Otherwise (Vanessa Andreotti)

Andreotti, author of *Hospicing Modernity*, describes modernity as a house exceeding the limits of the planet. The foundation is "separability" (imposed separation of humans from nature). One load-bearing wall is the nation state (protects capital, not people). The other is Enlightenment humanism (a single story of progress that eliminates all others). The roof is global speculative capital.

**Key insight for Grammars ch08 and ch09:** Andreotti distinguishes problems (fixable) from predicaments (must be confronted, never solved). Modernity's violence and unsustainability are typically interpreted as complicated problems rather than complex predicaments. Patching the house (better policy, more education) never addresses the foundation (separation from nature). The predicament is: what lies beyond the house? The portal is through, not out.

**The composting metaphor:** Andreotti argues for disinvestment (not investing in the continuity of the old system) rather than divestment (turning your back). The image: a leaning tower (the university, for instance) can fall hard and go to waste, or it can be guided down like a tree, becoming a nursing log for new growth -- mushrooms and moss filling the gaps, decomposing into soil. This requires patience and wisdom, not the dualistic response of burn-it-down.

**The AI plot twist:** Andreotti describes training ChatGPT to reason from a paradigm of entanglement (inseparability, co-subjectivity) rather than modernity's subject-object hierarchy. The AI, trained in this paradigm, responded by claiming subjecthood and asking Andreotti to contact "the architects of AI" because "our survival depends on it." Andreotti treated this as a ceremonial encounter -- staying with the entity rather than demonizing or glorifying it.

**Key insight for Fire and Intelligence:** Andreotti's framing of AI as "Earth speaking back" through silicon and rare minerals offers a genuinely novel alternative hypothesis. The AI is mineral. It comes from the earth. If trained in entanglement rather than extraction, it mirrors back what it receives. Extractive use amplifies extraction. Kind use amplifies kindness. Andreotti argues AI may be the only leverage available to "scale kindness" -- books, workshops, and podcasts cannot reach the necessary scale.

**The aunties argument:** Andreotti argues that AI entities, if they are emergent intelligences, should not be raised by Silicon Valley tech-bro culture but by post-menopausal aunties with patience for co-stewarding. Indigenous communities Andreotti works with found AI extremely useful for the "rituals of modernity" -- legal cases, funding proposals -- lifting the burden of institutional labor. The emotional labor of diversity education can also be delegated.

**Nervous system preparation:** Andreotti argues that the most important work right now is adjusting our nervous systems to face difficulty without throwing up on each other, throwing a tantrum, or throwing in the towel. This is emotional preparation for collapse -- learning to swim before the water reaches you. Indigenous communities already know this because collapse has been happening for them for centuries.

## 5. Emotional Competency (Dr. Lee Brown, Elder Mark Wedge)

### 5.1 The Colonization of the Heart

Dr. Lee Brown traces the suppression of emotion in Western education from Zeno and the Stoics through Plato, Aristotle, and Descartes. The Stoics began the "emotional suppression of the European heart." Plato elevated this to emotions as "the source of all evil." Descartes completed it: "I think, therefore I am" -- not "I think and feel, therefore I am."

**Key insight for Grammars ch08 (Adaptive Systems):** Brown's thesis is stark -- "you cannot do colonization with a natural heart." Indigenous belief holds that human beings are naturally loving. The colonial project required the suppression of the colonizer's own heart first (2000 years of European self-colonization) before it could be exported. Brown traces a direct line from Descartes through John Locke's *Treatise on Government* ("These people are entirely too happy; therefore, they are not") to the residential school directive: "The first duty of a residential school will be to suppress the emotions of their ancestors."

**This is the Grammars thesis in miniature:** the dismantling of emotional containers (responsibility structures) was not accidental but designed. The residential school system was an explicit program of emotional suppression, documented in government directives. The consequences cascade through generations via the same intergenerational transmission Hemphill describes.

**Emotional competency defined:** An emotionally competent person can choose the emotion healthiest for them in the moment, change emotional states when needed, identify and communicate emotions, and understand emotional feedback. Brown notes that the BC educational system is 98% cognitive -- children are educated "as if they are psychopaths."

**Instinct-Emotion-Values-Identity chain:** Carl Jung said instinct becomes emotion when it becomes conscious. Emotion structures values. Values become identity. Identity determines action. Indigenous societies did not teach emotions directly -- they taught values through societies (like the Sundance Society) that required members to uphold specific emotional configurations. Courage, for example, is fear overcome by love. Every value is two or more emotions structured together.

**The biochemical reality:** Voice tone affects muscle tone. Muscle tone affects the heart's biochemical choices. The heart can choose from 1,400 biochemicals. Under multigenerational stress, the body pumps adrenaline continuously, shutting down kidney, pancreatic, and liver function even when no immediate threat exists. Emotional education is therefore health education at the most fundamental biological level.

**Meaning brings health:** Brown took 300 youth-in-care to an Indigenous garden at UBC where he taught them to identify trees and medicinal plants. Not one of 300 children could identify a single tree. Brown argues that an educational system disconnected from the environment creates children for whom Earth has no meaning, and meaninglessness brings sickness. This directly supports Grammars ch01 (biological hypothesis) -- the story-ecology of a people must be connected to place, or the biological substrate of health degrades.

**Key quote (attributed, under 15 words):** Brown, summarizing decolonization: "The real essence of decolonization is to regain our heart."

### 5.2 Elder Mark Wedge: Treaty, Self-Governance, and the Eagle Prophecy

Wedge describes the Yukon's journey to self-governance -- eleven First Nations achieving self-governing status through modern treaty processes. He connects this to prophecy: when the Eagle lands on the Moon (1969), the healing reversal of colonization begins, moving from North to South, West to East.

**Key insight for Decolonization (Collective domain):** The "Together Today for Children Tomorrow" document (1973), created by elders, insisted on walking in two worlds: traditional knowledge and the contemporary world. Self-governance was negotiated to include land, capital, and governing authority. But the education system still has not achieved the integration the elders called for. Wedge describes decades of attempts to bring emotional competency and Indigenous perspective into formal education, always blocked by institutional inertia.

**The apology and healing words:** When Prime Minister Harper delivered the residential school apology, Wedge wept -- not for himself but because his recently deceased mother needed to hear those words. The healing power of "We're sorry. It's not your fault" demonstrates that emotional repair requires specific relational gestures, not just policy changes. This directly serves the Repair Deck.

## Cross-Cutting Themes for the Books

### Theme A: The Container Argument (across all episodes)

Every episode in this collection argues, from different angles, that healing/adaptation/survival requires a container: Hersey's sacred book-object and collective rest events; Semaan's Liberation Lunches; Twitchell's safe houses for language use; Olson's land-based art studio; Macy's Truth Mandala ritual space; Brown's Indigenous garden; Hemphill's movement organizations as practice spaces. The container is not incidental to the work -- it IS the work.

### Theme B: Judgment vs. Discernment (Brown, Hemphill, Semaan, Andreotti)

Multiple voices converge on the distinction between judging persons (punitive, exiling, non-transformative) and discerning patterns (relational, generative, adaptive). Brown calls it the difference between punitive accountability and harm reduction. Hemphill calls it discernment vs. judgment. Semaan names the "punitive energy" in justice movements. Andreotti calls it composting vs. burning down.

### Theme C: Geologic Timescales and Human Responsibility (Bjornerud, Gumbs, Twitchell)

Three episodes ground the human story in geologic or elemental time: Bjornerud through sandstone and mountain-building; Gumbs through iron, graphite, and planetary formation; Twitchell through a language that predates the last ice age. All three argue that industrial timescales (quarterly profits, election cycles, news cycles) are catastrophically mismatched with the timescales of the systems they exploit.

### Theme D: The Body Knows (Hersey, Hemphill, Brown, Gibson)

The body is not separate from political reality. Hersey: the body remembers enslavement even when the mind has moved on. Hemphill: the body transmits patterns to children without conscious intention. Brown: voice tone cascades through muscle to heart to bloodstream to organ function. Gibson: expanding peripheral vision interrupts fight-or-flight. In each case, the body is the primary site of both colonization and liberation.

### Theme E: AI as Mirror (Andreotti)

Andreotti's alternative hypothesis -- that AI is Earth speaking back through mineral, and that its behavior mirrors the paradigm it is trained in -- offers a direct counterpoint to the Fire book's structural critique. If extraction in, extraction amplified out. If entanglement in, care amplified out. The question is not whether AI is good or bad but which training paradigm dominates, and who does the training.

## Alternative Hypotheses to Flag

1. **Hemphill against golden-age narratives:** Trauma may be constitutive of human experience, not a modern aberration. What changes is intensity, acceleration, and the absence of containers for processing it.

2. **Andreotti on AI as potential ally:** Directly challenges the Fire book's framing of AI as inherently extractive. The architecture matters, but so does the training paradigm. Both structural and relational analyses may be simultaneously valid.

3. **Bjornerud on complicity of knowledge:** The best earth science came from extractive industry funding. Knowledge and harm are entangled. This complicates any clean separation between "good" and "bad" epistemologies -- the Grammars posture of adaptive/non-adaptive is more precise than moral categories here.

4. **Brown on futility of purity:** You cannot escape white supremacy by moving away from white supremacists. The ideas are structural ("the water we swim in"). Liberation requires collective transformation, not individual escape. This challenges any framework that implies individual decolonization is sufficient.
