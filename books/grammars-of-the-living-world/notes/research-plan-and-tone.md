## **Deep Research Plan**

The following research sessions should be conducted to fill specific gaps in the book's argument. Ordered by priority — which chapters they serve and how urgently the material is needed.

### **Research 1: Fred Rogers as Media Philosopher**

**Serves:** Chapters 2 and 8 (the emotional center of the book) **Topics:**

* Rogers's theology (Pittsburgh Theological Seminary, ordained Presbyterian minister) and how it shaped his media philosophy  
* Specific design decisions for *Mister Rogers' Neighborhood*: pace, direct address, real-time activities, the "television neighbor" concept  
* His 1969 Senate testimony before John Pastore that saved $20 million in PBS funding  
* His explicit statements about television's potential and its abuse  
* Christakis's specific studies comparing educational vs. entertainment programming: the 0% finding for Rogers-style content vs. 10% per hour for hyperpaced content  
* The Daniel Tiger research: prosocial behavior and emotion regulation outcomes, especially with parental discussion  
* Hedda Sharapan (Rogers's longtime associate) on his production philosophy  
* How Rogers's approach maps onto the "third way" — neither technophobia nor techno-utopianism  
* Maxwell King's biography *The Good Neighbor* as primary source

### **Research 2: Co-Viewing, Joint Media Engagement, and Family Ritual**

**Serves:** Chapter 2 **Topics:**

* The full Christakis/Radesky corpus on co-viewing and joint media engagement  
* The 2024 JAMA Network Open study on tablets and toddlers (Webb, Christakis et al.)  
* The 2026 Singapore longitudinal study on parental joint media engagement and prosocial behavior  
* The 2023 PMC study on joint media engagement and executive functions (the sibling vs. parent finding)  
* The "absent presence" research — not just the Bengkulu study but the broader literature  
* Technoference: how parental phone use disrupts parent-child interaction  
* Read-aloud vs. screen story research: what cognitive and developmental functions bedtime reading serves that screen stories don't  
* Children's preference for repetition and what it develops (the "again\! again\!" phenomenon)  
* The specific question: can curated, shared playlists function as family ritual in the screen era?

### **Research 3: The History of Shared Stories and Political Cohesion**

**Serves:** Chapter 3 **Topics:**

* Benedict Anderson's *Imagined Communities*: nations as narrative constructions made possible by shared media (print-capitalism, the newspaper, the novel)  
* Dayan and Katz's *Media Events: The Live Broadcasting of History* — how broadcast events create temporary ritual unity  
* Robert Putnam's *Bowling Alone* and its media dimensions  
* The "monoculture" debate: was shared media actually good for democracy, or did it just mask power?  
* The decline of the media event: when no broadcast can command universal attention  
* Jürgen Habermas's public sphere and its relationship to shared media  
* Michael Warner's *Publics and Counterpublics* — how publics are constituted through shared texts  
* The specific data: what percentage of Americans watched major broadcast events in the 1960s vs. today? What is the largest shared media moment now?

### **Research 4: Bayo Akomolafe and Vanessa Andreotti**

**Serves:** Chapter 7 **Topics:**

* Andreotti's *Hospicing Modernity* — the core argument and its reception  
* Her specific framing of "meta-relational AI" and complicity in extractive systems  
* Akomolafe's key texts and talks — particularly the argument that psychology creates rather than describes reality  
* Their intellectual lineage: where they draw from (decolonial theory, posthumanism, indigenous cosmologies) and where they diverge from more rigorous philosophical traditions  
* Critical reception: who finds them compelling and who finds them underdeveloped  
* The specific question of scholarly rigor: what claims are evidenced, what claims are poetic/gestural, and whether that distinction matters  
* How their critique applies (or doesn't) to digital commons projects like Recursive.eco  
* Comparison with more rigorously grounded critiques: Ngũgĩ wa Thiong'o, Linda Tuhiwai Smith, Boaventura de Sousa Santos

### **Research 5: Children's Folklore, Bedtime Stories, and Family Ritual Across Cultures**

**Serves:** Chapters 1, 2, and 8 **Topics:**

* How different cultures use storytelling as family ritual: Brazilian *contação de histórias*, Japanese *yomikikase* (read-aloud culture), Indigenous story circles, West African evening storytelling traditions  
* The developmental science of bedtime reading: attachment, language acquisition, theory of mind, emotional regulation  
* Charlotte Mason's "living books" pedagogy and the Waldorf approach to storytelling and media  
* The "story diet" concept: can families intentionally curate their narrative intake the way they curate food?  
* Historical models of community-curated children's media: Sesame Workshop's global localization (150+ countries), the BBC's early children's programming philosophy  
* The specific research on repeated story engagement in early childhood — why children demand the same story and what cognitive work repetition does  
* How digital platforms have changed children's story consumption patterns (Common Sense Media data)

### **Research 6: The Attention Economy's Impact on Children**

**Serves:** Chapter 2 **Topics:**

* Jonathan Haidt's *The Anxious Generation* (2024): the core argument and the serious critiques (Odgers, Orben, Ferguson)  
* Jean Twenge's generational screen time data and its contested interpretation  
* The Surgeon General's 2023 advisory on social media and youth mental health

### **Research 7: Successful Models of Community-Curated Contemplative/Children's Media**

**Serves:** Chapter 8 **Topics:**

## **Notes on Tone and Genre**

This book is **philosophical memoir grounded in scholarship** — the genre PlayfulProcess's writing profile identified as her primary fit. The voice is personal, contemplative, and intellectually rigorous. It reads like a writer thinking in public, not an expert delivering conclusions. The scholarship is extensive but worn lightly — citations support arguments rather than substituting for them.

The Mr. Rogers through-line provides the book's emotional register: warm, serious, respectful of the reader's intelligence, unwilling to simplify. Rogers never talked down to children. This book should never talk down to its readers. It holds them the way Rogers held his audience — with full attention, genuine respect, and the conviction that the questions are worth sitting with.

The CC BY-SA license and the colophon acknowledging AI collaboration are essential features, not afterthoughts. The book that describes the recursive public should itself be recursively public.
