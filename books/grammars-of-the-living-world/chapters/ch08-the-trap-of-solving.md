# Chapter 8: The Trap of Solving

Every chapter so far has ended with questions. The reader may be frustrated. Where are the answers? This chapter is about why the frustration is the point.

---

Paul Tillich, in *The Courage to Be* (1952), describes what he calls "the God above God" — a term designed to irritate people on both sides of the theism-atheism divide. The God above God transcends both the believer's deity and the atheist's rejection of it. Tillich's critique of what he calls "theological theism" — God conceived as "a being beside others" — is that this God inevitably becomes an object of control: "This is the God Nietzsche said had to be killed because nobody can tolerate being made into a mere object of absolute knowledge and absolute control."

The atheist who rejects this God is correct. The believer who worships this God is worshipping a projection. Both are trapped within a binary — theism or atheism, belief or unbelief — that the ground of being transcends.

This is not agnosticism. It is the recognition that both poles of the binary arise within a field that encompasses them both. Tillich calls it "being-itself" — the ground, power, and abyss of being. You cannot stand outside it to affirm or deny it. You can only attend to it.

---

Christopher Wallis, translating Abhinavagupta's *Tantraloka*, arrives at the same insight from a different continent and a different millennium. The one fundamental quality of Consciousness, Abhinavagupta teaches, is *svātantrya-śakti* — the Power of Autonomy. And this power belongs exclusively to the whole, not to individual parts.

The individual is the whole in contracted form. The same five powers that characterize the whole — awareness, bliss, will, knowledge, and action — exist in the individual but in limited, contracted versions. The path of *pratyabhijñā* — "recognition" — is the individual discovering that its apparent freedom was always the freedom of the whole expressing through it.

Wallis's nonduality explicitly includes duality rather than negating it: "Instead of a nondual view that excludes duality and says duality is wrong, this is a nondual view that includes duality, as a valid level of experience, though not an absolute one."

This is not mysticism requiring special belief. It is a structural claim: the part cannot possess the freedom that belongs to the whole. The individual cannot *solve* its way to liberation, because the solving — the attempt to fix, to manage, to control outcomes — is itself an expression of contracted consciousness. The part trying to function as the whole.

The operational distinction is between *karma* and *kriya*. Karma is action performed by a contracted self attempting to control outcomes. It binds because it originates from the illusion that the part is the author. Kriya is action that flows from recognition of the whole — spontaneous, appropriate, free. It does not bind because it does not originate from the illusion.

Parker Palmer reaches the same place from within the Quaker tradition. His core thesis: "Before you tell your life what you intend to do with it, listen for what it intends to do with you." Palmer's concept of "functional atheism" — the belief that ultimate responsibility for everything rests with us, "a conviction held even by people who talk a good game about God" — is karma in Western clothes. The ego believes it is the sole agent and must control outcomes. Palmer's alternative — listening to what life wants to do through you — is kriya in Quaker language.

Three traditions. Three continents. Three centuries. The same structural insight: the impulse to solve — to identify a problem, design a solution, implement it, evaluate it, and move on — is not merely ineffective at the level of totality. It is structurally incoherent. The part cannot fix the whole because the part does not possess the freedom the whole possesses.

---

Now apply this to the crisis this book has been tracing.

Part I described a wound: stories that have lost their homes, a hearth that has degraded, a polis that may have lost the capacity for shared myth. The natural response — modernity's deepest reflex — is to identify this as a problem and solve it. Build a platform. Design an algorithm. Create content. Measure engagement. Iterate.

But this is the reflex that produced the crisis. The attention economy is the most sophisticated problem-solving apparatus in human history. It identified a problem (how do we capture and monetize human attention?), designed solutions (infinite scroll, autoplay, variable reward), implemented them at scale, and measured outcomes with extraordinary precision. The result is a civilization drowning in stories and starving for meaning. The solving *worked*. The solving is the problem.

Vanessa Andreotti, in *Hospicing Modernity*, names this pattern with precision. Modernity, she argues, is not a problem to be solved but a dying patient to be accompanied. Its "most important area of domination was the mental universe of the colonised" — and this includes the mental universe of those who critique it. The impulse to identify a crisis, propose an alternative, build it, scale it — this is modernity doing what it does, even (especially) in movements that claim to resist it. Andreotti calls it "low-intensity struggle activism" — activism that reproduces the very logic it claims to oppose.

Bayo Akomolafe's version is more radical: "What if the way we respond to the crisis is part of the crisis?" The categories we use to diagnose the problem — "mythic belonging," "ritual misplacement," "polarization" — are themselves products of the epistemological framework that produced the crisis. Naming the problem is not the first step toward solving it. It is a way of domesticating it within familiar frames.

A caveat is necessary. Andreotti and Akomolafe are provocative thinkers whose work has genuine force. But they do not operate with the scholarly rigor of a Christopher Wallis parsing Abhinavagupta's Sanskrit, or a Wande Abimbola transmitting four thousand ninety-six couplets of Ifá. Their claims are often more poetic than demonstrable, more gestural than evidenced. This does not make them wrong. But the reader should hold them as this book holds everything — as questions, not authorities.

What grounds the argument is not their poetry but the philosophical tradition behind it. Tillich's transcendence of the binary. Wallis's demonstration that freedom belongs to the whole. Palmer's practice of listening before acting. The trap of solving is not Andreotti's invention. It is the shared finding of contemplative traditions across civilizations — the recognition that the ego's attempt to manage reality is itself a contraction of the freedom it seeks.

---

The cosmic topology provides the image.

The Planck mission measured the universe's curvature parameter as consistent with perfect flatness. But a flat universe could be infinite — like an endless plane — or finite — like a three-dimensional torus, where space wraps around on itself. The flatness you measure in your neighborhood tells you nothing about whether the universe is finite or infinite.

Local measurements cannot determine global shape. You cannot infer the topology of the whole from the geometry of the part. A flat torus is finite yet has no boundary — combining properties that binary thinking would call contradictory.

Applied to this book's argument: measuring screen time (a local measurement) tells you nothing about the global topology of a child's relationship to meaning. Measuring engagement (a local metric) tells you nothing about the global shape of a community's shared symbolic world. The attention economy measures locally — clicks, time on page, conversion rates. Grammars reveal topologically — the field within which the clicks occur, the shape of the space in which the metrics are generated.

You cannot solve the crisis of mythic belonging from inside the epistemological framework that produced it. You also cannot simply step outside that framework — you are in it, the way you are in the universe whose topology you are trying to determine. What you can do is make gestures. You can create conditions. You can build infrastructure and see what the community does with it.

---

What a "gesture toward adaptive futures" means in practice.

Not a solution but a wager. Not a product but an experiment. Not certainty but — to use Tillich's word — courage. The courage to build something that might not work. That does not claim to have the answer. That holds questions without answering them.

The previous chapter described the grammars as alive. This chapter has argued that the impulse to manage, systematize, and deploy them — the impulse to *solve* with them — risks killing what makes them alive. The gesture is to create the conditions under which living encounter can happen, and then to step back. To love the clay in front of the children, as Margaret McFarland said, rather than to teach sculpting.

---

Is it possible to build a technological platform without reproducing the technological logic that created the crisis? Can a digital commons genuinely serve contemplative practice, or does the medium inevitably shape the practice into something the attention economy can digest?

If the impulse to solve is part of the problem, what is the alternative to solving — and is naming that alternative just another form of solving?

These are the questions the final chapter lives inside. It does not escape them. It practices them.
