# Chapter 1: When Stories Lose Their Homes

In the first edition of the Brothers Grimm's *Kinder- und Hausmärchen*, published on December 20, 1812, a woodcutter's wife proposes abandoning her children in the forest. Not a stepmother. The biological mother. She speaks plainly: "Early tomorrow morning we will take the two children out into the thickest part of the woods, make a fire for them, and give each of them a little piece of bread, then leave them by themselves and go off to our work. They won't find their way back home, and we'll be rid of them."

The father protests weakly. He submits. The children are led into the forest.

This is Hansel and Gretel as it was first told — or, more precisely, as it was first written down, by Dortchen Wild, the Grimms' neighbor's daughter, who would later marry Wilhelm. In the 1810 manuscript, the children didn't even have names. They were simply "Little Brother" and "Little Sister." The story had no gingerbread house yet, no duck to carry them across water, no religious consolation. It had a mother who decided that her children's deaths were preferable to the family's starvation.

The tale was not a fantasy. The Great Famine of 1315–1321 had devastated Europe five centuries earlier, and the folklore it produced endured because the conditions it described kept recurring. Maria Tatar, in *The Hard Facts of the Grimms' Fairy Tales*, confirms that "child abandonment was a cruel social reality among the impoverished, even as late as 1806, when the Grimm brothers began collecting their tales." John Boswell's *The Kindness of Strangers* documents that abandoning children was not a fairy-tale trope but a routine survival strategy across centuries of European history. The woodcutter's wife was not a monster. She was making a calculation that countless real mothers had made.

The gingerbread house — when it appeared — was the cruelest detail of all. In a world of famine, a house made of food was a weapon. It weaponized the child's deepest desire against them. The very thing they craved was the trap. The witch understood what hungry children would do when they encountered plenty: they would eat, and in eating, they would be consumed.

Now consider telling this story to a child in 2026 who has never known hunger.

---

The gingerbread house is no longer a fantasy. It is a description of the average child's Tuesday. The American child lives surrounded by abundance, targeted by algorithmic lures designed to exploit desire, inhabiting a world where plenty itself is the pathology. The tale is more relevant than ever — but its meaning has inverted. The child hearing it cannot access the original terror because the material conditions that gave the tale its power have disappeared from their experience. The starvation trap reads as a set piece. The witch reads as a Halloween costume. The abandonment reads as something that happens in other centuries, to other people.

This is what I want to call mythic displacement: not that the story is false, but that the context which made it meaningful has dissolved. The story still exists. It is told in picture books, animated films, gingerbread-house decorating kits at Christmas. But it is homeless — severed from the lived conditions that produced it, circulating in a world that can no longer hear what it was trying to say.

And we did this to it deliberately.

---

D.L. Ashliman at the University of Pittsburgh has tracked the precise changes the Brothers Grimm made across seven editions, from 1812 to 1857. The mutations form a genealogy of softening.

In the first edition, the mother is the biological mother. She and the father share blame equally. In the second edition, published in 1819, the father becomes "more introspective and milder" — the line "It would be better to share the last bit with the children" was added. The tale grows, in Ashliman's words, "more dramatic, more literary, and more sentimental." By the fourth edition, in 1840, the Grimms introduce the word "stepmother" for the first time, though they retain "mother" in some passages. In the fifth edition, they add the famine as explicit context — "a way to rationalize the parents' decision," as Jack Zipes notes — as though the tale needed explaining rather than witnessing. And in the seventh and final edition of 1857, the wife is referred to once as "the stepmother," twice as "the mother," and about a dozen times as "the woman." Even after forty-five years of revision, the Grimms could not fully erase the biological mother. She persists in the text like a stain that will not wash out.

The pattern is consistent across every revision: the tale was progressively softened, sentimentalized, moralized, and de-historicized. The biological mother became a stepmother. The reluctant father became a victim. Religious consolation was inserted where none had existed. The material reality — starvation, the brutal arithmetic of too many mouths and not enough bread — was rationalized rather than confronted.

Zipes's explanation is the most direct: the Grimms "held motherhood sacred." Marina Warner's is the most devastating: Wilhelm's edits moved "from blaming both parents for agreeing to abandon the children in the first edition to explaining away the father's complicity in the final edition where the woman, no longer the natural mother but a stepmother, forces the father to comply." The father was innocent. The mother was replaced. The story became bearable — and in becoming bearable, it lost the capacity to bear witness.

---

The scholarly fault line over fairy tales runs deeper than editorial choices. It is a dispute about what stories *are*.

Bruno Bettelheim, in *The Uses of Enchantment* (1976), reads the gingerbread house as oral regression — the child's fantasy of a world made entirely of food, representing the desire to return to the breast. The witch is the "bad mother" projected outward. The tale's function is psychological and universal: it helps children work through separation anxiety, oedipal conflict, and the fear of abandonment. For Bettelheim, the tale's meaning transcends its historical context. It is a dream that all children dream.

Robert Darnton, in *The Great Cat Massacre* (1984), insists that fairy tales are primary sources. He disputes psychological interpretations "on the grounds that they fail to pay close enough attention to the narrative content and known historical context of the stories." The world of the fairy tale is the world of the peasant storyteller: "the omnipresence of hunger, the hazards of life on the road, the burden of children in poor households." The gingerbread house is not a symbol. It is a trap built from the specific fantasies of people who were starving.

Warner's critique is the most useful for the argument this book is making. She argues that psychological interpretations and Disney adaptations "leech the history out of fairy tales" and give them "a falsely archetypal and universal character." Warner reconnects the tales to their historical conditions — showing that the "absent mother" of fairy tales arises from demographic reality: women died in childbirth, widowers remarried, and the conflicts between stepmothers and stepchildren were material, not merely psychological.

The dispute matters because it is not really about fairy tales. It is about whether meaning can survive decontextualization. Bettelheim says yes — the psychological core persists regardless of material conditions. The historicists say no — strip the material conditions and you strip the meaning. This book sides with the historicists, but with a complication: the psychological dimension is real. Children *do* work through fear of abandonment by hearing Hansel and Gretel. But they work through it differently when abandonment is a lived possibility than when it is an abstraction. Context is not merely decorative. Context is constitutive of meaning.

---

In 1920, a son was born to a man who had recently returned from the Battle of the Somme.

A.A. Milne had been commissioned as an officer in the 4th Battalion, Royal Warwickshire Regiment, and fought in one of the bloodiest engagements in human history — over three million combatants, one million casualties. He came home with what was then called shell-shock. In his autobiography, *It's Too Late Now*, he wrote that it made him "almost physically sick" to think of "that nightmare of mental and moral degradation, the war." At home, he was triggered by buzzing bees, popping balloons, champagne corks. He moved his family from London to the countryside to escape the noise.

His son, Christopher Robin, was born into this silence. And the stories Milne wrote for him — *Winnie-the-Pooh*, published in 1926 — are widely understood as a father's attempt to process trauma through the creation of a counter-world. The Hundred Acre Wood is a forest where the worst thing that can happen is running out of honey. Each character carries a dimension of what the father was living through: Piglet's anxiety, Eeyore's depression, Tigger's impulsive energy, Rabbit's need for control, Owl's confabulation, and Pooh himself — gentle, slightly bewildered, dissociated from danger. The pastoral world was not an escape from reality. It was a therapeutic landscape built by a man who could not yet speak directly about what had happened to him.

The stories worked — as therapy, as literature, as a father's gift to his son. And then they became famous.

Christopher Robin resented it for the rest of his life. In his autobiography, *The Enchanted Places* (1974), he described the poem "Vespers" — published in *Vanity Fair* when he was a small child — as having brought him "more toe-curling, fist-clenching, lip-biting embarrassment than any other." As the books' popularity exploded, he was bullied at school, paraded before media, treated as a marketing artifact rather than a child. After his father's death in 1956, Christopher saw his mother only once in the remaining fifteen years of her life. The intimate play between father and son — the material that produced one of the world's most beloved children's stories — was extracted from its relational context and turned into a global commodity. The story meant to heal a father's trauma became the source of a son's.

---

The original Pooh, Piglet, Eeyore, Kanga, and Tigger — Christopher Robin's actual stuffed animals — now sit in a glass case at the New York Public Library's main branch on Fifth Avenue. They were donated by the publisher E.P. Dutton in 1987. Millions of tourists visit them. Most know the Disney version. Almost none know about the Somme.

Walter Benjamin, writing in 1935, gave us the vocabulary for what happened. In "The Work of Art in the Age of Mechanical Reproduction," he distinguished between an artwork's *cult value* — its meaning within the ritual context that produced it — and its *exhibition value* — its meaning as an object of display. Mechanical reproduction, Benjamin argued, destroys the artwork's *aura*: "that which withers in the age of mechanical reproduction is the aura of the work of art." The aura is the object's embeddedness in a specific time, place, and tradition — its here and now. Reproduction strips this away.

Christopher Robin's stuffed animals had cult value. They were mediators between a traumatized father and his bewildered son — objects of play through which a man who could not speak about war found a way to be present for his child. Behind glass at the New York Public Library, they have exhibition value. The tourists photograph them. The gift shop sells replicas. The cult value — the meaning that required those specific hands, that specific relationship, that specific wound — is gone.

The pattern extends wherever you look. Egyptian tomb objects, designed to serve the dead in the afterlife, sit in museum vitrines as decorative curiosities. Dan Hicks, in *The Brutish Museums*, argues that the Benin Bronzes — looted by British forces in 1897 — constitute "a systematic extension of warfare across time." Their continued display in Western museums is not neutral curation. It is ongoing violence. Amy Lonetree, in *Decolonizing Museums*, insists that indigenous objects behind glass are "living entities" — their decontextualization is not a completed historical act but an ongoing source of grief for the communities from which they were taken.

The display case is not a neutral frame. It is a technology of displacement.

---

Joseph Campbell, whose universalism this book will elsewhere resist, made one claim that is difficult to argue with: "Myth cannot be exported into another land or another time." In *The Masks of God*, he argues that mythology must be "transparent to transcendence" within the specific cultural context where it operates. When myths are displaced from their originating conditions, they become literature, entertainment, or ideology — but they cease to function as living mythology.

Mircea Eliade's *The Sacred and the Profane* names the larger process: modernity is characterized by progressive desacralization — the evacuation of the sacred from everyday experience. Sacred time, which was cyclical and mythic, gives way to profane time, which is linear and historical. Sacred space, which was specific and charged, gives way to profane space, which is homogeneous and interchangeable. The Grimms' tales originally participated in a world where forests were genuinely dangerous, witches were genuinely feared, and the boundary between the natural and supernatural was genuinely porous. The tale's sacred function — to orient the child within a cosmos of meaningful dangers — depended on the shared belief that such a cosmos existed. Desacralization does not falsify the tale. It evacuates the cosmological context that made it function.

Alan Dundes, the UC Berkeley folklorist, coined the term "autobiographical ethnography" to describe what folklore does: it is a culture's way of describing itself to itself. The stories a culture tells are constitutive of its identity — the culture knows itself through its stories. When the stories lose their connection to lived experience, the culture loses a mode of self-knowledge.

And Ngũgĩ wa Thiong'o, in *Decolonising the Mind*, extends the argument to its most radical form: colonial languages do not merely replace indigenous languages — they replace the entire mythic infrastructure that indigenous languages carried. To think in English is to inherit English myths, English categories, English ways of organizing experience. The indigenous myths carried by Gikuyu or Yoruba are not merely translated. They are displaced. The language that could hold them has been replaced by a language that cannot.

---

I want to be precise about what mythic displacement is and what it is not.

It is not the same as cultural appropriation, though the two can overlap. Cultural appropriation involves taking from another culture. Mythic displacement includes a culture's broken relationship to its *own* inherited stories.

A modern German parent telling Hansel and Gretel to a well-fed child is not appropriating. The tale belongs to that tradition. But it may still be displaced — the material conditions that gave the tale its meaning no longer exist in the teller's or the listener's world. The American parent reading Paul Bunyan, the Japanese parent reading Momotarō, the Brazilian parent telling the Saci-Pererê — all are offering tales whose constitutive contexts have dissolved, to varying degrees, in the world the child actually inhabits.

This distinction matters because it prevents the argument from collapsing into a simpler critique. The crisis is not that we are stealing each other's stories. The crisis is that even within cultures that own their stories, the stories have become homeless. Warner's historicist approach shows this clearly: displacement happens *within* traditions across time, not just between traditions across space. The Grimms themselves displaced their tales — softening them, moralizing them, sentimentalizing them across seven editions. Disney displaced them further. The attention economy displaces them again — algorithmically distributing fragments of stories without any of the relational context that made them meaningful.

Zipes names the economic mechanism: fairy tales "violated" by consumer capitalism. The stories are not borrowed or stolen. They are commodified — turned from communal practice into individual product.

---

What happens to the psychological architecture of a story when the conditions that produced it no longer exist?

Can a myth survive its own success — can universal distribution destroy what made a story locally powerful?

If context is constitutive of meaning, what does it mean that we now consume stories with no context at all — algorithmically surfaced, stripped of origin, optimized for engagement?

And the deepest question, the one that will occupy the rest of this book: are we mythically homeless — not because we lack stories, but because the stories we have no longer belong to the world we inhabit?
