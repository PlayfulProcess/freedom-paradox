# Chapter 5: The Colonial Reflex

---

The colonial reflex is not a historical event. It is a five-move pattern that operates wherever extractive culture encounters cooperative alternatives. The pattern is: particular practice → universal claim → enforcement through power → displacement of local systems → pathologizing the displaced.

Each move is rational within the extractive logic. And each move destroys a cooperative strategy the species may need.

---

## The five moves

**Move 1: A particular practice.** Kant's categorical imperative is a genuine logical constraint — don't universalize self-contradictory maxims. "Don't lie, don't coerce, don't treat people as instruments" is cross-cultural, not Prussian. The formal principle holds.

**Move 2: Universalized.** But the formal principle is packaged with a model of moral agency — individual, disembodied, deliberative — that is specific to post-Reformation Northern Europe. The principle is universal. The model of who counts as a moral reasoner is not. Sylvia Wynter names this the "overrepresentation of Man" — one genre of the human presenting itself as the human.

**Move 3: Enforced through power.** The universal claim, backed by colonial power, displaces every communal moral practice it encounters. Ifá's communal divination is defined as superstition. The Quaker gathered meeting is tolerated as quaint. Aboriginal ceremony is banned. Not by refuting these practices but by defining them as pre-rational — lower on a developmental scale that culminates in the autonomous Kantian subject.

**Move 4: Local systems displaced.** When the Australian government forbade Aboriginal ceremony, it did not just commit a moral wrong. It destroyed a sixty-thousand-year-old ecological management system encoded in Dreamtime stories and songlines — a cooperative strategy for maintaining landscape resilience that took longer to develop than any technology in human history. When the Middle Passage tore Africans from their communities, Ifá's survival through embodied memory was the preservation of a cooperative strategy — communal divination, distributed moral authority, relational decision-making — that the extractive system tried to eliminate.

**Move 5: The displaced are pathologized.** The communities that lose their cooperative practices are then diagnosed — as undeveloped, as dysfunctional, as mentally ill. The loss is attributed to their deficiency, not to the extraction that caused it. Maslow arrived at the Siksika reserve, observed a community where eighty to ninety percent flourished, took the observation, stripped the communal infrastructure, and produced a pyramid with one person climbing alone. The extraction was invisible because the extractive framework defined communal practice as the absence of individual achievement.

---

## Epistemicide as strategic catastrophe

Boaventura de Sousa Santos named it epistemicide — the systematic destruction of non-Western ways of knowing. But the game-theoretic reframe makes the stakes visible to people who do not respond to moral claims about colonialism but can understand strategic self-destruction.

Each epistemicide is the elimination of a cooperative strategy from the species' adaptive repertoire. A language dies approximately every forty days. A 2021 *Nature* study found that medicinal knowledge encoded in indigenous languages is overwhelmingly unique to those languages. When the language dies, the pharmacopoeia dies with it. When the grammar dies, the cooperative strategy it carried — the specific technology for matching power with responsibility in a specific ecological context — dies with it.

This is a species burning its own seed bank.

The biodiversity argument applies directly. A forest with two hundred tree species can absorb shocks that a monoculture plantation cannot — not because any single species is "better" but because diversity distributes risk. When conditions change, something in the diverse system is likely to be pre-adapted. The monoculture has one response. If that response does not fit the new conditions, the whole system fails.

Indigenous peoples constitute less than five percent of the global population but protect approximately eighty percent of remaining biodiversity. This is not coincidence. It is the direct result of cooperative strategies — grammars — maintained across millennia for matching human activity with ecological carrying capacity.

## The decontextualization machine

The five moves operate not just through conquest but through a subtler mechanism: technique extraction. Mindfulness stripped from Buddhist community and sold as corporate stress management. Yoga stripped from its relational-spiritual context and sold as exercise. Tarot stripped from its communal reading practices and delivered as an algorithm. Each extraction preserves the technique and destroys the cooperative infrastructure. What remains is individually useful and communally empty — the perfect product for an extractive civilization.

Jon Kabat-Zinn acknowledged the tension when he described his decision to strip Buddhist terminology from MBSR: "I bent over backwards to structure it and find ways to speak about it that avoided as much as possible the risk of its being seen as Buddhist, New Age, Eastern mysticism or just plain flaky." The clinical results are real — MBSR reduces stress, improves wellbeing, has been validated in hundreds of studies. But the extraction preserved the technique and discarded the *sangha* (community), the *sila* (ethics), and the *dharma* (cosmological context) that made the practice cooperative rather than merely individual.

The pattern the previous chapter identified — technique survives, telos is stripped — is the colonial reflex operating at the level of practice. It is not malicious. It is the logical consequence of an extractive framework applied to cooperative technologies. If you can only see individual outcomes, you will only extract what produces individual outcomes. The communal infrastructure becomes invisible because the framework has no category for it.

## The honest complication

And here is where the book must be honest about its own argument.

Extraction and cooperation are not opposites. They are scale-dependent. The British Empire required extraordinary internal cooperation — military coordination, bureaucratic administration, trade networks spanning the globe. It was one of the most cooperative human enterprises in history. It was also catastrophically extractive toward everyone outside the in-group. The Haudenosaunee Confederacy cooperated beautifully among its six nations and waged war on neighboring peoples. Every culture draws a circle of cooperation and extracts from whatever falls outside it.

The question has never been cooperation versus extraction. It has been: how wide is the circle of cooperation, and what are the consequences of extraction at the boundary?

What is genuinely new about the current moment is that the boundary is planetary. Climate makes the environment's memory visible — the consequences of extraction compound across every ecosystem simultaneously. Global communication makes recognition possible — we can see each other across every previous boundary. And existential risk makes the shadow of the future undeniable — the game cannot continue if the game board is destroyed.

The structural conditions for large-scale cooperation are emerging. Not because humans are becoming morally better but because Axelrod's conditions are changing at civilizational scale. The iteration is undeniable. The recognition is possible. The shadow of the future is long. Whether these conditions produce cooperation or collapse is the empirical question this book cannot answer. But the conditions themselves are real.
