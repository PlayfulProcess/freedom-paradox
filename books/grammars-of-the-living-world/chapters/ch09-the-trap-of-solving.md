# Chapter 9: The Trap of Solving

---

The problem-solving reflex goes like this: identify a deficiency. Design an intervention. Implement it through your framework. Evaluate it by your metrics. Declare success or iterate.

This is the structure of colonial governance. It is the structure of development economics. It is the structure of missionary work. It is the structure of psychiatric treatment. And it is the structure of every contemplative technology platform that proposes to solve the meaning crisis by delivering content to isolated individuals on personal screens.

Bayo Akomolafe asks the question this chapter must sit with: "What if the way we respond to the crisis is part of the crisis?"

---

## The Colonial Reflex

Vanessa Andreotti's *Hospicing Modernity* identifies the pattern with precision. Modernity is not primarily an intellectual project. It is an embodied, neurobiological one. It colonizes not just land and resources but "the ways we think, the ways we imagine things, the ways we hope, the ways we desire," and crucially, "our neurobiology in many ways: where we source pleasure, what we're afraid of."

The GTDF collective — the Gesturing Towards Decolonial Futures arts/research collective Andreotti leads — identifies neurochemical addictions to six patterns: comfort, control, convenience, certainty, consumption, and coherence. These are not merely preferences. They are compulsions with neurobiological roots — the reward circuits that fire when we buy something, the anxiety that surges when certainty is threatened, the dopamine release of being right. Intellectual critique alone cannot dislodge them, because "the neurophysical, neurochemical, and neurofunctional configurations that have created the problem won't get us into a different space."

This is why the trap of solving is a trap. The impulse to identify the crisis, design the response, build the platform, save the world — this impulse *is* the colonial reflex dressed in progressive clothing. It reproduces the very logic it claims to oppose: identify a deficiency (in others), design an intervention (from your framework), implement (through your power), evaluate (by your metrics). The dopamine hit of moral certainty, the oxytocin of tribal belonging to the "correct" group, the cortisol avoidance of never admitting complicity — all operate beneath conscious awareness.

Akomolafe's concept of postactivism challenges the binary thinking of traditional activism — us versus them, oppressor versus oppressed, problem versus solution — and questions whether conventional responses perpetuate the same logics that produced the issues. His concept of "onto-fugitivity" describes being as fundamentally fugitive — always escaping categorization. The escaped slave does not carefully preserve tradition. The escaped slave runs. And in running, something new and powerful emerges.

## Three Thinkers Who Sat With the Trap

Paul Tillich, writing in 1952, identified three existential anxieties that cannot be eliminated because they belong to existence itself: the anxiety of fate and death, the anxiety of guilt and condemnation, and the anxiety of emptiness and meaninglessness. The third — the one he identified as dominant in modernity — cannot be solved because the solving would require a ground of meaning that is precisely what has been lost. Unable to face this, people "construct a citadel of certainty, from which they fend off all threat of doubt on the basis of an absolutized authority." Fundamentalism, nationalism, techno-utopianism — each is a citadel built against the anxiety of meaninglessness.

Tillich's response was not a solution but a naming: the "God above God" — being-itself, which transcends the theism/atheism binary entirely. "It is as atheistic to affirm the existence of God as it is to deny it. God is being-itself, not a being." The courage to be, in Tillich's formulation, is rooted in the God who appears when God has disappeared in the anxiety of doubt. This is not faith in something. It is faith as the accepting of acceptance — without somebody or something that accepts.

Parker Palmer, from within the Quaker tradition, named the same trap as "functional atheism" — "the belief that ultimate responsibility for everything rests with us... a conviction held even by people who talk a good game about God." Functional atheism is the ego believing it is the sole agent and must control outcomes. Palmer's alternative: "Before you tell your life what you intend to do with it, listen for what it intends to do with you." This is not passivity. It is the recognition that the solving reflex — the compulsion to fix, to plan, to control — may itself be the obstacle.

Christopher Wallis, teaching from the nondual Shaiva Tantra of Abhinavagupta, provides the metaphysical architecture. *Svātantrya-śakti* — the Power of Autonomy — belongs exclusively to Consciousness as a whole, not to individual parts. When the Power of Acting operates through the contracted ego, the result is *karma* — bound, effortful action. When the same power operates through one who has recognized identity with the whole, the result is *kriya* — spontaneous, aligned action that does not bind. The distinction is not between action and inaction but between action from contraction and action from recognition.

## The Honest Question

This chapter must sit with a question it cannot answer.

Can the response this book gestures toward — grammars as responsibility structures, a recursive public, responsibility infrastructure built on open-source tools — avoid reproducing the colonial-solving structure while using tools that are themselves products of it? Technology, open source, AI — each was born within the very system of separability that the book critiques. Each carries the colonial payload in its architecture: the assumption of individual users, the optimization of individual outcomes, the measurement of individual metrics.

Andreotti's honest position is that we are not yet ready to build. We are in the composting phase — metabolizing the desires, attachments, and habits that created and sustain the current system. Hospicing, not solving. Providing palliative care to a dying system while offering prenatal care for what comes next, "without suffocating what's being born with projections and idealizations."

Akomolafe's honest position is that the direct path may not exist. When the direct path fails, the indirect path opens. The trickster's logic — the logic of Eshu in Yoruba cosmology — introduces chaos, misdirection, and play into situations that appear stuck.

The honest answer, drawing on Jonathan Rowson: "this move is forced, even if it loses." Inaction is also an action. The refusal to build, in a world where the attention economy is actively degrading the co-regulatory infrastructure described in the first chapter, is not neutrality. It is complicity by omission. The courage to build something that might not work, that doesn't claim answers, that holds questions — this is itself a practice. Not a solution but a gesture.

## What a Gesture Means

A gesture is not a solution. It is not certainty. It is the Linehan wager applied to building: we cannot know if this will work. We can ask if it is useful, if it fits the data, and if it is compassionate. And we can build it with the Protestant Principle built into the architecture — the commitment to continuously negate every concrete form that claims to be final.

Tillich's theonomy-heteronomy-autonomy cycle predicts what will happen: any grammar that crystallizes into an institution will become heteronomous — an imposed authority rather than a living practice. The Protestant Principle says: negate it before it hardens. Keep the draft open. Treat every version as provisional. Build the capacity for modification deeper than the capacity for certainty.

This is what distinguishes a gesture from a solution. A solution claims to close the gap between capacity and responsibility. A gesture acknowledges the gap, steps into it, and practices. Not with the confidence that the gap can be closed but with the courage that the practice itself — the shared, embodied, communal practice of maintaining grammars — is how communities have always lived inside gaps they cannot close.

The next chapter asks what that practice looks like when built as infrastructure.
