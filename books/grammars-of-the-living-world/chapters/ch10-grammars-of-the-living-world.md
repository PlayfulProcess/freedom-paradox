# Chapter 10: Grammars of the Living World

---

In 1969, Fred Rogers sat before the United States Senate Subcommittee on Communications. Richard Nixon had proposed cutting the budget for public television from twenty million dollars to ten. Senator John Pastore, a skeptic, gave Rogers six minutes.

Rogers did not argue. He did not present data. He described what his program did: it helped children deal with feelings that are "mentionable and manageable." Then he recited the words of a song: "What do you do with the mad that you feel? When you feel so mad you could bite..."

The senator, who had been dismissive all morning, said: "I'm supposed to be a pretty tough guy, and this is the first time I've had goosebumps for the last two days... Looks like you just earned the twenty million dollars."

The budget was restored. It was increased.

Rogers proved something that the entire argument of this book has been building toward: a technological medium can serve inner life rather than exploit it. Television — the same medium that was degrading the hearth, fragmenting attention, and replacing co-regulatory fire with flickering screen — could also be used to hold a child's question without answering it, to model emotional regulation through the pace of a human voice, to build the parasympathetic channel across the distance of a cathode-ray tube. The medium was not the message. The architecture was.

The question this chapter asks is whether the same can be done for the crisis this book has described.

## The Recursive Public

In 2008, the anthropologist Christopher Kelty published *Two Bits: The Cultural Significance of Free Software* — itself under a Creative Commons license, embodying the principles it described. Kelty's central theoretical contribution was the concept of the *recursive public*: a public that is vitally concerned with the material and practical maintenance and modification of the technical, legal, practical, and conceptual means of its own existence as a public. A collective independent of other forms of constituted power, capable of speaking to existing forms of power through the production of actually existing alternatives.

What distinguishes a recursive public from an interest group, a corporation, a church, or a movement is its focus on the radical modifiability of its own terms of existence. The community does not just use the medium to communicate. It creates and maintains that medium as a form of political action.

Kelty developed the concept to describe free software. But he anticipated its extension. And the extension this book proposes is: contemplative practice as recursive public. Grammars — the finite symbolic systems through which communities constrain their own power and maintain the ratio between capacity and responsibility — held as commons, maintained by the communities that practice them, modifiable by anyone, owned by no one.

The modulation works like this:

*Sharing grammars* modulates sharing source code. The grammars — tarot decks, I Ching hexagrams, children's stories, therapeutic frameworks — are the "source code." Created by people, not algorithms. Shareable, forkable, modifiable. The parent creating a grammar for the family. The community curating grammars together.

*Conceiving openness in sacred systems* modulates conceiving open systems. This is the hardest modulation. "Open" means something different when the content is a Yoruba divination system transmitted through initiated lineages than when it is a software protocol. The CARE Principles for Indigenous Data Governance — Collective Benefit, Authority to Control, Responsibility, Ethics — provide the ethical framework. Some grammars are structurally open and belong in the commons. Others are initiated and lineage-dependent and should be represented only with the explicit involvement and authority of tradition-holders.

*Creative Commons Attribution-ShareAlike* modulates copyleft. Anyone who builds on the grammars must share their derivatives under the same terms. The freedom propagates.

*Coordinating co-creation* modulates coordinating collaboration. The structured disagreement research is relevant here: not resolving conflicts but building the capacity to hold them. John Gottman's finding — that sixty-nine percent of relationship conflicts are perpetual problems that never get resolved — applies to communities as much as to couples. The design principle is not consensus but the capacity to hold disagreement with skill and grace.

## Three Layers

The platform that emerges from this book's argument has three layers, and each corresponds to one of the book's three movements.

**Layer 1 — The Body.** The platform supports practices that shift the nervous system toward parasympathetic dominance. It does not replace embodied practice — it points toward it. The research from the first chapter is unambiguous: the core mechanisms of co-regulation require physical co-presence. A digital platform cannot replicate the San healing dance or the Dagara grief ritual. What it can do is connect people to local practice communities, provide education about the full range of embodied practices, and be honest about its own limitations. The platform that acknowledges "you need to put down this screen and go be with other people" is, paradoxically, the most trustworthy platform. Digital threshold, embodied sanctuary.

**Layer 2 — The Story.** The platform provides grammars — shared symbolic structures for the intellect and for relationships. Created by people, not algorithms. The grammar does not tell you what to think. It gives you something new to think *with*. A tarot card as a reframing tool. An I Ching hexagram as a container for complexity. A children's story told four ways — the gentle version, the original, the cultural roots, and why it still matters. Each grammar is a machine for constructing stories, and the stories it constructs are always about the person constructing them — and about the relationships that hold them.

**Layer 3 — Responsibility.** The platform is designed as a commons, not a commodity. No ads, no algorithm, no notifications, no tracking. Not because those are nice design choices but because the ad-driven algorithmic platform is the purest expression of power without responsibility ever built — maximum extraction, zero accountability, no seventh-generation thinking.

Elinor Ostrom's eight design principles for commons governance — clearly defined boundaries, locally adapted rules, participatory decision-making, community-accountable monitoring, graduated sanctions, accessible conflict resolution, recognized self-governance rights, and nested enterprises for larger systems — describe the structural conditions under which human groups cooperate rather than compete. They are, in the language of this book, a grammar for governance. The platform that embodies them is not just technically open. It is responsible — accountable to the community that uses it, constrained by the principles that govern it, modifiable by anyone who participates.

## The Design Draws From

Fred Rogers proved that a medium can serve the child. His program's pace — scored at 14.95 on a temporal density metric versus *Power Rangers* at 41.90 — communicated that the child's tempo is the right tempo. Every design choice served developmental needs, not engagement optimization. The platform draws from this: pace matters. Slowness is not a bug.

Maria Montessori proved that structured autonomy — freedom within a carefully prepared environment — produces children who are more capable, not less, than children in unstructured environments. The platform draws from this: the grammar provides the structure. The practitioner provides the freedom.

Marsha Linehan proved that genuine dialectic — holding opposites in creative tension, accepting reality while simultaneously working to change it — is the most effective therapeutic framework for severe emotional dysregulation. The platform draws from this: the grammar holds the question without answering it.

The cohousing movement proved that the gradient between private and public — the design of shared spaces that support both solitude and community — is a structural problem with structural solutions. The platform draws from this: private practice and communal practice need different architectures.

Ghost — the open-source, nonprofit-protected publishing platform — proved that a mission-critical digital commons can sustain itself financially without investors, without owners, and without compromising its principles. Nearly nine million dollars annually, all reinvested. The platform cannot be sold, pivoted, or pressured by shareholders. The platform draws from this: the legal structure protects the mission.

## If We Succeed, We Will Disappear

Kelty quotes Creative Commons with the phrase "If we succeed, we will disappear." The idea is that if the licenses work — if they become the default infrastructure through which culture is shared — then the organization that created them becomes invisible. The licenses are just there, like plumbing.

The same applies here. The goal is not to build a company. It is not even to build a platform. It is to build infrastructure that becomes so fundamental it becomes invisible — the way Wikipedia became invisible, the way Linux became invisible, the way the protocols that run the Internet became invisible. Not noticed, not branded, not owned. Just there. Available. Modifiable. Maintained by the community that uses it.

The grammars are recursive publics. They always have been. The I Ching has been continuously modified for three thousand years. Tarot has been continuously modified since 1440. Ifá has been continuously modified through every Babaláwo who commits a new verse to memory and every diaspora community that adapts the practice to new circumstances. What changes now is the speed and scale at which this can happen — and the urgency.

The gap between capacity and responsibility is the gap in which everything burns. The species that got fire before it had the grammars to hold it is running out of time to learn what every adaptive system in the history of life already knows: power must be matched by responsibility, and the ratio between them determines whether a system builds complexity or destroys it.

This is not a solution. It is a gesture. A wager. The Linehan wager applied to civilization itself: we cannot know if it will work. We can ask if it is useful, if it fits the data, and if it is compassionate. And we can build it — treating every version as a draft, privileging the community's capacity to adapt over any individual's capacity to plan — and see what happens.

Not with answers but with an invitation to practice.
