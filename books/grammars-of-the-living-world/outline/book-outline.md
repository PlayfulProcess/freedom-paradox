# Grammars of the Living World

# **Grammars of the Living World**

## **A Book by PlayfulProcess**

**Working thesis:** We are living through a crisis of mythic belonging — we have inherited stories calibrated for a world that no longer exists and media systems that structurally prevent us from creating shared stories for the world we live in. The impulse to solve this crisis may itself be part of the crisis. What remains possible is not a solution but a gesture — toward commons, toward practice, toward more adaptive futures.

---

## **Book Architecture**

### **PART I: THE CRISIS**

**The reader enters through the wound.** No grammars, no solutions, no platform — just the diagnosis. Each chapter closes with questions it has raised, explicitly unanswered.

---

#### **Chapter 1: When Stories Lose Their Homes**

**Core argument:** Context is constitutive of meaning, not merely decorative. When narratives are severed from the lived conditions that produced them, they become dysfunctional — not false, but dislocated.

**Movement of the chapter:**

* Open with Hansel and Gretel. The 1812 version: a mother (not stepmother) agrees to abandon her children during famine. The Great Famine of 1315–1317. Child abandonment as documented demographic reality (Boswell). The Cambridge Group's 2024 finding that famine stories are absent from English folklore because England's last famine predated systematic collection — the absence proves the connection.

* The Grimms' revisions across seven editions: mother → stepmother, insertion of religious consolation, progressive softening. Each revision reflects affluent society's increasing inability to engage with the tale's actual subject. The KQED documentation of adaptations: from abandonment (1812) to "stranger danger" parable (1983).

* The scholarly fault line: Bettelheim (psychological universalism — the gingerbread house as oral regression) vs. Darnton/Zipes/Warner (historical materialism — "Parents, unable to feed another child, were forced to abandon newborns"). Warner's accusation that Bettelheim "leeches the history out of fairy tales."

* The unwritten argument: the specific paradox of telling a starvation story to a child who has never known hunger, in a culture where the dominant pathology is overconsumption. The gingerbread house was a lethal trap weaponizing the fantasy of plenty against starving children. It is now a description of the average child's Tuesday. Zipes on fairy tales "violated" by consumer capitalism. Warner's observation that the hardest task is now making child abandonment and cannibal witches "bearable for small children at Christmas."

* The Winnie the Pooh case: Milne's PTSD from the Somme producing a pastoral counter-world. Christopher Robin's childhood destroyed by the publicity machine. The stuffed animals in a glass case at the New York Public Library — intimate objects of play preserved as public artifacts. The Shea et al. satirical CMAJ paper escaping its Holiday Review context and becoming internet "fact." Frederick Crews' *Pooh Perplex* proving any text can be made to support any framework. A miniature of mythic displacement: meaning lost in transit.

* The Egyptian artifact analogy: Benjamin's aura — the shift from cult value to exhibition value. Tomb objects designed to serve the dead now "haunting" museums. The pharaoh's curse as anxiety about contextual violation. Dan Hicks on the Benin Bronzes as "a systematic extension of warfare across time." Amy Lonetree: objects in museums are "living entities" — decontextualization is ongoing grief.

* Campbell's strongest claim: "Myth cannot be exported into another land or another time." Eliade's desacralization. Hillman's admission that depth psychology is "historically European in origin." Dundes: folklore is "autobiographical ethnography." Ngũgĩ wa Thiong'o: colonial language imposes colonial myths, displacing indigenous ones.

* The distinction from cultural appropriation: mythic displacement includes a culture's relationship to its *own* inherited stories. A modern German telling Hansel and Gretel to a well-fed child is not appropriating — the tale belongs to that tradition. But it may still be displaced.

**Closing questions:**

What happens to the psychological architecture of a story when the conditions that produced it no longer exist? Can a myth survive its own success — can universal distribution destroy what made a story locally powerful? If context is constitutive of meaning, what does it mean that we now consume stories with no context at all — algorithmically surfaced, stripped of origin, optimized for engagement? And the deepest question: are we mythically homeless — not because we lack stories, but because the stories we have no longer belong to the world we inhabit?

#### **Chapter 2: The Degradation of the Hearth**

**Core argument:** The progression from fire to television to personal screens represents a systematic degradation of the ritual container for shared story. Each step preserved some functions while destroying others. The current configuration — each family member consuming their own algorithmically curated narrative stream — makes mythic belonging structurally impossible within the household itself.

**Movement of the chapter:**

* The fire. Oral storytelling in community: same story, same time, live teller embedded in the same material conditions as the listeners. This is what Campbell means by "living mythology." Stories had owners, seasons, occasions, authorized tellers (Library of Congress American Folklife Center documentation). Gregory Cajete (Tewa): indigenous oral traditions "have always been integrated with drawing, arts, and practical education" — not extractable content but embedded practice.

* The television hearth. McLuhan: TV replaced the hearth as the focus for family gatherings. Cecelia Tichi's *Electronic Hearth*: the "teleconsciousness" that begins with "a continuous reprioritizing of attention in the habitat of the 'always on' TV." What was preserved: the family still gathered, still shared a narrative, still had common symbolic vocabulary. The 1960s: 96% of Americans watched coverage of Kennedy's assassination. Everyone could reference Walter Cronkite or *The Wizard of Oz*. What was lost: Postman's insight that the screen is "so saturated with our memories of profane events, so deeply associated with the commercial and entertainment worlds that it is difficult for it to be recreated as a frame for sacred events." The medium was already compromised — but the gathering persisted.

* Mr. Rogers as proof of concept. Fred Rogers — a Presbyterian minister — went to television because he hated what it was doing to children. He didn't boycott it; he didn't celebrate it; he asked *how*. His 1969 Senate testimony that saved PBS funding. Christakis's finding: every hour of hyperpaced TV before age three increases attention problems by 10%. But educational programming like *Mister Rogers' Neighborhood* raised the risk by 0%. The medium is not the problem. The design of the medium is the problem. Rogers's specific choices: slow pace, direct address, real-time activities, respect for the child's temporality. The Daniel Tiger research showing prosocial outcomes when parents discuss the show afterward. Rogers as the third way between technophobia and techno-utopianism.

* The personal screen. The dissolution of even the degraded gathering. "Gone are the days of the family gathered around a single television set." Each family member consuming their own algorithmically curated stream. The Bengkulu study: "family members are frequently physically present but psychologically detached" — absent presence. Teenagers communicating with parents only through "practical reminders about schedules and schoolwork rather than deep conversations about values, emotions, or aspirations."

* Christakis on what screens destroy developmentally. Joint attention — shared awareness between caregiver and child — happens hundreds of times daily. Engaging tablet apps reduce toddlers' responses to joint attention prompts. Background TV decreases play quality and parent-child interaction quality — parents become slower to respond to bids for attention. The co-watching paradox: co-viewing with siblings improves inhibitory control, but co-viewing with parents decreases cognitive flexibility — suggesting that simply being present is not the same as genuine shared engagement. The quality of gathering matters, not just the fact of it.

* Tristan Harris and the attention economy. "Human downgrading" — addiction, distraction, isolation, polarization as mutually reinforcing harms. Platforms designed not for meaning or development but for engagement — "extractive attention economy that treats humans as resources to mine attention out of." Autoplay, variable reward, infinite scroll — mechanisms structurally incompatible with contemplative practice. Common Sense Media on children's dark patterns. Shoshana Zuboff's surveillance capitalism as the economic logic behind the extraction.

* The impossibility of mythic belonging inside the family. When each person feeds themselves their own stories, there is no shared symbolic ground. No common reference points. No ability to process experience through a shared narrative vocabulary. The family in the same room, each on a different device, is the spatial image of what this chapter diagnoses.

**Closing questions:**

If the fire created mythic community, the television created mythic family, and the personal screen creates mythic isolation — what creates mythic belonging now? Is it possible to design a medium that serves the child's development and the family's cohesion rather than extracting attention for profit? Fred Rogers proved the medium itself is neutral — but Rogers worked within broadcast television, a shared medium by definition. What does "Mr. Rogers for the algorithmic age" even mean when there is no shared channel? Can parents curate shared symbolic worlds for their families, or has the infrastructure of personalization made that impossible? And: if a family cannot share a story, can a nation?

#### **Chapter 3: The Polarization of the Polis**

**Core argument:** The fragmentation of family narrative mirrors and feeds the fragmentation of political narrative. A polis needs shared stories the way a family needs shared stories — without them, there is no commons, no shared symbolic ground from which to negotiate meaning. Polarization is not primarily an ideological problem but a mythic one.

**Movement of the chapter:**

* From household to nation. Benedict Anderson's *Imagined Communities*: the nation itself is a narrative construction, made possible by shared media — the newspaper, the novel, the broadcast. Dayan and Katz's "media events" — the moon landing, royal weddings, 9/11 — as temporary ritual unity created through shared viewing. What happens when no media event can command universal attention anymore.

* The media fragmentation research. Before talk radio, cable news, and the Internet, citizens received "a relatively consistent news package" — fostering "a common collective intelligence." Now, "each person will likely consume different fragments of news content." Audience fragmentation as the structural precondition of polarization. The Georgetown research: selectivity → fragmentation → echo chambers → polarization. But also the counterintuitive Springer finding: polarization sometimes increases with *more* diverse exposure, not less — when media itself is more polarized than the population.

* Putnam's *Bowling Alone* and its media dimensions. The decline of civic association correlated with the rise of privatized entertainment. Television's role in replacing communal leisure with isolated consumption. The further acceleration with smartphones and social media.

* Gottman's 69%. The most important number in dialogue design: 69% of relationship conflicts are perpetual problems that never get resolved — this holds for both happy and struggling couples. The difference is entirely in how they're managed. Mapped onto political life: most political disagreements are permanent features of pluralistic society, not problems to be solved. Haidt's moral foundations: liberals weight Care and Fairness; conservatives additionally weight Loyalty, Authority, Sanctity. Neither is wrong — they process moral information through different evolutionary channels. You cannot argue someone out of a moral intuition because the elephant decided before the rider got to work.

* Tim Urban's ladder: the real divide is vertical (how you think) not horizontal (what you think). A liberal scientist and a conservative scientist can productively disagree; a liberal zealot and a conservative zealot cannot. Idea labs vs. echo chambers. But also Urban's own failure: 48 pages on right-wing extremism, 353 on left-wing — the framework itself can become a weapon.

* The mythic reading of polarization. Without shared stories, we cannot even disagree productively — disagreement requires common ground to disagree *on*. The Talmudic model: "Both these and these are the words of the living God" — but the law follows Hillel because they studied their opponents' arguments before their own. Disagreement as sacred obligation. The chavruta tradition. What this requires: a shared textual ground. What we lack: any such ground.

* Structured dialogue as symptom, not cure. Braver Angels, deliberative polls, citizens' assemblies — all produce remarkable short-term results (Ireland's abortion assembly, Fishkin's 70% opinion change, Intelligence Squared's 47% persuasion rate). All struggle with scale, self-selection, and sustained effect. They prove that shared narrative spaces work. They cannot create the shared narrative spaces.

**Closing questions:**

If polarization is mythic before it is ideological — a crisis of shared stories before it is a crisis of policy disagreement — then what would it mean to address it at the mythic level? Can a society that has lost its shared symbolic ground recover it, or only build new ground? Is there a political equivalent of the family hearth — a place where a community gathers around shared stories? The Talmudic tradition preserved minority opinions alongside majority rulings because "the minority view may become relevant in future circumstances." Can a digital commons preserve disagreement without weaponizing it? And: given Gottman's 69%, should we stop trying to resolve our conflicts and start learning to live inside them with skill?

### **PART II: THE GRAMMARS**

**Having established the crisis, the reader now encounters humanity's oldest response to it: finite symbolic systems for encountering the unknown.**

---

#### **Chapter 4: Machines for Constructing Stories**

*This is the current Chapter 1, adapted.* The I Ching, Ifá, Tarot, Kabbalah as "grammars" — finite symbolic vocabularies that generate meaning through randomness, structure, and interpretation. Combinatorial completeness. Holographic structure (each part contains the whole). Calvino's "machines for constructing stories." Bohm's implicate order. The convergence across civilizations: why humans independently build these systems and why they keep converging on the same structural properties.

**New emphasis for restructured book:** These systems are not presented as solutions to the crisis but as evidence that humans have always needed — and always built — shared symbolic vocabularies for encountering the unknown. The question is whether the conditions for building and sharing such vocabularies still exist.

**Closing questions:**

Why do humans keep building these systems independently? Is there something about binary combinatorics that maps onto how consciousness itself organizes experience? If grammars are "humanity's oldest contemplative technologies" — older than meditation, older than organized religion, older than writing — what does it mean that we now live in a civilization that has no shared grammar? The I Ching survived because a bureaucrat classified it as divination. Ifá survived because it lived in memory. Tarot survived because it had no author to execute. What classification system, what memory practice, what structural openness would allow new grammars to survive our current moment?

---

#### **Chapter 5: Survival**

*This is the current Chapter 2, adapted.* The Qin burning and the I Ching's survival through bureaucratic classification. Ifá's survival through oral transmission across the Middle Passage. Tarot's survival through structural openness. The Maya codices as counterexample — what total destruction looks like. The Mawangdui silk texts and Tsinghua bamboo slips as archaeological recovery.

**New emphasis:** Each survival strategy teaches something about what a grammar needs to persist: compactness (can it be memorized?), utility (can it be classified as practical?), openness (can it exist without institutional support?). These become implicit design criteria for whatever comes next — but not named as such yet.

**Closing questions:**

What is the modern equivalent of hiding a text in a wall? If the threat to grammars today is not burning but drowning — not destruction but dilution in an ocean of content — what survival strategy fits? A grammar's compactness was once its defense against confiscation. Is compactness still protective when the problem is not scarcity of stories but overwhelming abundance? And the hardest question: the Qin burning destroyed texts, but the Old Text / New Text controversy that followed shaped Chinese thought for centuries — the argument about what was authentic became as consequential as the originals. What arguments about authenticity are we already in, without recognizing them?

#### **Chapter 6: Tarot's Double Life**

*Drawn from existing research.* The colonial architecture of esoteric tarot — Court de Gébelin's fabricated Egyptian origins, Lévi's appropriation of Kabbalah, the Golden Dawn's synthesizing project, Blavatsky's root races. Pamela Colman Smith's erasure. Strube's decisive claim: "if occultism is modern, it is inherently intertwined with colonialism."

And the turn: because tarot's esoteric meaning was fabricated rather than revealed, it carries no binding authority. There is no tarot pope. The same structural absence that allowed colonial capture allows decolonial reinvention. Marmolejo's *Red Tarot*, Road's *Next World Tarot*, Dore's secular therapeutic reframing, the HCI scholarship on tarot as "technology of care." The Greenberg-Sosteric debate in *Anthropology of Consciousness*: can the master's tools dismantle the master's house?

**Closing questions:**

If a system's openness is both its vulnerability to colonial capture and its availability for liberation, what does ethical stewardship look like? Is there a difference between a grammar that was always open (tarot — Italian playing cards that accumulated meaning through projection) and a grammar that is initiated and lineage-dependent (Ifá — transmitted through consecrated Babalawos)? Where is the line between gateway building and gatekeeping? Between honoring a tradition and freezing it? Between making knowledge accessible and stripping it of its context — which is, after all, the very crisis this book began with?

#### **Chapter 7: The Trap of Solving**

**Core argument:** Modernity's deepest reflex is to identify a problem and solve it. This reflex — problem → diagnosis → solution → implementation → evaluation — is so deeply embedded in Western thought that it operates even (especially) in movements that claim to critique modernity. The crisis of mythic belonging cannot be "solved" in this way without reproducing the very structures that created it. But this does not mean nothing can be done. It means what can be done is a gesture, not a fix.

**Movement of the chapter:**

* Open with the pattern: every chapter so far has ended with questions. The reader may be frustrated. Where are the answers? This frustration is itself the subject of this chapter.

* Vanessa Andreotti's "meta-relational AI" framing and the tension between complicity and engagement in extractive systems. Her argument (stated carefully, with the caveat that follows) that modernity's "problem-solving" orientation is itself a colonial habit — the assumption that reality is a set of problems awaiting solutions, that the correct response to suffering is intervention, that the goal of thought is actionable conclusions. The possibility that Recursive.eco, or any technological platform, participates in the very extraction it claims to resist.

* Bayo Akomolafe's more radical claim: that psychology creates rather than describes reality. That the categories we use to diagnose the crisis — "mythic belonging," "ritual misplacement," "polarization" — are themselves products of the modern epistemological apparatus that produced the crisis. That naming the problem is not the first step toward solving it but a way of domesticating it within familiar frames.

* **The honest caveat.** Andreotti and Akomolafe are provocative thinkers whose work has genuine force. But they do not operate with the scholarly rigor of a Christopher Wallis parsing Abhinavagupta's Sanskrit, or a Wande Abimbola transmitting 4,096 couplets of Ifá, or even a Jack Zipes reading fairy tales against their material-historical conditions. Their claims are often more poetic than demonstrable, more gestural than evidenced. This does not make them wrong. But it means the reader should hold them as they hold everything else in this book — as questions, not as authorities.

* The deeper tradition behind the claim: Tillich's "God above God" — the recognition that transcends both theism and atheism. Wallis's nondual Shaiva Tantra: freedom belongs to the whole, not to the individual; the individual cannot "solve" its way to liberation because the solving is itself an expression of contracted consciousness. Palmer's "functional atheism" — the belief that if anything decent is going to happen, I am the one who needs to make it happen. Kriya vs. karma: action that flows from alignment with the whole vs. action that binds through the ego's attempt to control.

* The cosmic topology metaphor: local flatness tells you nothing about global shape. You cannot determine the shape of the universe from inside your neighborhood. You also cannot solve the crisis of mythic belonging from inside the modern epistemological framework that produced it. But you can make gestures. You can create conditions. You can build infrastructure and see what the community does with it.

* What a "gesture toward adaptive futures" means in practice. Not a solution but a wager. Not a product but an experiment. Not certainty but — to use Tillich's word — courage. The courage to build something that might not work, that doesn't claim to have the answer, that holds questions without answering them.

**Closing questions:**

Is it possible to build a technological platform without reproducing the technological logic that created the crisis? Can a digital commons genuinely serve contemplative practice, or does the medium inevitably shape the practice into something the attention economy can digest? If the impulse to solve is part of the problem, what is the alternative to solving — and is naming that alternative just another form of solving? These are the questions the final section lives inside.

### **PART IV: THE GESTURE**

**One chapter. Recursive.eco arrives — not as a solution to the crisis but as a practice, an experiment, a wager.**

---

#### **Chapter 8: Grammars of the Living World**

*Draws on the existing recursive public chapter, restructured.*

* Open with Kelty's concept of the recursive public — a community that builds and maintains the very infrastructure through which it makes meaning. The five components modulated for contemplative practice: sharing grammars, conceiving openness in sacred systems (the CARE Principles), writing the license (CC BY-SA as contemplative copyleft), coordinating co-creation, the movement.

* The design philosophy: not anti-technology but pro-ritual. Not engagement-optimized but contemplation-designed. Not solving the crisis but creating conditions under which communities might create shared symbolic worlds. Fred Rogers didn't solve television. He showed what it looked like when the medium served the child.

* The specific architecture: no ads, no algorithm, no notifications, no tracking. Grammars created by people, not algorithms. Shareable, forkable, modifiable. The parent who creates a playlist of folk tales, tarot reflections, and I Ching hexagrams that the family returns to together — creating a shared mythical and ritual space in the personal-screen era. The community that curates grammars together — voting, contributing, maintaining. The practice of casting, reflecting, journaling, sharing.

* The wager: if AI has arrived and we need to build culture faster, the compassionate way to do this is through commons, not commodities. The hypothesis is stated; the proof is in the practice. The platform exists. The grammars are available. Everything else is practice.

* Close with the Kelty line: "If we succeed, we will disappear." The goal is not to build a company or even a platform but an infrastructure so fundamental it becomes invisible — like Wikipedia, like Linux, like the protocols that run the Internet. Not noticed, not branded, not owned. Just there. Available. Modifiable. Maintained by the community that uses it.

**No closing questions.** The book ends not with answers but with an invitation to practice.
