Chapter One

Grammars

In the last quarter of the ninth century before the common era, anonymous diviners in the Western Zhou dynasty scratched numerical symbols onto oracle bones and bronze vessels. The symbols were binary: broken lines and unbroken lines, arranged in sets of six. Two line types, combined exhaustively, generated sixty-four figures. Each figure carried a name and a judgment. Together they formed a system that claimed to map the entirety of change — every possible configuration of yin and yang across six positions.

Twenty-five hundred years later and five thousand miles south, Yoruba Babalawos in the sacred city of Ile-Ife cast sixteen sacred palm nuts and read the marks that remained. Their system was also binary: single marks and double marks, arranged in sets of eight. Two mark types, combined exhaustively, generated two hundred and fifty-six figures. Each figure carried a name, a hierarchy, and an oral corpus of hundreds of verses encoding mythology, medicine, ethics, and philosophy. Together they formed a system that claimed to map the entirety of existence — every conceivable life situation contained within the Odu.

The structural parallel is extraordinary. The I Ching uses six binary positions (2⁶ = 64 figures). Ifá uses eight binary positions (2⁸ = 256 figures). Both generate their primary figures by pairing smaller units — the I Ching pairs eight trigrams, Ifá pairs sixteen tetragrams. Both attach a literary corpus to each figure. Both employ physical randomization — yarrow stalks or coins for the I Ching, palm nuts or divination chain for Ifá — to select figures, interpreting the result as communication between the human and the divine. Both present reality as fundamentally dynamic rather than static: the I Ching's changing lines transform one hexagram into another, while Ifá's prescribed ebo serves as the mechanism of transformation — changing misalignment into alignment.

Information-theoretically, a single tarot draw provides approximately 6.3 bits of entropy. A single I Ching hexagram provides 6 bits. Ifá's opele generates close to 8 bits of Shannon entropy per cast. These are not trivially different — Ifá encodes four times as many distinct states as the I Ching — but they occupy the same order of magnitude, the same neighborhood of informational richness. They are, in a precise mathematical sense, comparable machines for introducing structured randomness into human decision-making.

The scholarly consensus treats these as parallel but independent inventions. What is not debated is that something remarkable happened: across civilizations that had no contact with each other, human beings independently constructed symbolic architectures claiming to map the totality of reality through interconnected parts.

I call these systems grammars.

Not grammars in the linguistic sense — not syntax rules for constructing sentences — but in a deeper sense: finite vocabularies of symbols that, through combination and interpretation, generate infinite meaning. A grammar, in the sense I mean, has five properties.

First, it has a finite symbolic vocabulary. Seventy-eight cards. Sixty-four hexagrams. Two hundred and fifty-six Odu. Ten Sefirot connected by twenty-two paths. The vocabulary is bounded. You can hold it in your hands, or at least in your memory. This finitude is not a limitation but the source of the system's power: because the vocabulary is finite, it can be learned, transmitted, and shared.

Second, the vocabulary is combinatorially generative. The meaning of any element depends on its relationships with other elements. A tarot card means one thing in the "past" position and another in the "future" position. An I Ching hexagram means one thing with no changing lines and another with three. The grammar generates meaning through juxtaposition, not through fixed assignment. This is what makes it a grammar rather than a dictionary: it has syntax, not just definitions.

Third, the grammar uses randomness as portal. The coin toss, the card draw, the palm nut cast, the birth moment — each introduces an element that the conscious mind did not produce. This randomness is not noise. It is the mechanism by which the grammar bypasses habitual thought and confronts the querent with something unexpected. Every grammar has a technology of randomness at its heart.

Fourth, the grammar requires a practitioner as participant. The diviner, the reader, the astrologer is not a detached observer decoding a message. They are an active participant in meaning-making, bringing their own experience, intuition, and context to the interpretation. The same card drawn by two different people in two different situations means two different things — not because the card changed, but because the act of interpretation is irreducibly personal. This is what distinguishes a grammar from a code. A code has one correct decryption. A grammar has infinite valid readings.

Fifth, the grammar models change rather than stasis. The I Ching is literally the "Book of Changes." Ifá's prescribed ebo transforms misalignment into alignment. Tarot's narrative spreads map temporal flow. Astrology tracks planetary motion through time. Every grammar is a technology of becoming, not being.

· · ·

The most vivid image of this convergence comes from Huayan Buddhism. The Avatamsaka Sutra describes Indra's Net: an infinitely vast net with a jewel at every node. Each jewel reflects every other jewel, and each reflection contains all the others — infinite reflections within reflections. The third patriarch Fazang demonstrated this with a hall of mirrors and a golden lion statue: every part of the lion is completely gold, and every point in the mirror hall reflects the entire hall.

The I Ching embodies this logic structurally. Through its changing lines, each hexagram enfolds pathways to every other hexagram. The system is genuinely holographic — each part contains the whole. Kabbalah's Tree of Life achieves the same through the central Sefirah, Tiferet, which connects directly to every other node, ensuring the graph is fully interconnected. Both systems model reality through the interplay of polar opposites at hierarchical levels — the Tree's three pillars mirror yin, yang, and their harmony.

David Bohm's implicate order — his distinction between the explicate order of separate things and the deeper implicate order where "everything is enfolded into everything" — represents the most rigorous modern scientific articulation of what the grammars have always modeled. The I Ching is a hologram of change. Ifá is a hologram of existence. Tarot is a hologram of human experience. Each fragment contains information about the whole.

None of this means these systems are "true" in the sense that they accurately predict the future. It means they are complete in a formal sense — they provide a vocabulary rich enough to describe any situation a human being might encounter, and a syntax flexible enough to generate meaningful interpretations of that situation. They are, in Italo Calvino's precise formulation, "machines for constructing stories." They work because the stories they construct are always about the person constructing them.

· · ·

The question is not why humans build grammars. The question is why we keep building them independently — and why they keep converging on the same structural properties.

One answer is cognitive. The human mind naturally categorizes, polarizes, and combines. We think in binaries and generate complexity by combining them. A system of six binary positions generates sixty-four states, which is enough to feel exhaustive without being overwhelming. A system of eight binary positions generates two hundred and fifty-six — pushing toward the limit of what a trained practitioner can hold in memory. These are cognitive sweet spots.

Another answer is mathematical. Ron Eglash, in African Fractals, traced a provocative chain: African binary divination → Islamic geomancy → European geomancy → Raymond Lull's logic machine → Leibniz → modern binary computing. The mathematics of grammars is not mysticism — it is combinatorics. Binary systems generate exhaustive possibility spaces efficiently. This is why digital computers use them. It is also why diviners use them.

But the deepest answer may be existential. Humans live in a world that exceeds our comprehension. We are finite beings confronting infinite complexity. We cannot process all the information available to us, cannot predict the consequences of our actions, cannot know what we do not know. Grammars are technologies for making this situation bearable — not by reducing complexity, but by providing a structured framework within which complexity can be encountered. The randomness of the draw is an honest acknowledgment of our ignorance. The finitude of the vocabulary is an honest acknowledgment of our cognitive limits. The requirement for interpretation is an honest acknowledgment that meaning is made, not found.

In this sense, grammars are humanity's oldest contemplative technologies. They predate meditation traditions, predate organized religion, predate writing itself — the proto-hexagram symbols on Shang dynasty oracle bones are among the earliest symbolic marks in the archaeological record. Before humans had books, before they had temples, before they had formal philosophies, they had grammars: structured systems for encountering the unknown and making meaning from the encounter.

· · ·

Recursive.eco — the platform I am building — hosts grammars. Not just one tradition's grammars, but grammars from across human civilization, side by side: the Rider-Waite-Smith Tarot next to the Dhammapada next to Alice in Wonderland next to the I Ching next to a book about couples therapy next to folk tales for children. The range is the point.

The platform treats each grammar as something that can be cast, reflected upon, and saved. You can draw a tarot card and journal about it. You can receive an I Ching hexagram and sit with it. You can encounter a passage from the Tao Te Ching and let it work on you. The technology is simple: randomness, structure, and reflection. What makes it distinct is what it does not do. It does not tell you what the card means. It does not predict your future. It does not claim authority over your experience. It holds your question without answering it.

This design philosophy — holding questions without answering them — is not a feature. It is the entire argument of this book.

The question this book asks is: How should these grammars be held? Who should own them? Who should interpret them? Who should decide which grammars are included and which are excluded? Who maintains the infrastructure — the code, the licenses, the community agreements — that allows these grammars to exist in digital form?

The answer I have arrived at — not from ideology, but from watching what works, what fails, and what is compassionate — is that grammars should be held as commons. Their source code should be freely and publicly available. The platform that hosts them should be open-source. The community that creates and curates them should govern itself.

This is not an obvious conclusion. It raises questions about cultural appropriation, about the difference between systems that are structurally open and systems that are initiated and lineage-dependent, about whether digitization inevitably desacralizes, about who profits and who is exploited. These questions occupy the chapters that follow.

But the hypothesis is simple, and I state it here so the reader knows where we are going: AI has arrived, and we need a faster way to build culture. The compassionate and effective way to do this is through a recursive public — a community that builds and maintains the very infrastructure through which it makes meaning. Grammars are that infrastructure.

The rest of this book is the evidence.

Chapter Two

Survival

In 213 BCE, the Chancellor of China made his move. Li Si, the most powerful Legalist politician of his era, presented his memorial to Emperor Qin Shi Huang: scholars who "use the past to criticize the present" were dangerous to state unity. Private learning bred private judgment, and private judgment bred dissent. The emperor approved. The edict was brutal in its specificity: all privately held copies of the Classic of Poetry, the Classic of History, and the writings of the Hundred Schools of Philosophy were to be surrendered for burning. Public execution for anyone caught discussing the banned classics. Extermination of the entire clan for using history to criticize the present. Tattooing and forced labor on the Great Wall for anyone who failed to surrender their books within thirty days.

The edict concluded with a chilling instruction: "Those who wish to study should take government officials as their teachers." The state would be the sole source of knowledge.

Yet the burning was not total. The edict explicitly exempted texts on medicine, agriculture, and divination. The I Ching — by then already layered with the philosophical commentaries of the Ten Wings, already one of humanity's most profound cosmological documents — was swept into the protected category. Qin bureaucrats classified it as a divination manual, a practical tool for fortune-telling. The irony is profound. The Xi Ci Zhuan, the Great Commentary that articulated "One yin and one yang — this is called the Dao," the text that became the cornerstone of Chinese metaphysics for two millennia — survived because a bureaucrat saw only a fortune-telling handbook.

The Classic of Music, the sixth of the legendary Six Classics, was not so lucky. It was completely and permanently lost. Only seven fragments survive, quoted in other works. An entire tradition of pre-Qin musical knowledge, vanished.

How a civilization classifies its texts determines which ideas live and which die.

· · ·

The true catastrophe came three years later. The imperial library had preserved copies of every banned text — the burning targeted private holdings, not state collections. Those copies survived the edict. They did not survive the rebel general Xiang Yu, who sacked and burned the Qin palaces at Xianyang in 206 BCE. The second conflagration consumed the only remaining authorized copies of many works. Martin Kern of Princeton has argued that the 206 BCE palace burning was the real point of no return — that earlier texts frequently cite the Classics in ways that would have been impossible if the 213 BCE burning had truly been thorough.

What survived did so through two mechanisms: walls and memory.

In the late second century BCE, workers demolishing a wall at the Kong family home in Qufu — the ancestral home of Confucius — discovered manuscripts hidden within. Versions of the Classic of History, the Rites of Zhou, the Analects, written in pre-Qin seal script. These were transcribed by Kong Anguo, an eleventh-generation descendant of Confucius, and became the foundation of the "Old Text" tradition.

And there was Fu Sheng. A former Qin-era court scholar, he had hidden a copy of the Shangshu in his own home's walls. After decades of exile during the civil wars, he returned to find the manuscripts damaged — only twenty-eight or twenty-nine chapters out of roughly one hundred had survived. By the time Emperor Wen of Han sent an official to learn from Fu Sheng around 160 BCE, the old scholar was over ninety and nearly unintelligible. His daughter served as translator, converting his thick Shandong dialect into language the scribe could record.

The Classic of Poetry survived almost intact because its songs had been transmitted orally for centuries. Verse is far easier to memorize than prose.

· · ·

Five thousand miles south and eighteen centuries later, a different grammar faced a different catastrophe. The collapse of the Oyo Empire in the 1790s, the Fulani jihad of the 1830s, and inter-Yoruba civil wars generated massive numbers of enslaved Yoruba, some from the priestly class. They were shipped across the Atlantic in the holds of slave ships. Their temples were left behind. Their sacred objects — the divination trays, the palm nuts, the ivory tappers — were confiscated or lost. Everything material was stripped away.

But the grammar survived. Because Ifá's corpus is oral, not written. You cannot confiscate an oral encyclopedia. You cannot burn what exists only in memory. The Babalawos carried in their minds a system of two hundred and fifty-six Odu, each containing hundreds of verses — mythology, medicine, ethics, philosophy — encoding the entirety of Yoruba cosmological knowledge.

In Cuba, the enslaved Yoruba preserved Ifá within cabildos — mutual aid societies organized by African "nations." Orishas were syncretized with Catholic saints: Changó with Santa Bárbara, Yemojá with the Virgin of Regla. The syncretism was a survival strategy, not a theological compromise. By the time of the Cuban Revolution in 1959, approximately two hundred active Babalawos practiced on the island. By the 1990s, they numbered in the tens of thousands. UNESCO recognized the Ifá divination system as a Masterpiece of the Oral and Intangible Heritage of Humanity in 2005.

The I Ching survived through bureaucratic classification. Ifá survived through the irreducible capacity of human memory. Both strategies exploited the grammar's defining property: its finitude. Sixty-four hexagrams can be memorized. Two hundred and fifty-six Odu can be held in a trained mind. The survival of grammars is a function of their compactness — their capacity to encode maximum meaning in minimum structure.

· · ·

Tarot survived through a third mechanism: structural openness. There was no tarot author to execute, no tarot scripture to burn, no tarot church to dismantle. The seventy-eight-card deck originated as an Italian card game in the 1440s — luxury hand-painted commissions for the Visconti and Sforza dynasties of Milan. For over three hundred years, no occult significance was attached to the cards, nor any use made of them save for playing games. Michael Dummett, Oxford's Wykeham Professor of Logic, established this conclusively in his six-hundred-page The Game of Tarot.

The deck survived because card games propagate through use. They spread horizontally, through play, without requiring institutional support. When the meaning-system was later layered onto the cards — first by Court de Gébelin in 1781, then by Lévi, the Golden Dawn, Waite, and others — the physical infrastructure of seventy-eight cards was already ubiquitous across Europe. The container preceded the content.

And when Pamela Colman Smith illustrated the Rider-Waite-Smith deck in 1909, creating full pictorial narrative scenes for every card — an innovation no prior deck had attempted — she made the grammar self-interpreting. You no longer needed an expert to read the cards. The images themselves told stories. This reduced the need for institutional mediation and accelerated the deck's spread. Over one hundred million copies have been sold.

Each grammar's survival strategy teaches a different lesson. The I Ching: classification determines survival — be useful to the powerful, even if your true significance lies elsewhere. Ifá: orality is resilient — what lives in memory cannot be confiscated. Tarot: structural openness is its own defense — a system with no center cannot be decapitated.

Together, they demonstrate that the grammars which endure are those that can be compressed into the human body: memorized, carried, played, practiced. The manuscript can be hidden in a wall or burned in a palace. The oral tradition can cross an ocean in the mind of a single practitioner. The card deck can travel in a pocket. The grammar that depends on infrastructure — on temples, institutions, state support — is the grammar most vulnerable to destruction. The grammar that depends only on human beings is the grammar that survives.

Chapter Three

Capture

In 1781, Antoine Court de Gébelin, a French Protestant pastor and Freemason, published an essay claiming that tarot was a remnant of the ancient Egyptian Book of Thoth — sacred priestly wisdom encoded in symbolic images. He claimed "Tarot" derived from Egyptian words Tar ("path") and Ro ("royal"). He had no evidence for any of this. The Rosetta Stone would not be discovered for another eighteen years. Jean-François Champollion would not decipher hieroglyphics until the 1820s. Court de Gébelin could not read a single word of Egyptian. Later Egyptologists found nothing in the Egyptian language to support his etymologies.

The myth stuck because it was published in a prestigious work whose subscribers included Louis XVI.

What followed was a century of elaborate invention built on this fabrication. Éliphas Lévi linked the twenty-two Major Arcana to the twenty-two letters of the Hebrew alphabet and the Kabbalistic Tree of Life — despite never formally studying Hebrew and making frequent grammatical errors. The Hermetic Order of the Golden Dawn synthesized tarot, Kabbalah, astrology, alchemy, Hermeticism, and Egyptian mythology into a single comprehensive initiatory system. And in 1909, Arthur Edward Waite commissioned Pamela Colman Smith to illustrate the most widely used tarot deck in history.

Each generation of occultists built upon the fabrications of the last.

· · ·

Court de Gébelin's fabrication was not merely a historical error. It was an act of what Edward Said would later theorize as Orientalism: an "Orient" that exists to be decoded, systematized, and possessed by European intellects. The timing is telling. His claims appeared seventeen years before Napoleon's Egyptian campaign, during a period of intense European colonial interest in Egypt. As Elliott Colla argues, "the genesis of western Egyptology went hand in hand with European imperialism."

Court de Gébelin created what amounts to a colonial knowledge loop: Egyptian wisdom was claimed as the origin of tarot, but Europeans — not Egyptians — were positioned as the rightful interpreters and custodians of that wisdom. The colonized provide the raw material. The colonizer provides the framework. Authority flows in one direction.

The colonial dimensions compound through the nineteenth century. Lévi's appropriation of Kabbalah extracted Jewish mystical tradition from its communal, hermeneutic context and reframed it within a universalized Christian-Hermetic framework. As Boaz Huss documents, "mysticism is a modern discursive structure that was created within the context of European colonialism." The transformation from Kabbalah — a living Jewish practice of receiving and tradition — to Hermetic Qabalah — a Western occult system — exemplifies what Boaventura de Sousa Santos calls "epistemicide": the systematic restructuring of other knowledge systems to conform to European frameworks.

Helena Blavatsky's Theosophical Society made the racial logic explicit. Her Secret Doctrine elaborated a theory of "root races" positioning the "Aryan race" as humanity's highest current form. Julian Strube's landmark article demonstrated that race was central to Theosophical doctrine yet has been consistently overlooked by most historians. Leading Theosophists were, as Gauri Viswanathan has stressed, "convinced that British colonization was necessary for the realization of universal brotherhood."

Strube's broader argument is decisive: "if occultism is modern, it is inherently intertwined with colonialism," reflecting modernity's rhetoric that "marginalizes all other epistemes that do not fit with the principles that aspire to build a 'totality of knowledge.'"

· · ·

The story of Pamela Colman Smith crystallizes these dynamics in a single biography. She created the illustrations for the Rider-Waite-Smith deck — every one of the seventy-eight cards, full narrative pictorial scenes, in approximately six months. Her innovation was revolutionary: prior tarot pip cards showed only abstract arrangements of suit symbols. Smith created full human narrative scenes for every card, making the deck immensely more readable and intuitive.

She was paid a flat fee. She received no royalties, no copyright, and no credit. The deck was named for the publisher (Rider) and the commissioner (Waite). She died penniless in Bude, Cornwall, in 1951.

Whether racialized, gendered, or both, her erasure reproduces precisely the colonial dynamic by which the labor of the marginalized creates value that accrues to the privileged.

The broader pattern — tarot's incorporation of Kabbalah, astrology, alchemy, Egyptian mythology, and Hermeticism into a single hierarchical system — mirrors what Walter Mignolo identifies as modernity's "totality of knowledge" project. The same impulse that produced the encyclopedia, the botanical classification system, and the ethnographic museum: the claim to contain all the world's knowledge traditions within a single European framework. The Golden Dawn's Book T, which assigns each card to a Hebrew letter, astrological sign, color, divine name, and position on the Tree of Life, is a miniature colonial encyclopedia of the world's esoteric traditions, reorganized under British authority.

· · ·

Here is the structural thesis of this chapter, and it is the hinge on which the entire book turns: the same openness that allowed grammars to survive is what made them available for colonial extraction.

A revealed tradition — one with an author-prophet, a scripture, an institutional church — is harder to colonize. You must contend with the authority that guards it. But an authorless system, a grammar with no center, no pope, no orthodoxy — this is maximally vulnerable to capture. Anyone can project anything onto it, and no one can say they are wrong.

Court de Gébelin projected Egypt onto Italian playing cards because there was no tarot authority to stop him. Lévi projected Kabbalah because there was no tarot tradition to contradict him. The Golden Dawn built their comprehensive synthesis because the cards, being authorless, offered no resistance.

This is not a reason to close the grammars. It is a description of the risk that comes with openness. And as the next chapter will show, the same structural feature that enabled colonial capture is precisely what enables liberation.

Chapter Four

Liberation

Here is the turn.

Tarot has no author in the religious sense. Unlike the Bible, the Quran, or the Vedas, it claims no divine revelation, no prophetic authority, no institutional church. Unlike initiated traditions like Freemasonry or the Golden Dawn themselves, it requires no initiation, no lineage, no master. It is, in Calvino's formulation, "a machine for constructing stories" — a combinatorial semiotic system whose meanings emerge through juxtaposition and interpretation, not doctrine.

This structural openness has profound consequences. Because the esoteric meaning layered onto tarot was fabricated rather than revealed, it carries no binding authority. There is no tarot pope, no tarot scripture, no tarot orthodoxy that cannot be contested.

And so the same structural absence that allowed Court de Gébelin to project his Orientalist fantasies onto seventy-eight Italian playing cards also allows Christopher Marmolejo to resituate those cards within Aztec cosmology. It allows Cristy C. Road to remake them as queer punk revolution. It allows Jessica Dore to read them through dialectical behavior therapy. It allows anyone who picks up the cards to become an author of meaning.

· · ·

The live academic debate staged in the Anthropology of Consciousness journal between 2023 and 2024 puts the question precisely: can the master's tools dismantle the master's house?

Mike Sosteric's critique is structurally compelling. He argues that tarot's archetypal imagery — the hierarchical structures of Emperor, Hierophant, the rigid social order encoded in the court cards — normalizes and naturalizes power structures. "The master's archetypes from the master's picture book are so deeply ingrained" that using them "reinforces the archetype no matter how much you might resist."

Yvan Greenberg's response argues that tarot divination can serve as a "decolonizing methodology" by healing "Cartesian mental models that underlie modernity's hold on our society." He critiques approaches that treat tarot imagery as "universal archetypes" or follow keyword lists as adhering to "the colonial need to catalog and categorize." The decolonial move is not to abandon tarot but to read it differently — to refuse the systematizer's authority.

Marmolejo's Red Tarot performs exactly this refusal. Drawing on Paulo Freire's critical pedagogy and bell hooks, he treats tarot reading as critical literacy: a means of subverting dominant power structures by resituating tarot imagery within non-Western cosmologies. His Death card works through the Hindu goddess Chhinnamasta. His High Priestess through the Aztec goddess Coyolxauhqui. The cards are the same. The projections are different. And because there is no tarot authority to declare his projections illegitimate, they stand.

· · ·

Rachel Pollack, a trans woman who came out in 1971 — the same year she discovered tarot — authored Seventy-Eight Degrees of Wisdom, the foundational modern tarot text. Her insight was that tarot offers LGBTQ+ people "a spirituality that we are otherwise shut out of," one that "is interactive, and relies on the very same journey of self-discovery and realisation that is built into the queer experience."

Jessica Dore, a licensed social worker, integrates tarot with dialectical behavior therapy, acceptance and commitment therapy, and mindfulness practice. Her Tarot for Change effectively decolonizes tarot from its occultist gatekeeping while retaining its symbolic power — making it a tool for autonomous self-interrogation rather than initiated expertise.

The emerging HCI scholarship positions tarot not as archaic mysticism but as a cutting-edge technology of imagination. Hong-An Wu locates tarot at "the worldmaking juncture where anti-oppression meets technological innovation." Lustig and Wu center tarot among QTBIPOC communities as "an evocative technology to think with and through." Stuart Candy and Jeff Watson's The Thing From The Future, developed at Carnegie Mellon, translates tarot's combinatorial logic into an explicit futures methodology — used at the UN Development Programme, IDEO, MIT Media Lab, and Stanford's d.School.

Aaron Talley frames tarot as a technology for Black futures: "For Black people, the future has never been promised… Tarot can predict the future; it also reminds us that we deserve futures in the first place."

· · ·

The framing that emerges is not "tarot despite its colonial history" but tarot because of its structural openness, which is the same feature that enabled its colonial capture.

A revealed tradition cannot be so easily colonized — but neither can it be so easily liberated. Tarot's lack of institutional authority is both its vulnerability (anyone can project anything onto it) and its emancipatory power (no one can claim final authority over it). This is the recursive logic: the same absence of foundation that made tarot available for colonial fabrication makes it available for decolonial reinvention.

"Gateway building, not gatekeeping" is not merely a slogan. It is a description of tarot's actual ontological structure.

And it is the design principle for everything that follows.

Chapter Five

The Field

"The road up and the road down are one and the same."— Heraclitus

In 1901, Bertrand Russell showed that the "set of all sets" cannot exist without contradiction. Consider the set R of all sets that are not members of themselves. Is R a member of itself? If yes, then by definition it shouldn't be. If no, then by definition it should be. The binary question — member or not? — has no valid answer at the level of totality. The very framing breaks down.

This is not an isolated quirk. Cantor's paradox showed the set of all sets would need to be both smaller than and equal to its own power set. Each paradox demonstrates the same principle: the whole has fundamentally different properties from its elements. A collection of teaspoons is not itself a teaspoon. But the collection of all sets tries to be a set — and collapses into contradiction.

The mathematical resolution reinforces the philosophical point. Zermelo-Fraenkel set theory resolved the paradox by denying the universal set exists as a set at all — it is a "proper class," an entity of a different logical type than its members. Both solutions impose hierarchy: you cannot flatten all levels of reality into a single undifferentiated plane. The whole belongs to a different category than its parts.

· · ·

The literal "cosmic topology" of this chapter provides the most vivid scientific illustration. The Planck mission's final results measured the universe's curvature parameter as consistent with perfect flatness. But a flat universe could be infinite — like an endless plane — or finite — like a three-dimensional torus, where space wraps around on itself. The flatness you measure in your neighborhood tells you nothing about whether the universe is finite or infinite.

The parts cannot determine the nature of the whole.

The leading candidate for cosmic topology, the Poincaré dodecahedral space, is a positively curved, finite space whose volume is one hundred and twenty times smaller than the hypersphere of equal curvature radius. Colloquially, it's "soccer-ball shaped." In such a universe, light wraps around space creating "topological mirages" — multiple images of the same cosmic sources, like a hall of mirrors. The method of detection is itself topological: you can only infer the whole from characteristic signatures that no local measurement provides.

This challenges binary thinking directly. The question "Is the universe finite or infinite?" may itself be ill-posed without specifying the topology. A flat torus is finite yet has no boundary — combining properties that seem contradictory from a binary perspective.

· · ·

Christopher Wallis, who holds an MPhil from Oxford and PhD from Berkeley in Sanskrit, provides the metaphysical architecture for what the mathematics and the physics point toward. His teaching, drawn from Abhinavagupta's Tantraloka, is that svātantrya-śakti — the Power of Autonomy — is the one fundamental quality of Consciousness, and it belongs exclusively to the whole, not to individual parts.

From Abhinavagupta: "He has only one fundamental quality, which implies all the others. Therefore, the truly correct precept with regard to Śiva is that He is united exclusively with the Power of Autonomy."

This does not mean the individual is an illusion. It means the individual is the whole in contracted form. The same five powers that characterize the whole — awareness, bliss, will, knowledge, and action — exist in the individual but in limited, contracted versions. The path of pratyabhijñā — "recognition" — is the individual discovering that its apparent freedom was always the freedom of the whole expressing through it.

Wallis's nonduality explicitly includes duality rather than negating it: "Instead of a nondual view that excludes duality and says duality is wrong, this is a nondual view that includes duality, as a valid level of experience, though not an absolute one." The third way does not eliminate the two poles but reveals them as expressions of a unity that encompasses both.

· · ·

The kriya/karma distinction operationalizes this. In Nondual Shaiva Tantra, kriyā-śakti is one of the five powers of absolute Consciousness — "the Power of Acting." When this power operates through the contracted individual self — the ego believing itself to be a separate agent — the result is karma: bound, effortful action that creates impressions and binds awareness to the past. When the same power operates through an individual who has "recognized" their identity with the whole, the result is kriya: spontaneous, frictionless, divinely-aligned action that does not bind.

Parker Palmer reaches the same structural insight from within the Quaker tradition. His core thesis: "Before you tell your life what you intend to do with it, listen for what it intends to do with you." Palmer's concept of "functional atheism" — the belief that ultimate responsibility for everything rests with us, "a conviction held even by people who talk a good game about God" — is karma dressed in Western clothes. The ego believes it is the sole agent and must control outcomes. Palmer's alternative — listening to what life wants to do through you — is kriya in Quaker language.

Paul Tillich provides the theological framework. His "God above God" transcends the theism/atheism binary outright. He critiques "theological theism" — God conceived as "a being beside others" — as the very thing that generates justified atheism: "This is the God Nietzsche said had to be killed because nobody can tolerate being made into a mere object of absolute knowledge and absolute control." The God above God is being-itself — the ground, power, and abyss of being that transcends the subject-object structure entirely.

This is not agnosticism. It is recognizing that both theism and atheism operate within a binary that the ground of being transcends.

· · ·

At least seven major traditions converge on the same insight: opposites can only be opposites because they share the same fundamental nature. Hot and cold are both temperatures. Light and dark are both degrees of luminosity. Up and down are both directions on the same road.

Heraclitus articulated this first: "The road up and the road down are one and the same." Nicholas of Cusa gave it the Latin name coincidentia oppositorum. Carl Jung made it the central principle of his psychology — individuation means integrating conscious and unconscious, persona and shadow. Tantric philosophy teaches that Shiva and Shakti "are never apart." Taoism encodes it in the taijitu symbol — the dot of white within black and black within white. Hegel's dialectic formalizes how thesis and antithesis generate synthesis through Aufhebung.

Every tradition names the same insight differently — Logos, God, the Self, Brahman, the Tao, Absolute Spirit — but the structure is identical: the whole is not one pole or the other but the field on which both poles arise.

This is how grammars work. The I Ching does not tell you whether to act or wait. It shows you the dynamic configuration in which both acting and waiting arise. Tarot does not resolve your dilemma. It reveals the field of forces within which your dilemma is situated. Ifá does not predict your fate. It maps the Odu that describes the quality of the present moment — and prescribes the ebo that might transform it.

The grammars work not by resolving opposites but by revealing the field that generates them.

This is not prediction. It is topology.

Chapter Six

The Mirror

John Gottman's research at the University of Washington — forty years, three thousand couples, cameras measuring everything from facial expressions to heart rate — produced a single finding that should govern the design of every tool meant to help human beings relate to each other: sixty-nine percent of relationship conflicts are perpetual problems that never get resolved.

This held equally for happy and struggling couples. The difference was entirely in how they managed them.

If sixty-nine percent of conflicts are perpetual between two people who love each other, then permanent disagreement in diverse societies is not a bug but a defining characteristic of shared life. The purpose of a dialogue tool — or a divination tool — is not resolving disagreements but teaching people to hold them.

· · ·

Divination systems liberate when they shift the locus of control inward. Paradoxically, the best research shows that deeply involved practitioners of divination tend toward internal locus of control, not external. Serious practitioners use divination as a tool for self-knowledge and strategic action, not passive fate-acceptance. The explanation: when you sit with a card or a hexagram long enough to generate your own interpretation, you are exercising the muscle of self-reflection. The randomness of the draw disrupts habitual thought. The finitude of the vocabulary constrains the field of interpretation. The requirement for personal meaning-making builds interpretive capacity.

Tarot functions as what cognitive behavioral therapy calls a reframing tool: the random card disrupts the querent's habitual narrative by introducing an image that was not already in their cognitive repertoire. Jessica Dore maps this explicitly: the Two of Swords becomes a lesson in "willingness," the Tower an opportunity to practice "radical acceptance," the Wheel of Fortune an illustration of impermanence. The mechanism is straightforward. The card does not tell you what to think. It gives you something new to think with.

From a Jungian perspective, tarot images function as entry points for dialogue with the unconscious. When a querent encounters The Emperor, they do not receive a fixed message about authority. They enter into a dialogue with whatever authority means in their particular psychic constellation. The archetype is a process, not a content.

As a projective technique, tarot is structurally identical to the Rorschach or the Thematic Apperception Test: complex, polysemous visual scenes that can be interpreted in multiple ways. The meaning resides in the reader, not the text.

· · ·

Divination systems enslave when they concentrate interpretive authority outside the querent.

The Barnum/Forer effect — accepting vague, generally applicable statements as personally meaningful — is well-documented. When users cannot distinguish between genuine insight and generic statements, they become dependent on the system for self-knowledge they could generate independently. When a reader or app claims privileged access to the cards' "true" meaning, the interpretive openness that makes divination valuable collapses into dogma. The reader becomes a gatekeeper rather than a gateway builder.

John Welwood's concept of spiritual bypassing applies directly: when tarot becomes a way of avoiding difficult emotions, decisions, or conversations — "the cards said I should wait" — it functions as avoidance behavior. And Sosteric's critique has force: the hierarchical imagery of the traditional deck — Emperor, Hierophant, the court's rigid social order — can normalize and naturalize power structures even as the reader believes they are transcending them.

· · ·

The hinge between liberation and bondage turns on a single variable: who holds interpretive authority.

Systems that concentrate authority in the reader, the app, or the tradition tend toward bondage. Systems that distribute authority to the querent — inviting them to make meaning rather than receive it — tend toward liberation.

This is the design principle for Jongu. The platform should be a mirror, not an oracle. It should hold your question without answering it. It should provide the grammar — the finite vocabulary, the combinatorial syntax, the technology of randomness — and then step back. The meaning is yours to make.

This is not a design choice made from philosophical preference. It is made from the evidence. Gottman's sixty-nine percent teaches that most of life's essential tensions are permanent. The contemplative traditions teach that sitting with tension — rather than resolving it — is the practice. The psychology of divination teaches that the value lies in the exercise of interpretation, not in the interpretation itself.

A mirror does not tell you who you are. It shows you yourself, and you decide what to make of it.

Chapter Seven

The Living Cosmos

The Greek word for the zodiac — zoidia, from ζῷον, zōion — means "living thing." The same root gives us "animal" and "animism." The zodiac is literally the "circle of living beings."

When modern astrology reduced planets to psychological functions — Mars equals aggression, Venus equals love — it performed a disenchantment, stripping the cosmos of its aliveness. The recovery of this aliveness is happening now, across several streams, and it matters for the argument of this book because it transforms what a grammar can be.

Jyotish — the "eye of the Vedas" — never lost its living relationship with planetary beings. The Sanskrit word Graha means "to seize or grasp," reflecting how these forces influence human tendencies. But the Grahas are not impersonal forces. They are devas — beings with consciousness, agency, and the power to deliver karmic results. Traditional relationship with them develops through mantra, gemstone, fasting, charity, and devotion. This is not metaphorical. In the Vedic framework, chanting the beeja mantra of the Sun one hundred and eight times on a Sunday morning is a relational act — an address to a living intelligence, not a psychological exercise.

Each of the twenty-seven Nakshatras possesses a specific Shakti — a power. Ashwini carries the power to quickly heal. Bharani carries the power of transformation through death and rebirth. Krittika carries the power to burn and purify. Each Shakti has a "basis above," a "basis below," and a "result" forming a triadic structure that maps onto tantric cosmology.

· · ·

The most explicit bridge between astrology and tantra runs through the Dashamahavidya — the Ten Great Wisdom Goddesses, central to Shakta Tantra. The Todala Tantra assigns each goddess to a specific Graha: Kali governs Saturn, Tara governs Jupiter, Tripura Sundari governs Mercury, Bhuvaneshvari governs Moon, Chinnamasta governs Rahu, Dhumavati governs Ketu, Bagalamukhi governs Mars, Matangi governs Sun, Kamala governs Venus. Worship of the corresponding Mahavidya is prescribed as a tantric remedial measure for planetary afflictions — qualitatively different from standard Vedic remedies because it involves full tantric sadhana.

The concept of Kala — both "time" and "death" — may be the deepest bridge. Mahakala, Great Time, is a form of Shiva representing eternity beyond cyclic time. Mahakali is his Shakti counterpart. Manifest time — kala — is what Jyotish maps. Unmanifest time — Mahakala — is what tantric realization aims to penetrate. The astrologer reads the clock. The tantric practitioner asks who is doing the reading.

· · ·

Christopher Wallis's non-dual Shaiva Tantra offers a framing that no other platform attempts. In the Pratyabhijña tradition, all phenomena — including planetary influences — are expressions of Cit-Shakti, consciousness-power. The planets are not external forces controlling us but aspects of awareness to recognize within ourselves. Practice becomes pratyabhijñā — recognition — rather than propitiation.

This framing resolves a tension that plagues most digital astrology: the fine line between insight and superstition. By grounding Jyotish in non-dual philosophy — where "the planets in the sky" and "the planets in consciousness" are recognized as non-different — a platform can offer genuine depth without the ethical problems of fortune-telling. The grammars become contemplative mirrors rather than predictive instruments.

And this is not mysticism requiring special belief. It is a phenomenological claim about the structure of experience — compatible with Bohm's implicate order, with Merleau-Ponty's embodied perception, with Thomas Csordas's "somatic modes of attention." The claim is simple: when you attend to Saturn — in your chart, in the sky, in your experience of limitation and discipline and time — something responds. Not because Saturn is a god beaming influence at you. But because consciousness, attending to a quality of itself, recognizes what was always already there.

Grammars are not dead symbol systems. They are invitations to enter into living relationship with a responsive cosmos. Whether you frame that response as divine agency, as synchronicity, as the projective capacity of the human psyche, or as the implicate order unfolding into the explicate — the phenomenological experience is the same. You cast. Something is cast back. You attend. Something attends to you.

The grammar is the space where the meeting happens.

Chapter Eight

The Commons

Marsha Linehan developed the biosocial hypothesis — and the dialectical framework that grew from it — not from ideology but because it was effective, fit the data, and was compassionate. She had tried everything with patients who were suicidal, who had been failed by every existing treatment. Cognitive behavioral therapy alone wasn't enough. Psychodynamic therapy alone wasn't enough. She arrived at dialectics — holding opposites in creative tension, accepting reality while simultaneously working to change it — because the people in front of her needed it. Three criteria guided her: Does it work? Does the evidence support it? Does it reduce suffering?

I want to apply those three criteria to a different problem. AI has arrived. Social structures are fraying. People are drowning in digital consumption but starving for authentic creation and connection. We need to build culture faster — not the culture of content and consumption, but the culture of meaning-making, of contemplative practice, of people creating together rather than consuming alone.

What organizational form is effective, fits the evidence, and is compassionate?

· · ·

Effective.

The evidence that open, community-maintained infrastructure outperforms closed alternatives at scale is now overwhelming. Linux runs virtually every server on the internet, every Android phone, one hundred percent of the world's top five hundred supercomputers. Android powers roughly 3.9 billion active devices. Wikipedia is the world's encyclopedia. Ghost — the publishing platform founded by John O'Nolan as a nonprofit under MIT license — generates nearly nine million dollars annually with no investors, no owners, zero percent transaction fees, and all revenue reinvested. Supabase, built entirely from open-source components, reached a five billion dollar valuation in five years. The pattern repeats: when knowledge is shared, it multiplies.

The key structural insight, documented by researchers analyzing forty-four open-source developer tools, is that business model choice predicts commercial outcomes more reliably than community adoption metrics. Projects controlling service-layer infrastructure achieve valuations orders of magnitude higher than community-funded alternatives with comparable community engagement. Control of distribution and operational infrastructure matters more than control of code.

This is the open-core model: open-source the foundation, sustain the platform through hosted services. Ghost does this. Supabase does this. The foundation is free and modifiable. The service layer generates revenue. The mission is protected by the legal structure — a nonprofit foundation, in Ghost's case, that legally prevents being sold, pivoted, or pressured by shareholders.

· · ·

Fits the evidence.

In 2008, the anthropologist Christopher Kelty published Two Bits: The Cultural Significance of Free Software — itself under a Creative Commons license, embodying the principles it described. Kelty's central theoretical contribution was the concept of the recursive public:

A recursive public is a public that is vitally concerned with the material and practical maintenance and modification of the technical, legal, practical, and conceptual means of its own existence as a public; it is a collective independent of other forms of constituted power and is capable of speaking to existing forms of power through the production of actually existing alternatives.

What distinguishes a recursive public from an interest group, a corporation, a church, or a movement is its focus on the radical modifiability of its own terms of existence. The community does not just use the medium to communicate. It creates and maintains that medium as a form of political action.

Kelty identified five interlocking practices that constitute free software as a recursive public: sharing source code, conceiving open systems, writing copyright licenses, coordinating collaboration, and proselytizing. Each practice has its own history, its own tensions, its own experiments. Together they form what Kelty calls "a system of thresholds, not of classification" — an experimental system whose infrastructure is the Internet and whose hypotheses concern the reorientation of knowledge and power.

Kelty's concept was developed to describe software. But he anticipated its extension. In Part III of Two Bits, he examined Creative Commons and the Connexions open textbook project as "modulations" of free software — instances where the five practices were adopted and transformed for new domains. He asked: do these modulations maintain the orientation of a recursive public? Do they extend the commitment to availability and modifiability into education, scholarship, cultural production?

I am asking the same question for contemplative practice.

· · ·

The five components, modulated:

Sharing grammars modulates sharing source code. The grammars — tarot decks, I Ching hexagrams, Nakshatras, Dhammapada verses, children's stories, wellness exercises — are the "source code" of Jongu. They are created by people, not algorithms. They are shareable, forkable, modifiable. Anyone can create a grammar. Anyone can contribute to an existing one. The platform makes every grammar publicly and freely available.

Conceiving openness in sacred systems modulates conceiving open systems. This is the hardest modulation. "Open" means something different when the content is a Yoruba divination system transmitted through initiated lineages than when it is a software protocol. The CARE Principles for Indigenous Data Governance — Collective Benefit, Authority to Control, Responsibility, Ethics — provide the ethical framework. Some grammars are structurally open (tarot, I Ching) and belong in the commons. Others are initiated, oral, and lineage-dependent (Ifá) and should be represented on the platform only with the explicit involvement and authority of tradition-holders. The principle is: gateway building, not gatekeeping — but the gateway must be built with the community's consent.

Writing the license modulates writing copyright licenses. Creative Commons Attribution-ShareAlike 4.0 International. The SA — ShareAlike — is the contemplative equivalent of copyleft. Anyone who builds on the grammars must share their derivatives under the same terms. The freedom propagates. The code for the platform itself is open-source. The content created by the community is commons. What is proprietary is nothing.

Coordinating co-creation modulates coordinating collaboration. The Discord server. The blog. The community voting on features, contributing tools, creating channels. The Jongo dance as metaphor — participants taking turns leading and supporting, individual expression within a collective rhythm. The design principle from the structured disagreement research: not resolving conflicts but building the capacity to hold them. Polis's insight that removing the reply button and surfacing consensus transforms discourse from combat to inquiry.

The movement modulates proselytizing. This book. The blog posts. The landing page. The argument — not for a specific metaphysics, but for a structural claim: humans need non-dogmatic, non-revealed symbolic systems to make meaning, and these systems should be commons, not commodities. The argument is made not through rhetoric but through the production of actually existing alternatives. The platform exists. The grammars are available. The community is forming. The proof is in the practice.

· · ·

Compassionate.

The alternative is the attention economy. Co-Star sending push notifications designed to generate engagement anxiety. Headspace charging seventy dollars a year for access to meditation — a practice that has been freely transmitted for twenty-five hundred years. Labyrinthos gamifying tarot into streaks and achievement badges. The three-hundred-and-seventy-six-billion-dollar spiritual services industry extracting value from people's desire for meaning.

A commons is compassionate because it makes meaning-making infrastructure available to everyone — not as a product to consume but as a practice to participate in. A ten-dollar tool feels very different to someone in Brazil than to someone in Silicon Valley. When access isn't tied to purchasing power, people can focus on genuine contribution rather than financial transactions.

Jongu's architecture already embodies this. No ads. No algorithm. No notifications. No tracking. Privacy by design — you can use every tool without creating an account. Optional AI — assistance is always opt-in, never required. Donation-sustained access — one hundred percent free, sustained by voluntary contribution. Everything exportable, everything deletable, everything yours.

The platform does not monetize attention. It does not gamify practice. It does not claim authority over your experience. It holds your question without answering it.

· · ·

Kelty quotes Creative Commons with the phrase "If we succeed, we will disappear." The idea is that if the licenses work — if they become the default infrastructure through which culture is shared — then the organization that created them becomes invisible. The licenses are just there, like plumbing.

The same applies to Jongu. The goal is not to build a company. The goal is not even to build a platform. The goal is to build an infrastructure that becomes so fundamental it becomes invisible — the way Wikipedia became invisible, the way Linux became invisible, the way the protocols that run the Internet became invisible. Not noticed, not branded, not owned. Just there. Available. Modifiable. Maintained by the community that uses it.

Kelty called this "adaptability over planning." The commitment to remain modifiable — to build in the capacity to be changed by the community — rather than to plan a fixed product and defend it. This is the opposite of how platforms are normally built. Normally you plan the product, build it in secret, launch it, defend it, scale it, sell it. The recursive public does something different. It builds the infrastructure in public. It invites modification. It treats every version as a draft. It privileges the community's capacity to adapt over the founder's capacity to plan.

This is also, I realize, what the grammars themselves do. The I Ching has been continuously modified for three thousand years — from oracle bones to the Ten Wings to Wang Bi's commentary to Zhu Xi's reconstruction to the Wilhelm-Baynes translation to whatever version you cast this morning. Tarot has been continuously modified since 1440 — from Visconti-Sforza to Court de Gébelin to the Golden Dawn to Pamela Colman Smith to Rachel Pollack to Cristy C. Road to whoever designs the next deck tomorrow. Ifá has been continuously modified through every Babalawo who commits a new verse to memory and every diaspora community that adapts the practice to new circumstances.

The grammars are recursive publics. They always have been. They are communities of practitioners who build, maintain, and modify the very symbolic infrastructure through which they make meaning. What changes now is the speed and scale at which this can happen. AI makes it possible to create a new grammar — a new tarot deck, a new set of contemplative prompts, a new children's story — in hours rather than years. The platform makes it possible to share that grammar with everyone on earth, instantly, for free.

The hypothesis is that this acceleration is a gift, not a threat — but only if the infrastructure is held as commons. If the infrastructure is held privately, the acceleration serves the attention economy. If it is held as commons, the acceleration serves the community.

· · ·

This is an experiment. It might work. It might not. But the hypothesis is clear, the evidence is substantial, and the need is urgent.

Grammars have survived millennia through compactness, openness, and the irreducible participation of practitioners. AI is here, and we need to build culture faster. The recursive public is the form that fits. The commons is the license that protects. The grammars are the source code.

Everything else is practice.
