# Chapter 6: Tarot's Double Life

She drew every card. Seventy-eight full pictorial narrative scenes in approximately six months — an innovation no prior tarot deck had attempted. Where earlier pip cards showed only abstract arrangements of suit symbols, Pamela Colman Smith created full human stories for every card, making the deck immensely more readable, more intuitive, more alive.

She was paid a flat fee. She received no royalties, no copyright, and no credit. The deck was named for the publisher (Rider) and the commissioner (Waite). She died penniless in Bude, Cornwall, in 1951.

Over one hundred million copies of her deck have been sold. Her name was largely forgotten until scholars began recovering it in the twenty-first century. Whether the erasure was racialized, gendered, or both — Smith was of mixed heritage and never married — it reproduces precisely the colonial dynamic by which the labor of the marginalized creates value that accrues to the privileged. The most widely used tarot deck in history was drawn by a woman whose contribution was made invisible by the men who commissioned it.

This is the story of tarot in miniature: a system that generates extraordinary value while displacing the people who create that value. A system whose openness is simultaneously its greatest vulnerability and its greatest power. A system that lives a double life — captured and liberated, again and again, by the same structural feature.

---

In 1781, Antoine Court de Gébelin, a French Protestant pastor and Freemason, published an essay claiming that tarot was a remnant of the ancient Egyptian Book of Thoth — sacred priestly wisdom encoded in symbolic images. He claimed "Tarot" derived from Egyptian words *Tar* ("path") and *Ro* ("royal"). He had no evidence for any of this. The Rosetta Stone would not be discovered for another eighteen years. Jean-François Champollion would not decipher hieroglyphics until the 1820s. Court de Gébelin could not read a single word of Egyptian. Later Egyptologists found nothing in the Egyptian language to support his etymologies.

The myth stuck because it was published in a prestigious work whose subscribers included Louis XVI.

What followed was a century of elaborate invention built on this fabrication. Éliphas Lévi linked the twenty-two Major Arcana to the twenty-two letters of the Hebrew alphabet and the Kabbalistic Tree of Life — despite never formally studying Hebrew and making frequent grammatical errors. The Hermetic Order of the Golden Dawn synthesized tarot, Kabbalah, astrology, alchemy, Hermeticism, and Egyptian mythology into a single comprehensive initiatory system. And in 1909, Arthur Edward Waite commissioned Smith to illustrate the deck that would carry his name but not hers.

Each generation of occultists built upon the fabrications of the last.

---

Court de Gébelin's fabrication was not merely a historical error. It was an act of what Edward Said would later theorize as Orientalism: an "Orient" that exists to be decoded, systematized, and possessed by European intellects. His claims appeared seventeen years before Napoleon's Egyptian campaign, during a period of intense European colonial interest in Egypt. As Elliott Colla argues, "the genesis of western Egyptology went hand in hand with European imperialism."

Court de Gébelin created a colonial knowledge loop: Egyptian wisdom was claimed as the origin of tarot, but Europeans — not Egyptians — were positioned as the rightful interpreters and custodians of that wisdom. The colonized provide the raw material. The colonizer provides the framework. Authority flows in one direction.

The colonial dimensions compound through the nineteenth century. Lévi's appropriation of Kabbalah extracted Jewish mystical tradition from its communal, hermeneutic context and reframed it within a universalized Christian-Hermetic framework. As Boaz Huss documents, "mysticism is a modern discursive structure that was created within the context of European colonialism." Helena Blavatsky's Theosophical Society made the racial logic explicit. Her *Secret Doctrine* elaborated a theory of "root races" positioning the "Aryan race" as humanity's highest current form. Julian Strube's landmark article demonstrated that race was central to Theosophical doctrine yet has been consistently overlooked by most historians.

Strube's broader argument is decisive: "if occultism is modern, it is inherently intertwined with colonialism," reflecting modernity's rhetoric that "marginalizes all other epistemes that do not fit with the principles that aspire to build a 'totality of knowledge.'"

The Golden Dawn's Book T — which assigns each tarot card to a Hebrew letter, astrological sign, color, divine name, and position on the Tree of Life — is a miniature colonial encyclopedia of the world's esoteric traditions, reorganized under British authority.

---

Here is the structural thesis of this chapter, and it is the hinge on which the entire book turns: the same openness that allowed grammars to survive is what made them available for colonial extraction.

A revealed tradition — one with an author-prophet, a scripture, an institutional church — is harder to colonize. You must contend with the authority that guards it. But an authorless system, a grammar with no center, no pope, no orthodoxy — this is maximally vulnerable to capture. Anyone can project anything onto it, and no one can say they are wrong.

Court de Gébelin projected Egypt onto Italian playing cards because there was no tarot authority to stop him. Lévi projected Kabbalah because there was no tarot tradition to contradict him. The Golden Dawn built their comprehensive synthesis because the cards, being authorless, offered no resistance.

This is not a reason to close the grammars. It is a description of the risk that comes with openness.

---

Here is the turn.

Tarot has no author in the religious sense. Unlike the Bible, the Quran, or the Vedas, it claims no divine revelation, no prophetic authority, no institutional church. Unlike initiated traditions like Freemasonry or the Golden Dawn themselves, it requires no initiation, no lineage, no master. It is, in Calvino's formulation, "a machine for constructing stories" — a combinatorial semiotic system whose meanings emerge through juxtaposition and interpretation, not doctrine.

This structural openness has profound consequences. Because the esoteric meaning layered onto tarot was fabricated rather than revealed, it carries no binding authority. There is no tarot pope, no tarot scripture, no tarot orthodoxy that cannot be contested.

And so the same structural absence that allowed Court de Gébelin to project his Orientalist fantasies onto seventy-eight Italian playing cards also allows Christopher Marmolejo to resituate those cards within Aztec cosmology. It allows Cristy C. Road to remake them as queer punk revolution. It allows Jessica Dore to read them through dialectical behavior therapy. It allows anyone who picks up the cards to become an author of meaning.

---

The live academic debate staged in the *Anthropology of Consciousness* journal between 2023 and 2024 puts the question precisely: can the master's tools dismantle the master's house?

Mike Sosteric's critique is structurally compelling. He argues that tarot's archetypal imagery — the hierarchical structures of Emperor, Hierophant, the rigid social order encoded in the court cards — normalizes and naturalizes power structures. "The master's archetypes from the master's picture book are so deeply ingrained" that using them "reinforces the archetype no matter how much you might resist."

Yvan Greenberg's response argues that tarot divination can serve as a "decolonizing methodology" by healing "Cartesian mental models that underlie modernity's hold on our society." The decolonial move is not to abandon tarot but to read it differently — to refuse the systematizer's authority.

Marmolejo's *Red Tarot* performs exactly this refusal. Drawing on Paulo Freire's critical pedagogy and bell hooks, he treats tarot reading as critical literacy: a means of subverting dominant power structures by resituating tarot imagery within non-Western cosmologies. His Death card works through the Hindu goddess Chhinnamasta. His High Priestess through the Aztec goddess Coyolxauhqui. The cards are the same. The projections are different. And because there is no tarot authority to declare his projections illegitimate, they stand.

Rachel Pollack, a trans woman who came out in 1971 — the same year she discovered tarot — authored *Seventy-Eight Degrees of Wisdom*, the foundational modern tarot text. Her insight: tarot offers LGBTQ+ people "a spirituality that we are otherwise shut out of," one that "is interactive, and relies on the very same journey of self-discovery and realisation that is built into the queer experience."

Jessica Dore, a licensed social worker, integrates tarot with dialectical behavior therapy, acceptance and commitment therapy, and mindfulness practice. Her *Tarot for Change* decolonizes tarot from its occultist gatekeeping while retaining its symbolic power — making it a tool for autonomous self-interrogation rather than initiated expertise.

The emerging HCI scholarship positions tarot not as archaic mysticism but as cutting-edge technology of imagination. Hong-An Wu locates tarot at "the worldmaking juncture where anti-oppression meets technological innovation." Stuart Candy and Jeff Watson's *The Thing From The Future*, developed at Carnegie Mellon, translates tarot's combinatorial logic into an explicit futures methodology — used at the UN Development Programme, IDEO, MIT Media Lab, and Stanford's d.School.

Aaron Talley frames tarot as a technology for Black futures: "For Black people, the future has never been promised… Tarot can predict the future; it also reminds us that we deserve futures in the first place."

---

The framing that emerges is not "tarot despite its colonial history" but tarot *because of* its structural openness, which is the same feature that enabled its colonial capture.

A revealed tradition cannot be so easily colonized — but neither can it be so easily liberated. Tarot's lack of institutional authority is both its vulnerability (anyone can project anything onto it) and its emancipatory power (no one can claim final authority over it). This is the recursive logic: the same absence of foundation that made tarot available for colonial fabrication makes it available for decolonial reinvention.

"Gateway building, not gatekeeping" is not merely a slogan. It is a description of tarot's actual ontological structure.

---

If a system's openness is both its vulnerability to colonial capture and its availability for liberation, what does ethical stewardship look like?

Is there a difference between a grammar that was always open — tarot, Italian playing cards that accumulated meaning through projection — and a grammar that is initiated and lineage-dependent — Ifá, transmitted through consecrated Babalawos? Where is the line between gateway building and gatekeeping? Between honoring a tradition and freezing it? Between making knowledge accessible and stripping it of its context — which is, after all, the very crisis this book began with?

These questions do not have stable answers. But they have a practice, which is what the chapters that follow explore.
