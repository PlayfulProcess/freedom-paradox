# Chapter 9: Grammars of the Living World

Marsha Linehan developed the biosocial hypothesis — and the dialectical framework that grew from it — not from ideology but because it was effective, fit the data, and was compassionate. She had tried everything with patients who were suicidal, who had been failed by every existing treatment. Cognitive behavioral therapy alone was not enough. Psychodynamic therapy alone was not enough. She arrived at dialectics — holding opposites in creative tension, accepting reality while simultaneously working to change it — because the people in front of her needed it.

Three criteria guided her: Does it work? Does the evidence support it? Does it reduce suffering?

I want to apply those three criteria to a different problem. AI has arrived. Social structures are fraying. People are drowning in digital consumption but starving for authentic creation and connection. We need to build culture faster — not the culture of content and consumption, but the culture of meaning-making, of contemplative practice, of people creating together rather than consuming alone.

What organizational form is effective, fits the evidence, and is compassionate?

---

Effective.

The evidence that open, community-maintained infrastructure outperforms closed alternatives at scale is now overwhelming. Linux runs virtually every server on the internet, every Android phone, one hundred percent of the world's top five hundred supercomputers. Wikipedia is the world's encyclopedia. Ghost — the publishing platform founded by John O'Nolan as a nonprofit under MIT license — generates nearly nine million dollars annually with no investors, no owners, zero percent transaction fees, and all revenue reinvested.

The key structural insight, documented by researchers analyzing forty-four open-source developer tools, is that control of distribution and operational infrastructure matters more than control of code. Projects controlling service-layer infrastructure achieve valuations orders of magnitude higher than community-funded alternatives with comparable community engagement.

This is the open-core model: open-source the foundation, sustain the platform through hosted services. Ghost does this. The foundation is free and modifiable. The service layer generates revenue. The mission is protected by the legal structure — a nonprofit foundation that legally prevents being sold, pivoted, or pressured by shareholders.

---

Fits the evidence.

In 2008, the anthropologist Christopher Kelty published *Two Bits: The Cultural Significance of Free Software* — itself under a Creative Commons license, embodying the principles it described. Kelty's central contribution was the concept of the recursive public:

A recursive public is a public that is vitally concerned with the material and practical maintenance and modification of the technical, legal, practical, and conceptual means of its own existence as a public. It is a collective independent of other forms of constituted power, capable of speaking to existing forms of power through the production of actually existing alternatives.

What distinguishes a recursive public from an interest group, a corporation, a church, or a movement is its focus on the radical modifiability of its own terms of existence. The community does not just use the medium to communicate. It creates and maintains that medium as a form of political action.

Kelty identified five interlocking practices: sharing source code, conceiving open systems, writing copyright licenses, coordinating collaboration, and proselytizing. He asked: do these practices extend beyond software?

I am asking the same question for contemplative practice.

---

The five components, modulated:

Sharing grammars modulates sharing source code. The grammars — tarot decks, I Ching hexagrams, Nakshatras, Dhammapada verses, children's stories, wellness exercises — are the "source code" of this platform. They are created by people, not algorithms. They are shareable, forkable, modifiable. Anyone can create a grammar. Anyone can contribute to an existing one. Everything is publicly and freely available.

Conceiving openness in sacred systems modulates conceiving open systems. This is the hardest modulation. "Open" means something different when the content is a Yoruba divination system transmitted through initiated lineages than when it is a software protocol. The CARE Principles for Indigenous Data Governance — Collective Benefit, Authority to Control, Responsibility, Ethics — provide the ethical framework. Some grammars are structurally open (tarot, I Ching) and belong in the commons. Others are initiated, oral, and lineage-dependent (Ifá) and should be represented only with the explicit involvement and authority of tradition-holders. The principle is: gateway building, not gatekeeping — but the gateway must be built with the community's consent.

Writing the license modulates writing copyright licenses. Creative Commons Attribution-ShareAlike 4.0 International. The SA — ShareAlike — is the contemplative equivalent of copyleft. Anyone who builds on the grammars must share their derivatives under the same terms. The freedom propagates. The platform code is open-source. The content created by the community is commons. What is proprietary is nothing.

Coordinating co-creation modulates coordinating collaboration. The community voting on features, contributing tools, creating together. The design principle from the structured disagreement research: not resolving conflicts but building the capacity to hold them.

The movement modulates proselytizing. This book. The argument — not for a specific metaphysics, but for a structural claim: humans need non-dogmatic, non-revealed symbolic systems to make meaning, and these systems should be commons, not commodities. The argument is made not through rhetoric but through the production of actually existing alternatives. The platform exists. The grammars are available. The community is forming. The proof is in the practice.

---

Compassionate.

The alternative is the attention economy. Co-Star sending push notifications designed to generate engagement anxiety. Headspace charging seventy dollars a year for access to meditation — a practice that has been freely transmitted for twenty-five hundred years. Labyrinthos gamifying tarot into streaks and achievement badges.

A commons is compassionate because it makes meaning-making infrastructure available to everyone — not as a product to consume but as a practice to participate in. A ten-dollar tool feels very different to someone in Brazil than to someone in Silicon Valley. When access is not tied to purchasing power, people can focus on genuine contribution rather than financial transactions.

The architecture embodies this. No ads. No algorithm. No notifications. No tracking. Privacy by design — you can use every tool without creating an account. Optional AI — assistance is always opt-in, never required. Donation-sustained access — one hundred percent free, sustained by voluntary contribution. Everything exportable, everything deletable, everything yours.

The platform does not monetize attention. It does not gamify practice. It does not claim authority over your experience. It holds your question without answering it.

---

Fred Rogers did not solve television. He showed what it looked like when the medium served the child. His design philosophy was not anti-technology. It was pro-ritual: slow pace, direct address, real-time activities, respect for the child's tempo, the deliberate inclusion of mistakes. He proved that a technological medium could serve the inner life. The question was never whether the medium was capable of care. It was whether anyone would design it that way.

Margaret McFarland, the developmental psychologist who collaborated with Rogers for thirty years, once told a visiting sculptor from Carnegie Mellon: "I don't want you to teach sculpting. All I want you to do is to love clay in front of the children." The children caught his enthusiasm. They did not need instruction. They needed someone who cared about the material in their presence.

This is the design philosophy. Not instruction. Not prediction. Not authority. Presence. The grammars are the clay. The platform is the workshop. The community is the teacher — not telling anyone what the cards mean, not predicting the future, not claiming expertise over anyone's experience. Just loving the material in front of the children.

---

Kelty quotes Creative Commons with the phrase "If we succeed, we will disappear." The idea is that if the licenses work — if they become the default infrastructure through which culture is shared — then the organization that created them becomes invisible. The licenses are just there, like plumbing.

The same applies here. The goal is not to build a company. The goal is not even to build a platform. The goal is to build an infrastructure that becomes so fundamental it becomes invisible — the way Wikipedia became invisible, the way Linux became invisible, the way the protocols that run the internet became invisible. Not noticed, not branded, not owned. Just there. Available. Modifiable. Maintained by the community that uses it.

This is also what the grammars themselves do. The I Ching has been continuously modified for three thousand years — from oracle bones to the Ten Wings to Wang Bi's commentary to the Wilhelm-Baynes translation to whatever version you cast this morning. Tarot has been continuously modified since 1440 — from Visconti-Sforza to Pamela Colman Smith to Rachel Pollack to whoever designs the next deck tomorrow. Ifá has been continuously modified through every Babalawo who commits a new verse to memory and every diaspora community that adapts the practice to new circumstances.

The grammars are recursive publics. They always have been. They are communities of practitioners who build, maintain, and modify the very symbolic infrastructure through which they make meaning. What changes now is the speed and scale at which this can happen. AI makes it possible to create a new grammar — a new tarot deck, a new set of contemplative prompts, a new children's story — in hours rather than years. The platform makes it possible to share that grammar with everyone on earth, instantly, for free.

The hypothesis is that this acceleration is a gift, not a threat — but only if the infrastructure is held as commons. If it is held privately, the acceleration serves the attention economy. If it is held as commons, the acceleration serves the community.

---

This is an experiment. It might work. It might not. But the hypothesis is clear, the evidence is substantial, and the need is urgent.

Grammars have survived millennia through compactness, openness, and the irreducible participation of practitioners. AI is here, and we need to build culture faster. The recursive public is the form that fits. The commons is the license that protects. The grammars are the source code.

Everything else is practice.
