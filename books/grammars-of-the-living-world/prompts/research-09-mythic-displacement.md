# Research Session: Mythic Displacement — When Stories Lose Their Homes

## Context
This is research for **Chapter 1** of *Grammars of the Living World*, a book about the crisis of mythic belonging and humanity's contemplative response to it. Chapter 1 is the book's opening — it must be vivid, concrete, and emotionally arresting. The reader enters through the wound.

## Core Argument to Support
Context is constitutive of meaning, not merely decorative. When narratives are severed from the lived conditions that produced them, they become dysfunctional — not false, but dislocated. We are mythically homeless not because we lack stories but because the stories we have no longer belong to the world we inhabit.

## What I Need

### 1. Hansel and Gretel as the Central Case
- The **1812 Grimm version**: the mother (not stepmother) agrees to abandon the children. What exactly does the original text say?
- **The Great Famine of 1315–1317**: documented evidence of child abandonment during famine (John Boswell's *The Kindness of Strangers*)
- **The Cambridge Group's finding** (Sara Sheringham or similar, 2024): famine stories are absent from English folklore because England's last famine predated systematic folklore collection — the absence proves the connection between material conditions and story content
- **The Grimms' seven editions** (1812–1857): the specific revisions. Mother → stepmother. Insertion of religious consolation. Progressive softening. What changed in each edition and why?
- **KQED or similar documentation** of how Hansel and Gretel adaptations shifted from abandonment narrative (1812) to "stranger danger" parable (1983 and later)
- **The scholarly fault line**: Bettelheim (*The Uses of Enchantment* — gingerbread house as oral regression, psychological universalism) vs. Darnton (*The Great Cat Massacre*), Zipes (*Breaking the Magic Spell*, *Fairy Tales and the Art of Subversion*), Warner (*From the Beast to the Blonde*). Warner's accusation that Bettelheim "leeches the history out of fairy tales." I need specific quotes from each side.
- **The unwritten argument I want to make**: the paradox of telling a starvation story to a child who has never known hunger. The gingerbread house weaponized the fantasy of plenty against starving children. Today it describes the average child's Tuesday. Zipes on fairy tales "violated" by consumer capitalism.

### 2. Winnie the Pooh as Secondary Case
- A.A. Milne's **PTSD from the Somme** producing a pastoral counter-world. Specific biographical details.
- **Christopher Robin Milne's** childhood destroyed by the publicity machine. His own writings about this.
- The **stuffed animals in a glass case** at the New York Public Library — intimate objects of play preserved as public artifacts. History of how they got there.
- **Frederick Crews' *The Pooh Perplex*** (1963) — the satirical academic reader proving any text can support any framework. What was his actual argument?
- The **Shea et al. satirical CMAJ paper** (Christmas Holiday Review issue) that escaped its context and became internet "fact." What did it actually claim?
- This case is a miniature of mythic displacement: meaning lost in transit across contexts.

### 3. Benjamin's Aura and Museum Objects
- Walter Benjamin's **"The Work of Art in the Age of Mechanical Reproduction"** — the shift from cult value to exhibition value. Key quotes.
- **Egyptian tomb objects** as paradigm case: objects designed to serve the dead now "haunting" museums. The pharaoh's curse as anxiety about contextual violation.
- **Dan Hicks** on the Benin Bronzes: "a systematic extension of warfare across time." From *The Brutish Museums* (2020). Specific arguments.
- **Amy Lonetree** (*Decolonizing Museums*, 2012): objects in museums are "living entities" — decontextualization is ongoing grief, not a completed act.
- How does Benjamin's framework apply to digital content? Is algorithmic distribution the ultimate destruction of aura?

### 4. Campbell, Eliade, and Myth Theory
- Joseph Campbell's claim: **"Myth cannot be exported into another land or another time"** — exact source and context for this quote
- Mircea Eliade's **desacralization thesis** — how does it apply to fairy tales and contemplative systems, not just religion?
- James Hillman's admission that depth psychology is **"historically European in origin"** — source and context
- Alan Dundes: folklore as **"autobiographical ethnography"** — what does this mean precisely?
- Ngũgĩ wa Thiong'o: **colonial language imposes colonial myths**, displacing indigenous ones. From *Decolonising the Mind* (1986). Key arguments.

### 5. The Distinction from Cultural Appropriation
- Mythic displacement includes a culture's relationship to its *own* inherited stories. A modern German telling Hansel and Gretel to a well-fed child is not appropriating — the tale belongs to that tradition. But it is displaced.
- Are there scholars who make this distinction explicitly? Between appropriation (taking from another culture) and displacement (losing connection to your own)?
