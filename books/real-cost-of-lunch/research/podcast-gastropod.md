## Overview

Gastropod is a food science and history podcast hosted by Cynthia Graber and Nicola Twilly, part of the Vox Media Podcast Network. Unlike Upstream (which provides structural political economy), Gastropod provides **commodity-specific supply chain detail** — exactly the gap the audit identified in Ch04-Ch11. These 17 episodes deliver concrete production histories, labor stories, breeding decisions, and trade dynamics for specific foods: tomatoes, corn, vanilla, avocados, dairy (yogurt/kefir), cinnamon, watermelon, sushi, tacos, pizza, durian/feijoa, and more.

The podcast's strength for RCL is its ability to trace how individual foods were transformed by mechanization, trade policy, colonialism, industrialization, and consumer culture — precisely the commodity-level narrative the book needs.

## Section 1: Grains, Corn, and Staple Crops

### Episode: The Colorful Tale of Mexico's A-maize-ing Grain
**Chapter mapping:** Ch04 (Grains — corn/milpa), Ch01 (System), Ch07 (Vegetables — nixtamalization)

**Key findings:**
- Corn was domesticated from teosinte roughly 10,000 years ago in the Balsas River Valley of Mexico. The transformation from a tiny, hard-seeded grass to the modern cob took millennia — far longer than wheat or rice domestication.
- Corn cannot survive without humans. Domesticated kernels never fall from the cob naturally. The plant is entirely dependent on human replanting — a metaphor for mutual dependence between food systems and human labor.
- **Nixtamalization** — cooking corn in alkaline water — unlocks niacin (vitamin B3) and essential amino acids. Without it, populations relying on corn develop pellagra. Europeans took corn back but not the processing wisdom, leading to pellagra epidemics in Italy and Eastern Europe. Pellagra symptoms (sun sensitivity, pallor, madness) may have contributed to vampire legends in Eastern Europe.
- Corn fueled the rise of Cahokia, the largest pre-Columbian settlement in what is now the US.
- Modern industrial corn (field corn) is fundamentally different from sweet corn — dense, hard, requires processing. The tortilla economy remains central to Mexican food culture.

**RCL integration:** Ch04 currently covers the Green Revolution and grain monocultures. The corn/nixtamalization story adds a powerful case study: when industrial systems strip indigenous processing knowledge from a crop, the health consequences are severe. The pellagra epidemic is a historical parallel to modern nutrient-stripping in industrial grain processing.

**Conflict check:** None. Strengthens existing Ch04 arguments about indigenous agricultural knowledge.

**Chapter mapping:** Ch04 (Grains — cultural uses), Ch11 (Kids' Food — pancake mixes)

## Section 2: Fruits and Vegetables

### Episode: Tomatoes: A Love Story
**Chapter mapping:** Ch06 (Fruits — tomato history), Ch07 (Vegetables), Ch01 (System)

**Key findings:**
- Wild tomatoes originated in the Andes as pea-sized fruits that nobody ate. Domestication happened in Mesoamerica, not South America.
- Europeans initially despised tomatoes. Galen's humoral theory classified them as dangerously cold and wet. A Flemish herbalist described their smell as sufficient proof of how "unhealthful and evil" they were to eat.
- Tomatoes only caught on in southern Italy because the population was extremely poor and tomatoes grew well in volcanic soil. They developed conserva nera (black preserve) — sun-dried tomato leather that lasted a year.
- In the US, a quack doctor's 1834 lecture on tomato health properties went viral across 200 newspapers, spawning a tomato pill craze — snake oil sellers marketed worthless "compound extract of tomato" pills.
- Florida fresh tomatoes had flavor bred out of them for shippability. Canning tomatoes retained flavor genes accidentally.

**RCL integration:** Ch06 (Fruits) and Ch07 (Vegetables). The Florida tomato story is a perfect case study for how industrial breeding prioritizes shippability over flavor and nutrition — a core RCL argument. The conserva nera history shows pre-industrial food preservation that industrialization erased.

### Episode: Canned Tomatoes and the Myth of the San Marzano
**Chapter mapping:** Ch06 (Fruits/Vegetables — tomato supply chain), Ch04 (Grains — mechanization parallel), Ch14 (Packaging — canning)

**Key findings:**
- The Civil War drove canned tomato adoption. Union soldiers carried canned tomatoes; Confederate troops raided them. Post-war demand created the canning industry. By 1870, 100 canneries produced 30 million cans per year.
- **Mechanization transformed the tomato itself.** UC Davis researchers Jack Hanna and Kobe Lawrenceon bred tomatoes specifically for mechanical harvesting — harder, jointless stems, determinate growth. The Galapagos connection: a key gene came from wild tomatoes whose seeds had to pass through giant tortoise digestive tracts to germinate.
- The Bracero program provided cheap, exploitable Mexican labor for California tomato fields. When it ended in 1964, mechanization accelerated: from 25% machine-harvested to 80% in just two years. Thousands of small growers went out of business. Tens of thousands of jobs were lost.
- **Fresh pack vs. paste processing:** 75% of California canning tomatoes become industrial paste (cooked twice, reconstituted later). Fresh pack operations cook once and can immediately — better flavor but more expensive because the factory sits idle most of the year.
- **San Marzano fraud:** US does not honor European DOP regulations, so "San Marzano DOP" labels on American shelves may not be authentic. The real San Marzano nearly went extinct from disease in the 1960s-70s. The Roma tomato that replaced it was actually developed by the USDA in Maryland.

**RCL integration:** HIGH for Ch06 and Ch14. The Bracero-to-mechanization arc is a textbook case of how labor exploitation and mechanization interact. The fresh pack vs. paste distinction reveals a hidden quality divide in canned goods that consumers are unaware of. The San Marzano fraud story illustrates how trade regulations (or their absence) enable food fraud.

**Conflict check:** None. Directly fills the gap the audit identified for Ch06.

### Episode: Ripe for Global Domination: The Story of the Avocado
**Chapter mapping:** Ch06 (Fruits — avocado supply chain), Ch01 (System — NAFTA), Ch18 (Choice Architecture)

**Key findings:**
- Avocados were domesticated at least 7,000 years ago in Mesoamerica. The Aztec word aguacatl means "testicle" (the fruit hangs in pairs). Spanish conquistadors renamed them.
- The Hass variety — 95% of California production — came from a chance seedling found by a postal worker named Rudolph Hass in the 1920s. All commercial Hass trace back to this one tree (died 2002 from root rot).
- **Ripening rooms** revolutionized the avocado business. Before ethylene-pumped ripening rooms (modeled on banana rooms), consumers would squeeze unripe avocados and walk away. Hidden cameras in 1980s supermarkets showed sale after sale lost to unripe fruit.
- **NAFTA was the decisive factor.** A ban on Mexican avocados existed since 1914, ostensibly over pest concerns but also protecting California growers. Lifting the ban was extremely contentious — one US distributor was spat at and shouted out of a meeting for supporting imports. Post-NAFTA, American avocado consumption went from 1 pound per person per year to over 7.
- The California Avocado Commission spent hundreds of thousands on marketing, including actress Angie Dickinson in a white leotard eating avocado to promote it as healthy despite fat-phobia era concerns.
- During the fat-is-bad era, avocados were marketed as unhealthy. The commission funded studies showing avocado fat aided nutrient absorption, partnered with Harvard to promote Mediterranean diet.

**RCL integration:** HIGH for Ch06. The avocado episode fills a gap the audit specifically identified: avocado cartel economics. The NAFTA story shows how trade policy transforms food systems — a small growers' protectionist regime gave way to industrial-scale imports from Mexico's Michoacan region. The ripening room innovation shows how infrastructure determines what consumers eat.

### Episode: Durian Delight and Feijoa Fun: Adventures in Banned, Forgotten, and Unusual Fruit
**Chapter mapping:** Ch06 (Fruits — monoculture/diversity), Ch01 (System — what counts as food)

**Key findings:**
- The feijoa's story illuminates why so few new fruits become commercial successes. Only kiwi and avocado really broke through in the past century. Commercial fruits must be uniform, high-yielding, sturdy for shipping, and have long shelf life.
- The feijoa fails commercially because it falls from the tree when ripe and doesn't keep in cold storage — the opposite of what industrial supply chains require.
- Colonial attitudes shaped which foods became global commodities. British colonizers described durian as uncivilized and immoral — the smell was framed as a marker of difference between colonizer and colonized. Northern Europeans who admitted enjoying durian were dismissed as having spent too long in the jungle.
- Durian was banned from public transit, hotels, and offices across Southeast Asia — not because of colonial attitudes but because the smell genuinely permeates ventilation systems.
- Colombia diversified away from coffee/cocaine dependence partly through feijoa cultivation.

**RCL integration:** Ch06. The feijoa story provides a counterpoint to industrial fruit monoculture: what we lose when only shippable, storable fruits succeed commercially. The colonial framing of durian supports Ch19's equity arguments about whose food preferences become global standards.

### Episode: The Most Dangerous Fruit in America (Watermelon)
**Chapter mapping:** Ch06 (Fruits — watermelon), Ch19 (Who Can Afford — racial dimensions of food)

**Key findings:**
- Watermelon was domesticated in northeastern Africa (Sudan, Egypt, Libya area) and required two key genetic mutations: loss of bitterness and development of red color.
- Watermelon became a symbol of independence and agency for enslaved African Americans who were allowed to garden and grow their own food. The drinking gourd (watermelon family) was a metaphor for the Big Dipper constellation guiding people north on the Underground Railroad.
- Post-Civil War, watermelon became weaponized as a racist symbol. Before emancipation, it was beloved by everyone — Jefferson, Thoreau, Mark Twain. After, it was reframed as a degrading stereotype.
- The Bradford watermelon (1840s) was so valuable that growers guarded patches with shotguns. More people were killed in watermelon patches than in any agricultural setting except cattle rustling. Growers poisoned individual melons to deter thieves — and sometimes forgot which ones, poisoning their own families.
- Industrial breeding prioritized shippability and disease resistance over flavor. Modern seedless refrigerator melons have high sugar but no complexity — a "perfect industrial product" that lost the Bradford's nuance.

**RCL integration:** Ch06 and Ch19. The watermelon's racial history adds a critical dimension to Ch19's equity analysis. The Bradford-to-industrial-seedless arc parallels the Florida tomato story: breeding for industrial needs destroys flavor and diversity.

## Section 3: Dairy and Alternatives

### Episode: From Fountain of Youth to Fruit on the Bottom: How Yoghurt Finally Made it Big in America
**Chapter mapping:** Ch08 (Dairy & Alternatives), Ch11 (Kids' Food), Ch15 (Ninth Cost)

**Key findings:**
- Yogurt has been made for roughly 9,000-10,000 years, since the domestication of milk animals. Two specific microbes — Streptococcus thermophilus and Lactobacillus delbrueckii bulgaricus — define yogurt.
- Yogurt reduces lactose by about half, which may explain its adoption in regions where most adults were lactose intolerant. Northern/Western Europe developed butter and aged cheese instead because yogurt microbes need warm temperatures.
- **Ant yogurt:** Traditional Bulgarian yogurt-making involved dropping ants into warm milk and burying the container in ant colonies overnight. The ants contributed formic acid (kickstarting fermentation), lactic acid bacteria, and enzymes.
- **Metchnikov and the yogurt-as-immortality craze:** Nobel Prize winner Elie Metchnikov (discoverer of the immune system via starfish) promoted yogurt as a cure for aging in a famous 1904 Paris lecture. He brought an old, shabby dog and a feisty 70-year-old parrot as visual props. The yogurt fad swept Europe and America. Metchnikov died at 71, halfway to his predicted 150-year lifespan.
- Americans initially found yogurt repugnant. A Kansas newspaper correspondent wrote that curdled milk was a Parisian fad and "he would prefer to die young." Bob Colombosian's teacher used yogurt as a moisturizer rather than food.
- **Dannon's transformation of yogurt:** Added fruit and sugar to appeal to American palates, marketed as health food for dieters. The 1970s Soviet Georgian centenarian ad campaign revived Metchnikov's longevity claims. Yogurt was made into dessert but marketed as health food.
- Greek yogurt caught on because of lower sugar content (keto diet appeal). The yogurt aisle expanded to half the store length.

**RCL integration:** HIGH for Ch08. The audit identified dairy as having zero podcast coverage. This episode provides the cultural history of how yogurt went from an immigrant ethnic food to an industrial product disguised as health food. The Dannon story — making dessert and marketing it as medicine — is a perfect Ch08 case study. The Greek yogurt/keto diet connection supports Ch15's analysis of how health anxiety drives food industry profits.

### Episode: Is Your Cinnamon Fake? Where Does Kefir Come From? (Ask Gastropod)
**Chapter mapping:** Ch08 (Dairy — kefir), Ch14 (Packaging — citric acid), Ch09 (Sweet Problem — food additives)

**Key findings:**
- **Citric acid:** Originally extracted from Italian lemons. When WWI submarine blockades cut supply, Pfizer developed deep-tank fermentation using Aspergillus niger (black mold) to produce citric acid industrially. This same deep-tank technology later enabled mass production of penicillin for WWII. Today most citric acid is made in China. It appears in an astonishing range of processed foods: canned tomatoes, salad dressings, sodas, chips, hummus, sauces, medicine.
- Some people react to manufactured citric acid (headaches, fatigue, brain fog, GI symptoms) but not to citric acid from actual lemons — possibly due to residual Aspergillus niger from production. The FDA considers it GRAS (generally recognized as safe) with no daily intake limits.
- **Kefir vs. yogurt:** Kefir requires physical grains (cauliflower-shaped globules of bacteria and yeast). No one has successfully created kefir grains in a lab. Most commercial kefir in stores is basically drinkable yogurt — not true kefir. The traditional story involves a Russian woman named Irina Sakharov obtaining grains from a Caucasus prince (either by kidnapping-lawsuit or as a gift, depending on the source).
- **Cinnamon fraud:** "True cinnamon" (Cinnamomum verum from Sri Lanka) was named by a Czech botanist — the "true" designation is arbitrary. Vietnamese/Saigon cinnamon has the highest cinnamaldehyde content (strongest flavor). Most US cinnamon is Indonesian (Cinnamomum burmannii), which is cheaper but not "fake."

**RCL integration:** Ch08 (kefir as authentic vs. industrial dairy), Ch14 (citric acid as hidden industrial ingredient — the Pfizer/penicillin connection shows how food processing infrastructure was built on war), Ch09 (cinnamon fraud illustrates the gap between marketing and reality in the spice trade).

## Section 4: Processed Foods, Fast Food, and Food Systems

### Episode: Pizza Pizza!
**Chapter mapping:** Ch11 (Kids' Food), Ch01 (System — food migration), Ch06 (Fruits/Vegetables — tomato industry)

**Key findings:**
- Pizza was exclusively Neapolitan street food for the urban poor until the mid-20th century. Most Italians had never heard of it. Foreign visitors described it as nauseating — Samuel Morse (telegraph inventor) called it a "species of most nauseating cake."
- Italian immigrants to New York adapted pizza using local ingredients: coal-fired ovens instead of wood, canned tomatoes instead of fresh (driving the Italian canning export industry), and low-moisture American mozzarella instead of fresh buffalo mozzarella. An entire cheese industry developed around pizza.
- The myth that GIs discovered pizza in wartime Naples is likely false — Naples was starving during the Allied invasion, and there probably was very little pizza available.
- Pizza's 1950s American boom was driven by postwar prosperity, the suburbanization of eating, and women's magazine promotion.

**RCL integration:** Ch11. Pizza illustrates how a single food can drive entire supply chains (canned tomatoes, mozzarella, wheat). The immigrant adaptation story shows how food systems reshape themselves around demand.

### Episode: Talking Taco
**Chapter mapping:** Ch04 (Grains — corn/wheat), Ch11 (Kids' Food — Taco Bell), Ch19 (Who Can Afford)

**Key findings:**
- The word "taco" originated in silver mines (Real Del Monte, ~60 miles from Mexico City). Miners' gunpowder charges wrapped in paper were called tacos (from the Spanish/French word for peg/plug). Miners wrapped their tortilla-and-potato lunches the same way, and the name transferred.
- Tacos were women's food economy: miners' wives sold tacos on Mexico City streets in the late 1800s. The taco vendor ecosystem was gendered — women sold breakfast stew tacos, men sold nighttime grilled meat tacos.
- **Corn stigma:** Wealthy Mexicans associated corn tortillas with indigenous people and ate wheat bread instead. Tacos were looked down upon as poor people's food.
- **Al pastor tacos** were introduced by Lebanese immigrants in the 1920s as "tacos arabis" — lamb on vertical rotisserie wrapped in flour tortillas. Over time: lamb became pork, spices replaced original flavoring, pineapple was added (origin unknown).
- **Taco Bell:** Glenn Bell in San Bernardino (same town as the original McDonald's) adapted Mexican tacos for white American consumers — pre-fried hard shells, ground beef, iceberg lettuce. His wife said white people would never handle the spice. He applied McDonald's industrial fast-food logic to a different cuisine.

**RCL integration:** Ch11 (Taco Bell as industrial food system) and Ch19 (the racial/class dynamics of corn vs. wheat, indigenous food stigma). The al pastor origin story — Lebanese immigrants creating a Mexican icon — illustrates how migration shapes food systems.

### Episode: Sushi's Extraordinary Evolution: From Pickle to Primetime
**Chapter mapping:** Ch01 (System — globalization of food), Ch05 (Protein — fishing industry)

**Key findings:**
- Original sushi (500s CE China) was fermented fish preserved in grain for 6-12 months — tasted like cheese. The rice was discarded as inedible mush.
- Modern nigiri was invented as street food in Edo (Tokyo) in the 1700s — the size of a hot dog, eaten standing up at outdoor stalls.
- WWII fundamentally reshaped sushi: wartime rationing shrank pieces to bite-size; government shut down outdoor stalls as health hazards, pushing sushi indoors to restaurants.
- **Flash-freezing on long-range trawlers** enabled global fish sourcing and the rise of bluefin tuna in sushi — connecting sushi to the industrial fishing supply chain.
- Conveyor belt sushi (invented 1958 by Shiraishi Yoshiaki, modeled on factory assembly lines) democratized access. The California roll (1960s-70s) adapted sushi for American palates by hiding seaweed inside.

**RCL integration:** Ch05 (Protein — the trawler/freezer connection between sushi and industrial fishing) and Ch01 (how a single food's globalization drives entire supply chain transformations).

### Episode: SNAP To It! Why Food Stamps Matter To All of Us
**Chapter mapping:** Ch19 (Who Can Afford), Ch01 (System — overproduction), Ch04 (Grains — surplus)

**Key findings:**
- **The food stamp paradox:** Born from the simultaneous existence of hunger and agricultural surplus during the Great Depression. The government destroyed food (slaughtered pigs turned to sludge, milk poured down drains, grain burned) while people stood in bread lines.
- The original 1939 food stamp program used two-colored stamps: orange (regular food, purchased) and blue (surplus food, bonus). It worked well — reduced stigma versus bread lines, supported retailers, improved diets. Ended in 1943 when wartime demand eliminated surpluses.
- **Reagan's "welfare queen" rhetoric** cut food stamp funding during the worst recession since the Depression, while simultaneously the government was buying and storing massive dairy surpluses (the origin of "government cheese").
- Post-WWII industrial agriculture created permanent overproduction. By 1960, surplus food storage was the second or third largest non-defense federal expenditure.
- The program's name change from "food stamps" to SNAP was partly to reduce stigma, but the political battles continued. Recent cuts of ~$190 billion threaten millions of recipients.
- **The structural tension:** Government wants prices high enough for farmers to survive but low enough for consumers to afford food. Food stamps are the political solution to this inherent contradiction.

**RCL integration:** HIGH for Ch19 and Ch01. The overproduction-meets-hunger paradox is central to RCL's system argument. The food stamp history shows that America's approach to hunger has always been shaped more by agricultural surplus management than by concern for the hungry. Directly supports Holt-Gimenez's overproduction thesis already in Ch01.

## Section 5: Spices, Flavors, and the Economics of Taste

### Episode: Forget Plain Vanilla
**Chapter mapping:** Ch09 (Sweet Problem — vanilla/sugar), Ch06 (Fruits — supply chain), Ch19 (Who Can Afford — colonial labor)

**Key findings:**
- Vanilla is one of the most expensive spices in the world, rivaling saffron. Every single flower must be hand-pollinated — a technique discovered by Edmund, a 12-year-old enslaved African on the French colony of Reunion (then called Bourbon) in 1841. Edmund's owner did not free him despite this breakthrough. He died in poverty.
- The hand-pollination window is just half a day per flower (6 AM to noon), for a two-month flowering season. Then nine months for pods to ripen. Then three months of intensive drying/curing.
- **Synthetic vanillin** (from wood pulp, ~1930s) is 150 times cheaper than real vanilla. Nine out of ten consumers cannot tell the difference. The word "vanilla" came to mean boring only after synthetic vanillin made the flavor ubiquitous — previously it was the summit of sophistication.
- All commercial vanilla worldwide comes from perhaps only 5-6 original plants (clonal propagation from cuttings). Genetic diversity is critically low, making the crop vulnerable to disease and climate change.
- Vanilla's gene bank at the University of Veracruz holds ~90,000 tiny orchid plants — critical insurance against crop failure.

**RCL integration:** Ch09. The vanilla story is a microcosm of how colonial labor systems, monoculture vulnerability, and synthetic substitution interact. Edmund's story — an enslaved child's discovery enabling a global industry that left him in poverty — is exactly the kind of hidden cost RCL documents.

## Section 6: Metabolism, Diet, and the Food Environment

### Episode: Everything You Know About Metabolism Is Wrong
**Chapter mapping:** Ch15 (Ninth Cost — psychic cost), Ch01 (System — food environment), Ch11 (Kids' Food — ultra-processed)

**Key findings:**
- Kevin Hall's Biggest Loser study overturned conventional wisdom about metabolism. People who lost the most weight had the greatest metabolic slowdown — and also kept the most weight off long-term. Metabolic rate is not the primary driver of weight gain/loss.
- **Set point theory:** The body regulates around a weight range. Ultra-processed foods appear to reset this set point upward — rats fed supermarket junk food gained weight rapidly despite normally stable weight. Kevin's controlled human studies showed the same: people offered ultra-processed foods consumed more calories than when offered the same calories in minimally processed form.
- The food environment is the most important population-level factor. Obesity rates doubled in 70+ countries since 1980 — metabolisms didn't collectively break. The food environment changed.
- Kevin Hall recently resigned from NIH because he experienced censorship of research results under RFK Jr.'s leadership — described as a huge loss for the field.
- **DNP (dinitrophenol):** A WWI explosives chemical that speeds metabolism. Still illegally sold as diet pills online despite killing at least 32 people. Illustrates the desperation metabolism myths create.

**RCL integration:** HIGH for Ch15. The set-point-reset-by-ultra-processed-foods finding is one of the strongest scientific arguments against the food environment. The metabolic myth debunking supports Ch15's argument that the psychic cost (guilt, anxiety about weight) is manufactured by a system that then profits from the distress.

## Section 7: Kitchen Technology and Food Access

### Episode: OXO, Cuisinart, and Julia Child: The Secret Accessible History Behind Your Kitchen
**Chapter mapping:** Ch18 (Choice Architecture), Ch19 (Who Can Afford)

**Key findings:**
- Standard kitchen design (36-inch counter height) was based on US military body measurements — implicitly a fit, white male figure. Standardization privileged production over users' bodies.
- Lillian Gilbreth (of Cheaper by the Dozen fame) pioneered accessible kitchen design after being refused industrial consulting work because she was a woman. Her "Kitchen Practical" (1929) introduced the work triangle (fridge-sink-stove) and rolling carts.
- The "Heart Kitchen" (1940s) was designed for women with cardiovascular disease — elevated dishwashers, adjustable work surfaces, seated work areas. It inspired nationwide accessible kitchen programs.
- Betty Crocker's audio recipe records (late 1940s) were among the first accessible cooking resources for blind homemakers, developed in collaboration with blind women who tested recipes and advised on adaptations.
- The Instant Pot became an accessibility tool for people with autoimmune disorders and physical disabilities — reducing the physical labor of cooking.

**RCL integration:** Ch18 (Choice Architecture). Kitchen design is food system infrastructure. The military-standard body that determined counter heights is a small but telling example of how built environments assume certain bodies and exclude others.

### Episode: Yes, You Really Can Make Food From Thin Air
**Chapter mapping:** Ch01 (System — future alternatives), Ch04 (Grains — alternative production)

**Key findings:**
- Multiple companies (Solar Foods, Savor) and government agencies (DARPA, NASA) are developing food from atmospheric CO2 using microbes or pure chemistry.
- The basic science: all food macronutrients (carbs, proteins, fats) are made of just carbon, hydrogen, oxygen, and nitrogen.
- Solar Foods and others use carbon capture technology (developed for the energy industry) to grab CO2, then feed it to engineered microbes that produce glucose, which other microbes convert into protein, fat, and carbohydrates.
- DARPA wants truck-mounted food-from-air systems for forward military positions — disconnecting soldiers from food supply chains.
- About 50% of habitable land on Earth is devoted to food production. The motivation for food from air: decoupling food production from land use.

**RCL integration:** Minor for current RCL scope but relevant to Ch20 (80/20 Plate) as a future alternative. The 50% land use figure supports Ch01's system argument.

## Section 10: Top 5 Integrations

### 1. Ch06 — The Avocado and NAFTA (Gastropod + existing Upstream)
The avocado episode provides the specific commodity-level trade story that Ch06 needs. Combined with Upstream's macro-level framing of trade agreements, this gives Ch06 both structural analysis and a vivid supply chain narrative.

### 2. Ch08 — Yogurt as Industrial Health Theater (NEW — fills zero-coverage gap)
Dannon's strategy of making yogurt into dessert (fruit, sugar) while marketing it as health food is the Ch08 case study. The Metchnikov-to-Activia arc shows how health claims drive dairy marketing across centuries. The kefir fraud angle adds consumer deception.

### 3. Ch06/Ch07 — Florida Tomato Flavor Destruction + San Marzano Fraud

### 4. Ch15 — Ultra-Processed Foods Reset the Body's Set Point (Hall)
Kevin Hall's Biggest Loser research is among the most rigorous evidence that the food environment — not individual metabolism or willpower — drives weight at the population level. This strengthens Ch15's argument that the psychic cost is systemic, not personal.

### 5. Ch09 — Vanilla's Colonial Labor Story
Edmund's story — a 12-year-old enslaved child whose discovery created a global industry that left him in poverty — is a powerful opening anecdote for Ch09's analysis of hidden labor costs in the sweetness supply chain.
