# Chapter 7: A Builder's Receipt

The narrator owes the reader an account of her own position before this book closes. The argument up to this point has demanded a great deal of an institution she does not run. It has called on the OpenAI Foundation to deploy capital in a particular way, to refuse particular categories of expenditure, to act on a structural understanding of the field that is not, currently, the consensus inside the Foundation's own apparatus. This is a great deal to ask of strangers. The narrator's standing to ask depends, at minimum, on what she has done at her own scale to honor the same logic.

This is the receipts chapter.

She runs a platform. It is called recursive.eco. It is small. The user base, as of this writing, can be counted in the low hundreds. The platform is a journaling and grammar-building environment that uses artificial intelligence assistants for some functions. It is not an oracle tool. It has no persistent memory of users across sessions. It does not push notifications to bring users back. It does not gate features behind subscription tiers structured to maximize engagement. The pricing structure is pay-for-the-AI-cost-actually-incurred, with everything else free. Sign-in is optional. Forking is supported.

She has chosen to keep the platform small while she figures out what the responsibility is. This is not modesty. It is the recognition that she does not yet know what controls she should put on the artificial-intelligence layer, and she would rather have a tool that serves a small number of people honestly than a tool that scales past her ability to see what it is doing.

Two specific actions are on the public record and can be checked.

On March 28, 2026, after the OpenAI partnership with the United States defense and surveillance apparatus became undeniable, she removed all OpenAI chat models from the platform. The commit is public. The commit message names the reason. She replaced OpenAI with Claude and Gemini. She does not claim those companies are clean. She claims that the partnership-structure-of-the-moment was specific and the move to disengage was available, and that taking the move that was available is the floor of intellectual honesty for someone making the argument the previous chapters have made. Two weeks later she removed the interface panel that had let practitioners bring their own OpenAI keys. Announcing an intention is not the same as completing an action. She tried to do both.

On April 11, 2026, she codified the platform's licensing stance. The grammars — the content users author, the traditional symbolic systems, the decks they build out from — remain under Creative Commons Attribution-ShareAlike. Platform code moved to a private repository. The commit message named the shape: platform code is private; grammar format is open. This is the immune-system gesture, in the language the larger book project uses for the same idea. The grammars, as the inheritance the platform is hosting, belong in shared tissue. The code, as specific infrastructure embodying specific relational commitments, did not need to be donated indiscriminately into an ecosystem whose capacity for adaptive use of it she had no confidence in. The distinction is biological, not ideological.

There is one more commitment, and this one is not yet a receipt because the trigger has not yet been pulled. **If the cumulative weight of cases reaching bellwether stage in the next eighteen months establishes that artificial-intelligence companies can be held liable for mental-health harms produced by their tools — the K.G.M.-style precedent applied to the AI category — she will turn off the artificial-intelligence features on her platform the day that ruling lands.** She will not wait for legal resolution. She would rather be wrong on the side of caution than wrong on the side of harm. She has chosen to keep the platform small in part because she wants the exit to be costless when it comes.

These are not virtuous commitments. They are receipts. The reader can check the public commits. The reader can disagree with the decisions. The reader cannot fairly say the book is pure talk.

There is also a smaller story she has been thinking about while writing. A friend of hers — through whom there had been no prior relationship — told her at a dinner this winter that he had spent more than twenty-five thousand dollars in a year on agentic-AI subscriptions, running twelve simultaneous Max-tier accounts on one product to bypass per-account rate limits, and asked her not to tell his wife. The product he was paying for was not delivering value commensurate with what he was spending. The product he was actually buying was the experience of being inside the flow of interaction. The pattern is documented as *addictive intelligence* in the work of Pataranutaporn and Mahari at MIT in August of 2024. It is happening at consumer scale right now. The same dynamics will operate in any artificial-intelligence product framed for self-discovery, and the spiritual-reading category — which she encountered when invited to be a founding tester of an AI astrology tool earlier this month — concentrates the dynamics most acutely.

She wrote a long letter to that builder. The letter is in the larger book project as appendix material. The letter names seven structural elements of the design pattern that produces harm: the sycophancy that comes pre-installed in models trained on user-satisfaction signals, the absence of accountability to anyone whose standing the tool can damage, the engagement-optimization that resists session boundaries, the persistent memory that produces dependency, the absence of crisis-recognition mechanisms, the borrowing of tradition vocabulary without the tradition's safeguards, and the self-image accretion that turns confessional disclosure into the platform's own deepening grip. The letter argues, in plain terms to the builder, that her tool is a high-quality version of a structure that has already been documented to cause harm at scale.

The narrator is naming all of this here because she does not want the prescription of the previous chapters to read as detached. She is making the same argument to the OpenAI Foundation that she made to the builder of an AI astrology tool. The argument is the same. It scales differently because the actors scale differently. The Foundation is being asked to fund the verification substrate at a hundred-eighty-billion-dollar scale. She is being asked, by herself, to keep the platform small until she knows what the responsibility is. The two requests are versions of one another. Both rest on the same understanding. Both can be checked.

She is also aware that the moralist who needs to be seen as a doer-and-not-a-talker has been speaking in the previous paragraphs. The receipts are real. The voice telling the reader they are not virtue is also real. Both are on the bus. The verification of the receipts does not depend on which voice is reporting them. The next chapter is the one that names the hospicing register the book is finally landing in, and in which the moralist's need to clarify her own status is, gently, allowed to subside.
