# Chapter 7: The Imperial Method

The narrator owes the reader a chapter she has been postponing because it is the chapter she is least sure of. The previous chapter named, among the things the Foundation should refuse to fund, *AI-for-the-Global-South deployments authored from outside it.* That refusal is easy to write as a single line in a list. It is harder to defend as a position once you sit with the counter-argument, which is real, which she has not yet answered, and which she wants to answer here in the open before the book closes.

The counter-argument runs as follows. If the race is not contained, the artificial-intelligence systems being built will be deployed everywhere whether anyone wishes them to be or not. The systems will arrive as products, as tutors, as agricultural advisors, as medical-triage tools, as judicial-backlog software, as the substrate of public administration. They will arrive in Lagos, Jakarta, Karachi, Kinshasa, Recife, Manila, Dhaka, Lima, on a timeline that does not wait for any of those cities to develop the regulatory capacity, the linguistic resources, or the institutional sovereignty to direct the deployment on their own terms. In that case, the argument continues, refusing to fund the conditions for those cities to build their own absorption capacity is itself a form of abandonment. The Foundation that says *I will not fund AI-for-the-Global-South* is the Foundation that lets the unregulated commercial deployment happen anyway, on terms set by the racing center, with no support for the receiving institutions.

This is the strongest version of the case for the deployment frame. The narrator wants to honor it as the strongest version before answering it.

The honest answer begins with empirical history. The track record of what happens when wealthy Northern actors deploy technology at scale to the Global South under the banner of helping is not a record of help. It is a record of harm produced under good intentions, repeated reliably enough that the pattern is now diagnosable.

The Alliance for a Green Revolution in Africa, founded in 2006 by the Gates Foundation and the Rockefeller Foundation, raised approximately one billion dollars against a goal to double yields and halve food insecurity for thirty million smallholder households across thirteen African countries by 2020. Productivity grew eighteen percent. The number of severely undernourished people in those same countries grew thirty percent. AGRA quietly removed its public goals from its website in June of 2020. One Laptop Per Child delivered four hundred thousand machines to Uruguay; a ten-year, five-hundred-thirty-one-school study in Peru found no improvement in cognitive skills, mathematics, or language ability. Free Basics was banned in India in February of 2016 for what the Telecom Regulatory Authority of India named in plain terms. Worldcoin, run by Sam Altman's other company, was scanning the irises of Buenos Aires pensioners during 288 percent inflation for fifty dollars in tokens; Kenya ordered the data deleted in May of 2025. OpenAI itself ran the toxicity filter for ChatGPT through Sama-Kenya labelers paid one dollar thirty-two cents to two dollars per hour to read child sexual abuse material and torture descriptions for forty hours a week. This is the sector's track record. The honest priors say a one-hundred-eighty-billion-dollar "AI for development" thesis will fail along the same axis it has failed on for sixty years — by treating the receiving population as raw material for the donor's redemption arc.

The pattern is the *imperial method* Karen Hao has named in *Empire of AI*: ideological mission, resource extraction, labor exploitation, manichaean rivalry framed as helping. The method does not depend on the donor being malicious. It depends on the donor authoring the intervention. The receiver is, in Hao's framing, civilized — meaning made legible to the donor's institutional logic, on the donor's timetable, in the donor's vocabulary. The vocabulary survives the donor's exit. The institutions outlast the program. The receiver is left with a structure that is shaped to the donor's understanding of the problem, which is rarely the same as the receiver's understanding of the problem, and which is now expensive to dismantle.

This is the structural reason the previous chapter listed deployment as a refusal item. Not because deployment cannot help. Because the *form* of the deployment, when it is authored from the racing center, reproduces the same imperial method whose track record the preceding paragraphs documented. A sovereign Indian or Nigerian foundation model trained on the same scaling logic, the same English-as-default architecture, the same safety-as-RLHF-on-Western-annotators pipeline does not exit the structure that makes the imperial method imperial. It joins the structure as a smaller franchise. Sabelo Mhlambi has argued from ubuntu that artificial intelligence's foundational error is the rationality-as-personhood ontology that makes intelligence calculable in the first place; Yuk Hui has argued from cosmotechnics that the artificial-general-intelligence race actively destroys the technodiversity that would allow other ways of being intelligent to flourish; the Indigenous Protocol and Artificial Intelligence Position Paper edited by Jason Edward Lewis, Suzanne Kite, and Noelani Arista has argued that artificial intelligence might be approached as kin one establishes protocols with rather than property one races to own. None of these arguments is a refusal of artificial intelligence as such. All three are refusals of the form artificial intelligence has taken under the imperial method.

So the answer to the counter-argument is not the false binary the counter-argument tries to produce. The choice is not *deploy AI-to-save-the-Global-South* or *abandon the Global South to whatever the racing center produces.* The choice is between two forms of the same act. **You can author the deployment from the racing center, with all the imperial method's pathologies, or you can fund the conditions for the receiving population to author its own deployment.** The first is the form the track record has already condemned. The second is what the verification body of the previous chapters is, at the deployment scale, the analog of.

Concretely: the Foundation should not fund Krutrim. It should not fund a Foundation-authored sovereign Nigerian model. It should fund the Global South AI Evaluation Commons that Ritu Aneja's work at the Digital Futures Lab has begun to sketch, co-hosted across Indian Institute of Technology Madras, Strathmore CIPIT in Nairobi, the African Institute for Mathematical Sciences, and the Tecnológico de Monterrey, on the bloc's terms, evaluating models on the bloc's harms. It should fund Lelapa, AI4Bharat, Awarri, Maritaca on operating endowments without governance strings. It should fund the linguistic and ceremonial-knowledge sovereignty work — Te Mana Raraunga, the CARE Principles, the First Nations Information Governance Centre, the Lakota Language Consortium — at perpetual scale, so that the next training corpus is built under the receiving communities' regimes rather than under the donor's.

This is not a refusal to deploy. It is a refusal to deploy *from the wrong direction*. It is the same structural move as the verification body. It treats the question of who decides as the load-bearing question, and the question of what is decided as downstream of that.

The narrator wants to be honest that she has held this position only recently and that holding it costs her something. She was raised inside a Brazilian context where being benefited by Northern institutions was framed as desirable, where catching up was framed as the right response to the question Northern institutions had set. She has had to unlearn that framing and is not done unlearning it. Vanessa Andreotti has called the framing a *single story of forward.* It is the story the imperial method depends on the receiver believing. The Foundation, if it believes the story, will produce one hundred eighty billion dollars of damage even with the best intentions.

The wager is that the receiving populations, given the conditions to author their own deployment, will produce something the racing center cannot. They will produce, plausibly, the form of artificial intelligence that the racing center's institutional pathology prevents it from imagining. That is not a guarantee. It is a refusal to accept the guarantee the imperial method has been offering for two centuries, which is that the donor knows what the receiver needs better than the receiver does.

The next chapter is the receipt the narrator owes the reader on her own scale, so that the position taken here is not detached from a builder's actual practice.
