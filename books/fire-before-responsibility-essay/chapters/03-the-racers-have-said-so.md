# Chapter 3: The Racers Have Said So in Public

There is a certain kind of testimony that, when it comes from inside the institution being examined, becomes more difficult to dismiss than any external critique. The testimony does not require argument. It requires only attention, and the willingness to take what the witness is saying seriously.

In late January of 2026, Dario Amodei published an essay on his personal website titled "The Adolescence of Technology." It runs to roughly twenty thousand words. The narrator believes it is the most important primary source for the argument this chapter is making, because in that essay Amodei lays out, in his own words, the three structural facts the book has been building toward.

The first is the captive-R&D thesis stated from inside one of the labs. Discussing the bioweapon-classifier work Anthropic has been doing — the safety filters that detect and block the model's bioweapon-related outputs — Amodei observes that voluntary classifier maintenance is a "classic negative externalities problem that can't be solved by the voluntary actions of Anthropic or any other single company alone." This is, in plain English, a confession that the most safety-conscious company in the field, doing the most expensive safety work it can find, cannot win the externality battle without the other companies cooperating, and that the other companies have economic incentive to defect. The chief executive officer of Anthropic is naming, on the record, that his lab's safety investments are subject to a prisoner's dilemma he cannot solve unilaterally.

The second is the explicit statement about international cooperation. Amodei writes, in the same essay: "I am generally a skeptic about most kinds of international cooperation on AI, but this may be one narrow area where there is some chance of achieving global restraint." The narrow area is bioweapons. The reasoning: even dictatorships do not want massive bioterrorist attacks, so the toehold for global restraint exists in the bioweapon domain even when the broader cooperation looks impossible. This is a load-bearing concession. Amodei is the most articulate skeptic of multilateral AI cooperation among the frontier-lab founders. He has just opened a door, in writing, on the bioweapon subset.

The third is the most devastating sentence in the essay for the standard "race-coalition" argument. Discussing why China cannot be persuaded to stop, Amodei writes: "Given the incredible economic and military value of the technology, together with the lack of any meaningful enforcement mechanism, I don't see how we could possibly convince them to stop." The two clauses together are the diagnosis the book has been building toward. The lack of any meaningful enforcement mechanism is named explicitly. The convincing is identified explicitly as something that depends on the existence of the mechanism. **Amodei is naming the gap. The Foundation's job is to fill it.**

Nine days after publishing the essay, Amodei sat with Demis Hassabis at the World Economic Forum for a thirty-one-minute panel discussion. The exchange most quoted from that panel — Amodei's "we have geopolitical adversaries building the same technology at a similar pace, it's very hard to have an enforcable agreement where they slow down and we slow down" — is the verbal expression of the position laid out at length in the essay. The two are the same testimony in different registers. The essay is the considered version. The panel is the off-the-cuff confirmation.

This is the testimony the rest of the chapter rests on.

Amodei is not asking for help. He believes containment is currently impossible. The Foundation's job is not to grant the help he is asking for. The Foundation's job is to produce the empirical evidence that updates his belief. He has, in writing, named the precondition: a meaningful enforcement mechanism. He has, in writing, allowed that the bioweapon subset is where the toehold exists. He has, in writing, identified the negative-externality problem that voluntary action cannot solve. **The book's prescription is the structural reply to the diagnosis Amodei himself has made.** It is not an argument against him. It is an argument that the gap he has named, when filled, would update the prior he currently holds.

This testimony is not isolated. It compounds with public statements from inside the same labs that have circulated for two years.

Anthropic's Responsible Scaling Policy, version 3.0, dropped the unilateral-pause commitment that earlier versions had carried. Jared Kaplan, the company's chief science officer, explained the decision in plain terms: it did not make sense to make unilateral commitments while competitors were blazing ahead. The commitment that came out of the policy was not "we will slow"; it was "we will publish the conditions under which we would slow if everyone else did." This is not a moral failure. This is the recognition that unilateral commitment without coordination is competitive suicide, and competitive suicide is incompatible with continued operation, which is the precondition for any capacity to influence the field at all.

Mrinank Sharma, a senior member of Anthropic's alignment team, resigned in 2025. His resignation statement named the same fact in the same plain terms. The pressures to ship are winning. He did not say that Anthropic was not trying. He said that the trying was being defeated by the structural pressure of the field.

Leopold Aschenbrenner, formerly of OpenAI's superalignment team, in his "Situational Awareness" essay of mid-2024 — and in subsequent interviews in the local Dwarkesh Patel transcripts archive, including the public conversations on AI nationalization and on lab espionage vulnerability — has described the same dynamic from a different angle. The vulnerability of the frontier labs to espionage means that any safety improvement made by one lab is, on a several-year horizon, available to all labs including the ones least committed to safety. The leak window is short. Differential safety advantage does not survive it.

Daniel Kokotajlo and Scott Alexander, in their AI 2027 month-by-month model of an intelligence explosion, have argued that the problem is not the existence of safety research but its inability to keep pace with capability gains driven by competition. Their disagreement with each other is about the timeline, not about the structural mechanism.

These are not the doomers. These are not the external critics. These are the people who have worked at the frontier labs, who have published from inside them, who have access to internal data the public does not see. They agree on one thing across their disagreements: the constraint on responsible AI development is not a values problem, not a research problem, and not a corporate-governance problem. **It is a coordination problem.** And without an enforcement mechanism, the coordination problem cannot be solved by any one lab acting unilaterally.

The smokescreen of the previous chapter is now visible from a new angle. If "regulate AI like social media" assumes that domestic tort law will eventually create the constraint that corporate self-regulation cannot, the fact this chapter is documenting is that the racers themselves have already named domestic tort as insufficient. They are not asking for K.G.M. They are asking for an enforceable international agreement they do not believe is currently possible. Domestic regulation is not the unit of analysis they are working at. They are working at the geopolitical-coordination layer, and they are saying, on the public record, that the layer is empty.

This is what makes the prescription that follows actionable rather than utopian. It is not the case that the labs must be persuaded that coordination is desirable. They have already said so in public. It is also not the case that the labs must be coerced into compliance against their stated preferences. **The labs have publicly stated that their preference is for coordination, and that the missing piece is enforcement.** The remaining task is to build the missing piece.

The next chapter argues that the missing piece is technically feasible, that it has a working historical precedent in chemical-weapons governance, and that the gap between current capability and required capability is bounded and addressable. The chapter after that names what would have to be funded, and by whom.
