# Chapter 5: Verification Before Treaty

The standard objection to anything that sounds like an arms-control proposal for artificial intelligence is that the nuclear analogy does not transfer. Michael C. Horowitz of the University of Pennsylvania and Lauren A. Kahn of the Council on Foreign Relations have published the strongest version of this objection. Their argument runs in three steps. Nuclear technology's primary strategic value is its weapons applications; artificial intelligence is a general-purpose technology with broad civilian applicability. Nuclear weapons depend on excludable inputs — plutonium, highly enriched uranium — that are scarce and geographically concentrated; artificial intelligence depends on compute, which is fungible and increasingly commoditized. Nuclear weapons deliver binary strategic value; artificial intelligence delivers continuous, graduated value. The deterrence logic of the Non-Proliferation Treaty does not apply.

The argument is correct in the parts that are correct. It is, also, looking at the wrong question.

The lesson from nuclear governance that transfers to artificial intelligence is not the existence of the Non-Proliferation Treaty. It is the difference between two later treaties that are usually discussed as a pair, the Chemical Weapons Convention and the Biological Weapons Convention. The CWC was signed in 1993, twenty-one years after the BWC. The two treaties cover analogous categories of weapons. They have produced opposite results. The Chemical Weapons Convention works. The Biological Weapons Convention does not.

The reason for the difference is simple and is not about the treaties' words. It is about whether the treaties are paired with verification. The Chemical Weapons Convention is enforced by the Organisation for the Prohibition of Chemical Weapons, the OPCW, which conducts approximately two hundred forty-one facility inspections each year. Any state party to the Convention can demand a challenge inspection of any other state party, with no right of refusal. Surprise inspections by treaty partners examining one another have been the operational mechanism. The treaty has functioned through the Syrian civil war, through the war in Ukraine, through the Skripal poisoning. It is imperfect. It is operative.

The Biological Weapons Convention has nothing equivalent. It has an Implementation Support Unit operating with three full-time staff, on a budget that, as the *Bulletin of the Atomic Scientists* noted in March of 2024, is smaller than that of an average McDonald's restaurant. It has no challenge-inspection mechanism. It has no surprise-inspection mechanism. It has no inspectorate. It is, in operational terms, a piece of paper. The Ninth BWC Review Conference in late 2022 finally formed a working group to discuss verification — fifty years after the treaty's signing.

**The lesson is that a treaty without a verification body produces no constraint, and a verification body without a treaty produces a constraint anyway.** The OPCW has working tools that bind state behavior in advance of any state's signature; states that have not signed the CWC nonetheless conform to its norms because the apparatus exists and would catch them if they did not. The BWC, by contrast, produces no constraint because no one is looking. **The verification body is the actual technology of constraint. The treaty is the documentation of what the verification body is constraining.**

Build the body first.

The natural skepticism here is that the technology required to verify the AI equivalent of weapons-grade chemicals does not exist. That would be true if it had been written in 2022. It is no longer true.

NVIDIA's H100 GPU, shipped in 2022 with the Hopper architecture, includes an on-die hardware root of trust, encrypted firmware, attested boot, fault-injection countermeasures, and dedicated AES-GCM hardware that encrypts traffic over the PCI Express bus. The Blackwell architecture, shipped through 2024 and 2025, extended these features. As of 2024, single-GPU confidential computing is production-ready. The Trusted Execution Environment is not a research proposal. It is shipping silicon.

The single-GPU layer does not by itself solve the verification problem, because frontier training runs span hundreds or thousands of GPUs and the cluster-scale verification has not yet been productized. NVIDIA has indicated multi-GPU support in future product generations. The technical proposals that would close the gap — Steven Petrie's embedded security blocks at fewer than forty thousand transistors per block, ten thousand blocks per chip, less than one percent of the H100's total die area; the FlexHEG architecture's auxiliary guarantee processor in a tamper-proof enclosure; Lennart Heim and the Centre for the Governance of AI's training-versus-inference signature distinction; the conditional AI safety treaty technical specification published on arXiv earlier this year — together describe a feasible engineering roadmap. The single-GPU TEE is approximately eighty percent feasible today. The cluster-scale TEE is approximately thirty percent feasible today, with a clear engineering path to closure within two product generations.

**The technical gap is bounded, addressable, and on a timeline shorter than the political timeline of any treaty negotiation.**

The institutional form for the verification body should be modeled on the OPCW, not on the IAEA. The IAEA reports to the United Nations Security Council, which is paralyzed by the P5 vetoes. The OPCW reports to its own Conference of State Parties. This is not a small distinction. The IAEA cannot act on a finding without Security Council authorization, and the Security Council will not authorize action against a member of the Security Council. The OPCW, by contrast, can act because its enforcement is governed by the conference of its own members, not by a body whose membership overlaps with the actors most likely to violate the treaty.

The verification body for artificial intelligence should be Geneva-based. It should employ a multilateral inspectorate with mutual access — Russian and Chinese inspectors examining American facilities, American inspectors examining Russian and Chinese facilities, and inspectors from the Global South examining all of them. It should be funded at OPCW scale, which is approximately eighty million dollars in annual operating costs, plus a capital reserve for the technical infrastructure. It should be capitalized but not controlled by any single funder, including the OpenAI Foundation. The independence of the inspectorate is the property that makes its findings credible to all sides. A body funded by the United States that inspects Chinese facilities is not an inspectorate. It is an intelligence operation. The OPCW's success has been a function of its institutional independence from any single sponsor.

The body should be built before the treaty. It should publish reports. It should, in the period before any state has signed, conduct voluntary consultations with frontier labs and frontier states. It should establish, by the time the political window opens, a track record of credible verification. The treaty, when it arrives, will be the formalization of arrangements the body has already been operating.

This is the answer to Hassabis's question at Davos. He said that a slower pace would require coordination. Amodei said that coordination was impossible without an enforceable agreement. The enforceable agreement is impossible without a body that can verify it. The body is what is missing. The body is what can be built.

The next chapter names what the OpenAI Foundation should do with one hundred eighty billion dollars to build it.
