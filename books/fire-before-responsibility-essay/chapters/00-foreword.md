# Foreword

*The 1000-word essay this book unpacks.*

---

A million years ago, in what is now South Africa, in a cave at Wonderwerk, our ancestors learned to keep fire alive overnight. The hominid who tended that hearth could not have explained what fire was. She did not know about oxidation, did not know that the gathered light was the chemical undoing of wood at the molecular level. She knew that it cooked the meat and kept the predators at the edge of the dark. She knew her hands could be burned. She knew the children must be watched. The first technology was given before the understanding, and the species learned to hold it by tending it, generation upon generation, in circles around a thing more powerful than any of them.

We have done this every time. The agricultural fire, the industrial fire, the fossil fire, and now this one. Each time the species got the fire before the wisdom to tend it, and each time, so far, we have survived. We do not know if we will survive this one.

This essay is about what to do with the OpenAI Foundation's $180 billion if you accept three structural facts almost no one in the public discussion is allowed to say at once.

**The first fact is that AI is not social media.** The proposal to regulate AI like social media — through tort, through bellwether trial, through the slow construction of liability infrastructure that K.G.M. v. Meta finally cracked open in March of this year — is a frame that sounds responsible and is, almost certainly, the smokescreen that buys the labs another twenty years. Social media took twenty years to be regulated. AI does not have twenty years. Social media was a consumer category with no national-security framing and no geopolitical race. AI has both. Social media had a clear adaptive remedy by the time *The Anxious Generation* landed: turn off the screens, set the age limits, get the phones out of schools. AI has no equivalent because AI is not a discrete product. It is becoming the substrate of education, work, government, and defense. There is no parental control on a substrate.

**The second fact is that the labs cannot regulate themselves and they have said so in public.** Dario Amodei's January 2026 essay *The Adolescence of Technology* contains the line: <cite index="0">"Given the incredible economic and military value of the technology, together with the lack of any meaningful enforcement mechanism, I don't see how we could possibly convince them to stop."</cite> The "them" is China. The clause that matters is *the lack of any meaningful enforcement mechanism.* The chief executive officer of the most safety-conscious frontier lab in the world has, in writing, named the gap. Nine days after publication he sat with Demis Hassabis at Davos and confirmed it on stage: enforceable agreement is what is missing. Anthropic dropped its unilateral pause commitment from RSP v3.0 because Jared Kaplan said it didn't make sense to slow while competitors blazed ahead. Mrinank Sharma resigned naming the same fact: the pressures to ship are winning. This is testimony from inside the redline-holders. It is not from the doomers. It is from the racers themselves saying they cannot stop *and naming the precondition that would let them.*

The deeper structural form is that capital under no obligation to slow drives racing regardless of corporate form. Many frontier labs are captive R&D divisions of hyperscalers, whose margin is at the compute and grid layer and who have no incentive to verify anything. The labs that look independent are investor-controlled, and Sam Altman's five-day November 2023 firing-and-reinstatement showed what happens to founders who try to slow. The cap table is the parent. The board is at the mercy of Series E. **Whether the lab is captive or independent does not matter.** The constraint is that no actor with skin in the game has the structural freedom to refuse the race.

**The third fact is what follows from the first two.** The Foundation has $180 billion, no investors, no fiduciary duty to slow-incompatible LPs, and a singular relationship with NVIDIA, the chip vendor whose hardware is the substrate any verification regime would run on. The Foundation is the only actor on Earth with the capital and the structural absence of obligation needed to fund what is missing.

What is missing is not a treaty. It is the *verification body* without which no treaty is possible. The Chemical Weapons Convention works because the Organisation for the Prohibition of Chemical Weapons conducts mutual challenge inspections. The Biological Weapons Convention does not work because it has no inspectorate. AI governance today is the BWC: declarations without verification, summits whose communiqués the United States and the United Kingdom refused to sign in Paris in February 2025, voluntary commitments that erode quietly while the press is looking elsewhere. No frontier state will commit to compute-cap obligations whose compliance it cannot verify. That verification regime does not exist. No state actor is funding it. The labs are racing.

The Foundation should fund the OPCW-equivalent for AI. Not as the United States supervising its rivals, which is the framing that broke the JCPOA. As a Geneva-based multilateral inspectorate with mutual access — Russian and Chinese inspectors examining American facilities, American inspectors examining theirs — endowed at OPCW scale plus capital reserve, capitalized but not controlled by the Foundation, technically equipped with the hardware-attestation regimes (Trusted Execution Environments at the cluster scale, FlexHEG-style guarantee processors, jurisdictional compute reporting) that current research has shown are feasible at single-GPU layer and on roadmap for multi-GPU within two product generations. This is the substrate the treaty would need. Build it before the treaty exists. The treaty becomes politically possible the moment the next capability shock arrives, which it will.

This is not a Pause. This is the structural intervention that lets the racers stop racing without unilateral disadvantage. It is also the only intervention with $180 billion that no other actor on Earth can fund.

If the species got fire before responsibility every time and survived, what we are being asked to do now is build the responsibility ahead of the fire for the first time. That is the wager the rest of this short book defends.
