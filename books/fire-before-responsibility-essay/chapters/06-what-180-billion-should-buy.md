# Chapter 6: What $180 Billion Should Buy

The OpenAI Foundation, by virtue of holding approximately twenty-six percent of OpenAI Group PBC and a Class N veto over mission changes, has a position no other actor on Earth can replicate. The recent recapitalization values the Foundation's stake at one hundred eighty billion dollars. The standard expectation is that the Foundation will deploy this capital through familiar philanthropic instruments — grants to alignment research, grants to ethics centers, conference series, fellowships, the apparatus of the existing AI-safety field.

The argument of this book is that the standard expectation is wrong because it pours new capital into an institutional form that is already at saturation. Coefficient Giving, the successor to Open Philanthropy's AI safety program, processed approximately one hundred forty million dollars in 2025 with three technical-safety grant investigators. The bottleneck the program has been reporting publicly is not capital — it is grantmaker capacity. The Anthropic cofounders' eighty-percent pledge alone, at current valuations, is ten times Coefficient Giving's annual outlay. The marginal one hundred eightieth billion dollars from the Foundation, deployed through the existing apparatus, would not buy more alignment research. It would buy diminishing returns on a flow that the field cannot absorb in its current institutional form.

What the Foundation can buy that no other actor can is the verification substrate the previous chapter described. The five allocations below are not exhaustive. They are the structural minimum.

**Hardware verification research and development. Thirty billion dollars over eight years.** Multi-GPU TEE attestation for cluster-scale training runs. NVIDIA has indicated this on its roadmap; the Foundation can fund the engineering acceleration that compresses the timeline from three product generations to one. Petrie's embedded security blocks. The FlexHEG flexible hardware-enabled guarantee architecture. Heim and Koessler's training-versus-inference signature work. The conditional AI safety treaty technical specification. None of this is hypothetical. All of it has been published. What is missing is the funded engineering pipeline that would take the published designs into shipping silicon and shipping cluster software within thirty-six months.

**The verification body itself. Sixty billion dollars endowed.** Geneva-based. Modeled on the Organisation for the Prohibition of Chemical Weapons, not on the International Atomic Energy Agency. Multilateral inspectorate with mutual access — Russian and Chinese inspectors examining American facilities, American inspectors examining theirs. Endowed at OPCW scale, which is approximately eighty million dollars in annual operating costs, plus capital reserve sufficient to support one hundred or more full-time technical staff and a fleet of attestation systems for two decades. Capitalized but not controlled by the Foundation. Independent board. Civil-society oversight. Technical-staff hiring outside the Bay Area filter. The independence is the property that makes the findings credible across the political alliances they will need to cross.

**Track-II diplomatic infrastructure. Twenty-five billion dollars over ten years.** Pugwash conferences ran on small foundation money for thirty years before they made arms control possible during the Cold War. Pugwash itself, founded in 1957, was funded for its first two decades by Cyrus Eaton on a budget that would not register in the line items of contemporary AI-safety budgets. The result was that when the political window for SALT, the Anti-Ballistic Missile Treaty, and the Nuclear Test-Ban Treaty arrived, the diplomatic substrate had been built. The analogous infrastructure for artificial intelligence is empty in 2026. The Foundation can fund bilateral United States-China safety dialogues. It can fund onboarding for India, Brazil, Indonesia, the Republic of Korea, Japan. It can fund the back-channel working groups that produce draft treaty language before any state signs. No state actor will fund this — the United States administration has cut funding for the AI Safety Institute; the United Kingdom institute runs on sixty-six million pounds annually; the European Union has redirected energy from regulation to deployment. The Foundation can fund what the state-actor track has been hollowed out from doing.

**The legal and regulatory infrastructure of mutual obligation. Twenty-five billion dollars over ten years.** Harmonize artificial-intelligence compute regulation across the Wassenaar Arrangement signatories. Fund the technical-legal scholarship that produces the harmonized statute language. Fund the customs-and-export-control infrastructure in allied capitals that would make a multilateral compute-cap regime implementable in domestic law. Adjacent: capitalize K.G.M.-style litigation finance for AI-harm cases in the United States and parallel jurisdictions, as a complementary domestic mechanism — not the primary one. The plaintiffs' bar matters. It is also not enough.

**Reserve and contingency. Forty billion dollars.** Operating reserve for the verification body through political turbulence. Bridge funding for any frontier lab that holds a redline and loses market position to a competitor that did not — the Anthropic Pentagon-refusal analog generalized. Contingency for the capability shocks the Foundation cannot predict.

**Total. One hundred eighty billion dollars.**

What the Foundation should refuse to fund.

First, more alignment research at the existing labs. The field is at saturation. Adding capital reproduces the institutional form rather than the substrate.

Second, AI-for-the-Global-South deployments authored from outside it. The empirical track record of philanthropic deployment in the South — AGRA's missed yields, OLPC's null Peruvian outcomes, the Free Basics ban in India, Worldcoin's iris collection in Buenos Aires during 288% inflation, the Sama-OpenAI Kenya labelers paid 1.32 to 2 dollars per hour to filter trauma material — is the empirical case against the deployment frame. The Foundation should fund sovereign absorption capacity within Global South countries, on their terms, under their data-sovereignty regimes. It should not deploy onto them.

Third, single-tranche grants above one billion dollars to any institution whose governance has not been audited for absorption capacity by an independent third party with the power to refuse the gift. The Corporation for Public Broadcasting voted unanimously to dissolve in January of 2026, fifty-eight years in, after Congress rescinded one billion dollars in appropriated funding the previous summer. Pat Harrison's resignation memo named the cause: single-funder dependency. Every ten-billion-dollar grantee of the Foundation will look like CPB in five years if the architectural problem is not solved upstream of the disbursement.

Fourth, anything that reproduces the K.G.M.-style social-media-analogy timeline as the primary mechanism. The plaintiffs' bar should be funded as a complementary domestic mechanism, not as the centerpiece. The centerpiece is the verification substrate.

Fifth, AI-safety summits with no operational outputs. The Bletchley-Park-to-Seoul-to-Paris trajectory has demonstrated that the soft-law summit track has been hollowed out. The Foundation should not fund more theater.

These five refusals are part of the proposal. The clarity of the refusal is what makes the allocation defensible against the absorption critique that destroys most large-philanthropic deployments. The Foundation that knows what it will not fund will not have its capital absorbed by the institutional form that produced the gap.

The second of the refusals — the one against AI-for-the-Global-South deployments authored from outside it — is the one the narrator wants to defend at length, because it is the one she is least sure of, and because the strongest counter-argument to the book's prescription lives there. The next chapter is that defense. The chapter after is the receipt the narrator is offering on her own scale, so the proposal is not pure talk.
