# Chapter 4: The Wealth Effect

There is a fourth layer beneath the three the previous chapters have set out, and it is the layer that explains why no political actor will help the racers stop racing even though the racers themselves have asked for help.

The fourth layer is the public market.

It has been seventeen years since the 2008 financial crisis. In those seventeen years, the United States stock market has produced, on long horizon, the strongest aggregate returns of any equivalent period in the recorded history of public capital markets. The cumulative gain has not been distributed evenly. By April of 2026, seven companies — Alphabet, Amazon, Apple, Meta, Microsoft, Nvidia, Tesla — accounted for thirty-four percent of the S&P 500's total market capitalization, up from twenty-eight percent at the end of 2023. The collective capital expenditure of those seven companies has risen from roughly ten percent of the S&P 500's total capital expenditure six years ago to roughly thirty percent today. The growth narrative of the index, the wealth effect that feeds consumer spending and pension solvency and tax receipts, has been compressed into seven names. The compression accelerated after November of 2022, when ChatGPT was released and the artificial-intelligence story became the only story.

The price-to-earnings ratio of the top ten companies in the S&P 500 is, as of this writing, around fifty. The total value of all United States corporate equity stands at three hundred sixty percent of nominal gross domestic product. This is an all-time high. It exceeds the peak of the dot-com bubble in 2000.

The chief executive officers of the largest of these companies have begun, in public, to describe what they are presiding over.

Sam Altman, asked directly in 2025 whether investors were overexcited about artificial intelligence, said yes. He predicted that someone — he did not say who — would lose a phenomenal amount of money. He used the word "insane." Sundar Pichai, the chief executive of Alphabet, told the BBC in late 2025 that there was "irrationality" in the AI boom and that "no company is going to be immune, including us" when the correction came. Jamie Dimon, the chief executive of JPMorgan, has said publicly that he believes a lot of the AI money will be lost and that the probability of a meaningful drop in equities over the next two years is higher than the market is currently pricing.

These are not bears. These are the operators of the largest enterprises in the global economy describing, with their full presence on the public record, the market structure they are participating in.

The structure they are describing is one in which the appearance of macroeconomic stability — the consumer spending, the wealth effect on housing, the funding of state and corporate pensions, the tax receipts that allow the federal government to operate — depends to an unprecedented degree on the continued upward trajectory of the seven companies whose growth narrative is now the artificial-intelligence narrative.

This is the political constraint that the previous three chapters do not by themselves explain.

Even if the captive-research-and-development structure could be undone. Even if investor incentives could be redirected. Even if the racing labs collectively decided to slow. The political class of the United States — and, by extension, the political class of every country whose pension funds and sovereign-wealth accounts are exposed to the S&P 500 — cannot tolerate the slowing. Slowing the artificial-intelligence narrative means slowing the only growth story that has been keeping the post-2008 wealth effect aloft. Slowing the wealth effect means recession. Recession means electoral consequence. **No administration that allows the AI bubble to be slowed by its own regulatory action will survive the next election cycle.**

This is not a prediction that the bubble will pop, and it is not a moral indictment of the political class. The narrator does not know whether the artificial-intelligence valuations are a bubble in the technical sense or whether they will resolve into the unit economics that justify them. Reasonable people disagree, and reasonable arguments exist on both sides. The structural claim of this chapter does not rest on knowing the answer. It rests on the observation that **the political class cannot know the answer either, and is making decisions under that uncertainty.** A political class that has presided over seventeen years of post-crisis recovery in which the recovery has been narrowly concentrated in technology cannot, at the moment when the technology becomes a strategic concern, take actions whose consequence under the bubble-pop scenario would be to unwind the recovery on its own watch. The political response is shaped by the *possibility* of bubble dynamics, not by their certainty. And the response, accordingly, has been — and continues to be — to frame the artificial-intelligence buildout as a national-security necessity that must not be slowed.

The Pentagon's October 2025 demand on Anthropic, which Anthropic refused, was an early visible expression of this. The renaming of the United States AI Safety Institute to the Center for AI Standards and Innovation, and its effective defanging in the months that followed, is a second. The United States government's refusal to sign even the non-binding communiqué at the Paris AI Action Summit in February of 2025 was a third. The pattern is not random. The pattern is the political class doing what its own structural position requires it to do, which is to refuse any constraint on the buildout that could be perceived as causing the correction.

The buildout has therefore acquired a political constituency that is broader than the labs themselves. The pension fund manager whose fund holds Microsoft and Nvidia is now structurally aligned with the racers, not against them. The state treasurer whose tax receipts depend on capital-gains realizations from technology stocks is structurally aligned with the racers. The voter whose 401(k) has appreciated past the threshold at which retirement is plausible is structurally aligned with the racers. **The artificial-intelligence buildout has acquired a coalition of beneficiaries whose interest in continued buildout is now greater than their interest in any safety property the buildout might violate.**

This is the missing layer. This is why, even after Hassabis and Amodei sit on a stage at Davos and say in public that they would prefer a slower pace, no political actor will deliver the slower pace they are asking for. The political actors are not free. The political actors are at the end of a wealth-effect chain that runs, ultimately, back to the racing the labs are doing.

The implication, for the prescription that follows, is that the funding source for any verification regime cannot be the political class and cannot be the labs and cannot be the investors. It has to be an actor structurally outside all three coalitions. The Foundation, by virtue of its capital scale and its absence of fiduciary obligation, is the only actor on Earth that satisfies the description. **This is not because the Foundation is virtuous. This is because the structural position is unique, and the structural position is what makes the prescription possible at all.**

The next chapter argues that the technology to do the verification exists, that there is a working historical precedent for the institutional form, and that the gap between current capability and required capability is bounded.
