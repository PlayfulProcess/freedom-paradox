# Chapter 2: Captive R&D, Investor Control, and the Constraint Beneath Both

The mainstream financial press has spent two years trying to answer the question of when foundation-model companies make money. The press has produced a great quantity of writing and very few clean answers. The reason it has produced very few clean answers is that the question is malformed.

**Foundation-model companies do not make money because they are not, in economic substance, the right unit of analysis.**

The frontier lab is the intellectual-property and talent core of a hyperscaler-lab combined entity. Its losses are the accounting result of a transfer-pricing structure that was designed at the entity's founding to put profits at the parent and costs at the lab. Asking when OpenAI starts making money is, structurally, the same question as asking when Microsoft Research starts making money. The question is malformed at the unit of analysis. With the right boundary — lab plus the hyperscaler revenue its workloads generate — the answer is mundane. The combined entity is marginal-cost profitable today. The proof is in the cloud-segment 10-Ks.

The pattern repeats across every major lab-hyperscaler pairing. Amazon committed up to twenty-five billion dollars to Anthropic in late April 2026; Anthropic committed one hundred billion dollars to AWS over ten years for five gigawatts of Trainium capacity. At AWS's roughly thirty-five percent operating margin, that compute commitment returns approximately thirty-five billion dollars at the operating line — positive net present value against Amazon's investment before any equity is counted. Microsoft's six hundred twenty-five billion dollars in commercial remaining performance obligations is forty-five percent OpenAI-attributable; OpenAI reportedly paid Microsoft twelve billion dollars or more for inference since 2024 while Microsoft pays OpenAI a twenty-percent revenue share on resold services. Capital flows from hyperscaler to lab, returns as cloud revenue at full margin, books as matching expense on the lab's profit-and-loss statement. The hyperscaler funds research and development through an arms-length subsidiary, captures inference economics at compute margin, and books equity upside as option value. The lab "loses money" the way an internal R&D division loses money — by design, not by failure.

Even Stargate, which originated in 2024 as OpenAI's attempt to escape this structure by owning its own data centers, collapsed back into it. Investors would not fund the upfront capital expenditure on terms OpenAI could survive. SoftBank took financial responsibility; OpenAI took operational responsibility. The most ambitious attempt by a frontier lab to escape the captive structure ended with the lab in possession of the keys but not the deed.

This is the standard version of the captive-R&D thesis. It is correct as far as it goes. It is also, by itself, not enough.

---

The narrator was advised, in writing this chapter, by a consideration that sharpens the argument and is worth naming directly. The deeper structural fact is not that the labs are captive R&D divisions of hyperscalers. The deeper structural fact is that **capital under no obligation to slow drives racing regardless of corporate form.** The captive-R&D structure is one expression. The investor-controlled "independent" frontier lab is another expression. Both produce the same equilibrium because the binding constraint is not the corporate structure. The binding constraint is the absence of any external floor on the speed of competition.

There is testimony for this. Anthropic, the most safety-conscious of the frontier labs, dropped its unilateral pause commitment from the 3.0 version of its Responsible Scaling Policy in 2024. The reason given by Jared Kaplan, the chief science officer, was that it did not make sense to make unilateral commitments while competitors were blazing ahead. Anthropic's seven cofounders pledged eighty percent of their wealth to philanthropy in January of 2026. The pledge is not a substitute for the unilateral pause. It is a downstream artifact. The cofounders cannot make Anthropic slow down; they can only direct what happens to their own wealth after the fact.

Mrinank Sharma, a senior alignment researcher, resigned from Anthropic in 2025 with a public statement that the pressures to ship are winning. This is testimony from inside the most mission-locked frontier lab. If unilateral commitment does not hold there, it will not hold at any frontier lab whose cap table includes Series-E investors with normal fiduciary expectations. The fiduciary expectation is not "slow down." The fiduciary expectation is "deploy capital, return capital, redeploy capital, faster."

Sam Altman's firing in November of 2023 and his reinstatement five days later was the same fact in operational form. The board of a non-profit-controlled frontier lab attempted to remove the chief executive officer. The investor coalition reorganized itself within a week and returned the chief executive officer to the seat. The investor coalition was the parent in this case, even when the formal corporate structure indicated otherwise. The non-profit board's authority on paper did not survive the actual capital relationship.

These are not three failures. These are three forms of the same fact. **In the absence of an external regulatory floor, capital with normal fiduciary obligations drives racing regardless of how the corporate entity is structured.** Captive R&D divisions of hyperscalers cannot slow because the parent will not. Investor-controlled independent labs cannot slow because the cap table will not. Founder-controlled labs that try to slow will be replaced. The corporate-form variation is a surface phenomenon. The constraint beneath it is structural.

---

This matters for what follows because it determines the answer to who can fund the verification regime that the next chapters will argue is the only intervention that can bind the race. If the labs themselves cannot slow, the labs themselves cannot fund a slow-down apparatus. The hyperscalers will not, because their margin is at compute and grid and they have no incentive to verify any constraint that would limit compute consumption. The investors will not, because verification has no return profile. The state actors will not, because the United States administration has cut funding for the AI Safety Institute and renamed and defanged the Center for AI Standards and Innovation; because the United Kingdom has limited its institute to a sixty-six-million-pound annual budget; because the European Union has moved its energy from regulation to deployment under competitive pressure from the United States.

The Foundation has one hundred eighty billion dollars, no investors, no fiduciary obligation to limited partners whose return profile is incompatible with slowing down, and a singular relationship with NVIDIA, the chip vendor whose hardware is the substrate any verification regime would run on. **The Foundation is the only actor on Earth with the capital and the structural absence of obligation.** Not because the Foundation is virtuous. Because the structural position is unique.

The next chapter is the testimony from the racers themselves that they cannot slow without coordination. The chapter after presents what coordination would have to look like, and why the verification body is the substrate without which no coordination is achievable.
