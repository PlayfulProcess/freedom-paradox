# Chapter 1: The Smokescreen

Every time the species has been given a new fire, the public discussion has eventually settled around an argument about responsibility. The arguments take different shapes in different centuries. In ours, the argument has begun to settle around the social-media analogy: that AI is the new Facebook, that the path to accountability runs through tort and product-design liability, that the slow construction of liability infrastructure that culminated in K.G.M. v. Meta in March of 2026 — the first social-media-addiction bellwether verdict, $6 million against Meta and YouTube, the first substantive judicial breach of the Section-230 shield in thirty years — will eventually do the same work for AI.

The K.G.M. ruling is real. It matters. The narrator of this book has written about it elsewhere; the larger book project this essay attends names it as a receipt. This chapter is not arguing that K.G.M. is unimportant. It is arguing something narrower and harder.

**The argument is that "regulate AI like social media" is the smokescreen that buys the labs another twenty years.** And that anyone proposing the K.G.M. arc as the *primary* mechanism of AI accountability is, perhaps unintentionally, accepting the timeline of a problem that is structurally different from the one we have.

There are five asymmetries.

**The first is time.** Facebook was launched in 2004. The first multi-state attorneys-general lawsuit was filed in October 2023. The first JCCP bellwether trial verdict landed in March 2026. Twenty-two years from launch to verdict. The Surgeon General's advisory came in 2023. *The Anxious Generation* was published in 2024. The federal MDL contains over two thousand three hundred cases as of the latest count. This is what the legal system looks like when it is functioning. It is also what it looks like when it has been given two decades to gather evidence, develop legal theory, organize a plaintiffs' bar, and finally produce a verdict that opens the door for the next thousand cases. **AI does not have two decades.** The current frontier labs are training successive model generations on year-and-a-half cycles. By the time the first AI-harm bellwether trial returns its verdict — projected, optimistically, for the late 2020s — the technology will have moved through three or four generations. The legal arc that worked for social media cannot, by its own pace, work for AI.

**The second is national-security framing.** Social media was, throughout its scaling, a consumer-product category. There was no Pentagon partnership. There was no defense-technology argument. There was no equivalent to the OpenAI-Stargate $500 billion compute commitment, the Pentagon's October 2025 demand that Anthropic make Claude available for autonomous weapons and mass surveillance, the Anthropic refusal and the subsequent Department of War lawsuit and the parallel OpenAI deal announced the same afternoon. AI is being framed by the United States government as a civilizational asset whose advance must not be slowed, by China's leadership as a strategic priority, by the European Union as both a regulatory subject and a competitive necessity. The legal infrastructure that treats Meta as a consumer-products defendant cannot treat OpenAI the same way. The Pentagon does not appear in the K.G.M. complaint. It appears in the AI complaints from the first paragraph onward.

**The third is the geopolitical race.** Social media had no equivalent. There was no rival-civilizational competition, no DeepSeek moment in the social-media space, no equivalent of the BIS export-control sequence that has been the operational AI governance regime since October 2022. AI has all of these. DeepSeek V4-Pro, released in April 2026 — open weights, theoretical 85% inference gross margins, matching frontier closed models on benchmark performance at fourteen percent of the price — is not a regulatory event. It is a strategic event. The Federal Reserve does not regulate the trade balance. Meta is not Hisense.

**The fourth is industry structure.** This is the deepest of the five and it deserves its own chapter, which follows. In short: the frontier AI labs are not firms in the way Meta is a firm. Most are captive research-and-development divisions of hyperscalers, whose accounting losses are by design and whose actual P&L lives at the parent. The remaining few that look independent are investor-controlled in ways that prevent unilateral slowdown. The K.G.M. complaint sues a firm with shareholders. The AI complaints, when they finally arrive at trial, will be suing cost centers.

**The fifth is the adaptive remedy.** By the time *The Anxious Generation* was published, parents and schools had a remedy ladder they could implement bottom-up: no smartphones before high school, no social media accounts before sixteen, phone-free schools, more independent play. The four norms are imperfect, contested, and genuinely available. Whether or not Jonathan Haidt's causal claims hold, the remedy framework is implementable in the household and the school district. AI has nothing equivalent. There is no "phone-free school" version of "the substrate of education has been replaced by AI." There is no parental control on a tutor that has become the default mode of homework. There is no setting that turns off the AI a teenager's college admissions essay was written through. AI is not a thing kids use that can be restricted. It is a layer that is being added under everything kids touch, and the layer is being added by adults whose institutions are, for a different set of reasons, also adopting it as their substrate.

The five asymmetries together produce an argument that is uncomfortable for the responsible discourse in this category. The argument is that the frame we are settling into is wrong, and that the people who care most about AI accountability — including the narrator of this book — are at risk of advocating, in good faith, for a regulatory architecture that will arrive too late to do the work the architecture would otherwise do.

The fire we are now learning to tend will not be tended by the same circle of hands that learned to tend the previous one. The previous one is still being learned. We have not had time to teach a generation how to tend the social-media fire before this new one was placed on the table.

What follows is an argument for what to do given that fact. It will not be an argument for stopping. It will be an argument for what the species has actually done every other time the fire arrived: we did not extinguish it; we built the conditions under which it could be tended.

The conditions for tending this one are not on the table. They have to be built. The next chapter argues that the labs cannot build them, and the chapter after argues that the labs themselves know it. The two together set up the prescription that the rest of the book defends.
