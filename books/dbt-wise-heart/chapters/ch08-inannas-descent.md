# Inanna's Descent

*Distress-Tolerance*

## The Story

Inanna was the Queen of Heaven. She had everything — a throne, a crown, jewels, power.

But she needed to visit the underworld. And the underworld has rules.

At the first gate, the gatekeeper said: 'Remove your crown.'

'But I'm the QUEEN,' said Inanna.

'Remove your crown.'

She removed it.

At the second gate: 'Remove your jewels.'
At the third: 'Remove your robe.'
At each of the seven gates, something was taken.

By the time Inanna reached the bottom, she had nothing. No crown. No robe. No power. No identity.

She was naked in the dark.

And she stayed there. Three days.

She didn't fight it. She didn't pretend it wasn't happening. She didn't try to trick her way out.

She accepted where she was.

And only THEN did help come. Her faithful friend sent messengers. Slowly, gate by gate, she rose back up.

But she was different now. Not because the dark changed her. Because she stopped fighting the dark — and that changed everything.

## The Song

I can't change this — it's here right now.
I'll breathe and bend but I won't break down.

## The Skill

This story teaches RADICAL ACCEPTANCE — accepting reality as it is, even when it hurts. Not liking it, not approving of it, just stopping the fight against what IS. Inanna didn't fight the gates. She walked through them.

## For Parents

DBT Skill: RADICAL ACCEPTANCE (Distress Tolerance). Radical acceptance means fully accepting reality without judgment — not approving of pain, but stopping the war against it. Pain + non-acceptance = suffering. Pain + acceptance = pain (which passes). Practice: When something hard happens, model saying: 'I don't like this, AND it's what's happening. What can I do from here?' The 'and' is everything.

## Try This

Hold an ice cube in your hand. It's cold and uncomfortable. Now try two things: first, fight it — tense up, say 'I hate this,' squeeze hard. Then try again: hold it gently, breathe, say 'This is cold. It will melt.' Which one hurts more?

## Reflection

What's something hard in your life that you've been fighting against? What would it feel like to stop fighting — not to give up, but to stop the war with reality?
