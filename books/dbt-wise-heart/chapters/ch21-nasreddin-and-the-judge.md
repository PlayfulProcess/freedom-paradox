# Nasreddin and the Judge

*Interpersonal-Effectiveness*

## The Story

A man came to the judge and said: 'Nasreddin's donkey bit me! I demand payment!'

The judge turned to Nasreddin. 'Is this true?'

Nasreddin said: 'Your Honor, perhaps the man provoked the donkey.'

The man shouted: 'The donkey is a menace! I demand you give me Nasreddin's donkey as payment!'

Nasreddin smiled. 'Your Honor, may I speak with the donkey privately for a moment?'

The judge, amused, agreed.

Nasreddin went to his donkey. He whispered in its ear. Then he came back.

'Well?' asked the judge. 'What did the donkey say?'

'The donkey says,' replied Nasreddin, 'that the man smelled like carrots and the donkey was confused. But the donkey apologizes and promises to bite only actual carrots in the future.'

The court laughed. The man blushed. The judge dismissed the case.

Nasreddin kept his donkey, his dignity, and his self-respect. He didn't lie (the man probably DID smell like carrots). He didn't attack. He didn't apologize for something that wasn't his fault.

He was Fair. He was (no unnecessary) Apologies. He Stuck to his values. He was Truthful.

And he did it all with a smile.

## The Song

Stay true to you, don't say sorry when you're not —
Your self-respect is all you've got.

## The Skill

This story teaches FAST — keeping your self-respect in relationships. Be Fair (to yourself AND others). No unnecessary Apologies. Stick to your values. Be Truthful. You can be kind without losing yourself.

## For Parents

DBT Skill: FAST (Interpersonal Effectiveness — Self-Respect Effectiveness). FAST skills protect self-respect: be Fair, (no unnecessary) Apologies, Stick to values, be Truthful. Children often over-apologize or abandon their own position to keep the peace. Practice: When your child apologizes for something that isn't their fault, gently say: 'Was that really yours to apologize for?' And when they stand their ground honestly and kindly, celebrate it.

## Try This

The Nasreddin Check: Next time someone wants you to apologize for something that isn't your fault, ask yourself the FAST questions: (1) Is this Fair to me? (2) Am I apologizing because I'm wrong, or just to make them stop being mad? (3) Does this match my values? (4) Am I being Truthful?

## Reflection

Do you sometimes say sorry just to make people stop being upset? What would Nasreddin do instead?
