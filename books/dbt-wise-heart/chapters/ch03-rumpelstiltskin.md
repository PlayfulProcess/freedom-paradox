# Rumpelstiltskin

*Mindfulness*

## The Story

A strange little man appeared and spun straw into gold for a young woman. But he wanted something terrible in return — her first child.

'Unless,' he laughed, 'you can guess my NAME!'

The young woman was terrified. But she didn't give up.

She sent messengers across the kingdom. She listened. She gathered clues.

And on the third night, she said: 'Your name is... RUMPELSTILTSKIN.'

The little man screamed and vanished.

Because when you can NAME something — really name it, say exactly what it is — it loses its power over you.

The feeling that was huge and dark and nameless? When you say 'I feel scared' or 'I feel angry' or 'I feel sad' — you've named it.

And named things can't spin you into straw anymore.

## The Song

Name what you feel, say it out loud —
Feelings named aren't half as loud.

## The Skill

This story teaches DESCRIBE — putting words on what you feel. A feeling that has no name feels huge and scary. When you name it — 'I'm angry,' 'I'm sad,' 'I'm scared' — it gets smaller. You can hold it.

## For Parents

DBT Skill: DESCRIBE (Core Mindfulness — 'What' Skills). Describing means putting words on experience — labeling feelings, thoughts, and sensations without judgment. Research shows that simply naming an emotion (affect labeling) reduces amygdala activation. Practice: When your child is upset, help them name it precisely: 'That sounds like frustration' or 'I wonder if that feeling is disappointment.' The naming IS the regulation.

## Try This

The Rumpelstiltskin Game: When a big feeling comes, try to give it the most exact name you can. Not just 'bad' — is it disappointed? embarrassed? jealous? left out? The more exact the name, the more the feeling shrinks.

## Reflection

Has a feeling ever felt huge and scary until you said its name out loud? What happened then?
