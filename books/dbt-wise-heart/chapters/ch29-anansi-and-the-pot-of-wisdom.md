# Anansi and the Pot of Wisdom

*Emotional-Regulation*

## The Story

Anansi had a problem.

He'd gathered ALL the wisdom in the world into a big clay pot. Every clever thought, every good idea, every solution to every problem — all in one pot.

Now he wanted to hide it at the top of a tall tree, where no one else could reach it.

He tied the pot to his FRONT and started climbing. But the pot kept bumping the trunk. He slipped. He scrambled. He fell.

His small son watched from below.

'Father,' the boy said, 'if you tied the pot to your BACK, it wouldn't bump the tree.'

Anansi looked down. He looked at the pot. He looked at the tree.

The boy was right.

Anansi was so startled that he dropped the pot. It smashed on the ground. All the wisdom scattered everywhere — into the rivers, the forests, the villages. That's why wisdom is found everywhere, in everyone, and no one person has all of it.

But here's the hidden teaching: Anansi had a problem and he tried one approach. It didn't work. His son saw the problem differently and offered a solution.

This is problem solving:
1. What's the problem? (The pot bumps the tree.)
2. What have I tried? (Tying it to the front.)
3. What else could I try? (Tying it to the back.)
4. Pick one and test it.
5. Did it work? If not, try the next idea.

Anansi was so attached to his first idea that he couldn't see the simple answer. His son, with fresh eyes, could.

## The Song

What's the problem? What's the plan?
Try one thing and try again.
Fresh eyes see what stuck eyes miss —
Every problem starts like this.

## The Skill

This story teaches PROBLEM SOLVING — the step-by-step skill of naming a problem, brainstorming solutions, picking one, testing it, and adjusting. Sometimes the answer is simple. Sometimes you need someone else's fresh eyes. The key is: don't keep banging the pot against the tree.

## For Parents

DBT Skill: PROBLEM SOLVING (Emotion Regulation). This skill is used AFTER checking the facts — when the emotion fits the situation and the situation CAN be changed. Steps: (1) Describe the problem specifically, (2) Check the facts, (3) Identify your goal, (4) Brainstorm solutions (many, including silly ones), (5) Choose one, (6) Try it, (7) Evaluate. Practice: When your child is stuck, resist solving it for them. Instead, walk through the steps: 'What's the pot? What's the tree? What's bumping?' Let THEM find the back-of-the-tree solution.

## Try This

The Anansi Method: Next time you're stuck, write down the problem at the top of a page. Then list FIVE possible solutions — including weird ones. Circle the one you want to try first. Try it. If it doesn't work, circle the next one. You're not failing — you're climbing the tree.

## Reflection

When you're stuck on a problem, do you keep trying the same thing harder? Or do you step back and try a different approach? What helps you see the 'back of the tree'?
