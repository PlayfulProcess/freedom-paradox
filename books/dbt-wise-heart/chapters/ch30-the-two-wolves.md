# The Two Wolves

*Interpersonal-Effectiveness*

## The Story

A grandparent sat with their grandchild by the fire.

'There are two wolves inside me,' the grandparent said. 'They fight all the time.'

'One is angry, jealous, full of self-pity, resentful, and afraid. The other is joyful, peaceful, loving, generous, and kind.'

The child's eyes went wide. 'Which one wins?'

The grandparent smiled. 'The one I feed.'

But here's what the simple version misses:

The grandparent DIDN'T say 'kill the angry wolf.' Didn't say 'the angry wolf is bad.' Said: 'They fight all the time.' Both wolves are IN there. Both are real. Both have teeth.

The angry wolf protects you when you're in danger. The kind wolf connects you to others. You need BOTH.

The skill isn't choosing one wolf. It's learning which one to feed in which moment. That's the middle path — not 'always be kind' and not 'always be angry' but 'both are real, and I can choose.'

When someone hurts you: feed the angry wolf enough to protect yourself, then feed the kind wolf so you don't become cruel.

When someone loves you: feed the kind wolf fully, and let the angry wolf rest.

The dialectic: two things that seem opposite can BOTH be true. You can be angry AND loving. You can set a boundary AND be kind. You can say no AND still care.

The wolves don't have to fight. They can learn to run side by side.

## The Song

Two things true at the same time —
Both are real, and both are mine.
Walk the middle, hold them both —
That's the path of wisest growth.

## The Skill

This story teaches WALKING THE MIDDLE PATH — the dialectical skill of holding two opposite truths at the same time. Not 'either/or' but 'both/and.' You can be angry and still be kind. You can need help and still be strong. The middle path isn't compromise — it's holding the whole truth.

## For Parents

DBT Skill: WALKING THE MIDDLE PATH / DIALECTICS (Interpersonal Effectiveness — Adolescent module). The core dialectic: two seemingly opposite things can both be true. I can love you AND be furious. You can be doing your best AND need to do better. This skill reduces all-or-nothing thinking, which is the driver of most interpersonal conflict. Practice: When your child makes an either/or statement ('She ALWAYS does that!' or 'NOBODY likes me'), gently add 'AND': 'She does that sometimes AND she's also been kind.' Both wolves.

## Try This

The Both-Wolves Game: Think of something you feel two ways about. Maybe a friend who's fun AND sometimes mean. Or a rule that's annoying AND keeps you safe. Write both on paper. Can you hold both at the same time without choosing? That's the middle path.

## Reflection

Is there something in your life where two opposite things are both true? Can you name both without choosing one?
