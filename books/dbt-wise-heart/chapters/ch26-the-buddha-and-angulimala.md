# The Buddha and Angulimala

*Distress-Tolerance*

## The Story

Angulimala was a murderer. He wore a necklace of fingers. Everyone ran from him — soldiers, merchants, entire villages.

The Buddha walked toward him.

Angulimala ran at the Buddha, sword raised. But no matter how fast he ran, he couldn't catch up. The Buddha was only walking.

'STOP!' Angulimala screamed. 'Stand still!'

The Buddha turned. His face was soft. Not smiling exactly — just open. A half-smile. His hands were at his sides, palms open, fingers relaxed.

'I have stopped,' the Buddha said. 'It is YOU who have not stopped.'

And Angulimala understood. The running, the violence, the rage — it was all because he couldn't stop.

The Buddha's body was calm because his BODY had chosen calm. Not his thoughts — those come and go. His body. The half-smile on his face sent a message to his brain: 'We are safe. We are willing.' The open hands said: 'We are not fighting.'

Angulimala dropped his sword.

Not because the Buddha argued with him.
Because the Buddha's body showed him what peace looked like.

Your body speaks to your brain. A half-smile — even when you don't feel like smiling — tells your brain: 'We're okay.' Open hands — willing hands — tell your brain: 'We're not fighting anymore.'

## The Song

Soft face, open hands held wide —
Let my body change inside.

## The Skill

This story teaches HALF-SMILE AND WILLING HANDS — using your body to change your feelings. A tiny half-smile (not fake-happy, just soft) sends calm signals to your brain. Hands open, palms up, fingers relaxed — that's willing hands. Your body leads; your mind follows.

## For Parents

DBT Skill: HALF-SMILE AND WILLING HANDS (Distress Tolerance — Reality Acceptance). This is a body-based acceptance skill drawn from Buddhist meditation and facial feedback research. The half-smile activates the zygomaticus major muscle, which sends signals to the brain that reduce stress hormones. Willing hands (palms up, fingers relaxed) counteract the body's fight/flight posture. Practice: Try it yourself first. When you're frustrated, let your face soften into the tiniest hint of a smile. Open your hands. Notice what shifts. Then teach your child: 'Buddha face, open hands.'

## Try This

Buddha Face practice: Sit quietly. Let your face relax completely. Now let the tiniest smile appear — barely there, just softness. Let your hands rest on your knees, palms up, fingers loose. Breathe three breaths. Does anything change? Many people feel a tiny shift — a softening, a loosening. That's your body talking to your brain.

## Reflection

What does your body do when you're angry? Fists? Tight jaw? Scrunched face? What happens when you try the opposite — soft face, open hands? Does it feel fake, or does something shift?
