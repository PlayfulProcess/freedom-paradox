# Scheherazade's Thousand Nights

*Distress-Tolerance*

## The Story

The king had gone mad with grief. Every morning, another death. The kingdom was drowning in his pain.

Scheherazade knew she couldn't FIX the king's grief. She couldn't argue him out of it or fight him. She could only survive until morning.

So she distracted.

Not foolishly. Not pretending the danger wasn't real. But wisely — because sometimes the only way through unbearable pain is to give your mind something else to hold for a while.

She told stories. (Activities.)
She helped the king imagine other people's worlds. (Contributing — to his understanding.)
She showed him that others had suffered too. (Comparisons.)
She made him feel wonder instead of rage. (Opposite Emotions.)
She pushed the worst thoughts away — not forever, just for tonight. (Pushing away.)
She filled his mind with new Thoughts — clever plots, surprising twists.
And she spoke to his Senses — her voice was calm, the room was warm, the night was soft.

One thousand and one nights.

Not because distraction solves everything. But because sometimes you need to survive until morning. And morning always comes.

## The Song

When the pain is way too much,
Find a story, sight, or touch.
Distract your mind — it's not a lie —
Sometimes surviving IS the why.

## The Skill

This story teaches ACCEPTS — seven distraction skills for surviving overwhelming moments. Activities, Contributing, Comparisons, Emotions (opposite), Pushing away, Thoughts, Sensations. Scheherazade used all seven to survive a thousand nights. Distraction isn't denial — it's survival.

## For Parents

DBT Skill: ACCEPTS (Distress Tolerance — Distraction Skills). A = Activities (do something engaging). C = Contributing (help someone else). C = Comparisons (remember you've survived hard things before). E = opposite Emotions (watch something funny when sad). P = Pushing away (mentally set the problem aside temporarily). T = Thoughts (count, puzzle, recite). S = Sensations (ice cube, strong taste, cold water). Practice: Make an 'ACCEPTS kit' with your child — a box containing: a puzzle (A), a letter to write to someone (C), photos of past hard-things-survived (C), a funny video bookmarked (E), a 'worry parking lot' notebook (P), a Sudoku (T), a sour candy (S).

## Try This

Build your own ACCEPTS kit! Find one thing for each letter: an Activity you love, a way to Contribute, a photo that shows Comparison (you survived something hard before), something that makes opposite Emotions (laughter when sad), a Pushing-away trick (a 'worry box'), a Thought game (counting backwards from 100 by 7s), and a Sensation (an ice cube, a sour candy).

## Reflection

When you're really upset, what helps most — doing something active, helping someone else, or changing what your body feels? Everyone's different. What's YOUR best distraction?
