# The Velveteen Rabbit

*Interpersonal-Effectiveness*

## The Story

'What is REAL?' asked the Rabbit one day.

The Skin Horse was the oldest toy in the nursery. He was wise because he had been loved for a very long time.

'Real isn't how you are made,' said the Skin Horse. 'It's a thing that happens to you. When a child loves you for a long, long time, not just to play with, but REALLY loves you, then you become Real.'

'Does it hurt?' asked the Rabbit.

'Sometimes,' said the Skin Horse, because he always told the truth. 'When you are Real, you don't mind being hurt.'

'Does it happen all at once?' the Rabbit asked.

'It doesn't happen all at once. You become. It takes a long time. That's why it doesn't often happen to toys who break easily, or who have sharp edges, or who have to be carefully kept. Generally, by the time you are Real, most of your hair has been loved off, and your eyes drop out, and you get loose in the joints and very shabby.

But these things don't matter at all, because once you are Real you can't be ugly, except to people who don't understand.'

The Skin Horse didn't tell the Rabbit to cheer up. He didn't say 'It'll be fine.' He told the TRUTH about what it means to be real — that it takes time, that it hurts sometimes, and that it's worth it.

That's validation. Seeing someone exactly as they are and saying: 'Yes. That's real. And you matter.'

## The Song

I see you there, I hear you too —
Your feelings matter, just like you.

## The Skill

This story teaches VALIDATION — seeing and acknowledging someone's experience as real and understandable. Not fixing. Not cheering up. Just seeing. 'Yes. That's real.'

## For Parents

DBT Skill: VALIDATION (Interpersonal Effectiveness — Level 3-6 validation). The six levels: (1) Paying attention, (2) Reflecting back, (3) Reading unspoken feelings, (4) Understanding based on history, (5) Normalizing, (6) Radical genuineness. The Skin Horse does levels 2-6 in one answer. Practice: When your child is upset, resist the urge to fix. Try: 'That sounds really hard. Tell me more.' Validation IS the intervention — often nothing else is needed.

## Try This

The Skin Horse Practice: When a friend is sad, don't say 'Cheer up!' or 'It'll be okay.' Instead, try: 'That sounds hard. I'm glad you told me.' See how it feels different — for both of you.

## Reflection

Who is your Skin Horse — the person who sees you as you really are? What did they say that made you feel Real?
