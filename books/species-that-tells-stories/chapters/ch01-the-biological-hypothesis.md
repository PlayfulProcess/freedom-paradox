# Chapter 1: The Biological Hypothesis

*Before argument, before history, before morality — the biological ground.*

---

A child's body is warm against a parent's. They are on the couch, a book propped between them. Outside, the light is going. Inside, the lamp makes the room a world.

The story is old. A girl walks into a forest. The forest is dark. Something in the forest wants to eat her. The child's body shifts — a slight tension in the ribcage, a hand reaching for the parent's sleeve. The parent doesn't stop reading. Doesn't rush. Somewhere between the two bodies, something is happening that neither has words for yet.

This is the oldest technology on earth.

Not the book. Not even the story. The thing that is happening between the bodies — the warmth, the voice, the calibrated darkness arriving inside the safety of a lap — this is what humans have been doing for longer than we have been planting seeds or shaping clay. Before writing. Before agriculture. Before the first village. A parent's body holding a child's body while a story carries them both into the dark and back again.

What happens in the nervous system when a child hears a scary story in a loving embrace? Why does every culture on earth do this? And what does it mean that we are, for the first time in our species' history, systematically removing the embrace while intensifying the story?

This chapter does not begin with that crisis. It begins with the biology — because if you do not understand what is being lost, the crisis is just noise. Another alarming story in an infinite scroll of alarming stories. The body comes first because the body is where cooperation starts.

---

## The brain expects company

The most counterintuitive finding in modern neuroscience is also the simplest: being alone is not your brain's default. Being with someone is.

James Coan, a clinical psychologist at the University of Virginia, discovered this almost by accident. He was studying how the brain processes threat — specifically, what happens in an fMRI scanner when you tell someone they might receive an electric shock. The expected finding was straightforward: threat activates threat circuits. The brain lights up. Fear is processed.

But Coan added a variable. In his foundational 2006 study, sixteen married women underwent threat of electric shock while in a brain scanner under three conditions: holding their husband's hand, holding a stranger's hand, or holding no hand at all.

The results were not subtle. The women who held their husband's hand showed pervasive attenuation of the brain's threat response. Not just in one region — across multiple neural systems involved in processing danger, generating emotional distress, and mobilizing the body for action. The stranger's hand helped too, but less. And the quality of the marriage mattered: women in the strongest marriages showed the least threat activation of all — specifically less activation in the right anterior insula, superior frontal gyrus, and hypothalamus.

Here is the crucial finding: the brain holding the spouse's hand was not showing a "bonus" from social contact added on top of a baseline of aloneness. The brain alone — the brain without the hand — was the one working harder. It was burning more metabolic resources, recruiting more neural circuits, efforting more. The brain in relationship was the brain at rest.

In 2017, Coan replicated this with 110 participants of diverse racial and socioeconomic backgrounds — men and women, married, cohabiting, dating, or platonic friends. The basic finding held: handholding by a familiar relational partner attenuated both subjective distress and neural threat responses. And again, the quality of the relationship determined the size of the effect.

Coan called this Social Baseline Theory, and the name is precise. The human brain, he argues, does not treat the isolated individual as its baseline state. It treats the socially embedded individual as baseline. Social proximity is the baseline. Isolation is the deviation. The brain evolved to expect other nervous systems nearby — regulating it, sharing its load, distributing its risk — and when those nervous systems are absent, it treats the situation the way it treats any other resource deficit: with alarm, with heightened vigilance, with the costly activation of backup systems that were never meant to run full-time.

Coan's way of putting this is precise: the brain "expects access to social relationships that mitigate risk and diminish the level of effort needed to meet a variety of goals." When those relationships are present, the brain outsources some of its threat-processing work. When they are absent, it has to do everything itself. Being alone is not the removal of stimuli. It is the addition of labor.

This is not a metaphor for loneliness. It is a description of metabolic architecture. The brain at rest is the brain in relationship. The brain alone is the brain working overtime.

And the evidence goes deeper than fMRI. People standing next to a friend perceive hills as less steep than people standing alone. The brain literally encodes close others as extensions of the self — "friend" activates neural patterns similar to "self," while "stranger" does not. The philosopher's bounded individual, making rational choices in magnificent isolation, is a metabolic fiction. The actual human brain is a social organ that outsources a significant portion of its regulatory work to other brains, and when those other brains are absent, it pays a tax on every calculation.

This matters for understanding stories. Because if the brain's baseline is relationship — if co-regulation is not a bonus but a prerequisite — then the parent's body holding the child during the bedtime story is not providing comfort alongside the narrative. The parent's body is the infrastructure that makes the narrative work.

## The messy dance

There is a romantic version of the parent-infant bond — two beings in perfect attunement, a sacred flow of love and connection, Fred and Ginger gliding across the floor. Ed Tronick, who created the still-face experiment at Harvard in 1975, spent fifty years demolishing this fantasy and replacing it with something more interesting.

Tronick's Mutual Regulation Model, built on decades of second-by-second video microanalysis, reveals that mothers and infants are mismatched — uncoordinated, out of sync — roughly seventy percent of the time. They are not Fred and Ginger. They are the rest of us: stepping on each other's feet, losing the rhythm, bumping knees. What makes the dance generative is not synchrony. It is repair. Mismatches are repaired approximately every three to five seconds, in a continuous cycle of disconnection and reconnection that constitutes the actual texture of early relationship.

Sit with that number for a moment. Seventy percent mismatch. If you have ever held an infant and felt like you were doing it wrong — missing cues, guessing, overcorrecting — you were doing it exactly right. Not because failure is beautiful, but because the repair is the point. Each micro-repair builds something in the infant: a procedural knowledge, deeper than language, that rupture is survivable. That connection can be lost and found again. That the world breaks and mends, breaks and mends, and the mending is reliable.

Winnicott called this the "good enough mother," and fifty years of research confirms the name. Mid-range levels of synchrony — not too much, not too little — predict the best developmental outcomes. Perfect attunement would deprive the child of the ruptures that spark the development of repair capacity. Imperfect attunement, within a reliably responsive relationship, is the optimal developmental environment. It is the repair of ruptures, not the prevention of them, that builds the capacity to face the dark.

Tronick's still-face experiment made the mechanism visible. In the standard protocol, a mother plays normally with her infant for two minutes. Then she stops. She holds a neutral face, makes no response, maintains eye contact but offers nothing. The infant — even at six weeks old — notices immediately. She tries harder: smiling, vocalizing, reaching. When these bids fail, she turns away, self-soothes, grows distressed. Within seconds the heart rate elevates. By the seventh second, cortisol mobilizes. The infant's autonomic system reorganizes from "I am with someone" to "I am alone."

And when the mother resumes normal interaction, the baby does not simply reset. The rupture echoes. A 2025 study in *Infancy* found a twenty-four-hour carryover: two minutes of withdrawn co-regulation created a stress memory lasting at least a full day.

Two minutes. Twenty-four hours. The infant's nervous system does not treat the mother's withdrawal as an interesting stimulus to process. It treats it as a change in the environment's fundamental safety. Because that is what it is.

Now multiply that — not the experiment, but the condition. A parent physically present but absorbed in a phone. A caregiver whose attention fragments every ninety seconds. The smartphone has made the still-face a chronic condition. But that is a later chapter. For now, we need to understand what the container is before we can understand what happens when it breaks.

## The container

When a parent reads to a child, five things are happening simultaneously. Most parents do not know this. They think they are just reading a story. They are building a brain.

**The fiction frame.** "Once upon a time" is not a throwaway phrase. It is a neural instruction. It tells the developing brain: what follows is real enough to learn from but not real enough to require action. The story activates emotional circuits — fear, grief, longing, wonder — but within a frame that signals safety. The brain registers the threat but also registers the frame; the learning happens without the full cost of the experience. This is why children can tolerate in stories what they cannot tolerate in life. The frame is the first layer of containment.

Make-believe deserves to be named as its own ontological category. Not true, not false, not a lie — its own kind of reality, like theater, fiction, and myth. Children navigate this frame naturally. They know the bear is not real and that the bear is also real, and they hold both without contradiction. Adults often collapse this into "real vs. fake." The child's native fluency with make-believe is a distinct mode of knowing, and it is one of the first casualties of a media environment that deliberately blurs the line between fiction and reality.

**The co-regulatory presence.** The parent's body is the second layer. The child's nervous system is not yet fully capable of regulating its own arousal — it will not be until the prefrontal cortex matures, sometime in the mid-twenties. In the meantime, the child borrows the parent's regulation. Ruth Feldman's research has shown that mothers and infants coordinate heart rhythms within lags of less than one second during face-to-face interaction. When the story gets scary, the parent's breathing stays steady. The heartbeat does not spike. The parent's body communicates, beneath the level of language: this darkness is survivable. We are safe.

Feldman's bio-behavioral synchrony research shows that this coordination operates across multiple channels simultaneously — behavioral, cardiac, hormonal, and neural — and that the quality of this early synchrony predicts the child's self-regulatory capacities years later. In her 2021 paper, she documented for the first time the brain-to-brain synchrony between parent and child during shared attention — their neural oscillations literally entraining, the two brains operating as a coupled system. What happens on the couch at bedtime is not only a pleasant domestic ritual. It is the construction of a nervous system's regulatory architecture.

**Graduated dosing.** The parent chose this story for this child at this age. Not the story that was right last year. Not the story the child will be ready for next year. This one. Tonight. The parent as curator — calibrating the darkness to the child's developmental window — is the third layer of containment. Too little darkness and there is nothing to metabolize, nothing to build capacity from. Too much and the system is overwhelmed. The art is in the dosing, and the parent's attunement to the child — knowing when to press forward and when to stop — is itself a form of co-regulation.

**The child's agency.** "Again! Again!" Or: "Stop. I don't like that part." The child controls the dose. This is self-directed exposure therapy, and it is remarkable in its precision. A child who asks to hear the scary story five nights running is not being morbid. She is titrating her own encounter with the dark — approaching it in manageable increments, from inside the safety of a relationship, building her tolerance one reading at a time. A child who says "skip that page" is exercising the same agency: not tonight. Not yet. The child's capacity to start and stop the encounter is the fourth layer of containment.

A note: on YouTube, the child also "controls everything" — choosing videos, replaying, skipping. But bedtime story agency operates within a relationship. The child negotiates with a present adult who modulates, who notices distress, who can say "that's enough for tonight." Screen agency operates in isolation. The child negotiates with an algorithm that has its own interests, and those interests are not developmental. The child saying "again" to a parent is different from a child clicking replay alone. The distinction is not the control. It is the relationship within which the control operates.

**Bounded structure.** The story ends. This is perhaps the most underappreciated feature. The danger passes or the morning comes or the lost child finds her way home, and the book closes, and the light goes off. The arousal that built during the scary parts — the cortisol, the elevated heart rate — now has somewhere to go. The resolution metabolizes the stress. The narrative arc, from safety through danger back to safety, mirrors the co-regulatory cycle itself: rupture and repair, disruption and return, the reliable pattern that teaches the nervous system that distress is temporary and that return is always possible.

Five conditions. Five layers of containment. When all five are present, dark content heals. When they are systematically removed — when the fiction frame is replaced by the algorithmic feed, when the co-regulatory body is replaced by a solitary screen, when graduated dosing is replaced by whatever the algorithm serves next, when child agency is replaced by autoplay, when bounded structure is replaced by infinite scroll — the same darkness that builds regulatory capacity begins to erode it.

## How love builds a brain

What does "love" mean here? Not a feeling. A physiological state in which one nervous system regulates another.

Allan Schore's work on right-brain-to-right-brain communication between caregiver and infant describes the mechanism in neuroanatomical detail. The mother's right hemisphere processes the infant's nonverbal signals — facial expressions, vocalizations, posture, gesture — and responds with regulatory inputs that match and modulate the infant's arousal. Schore calls the caregiver an "auxiliary cortex" for the infant's still-developing brain. Through what he terms "psychobiological attunement," the caregiver intuitively tracks the infant's shifting autonomic states and adjusts her own output to keep the infant within a tolerable window.

This is not conscious. It is not effortful. It is not something you learn from a parenting book. It is what happens when a regulated adult is present with a child — the same thing that happens, on a different timescale, when a parent's voice stays steady as the story reaches its darkest moment.

The infant's prefrontal cortex — the brain's regulatory control center — undergoes its most rapid development in the first two years of life. The orbitofrontal cortex, which Schore identifies as the "senior executive of the emotional brain," begins its critical period at ten to twelve months. Full structural maturity does not arrive until approximately age twenty-five. During this long developmental window, the caregiver's nervous system serves as external scaffolding for neural circuits that will eventually support independent regulation. What begins as the caregiver's rhythm gradually becomes the child's own rhythm. The external becomes internal. Co-regulation becomes self-regulation.

But "becomes" is misleading if it implies replacement. Self-regulation does not replace co-regulation the way an adult bicycle replaces training wheels. The training wheels metaphor assumes a destination of autonomous balance. The actual neuroscience suggests something different: self-regulation is a secondary system, built from co-regulatory templates, that supplements but never fully replaces the primary system of regulation-through-relationship. This is what Coan's handholding studies show in adults: even a fully mature nervous system — twenty-five, forty, sixty years old — processes threat differently when a familiar hand is nearby. We never outgrow co-regulation. We refine it.

Megan Gunnar's research on social buffering makes this vivid. In a study of eighteen-month-olds, children who were securely attached to their parent showed no cortisol elevation when frightened — even when they showed clear behavioral distress. The parent's regulatory presence was operating beneath behavior, at the level of the stress hormone system, silently keeping the child's physiology within bounds while the child's visible distress played out on the surface. The body knew it was safe even when the mind did not.

By twelve months, even painful stressors like inoculations cease to produce cortisol elevations in most infants — because the parental social buffer has become effective. The love is not soothing the child's feelings about the needle. The love is intercepting the cortisol cascade before it happens.

This is love as mechanism. Not sentiment. Not intention. Physiology. A regulated nervous system, in proximity to a dysregulated one, modulates the dysregulation through channels that operate below conscious awareness — cardiac synchrony, vagal tone, hormonal alignment, right-hemispheric attunement. The parent does not have to be perfect. The system runs on repair, not on perfection. The seventy percent mismatch is not a bug. It is the feature.

## What this does not mean

I need to be careful here. I could use this science to build a fortress — an argument so tight it becomes doctrine. The body requires co-regulation, therefore relationship is the ground, therefore anyone who disagrees is fighting biology. I have seen this move made with polyvagal theory, with attachment theory, with every body of research that touches the sacred ground between neuroscience and love. The move is always the same: genuine findings, extrapolated into metaphysics, defended as truth.

I will not make that move, and I need to say why.

Stephen Porges's Polyvagal Theory has been enormously influential in clinical practice — the idea that we have a "ventral vagal" system for social engagement, a sympathetic system for fight-or-flight, and a "dorsal vagal" system for shutdown, arranged in an evolutionary hierarchy. Therapists use this framework daily. It helps people understand their own nervous system responses. It has clinical utility.

It also has serious scientific problems. Paul Grossman, a physiological researcher, has published detailed critiques arguing that several of Porges's specific neuroanatomical claims — particularly the evolutionary phylogenetic story and the neat hierarchy of vagal circuits — are not well supported by comparative anatomy. Edwin Taylor, a comparative physiologist, has raised similar objections. The basic anatomy — myelinated versus unmyelinated vagal pathways — is established. The specific hierarchy and the evolutionary narrative are contested.

What survives the critique? The functional claim. Felt safety enables connection. Connection enables cooperation. When we feel safe — however we arrive at that state — we relate better. When we relate better, we cooperate more effectively. When we cooperate more effectively, we adapt. This pipeline is supported by Coan's work, by Tronick's work, by Feldman's work. It does not depend on Porges's specific evolutionary story.

But I want to hold even this more carefully. The pipeline is not a truth. It is a hypothesis. I learned this posture from Marsha Linehan — not from her therapy techniques, but from her epistemology. Linehan built Dialectical Behavior Therapy on the principle that a therapeutic framework does not need to be metaphysically true. It needs to be useful, it needs to fit the data, and it needs to be compassionate. If it meets those three criteria, you can work with it. If new data contradicts it, you revise.

So here is the hypothesis, stated plainly:

*There is a physiological state — call it felt safety, call it parasympathetic dominance, call it whatever you like — that is the precondition for genuine relating. When we relate well, we cooperate more effectively. When we cooperate more effectively, we build and maintain adaptive cultures. Every long-lasting storytelling tradition in human history can be understood as a technology for accessing this state. Every healthy community can be understood as an infrastructure for sustaining it.*

Is this true? I don't know. I don't need to know. I need to know three things:

**Is it useful?** Can people work with this hypothesis in ways that improve their lives, their relationships, their communities? The clinical evidence suggests yes. Co-regulation interventions work. Attachment-informed therapies work. Contemplative practices that regulate the nervous system have measurable effects on prosocial behavior.

**Does it fit the data?** The evidence is strong but not complete. Coan's handholding studies replicate. Tronick's mismatch-repair cycle is robust across cultures — studies in the Netherlands, China, and elsewhere show the same basic still-face effect. Feldman's synchrony research spans two decades and multiple labs. The pipeline from felt safety to connection to cooperation is well-supported. The extrapolation from cooperation to adaptive culture is reasonable but involves a larger inferential leap.

**Is it compassionate?** Does this hypothesis, if taken seriously, maintain the conditions for future adaptation — or does it collapse the world into a single story? A hypothesis about the importance of co-regulation does not prescribe a single form of relationship, a single culture, or a single practice tradition. It says: the mechanism is relational. How you enact it is yours to discover.

This three-filter test — useful, fits the data, compassionate — is not a scientific criterion. It is a practice. It is the epistemological method this book uses throughout, and the book will apply it to its own thesis before the final chapter closes.

## Every culture, every era

"The oldest technology" is not a rhetorical device. It is a descriptive claim.

Every culture for which we have ethnographic evidence practices some form of adult-child shared narrative within physical proximity. The modes vary — but the structure is remarkably consistent.

In Japan, the practice is called *yomikikase* — a compound word that means "reading-listening," capturing the relational nature of the act in its very grammar. It is not one person reading. It is a shared act of reading and listening together. Japanese elementary schools institutionalize the practice, normalizing shared reading as a community responsibility rather than a private family ritual.

In West Africa, the tradition is called Tales by Moonlight — adults gathering children after the day's work, by firelight, under open sky. The format is ritualized. In Igbo tradition, the storyteller opens with a formulaic phrase and the audience responds: the equivalent of "once upon a time" as a co-constructed fiction frame. Songs, call-and-response, riddles, and games weave through the telling. The storytelling is not passive reception. It is participatory, communal, embodied — multiple nervous systems co-regulating through shared attention to shared narrative content.

In Brazil, *contacao de historias* weaves together three streams — indigenous Tupi oral traditions, West African Grio storytelling carried through the Atlantic slave trade, and Portuguese colonial narrative forms. The practice survived four hundred years of slavery and colonization. The stories adapted, blended, recombined. But the structure persisted: adults gathering children, transmitting adaptive wisdom through felt narrative, within the safety of a communal embrace.

Among the Mbendjele hunter-gatherers, each child has approximately fifteen to twenty caregivers, with alloparents providing roughly thirty-six percent of close care. Among the Agta of the Philippines, alloparents provide nearly seventy-five percent. The bedtime story as a private act between one parent and one child is a historically recent and culturally narrow arrangement. The ancestral version — the version that shaped the nervous system we still carry — was communal. Many bodies. Many voices. The child held not by one regulated adult but by a network of them.

This is what Sarah Hrdy calls the cooperative breeding hypothesis: human infants evolved to rely not on one caregiver but on a web of caregivers, and the regulatory infrastructure that supports their development is distributed across that web. The mother's heartbeat. The grandmother's voice. The uncle's steady hand. The village elder's story told under stars while the fire crackled and every body within earshot co-regulated with every other body, the way bodies do when they share attention and proximity and warmth.

Heidi Keller's cross-cultural research adds a crucial nuance. Western middle-class cultures employ what she calls distal parenting — face-to-face interaction, verbal communication, treating the infant as an independent agent. Most of the world employs proximal parenting — body contact, carrying, rhythmic vocalizing, treating the infant as part of a communal whole. Over seventy percent of the world's peoples co-sleep; solitary infant sleep is historically anomalous.

The regulatory consequences are measurable. Keller's longitudinal studies found that proximal parenting produced children who developed self-regulation earlier, while distal parenting produced children who developed self-recognition earlier. Different modes of co-regulation literally produce different types of selves — the communal, well-regulated self or the autonomous, self-recognizing self. Neither is universally better. Each is adaptive within its context.

But both require the same thing: the presence of a regulating body. Whether you are a Cameroonian Nso farmer holding your infant for ten hours a day or a Brooklyn parent reading *Goodnight Moon* in a shared bed or a Yoruba elder telling Anansi stories under the stars — the mechanism is the same. A developing nervous system borrows the regulation it cannot yet generate. The vehicle for the borrowing is physical proximity, shared attention, and a story that carries them both into the dark and back.

## The economic counter-argument

There is an obvious objection, and it is better addressed now than left as a vulnerability.

If felt safety is the precondition for relating, and material security is a route to felt safety, then doesn't economics subsume the whole argument? Get people out of poverty, and the nervous system takes care of itself. Build a wealthy society, and co-regulation follows.

The data says otherwise. The wealthiest societies on earth are also running epidemics of loneliness, anxiety, and depression that map poorly onto material deprivation. The United States, the wealthiest nation in history, has declining life expectancy, rising "deaths of despair" — Anne Case and Angus Deaton's term for the suicides, overdoses, and alcohol-related deaths that have driven down American life expectancy for three consecutive years — and survey data showing that a third of adults report feeling lonely. Material security is real and matters. But the vagus nerve reads the room, not the bank statement. A wealthy family in which every member retreats to a separate screen, eating separate meals, consuming separate stories, has material security but not felt safety. The nervous system is not tracking the mortgage. It is tracking whether there is a regulated body nearby.

This is why the biological hypothesis subsumes the economic one rather than competing with it. Material security is one route to felt safety. It is not the only route, and alone it is insufficient. A society that provides material abundance but destroys relational infrastructure — that atomizes families, replaces communal gathering with algorithmic feeds, substitutes parasocial relationships for co-present ones — has solved the economic problem while worsening the biological one.

The infant in Tronick's still-face experiment is not hungry. She is not cold, not threatened, not materially deprived. She has everything except a responsive human face. And her entire physiology reorganizes around that absence.

## What the body already knows

Most parents do not know any of this. They do not know about Coan's handholding studies or Feldman's brain-to-brain coupling or Tronick's twenty-four-hour carryover. They do not know that their child's cortisol is being buffered by their vagal tone or that the orbitofrontal cortex is being wired by their right hemisphere's attunement to the child's breathing.

They just know that holding a child while reading feels important. That the scary parts are important. That the way the child's body relaxes after the danger passes is important. The body knows something that took neuroscience decades to confirm: this is how it works. This is how you build a human being's capacity to face the dark. You hold them. You tell a story. You do not flinch at the scary parts. You stay.

This is not sentimental. It is not nostalgic. It is a description of a technology — the most successful technology our species has ever deployed for building regulatory capacity in its young. It worked for sixty thousand years and more. It worked in every climate, every ecology, every social arrangement. It worked because it is grounded in the architecture of the mammalian nervous system, which evolved to regulate through proximity, through shared attention, through the steady heartbeat of another body nearby.

Everything that follows in this book — the fire myths, the megafauna extinctions, the AI governance experiments, the dying grammars, the colonial extractions — is an elaboration of what happens when this basic mechanism is honored or betrayed, supported or destroyed, nourished or starved.

The biological hypothesis does not tell us what to do. It tells us what we are working with: a species whose members regulate each other's nervous systems, whose capacity for cooperation depends on feeling safe enough to connect, and whose most powerful technologies — from fire to nuclear fission to artificial intelligence — have consistently outrun the relational infrastructure needed to hold them.

That gap between power and relationship is the subject of this book. We begin with the relationship because it was there first.
