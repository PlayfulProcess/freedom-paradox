# Chapter 8: The Trap of Solving

*Even the impulse to solve this is part of the problem.*

---

I need to tell you something uncomfortable about this book.

Everything I have written so far — the biological hypothesis, the fire pattern, the myths, the grammars, the extraction dynamics — has been building an argument. The argument has a shape: here is what we are (co-regulatory beings), here is what went wrong (extraction outran cooperation), here is what we need (grammars). The shape is satisfying. It moves from diagnosis to prescription, from wound to remedy, from problem to solution.

And the shape is a trap.

---

## The Modernity Reflex

Vanessa Andreotti, a scholar of global education, and Bayo Akomolafe, a philosopher and psychologist working at the intersection of postcolonial thought and ecology, have articulated something that I cannot paraphrase without weakening, so I will state it in my own words and risk getting it wrong:

Modernity's deepest reflex is to solve problems. When modernity encounters suffering, complexity, contradiction, or crisis, its instinctive response is to analyze, diagnose, and fix. This reflex is so deep that it operates even when the problem is modernity itself. When you point out that modern systems are causing ecological collapse, modern systems respond with more modern systems — carbon markets, geoengineering, efficiency optimization. When you point out that the extractive economy is destroying communities, the extractive economy responds with community-development programs funded by extraction. When you point out that technology is eroding relational capacity, technology responds with apps for mindfulness and platforms for connection.

The fixing reflex, Andreotti argues, is itself a product of the system it purports to fix. A civilization built on extraction cannot extract itself from extraction. A cognitive mode organized around optimization cannot optimize its way out of over-optimization. The solution is not more solution. The solution — and here language starts to fail — is a different relationship to the problem.

Akomolafe puts it differently, in language that is deliberately disorienting: "The times are urgent; let us slow down." His claim is not that we should do nothing. It is that the urgency itself — the feeling that we must act now, that the situation demands immediate response, that something must be done — is a symptom of the very modernity that produced the crisis. The urgency is the extraction mindset applied to its own consequences. It says: this is a problem, problems have solutions, solutions require action, action requires speed. And the speed forecloses the slow, relational, embodied processes that are the only ones capable of generating genuinely adaptive responses.

I want to be careful here. Andreotti and Akomolafe are provocateurs more than systematic philosophers. Their work is suggestive, disturbing, and generative. It does not have the scholarly rigor of, say, a Christopher Wallis parsing the Pratyabhijñā texts or an Elinor Ostrom documenting commons governance. Their arguments resist the very systematization that would make them legible to the academic frameworks they critique. This is intentional. It is also a limitation.

But the core observation passes the three-filter test:

**Is it useful?** Yes — it prevents the book from becoming another extraction: taking the world's problems, processing them through a Western analytical framework, and producing a "solution" that reproduces the dynamics it critiques.

**Does it fit the data?** The data is suggestive. Every large-scale attempt to "solve" complex social-ecological problems through top-down intervention has produced unintended consequences proportional to its ambition. The Green Revolution solved hunger and created pesticide dependency and soil degradation. Structural adjustment programs solved debt crises and created inequality. The War on Poverty, the War on Drugs, the War on Terror — each a "solution" that generated new problems at least as severe as the ones it addressed.

**Is it compassionate?** This is where the observation earns its place in the book. If modernity's fixing reflex reproduces extraction, then the compassionate response is not to fix harder. It is to practice a different kind of attention — one that sits with the problem rather than immediately reaching for the wrench.

## The Three-Filter Test, Applied

This chapter is the book's checkpoint. I have been building an argument for eight chapters. The argument says: bodies need co-regulation, stories encode cooperative strategies, extraction outpaces cooperation, grammars are the infrastructure that maintains the balance, and we are losing them. The argument implies a response: rebuild grammars, strengthen cooperative infrastructure, create alternatives to extraction.

The three-filter test must be applied to this argument itself. I will not exempt my own thesis from the tool it proposes.

**Is the book's thesis useful?** I think so — but with a caveat. It is useful as a lens, a way of seeing patterns across domains that are usually studied separately. It is useful as a diagnostic: when you encounter a system that is failing, ask whether the grammar has been extracted. It is useful as a design principle: build technology that strengthens relational containers rather than replacing them. It is less useful as a program — "rebuild grammars" is a direction, not a plan, and anyone who tells you they have a plan for civilizational transformation at this scale is selling something.

**Does the thesis fit the data?** The biological evidence for co-regulation is strong. The ecological evidence for extraction-cooperation dynamics is strong. The historical evidence for grammar loss through colonial extraction is strong. The game-theoretic argument for cooperation in iterated games is well-established. But the extrapolation — from these specific domains to a unified theory of civilizational crisis — involves inferential leaps that the data does not fully support. The thesis is a story. It is, I believe, a good story: useful, fitting, compassionate. It is not the truth. I do not know the truth. I know what passes the filter.

**Is the thesis compassionate?** Does it maintain conditions for future adaptation? Does it preserve strategic diversity? Here I must be honest: there is a risk that this book's argument, taken as doctrine rather than hypothesis, could itself become a monoculture — one more framework that claims to explain everything, one more grammar that displaces rather than coexists with others. The compassion criterion requires that the thesis hold itself lightly. That it present itself as one adaptive strategy among many, not as the final word.

This is why the Linehan epistemological posture matters so much. Marsha Linehan did not build DBT on the claim that her framework was true. She built it on the claim that it was useful, evidence-based, and oriented toward reducing suffering. When new evidence appeared, the framework revised. The posture is radical: you can commit fully to a hypothesis while acknowledging that it might be wrong. You can act decisively on incomplete knowledge while remaining genuinely open to correction.

This is what I am asking of you, as a reader. Not belief. Practice. Hold the hypothesis. Test it against your own experience. Apply the three filters to your own moral claims, your own inherited stories, your own assumptions about what is true. The tool is more important than the thesis.

## The Campbell Test

Let me demonstrate the three-filter test on a specific claim — one that is widely believed and deeply influential.

Joseph Campbell's "monomyth" — the Hero's Journey — has become, through Hollywood's adoption, perhaps the most influential theory of narrative in the world. The version most people know says: all stories share a common structure (departure, initiation, return), all heroes undergo the same transformation, and this pattern reflects universal features of human psychology.

What did Campbell actually say? This matters, because the gap between Campbell's actual claims and the culture industry's use of them is an example of grammar extraction.

Campbell was careful. He wrote that myths cannot be exported — that a myth severed from the community that tells it loses its living function and becomes literature, which is a different thing. He described the monomyth as one pattern among many, not as the only pattern. He emphasized the diversity of mythic traditions, not their uniformity.

Hollywood extracted the monomyth from Campbell's larger argument, stripped it of the caveats, and deployed it as a formula. The result is a monoculture of narrative — the same hero's journey, told and retold across thousands of films, games, and books, crowding out the structural diversity of mythic forms that Campbell himself documented and valued.

Apply the three filters to the monomyth-as-formula:

**Useful?** For Hollywood, yes — it produces commercially successful films. For cultural diversity, no — it replaces a thousand narrative forms with one.

**Fits the data?** Partially. Some stories do follow the hero's journey structure. Many do not. Aboriginal Dreamtime stories are not hero's journeys. The Samudra Manthan is not a hero's journey. The structure of the Zen koan is not a hero's journey. The monomyth fits some data and ignores the rest.

**Compassionate?** Does it maintain conditions for future adaptation? No. The monomyth monoculture reduces the species' repertoire of narrative strategies, replacing diverse grammars with a single formula. It is the cultural equivalent of replacing a thousand crop varieties with one high-yield strain. It works — until it doesn't.

The gap between Campbell's actual scholarship and the culture industry's extraction of it is the gap between grammar and content. Campbell held the grammar — the rich, diverse, communally embedded practice of mythic storytelling. Hollywood extracted the content — one pattern, universalized, commodified, deployed at scale. The knowledge survived. The grammar died.

## What Sits With the Problem

If the fixing reflex is part of the problem, what is the alternative?

I don't think the alternative is inaction. I think the alternative is a different quality of action — one that emerges from sitting with the problem rather than from leaping at it. The contemplative traditions that Chapter 6 described as grammars are, among other things, technologies for this specific capacity: holding a difficulty without reaching for a premature resolution.

The farmer in Zhuangzi who refused the irrigation machine was not refusing to water his fields. He was refusing a relationship to efficiency that would change his interior. Shiva drinking the halahala was not refusing to act. He was acting in a way that contained toxicity without either destroying himself or passing the poison on. The Zen student sitting with the koan is not doing nothing. She is training a capacity for holding contradiction without collapse — a capacity that, once developed, allows her to act in complex situations without the false simplicity that the fixing reflex demands.

The trap of solving is not a trap of doing. It is a trap of doing before listening. It is a trap of analysis before attention. It is a trap of reaching for the wrench before understanding what is broken and whether a wrench is what it needs.

This book will not escape the trap entirely. It is, after all, a book — a modern form, distributed through modern channels, written by an author trained in modern analytical methods. It is part of the system it critiques. The best it can do is name the trap, apply the three-filter test to itself, and proceed with honesty about its own limitations.

Which brings us, finally, to the gesture.
