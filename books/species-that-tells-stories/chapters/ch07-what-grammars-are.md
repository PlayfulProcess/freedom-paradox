# Chapter 7: What Grammars Are

*The cooperative strategies that sustain iterated games are encoded in precisely the cultural systems the extractive monoculture is destroying.*

---

I have been using the word "grammar" for six chapters. It is time to say precisely what I mean — and to show you that grammars are not abstractions. They are as concrete as a fishing schedule, as specific as a breathing technique, as enforceable as a law that no one wrote down.

A grammar, in linguistics, is a finite set of rules that generates an infinite number of possible sentences. You do not need to memorize every sentence in English. You need to internalize a grammar — subject-verb-object, tense markers, subordination rules — and then you can produce and understand sentences you have never encountered before. The grammar is finite. What it generates is inexhaustible.

I am using the word in a broader sense, which I believe is its more fundamental sense. A grammar, as this book uses the term, is a finite symbolic structure that generates infinite meaning and requires a community of speakers to come alive.

A Yoruba ceremony for Ogun is a grammar. The specific gestures, songs, invocations, and offerings are finite — they can be learned, transmitted, modified within bounds. But what the ceremony generates — felt connection to the force of iron, communal acknowledgment of technology's ambivalence, relational containment of power — is inexhaustible. It means differently each time it is performed, for each person present, in each historical moment. And it requires a community. One person performing the ceremony alone is doing something, but it is not what the grammar is for.

A bedtime story tradition is a grammar. The structural elements are finite: a protagonist who faces darkness, a container of loving presence, a resolution that is honest about what was lost. What these elements generate — a child's developing capacity to face fear, tolerate ambiguity, trust relationship, integrate shadow — is inexhaustible. And it requires two: the teller and the told, the body that holds and the body that is held.

This definition carries a structural implication that will matter for the rest of the book: a grammar without its community is an artifact. A text without its readers. A ceremony without its practitioners. A koan without the teacher who watches the student struggle. The grammar lives in the practice, not in the description of the practice. This is why you cannot save a grammar by writing it down. You can only save it by maintaining the community that enacts it.

## The Rules No One Wrote Down

In 1990, Elinor Ostrom published *Governing the Commons*, a book that quietly dismantled one of the most influential ideas in modern economics: the tragedy of the commons. Garrett Hardin's famous argument, published in 1968, claimed that any shared resource — a pasture, a fishery, a forest — will inevitably be destroyed by rational self-interested actors, because each individual benefits from taking more while the cost of depletion is shared. The only solutions, Hardin argued, were privatization (let someone own it and they'll protect it) or state control (let the government regulate it).

Ostrom did something radical. She looked at what actually happened. She studied communities around the world that had successfully governed shared resources for centuries — Swiss alpine meadows, Japanese mountain commons, Philippine irrigation systems, Maine lobster fisheries — and found that Hardin was wrong. Communities routinely develop effective governance of shared resources without either privatization or state control. But they do it only when certain conditions are met.

Ostrom identified eight design principles that characterized successful commons governance. The list reads like a description of what every healthy community has always known:

Clearly defined boundaries — who is in the commons and who is not. Rules adapted to local conditions — not one-size-fits-all regulation but governance shaped by the specific ecology of the specific place. Collective decision-making — the people affected by the rules participate in making them. Monitoring — someone watches, and it's usually members of the community rather than outside enforcers. Graduated sanctions — violations are met with proportional responses, not zero-tolerance punishment. Conflict resolution mechanisms — accessible, low-cost ways to settle disputes. Recognition by higher authorities — the community's right to self-govern is not undermined by the state. Nested enterprises — for larger commons, governance is organized in multiple nested layers from local to regional to global.

These are grammars. Finite rules that generate infinite adaptive possibility, embedded in communities, sustained by participation, requiring neither top-down control nor bottom-up chaos. Ostrom demonstrated what the previous chapters of this book have argued from biology, mythology, and ecology: cooperation is not a utopian ideal. It is a strategy — observable, documentable, and structurally describable.

## The Ancient Technologies

Ostrom studied contemporary commons. But grammars are far older than any institution she documented. They are as old as the species' need to cooperate under conditions of scarcity and uncertainty.

Consider the structure of Aboriginal Australian ceremony — what anthropologists sometimes call "the Law." Not a legal code in the Western sense but a total system: kinship obligations that determine who can marry whom and who must care for which tract of land; songlines that encode navigational, ecological, and spiritual knowledge in musical form; initiation sequences that transmit dangerous knowledge in graduated stages, each stage requiring demonstrated readiness; restrictions on who can know what, when, and under what conditions. The system is finite — learnable, transmissible, specific. What it generates — sixty thousand years of adaptive coexistence with one of the harshest landscapes on Earth — is extraordinary.

Consider the monastic hours of the Christian contemplative tradition — the Liturgy of the Hours. Matins, Lauds, Prime, Terce, Sext, None, Vespers, Compline. Eight interruptions of the day, each structured with specific psalms, readings, and prayers. The grammar is simple: stop what you are doing at regular intervals and remember what you are for. The practice constrains the tendency of work to consume everything. It imposes a rhythm that the individual cannot set and the community maintains. It is, functionally, an Ostrom design principle — collective decision-making about the allocation of attention, monitored by the community, sustained across centuries.

Consider the Zen koan — a paradoxical statement or question posed by a teacher to a student, to be held without rational resolution. "What is the sound of one hand clapping?" The koan is a grammar of a very specific kind: it gives the intellect something to do that the intellect cannot do, thereby creating the conditions for a non-intellectual recognition. The structure is finite (a question with no logical answer). What it generates (the student's encounter with the limits of discursive thought) is infinite. And it requires two: the teacher who poses the question and the student who sits with it. The koan extracted from the relationship — printed in a book, read alone — is an artifact, not a grammar.

Consider the 112 techniques of the Vijñāna Bhairava Tantra. "Focus on the space between two breaths." "At the moment terror strikes, enter the terror." "When eating, become the taste." Each technique is a few sentences long. Together they constitute a grammar of attention — 112 ways to use the specific conditions of embodied life (breathing, eating, loving, fearing, dying) as doorways to recognition. The grammar is finite. What it generates is not an experience but a recognition — of consciousness as the ground of all experience. And the tradition that holds it insists that the techniques live within a relationship between guru and disciple, within a lineage that maintains interpretive authority, within a community that provides context for what the recognition means.

In every case, the pattern is the same: finite structure, infinite generation, communal embedding. And in every case, the grammar does the same work: it gives the intellect something to do with the body's opening, and it constrains the tendency of power (including the power of the intellect) to run unchecked.

## What Grammars Do

Across all these examples, grammars perform five functions:

*They constrain power.* The fishing schedule limits how much anyone can take. The monastic hours limit how much anyone can work. The koan limits how much the intellect can dominate. The songline limits who can access which knowledge and when. Constraint is not the enemy of freedom. It is the condition for sustainability.

*They distribute responsibility.* No one person maintains the commons. The burden is shared, rotated, specified by role and relationship. The elder who holds fire knowledge, the abbess who sets the prayer schedule, the Zen master who assigns the koan — each holds a piece of the system, and the system holds them.

*They scaffold relationships.* Grammars give people something to do together. A ceremony is a grammar for communal attention. A story is a grammar for shared emotional processing. A fishing schedule is a grammar for coordinated resource use. Without the grammar, the relationship has no structure. With it, even adversaries can cooperate (the Samudra Manthan's lesson).

*They transmit across time.* A grammar can be taught. This is its most important property. The knowledge encoded in a ceremony, a practice tradition, a commons governance system, or a storytelling form can survive the death of any individual practitioner because it is distributed across the community and structured for transmission.

*They maintain diversity.* Because grammars are local — shaped by specific ecologies, specific histories, specific communities — the global repertoire of grammars constitutes a vast library of adaptive strategies. This is the seed vault argument from Chapter 2: the species' resilience depends on maintaining many grammars, not optimizing for one.

## Rwanda's Gacaca

There is a case study that tests the grammar hypothesis at its most extreme: can grammars be rebuilt after they have been completely destroyed?

In 1994, Rwanda experienced a genocide that killed approximately eight hundred thousand people in one hundred days. The social fabric — the grammars of coexistence between Hutu and Tutsi communities — was not merely damaged but annihilated. Neighbors killed neighbors. Teachers killed students. The communal infrastructure for cooperation was converted, in a matter of weeks, into infrastructure for extermination.

The formal justice system could not handle the aftermath. There were over a hundred thousand accused perpetrators and almost no surviving judges or lawyers. The backlog of cases would have taken over a century to clear through conventional courts.

Rwanda's response was the gacaca — community-based tribunals rooted in a traditional conflict-resolution practice. The gacaca courts were not traditional courts restored. They were a new grammar, built from the memory of an old one, adapted to an unprecedented situation. Community members elected judges from among themselves. Proceedings were held outdoors, in the places where the violence occurred. Accused persons faced their communities directly. Confessions were met with reduced sentences. The process was imperfect, coercive in places, and has been criticized by international human rights observers.

But it processed nearly two million cases. And it did something that no formal court could do: it rebuilt the communal practice of sitting together and making meaning of shared horror. The gacaca was not primarily a justice mechanism. It was a grammar-reconstruction project — an attempt to create a finite structure (the tribunal format, the roles, the procedures) that could generate the infinite adaptive work of communal repair.

Whether it succeeded is debatable. That it was attempted is the data point. After total grammar collapse, the impulse to rebuild shared structure reasserted itself — not as nostalgia but as survival strategy.

## The Seed Vault

There is a vault in Svalbard, Norway, cut into the permafrost of an Arctic mountain. It holds over a million seed samples from nearly every country on Earth — a backup of the world's agricultural biodiversity, stored against the possibility that any given crop variety might be lost to disease, climate change, or war. The vault exists because agricultural scientists understand that monocultures are fragile. When you grow one variety of wheat across an entire continent, a single pathogen can destroy the food supply. Diversity is resilience. The seed vault is humanity's insurance policy against its own tendency toward simplification.

Grammars are the cultural equivalent of the Svalbard seed vault.

Every distinct cultural tradition — every language, every ceremonial practice, every storytelling form, every relationship to landscape — encodes a strategy for being human that was developed over centuries or millennia in response to specific conditions. The Aboriginal fire grammar encodes a strategy for coexisting with fire in Australian ecosystems. The Yoruba Ogun grammar encodes a strategy for containing the ambivalence of technology. The Kashmir Shaivism grammar encodes 112 distinct strategies for recognizing consciousness as the ground of experience. The Ostrom commons encodes strategies for governing shared resources without privatization or state control. Each is a seed — a specific solution to a specific version of the human problem, preserved in transmissible form.

When these grammars are destroyed — through colonization, forced assimilation, economic displacement, or the subtler violence of cultural homogenization — what is lost is not just beauty or heritage. What is lost is strategic diversity. The species' repertoire of responses to unknown future challenges narrows. We become, culturally, a monoculture. And as Chapter 4 established: monocultures collapse.

This transforms the conversation about cultural preservation from a sentimental register — we should save dying languages because they are beautiful — to a strategic one: we should maintain cultural diversity because monocultures collapse and we do not know which strategy we will need next.

This is the compassion criterion in the three-filter test, given a game-theoretic definition: compassion means maintaining conditions for future adaptation. It means preserving strategic diversity. It is the equivalent of a farmer saving seeds. You do it not because you are sentimental but because you are paying attention to what happens when you don't.

*Questions this chapter raises but does not answer:*

*If grammars are the cooperative structures that sustain iterated games, and they require communities to function, what happens when the community is a global network rather than a village?*

*If Ostrom's design principles describe successful commons governance, can they scale to the AI commons — a resource generated by billions of data points, governed by no community, owned by a handful of corporations?*

*If grammars can be rebuilt after genocide, what does that imply about their resilience? And what does it imply about the cost?*

*If the five functions of grammars (constrain, distribute, scaffold, transmit, diversify) describe what AI governance needs, which functions are currently being attempted and which are missing?*
