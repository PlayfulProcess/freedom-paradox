# Ostrom's Framework: Stretches But Doesn't Break for AI

**Summary:** Ostrom's eight design principles can be productively applied to AI governance but only with significant modifications. The emerging consensus is that the framework must be hybridized.

## The five fractures:
1. Rivalry problem: AI models are non-rivalrous (unlike fish)
2. Scale: Ostrom validated only at local scale; AI is global
3. Speed: Commons institutions develop over decades; AI changes in months
4. Power asymmetry: Google vs individual users dwarfs anything Ostrom studied
5. Harm type: AI harms (bias, surveillance) are not depletion problems

## What transfers:
- Polycentric governance (multiple overlapping centers of authority)
- Institutional analysis methodology (IAD framework)
- The insight that governance must be context-sensitive

## What doesn't transfer directly:
- Boundaries (what is the "resource" in AI?)
- Monitoring (of what, by whom?)
- Graduated sanctions (who enforces?)

## What the book should do:

The book uses Ostrom extensively in Ch12 (Commons That Can Kill) and Ch38 (The Commons). It should:

1. Add one paragraph acknowledging the category-mistake critique: "Ostrom's framework was built for fish, not algorithms. AI models are non-rivalrous, global, and change faster than any institution she studied. At least five scholars have argued this constitutes a category mistake (Wiley 2018, Araral 2014, China-CEE Institute 2026). The framework's value for AI lies not in its specific principles but in its analytical methodology — the insistence on polycentric, context-sensitive governance over both state centralization and market laissez-faire."

2. Name the successful small-scale implementations: Te Hiku Media (Māori language AI), READ-COOP (Transkribus), GovAI Coalition

Source: Olugbade 2025 Global Public Policy and Governance; Stern 2011; Huang & Siddarth 2023; China-CEE Institute 2026; Harré 2025
