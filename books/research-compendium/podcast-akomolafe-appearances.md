## 1. Postactivism: Responding to Crisis Without "Solving"

### The Core Claim

Across all 13 appearances, Akomolafe returns to one question with remarkable consistency: what happens when our responses to crisis reproduce the crisis? The term "postactivism" appears in at least 7 of the 13 conversations, always deployed not as a rejection of action but as a reorientation of where action comes from.

In the Garrison Institute conversation (Contemplating Postactivism), Akomolafe connects postactivism to Andreotti directly, calling her "my dear sister Vanessa Andreotti" -- confirming their intellectual alliance is personal, not merely citational. He frames postactivism as an invitation to notice how activism can function as what he calls a "pheromone incarceration" -- the ant death spiral.

### The Ant Death Spiral

The ant death spiral metaphor appears in at least three podcasts (HITW #117, Contemplating Postactivism, Cracks/Tricksters/Collapse) and is Akomolafe's most concrete illustration of the postactivist insight. Army ants navigate by following pheromone trails. When the trail loops back on itself, the ants circle endlessly, each following the one ahead, until they die of exhaustion. The metaphor operates at multiple levels:

- **Activism trapped in its own logic:** Progressive movements that keep circling the same strategies (petitions, protests, electoral politics) without noticing the circularity.
- **Psychology trapped in its own logic:** The discipline that claims to heal the individual without questioning whether the concept of "the individual" is itself the wound.
- **Technology trapped in its own logic:** Building platforms to solve the problems that platforms created -- the precise trap ch09 already names.

**Book relevance:** The ant death spiral is a powerful concrete metaphor that ch09 (Trap of Solving) could use to make the abstract argument visceral. It parallels the Grammars argument that the attention economy's response to the attention economy (more apps, more platforms, more content) reproduces the extractive structure.

### Slowing Down as Method

The phrase Akomolafe is most famous for appears across multiple podcasts: the call to slow down in urgent times. He is careful in the Sacred Sons and Fugitivity podcasts to clarify that this is not passivity. Slowing down means noticing what the urgency itself is made of -- the neurochemical rewards of being the solver, the dopamine of moral certainty, the avoidance of sitting with what cannot be fixed.

This maps directly onto the GTDF collective's neurochemical addictions (comfort, control, convenience, certainty, consumption, coherence) that ch09 already discusses via Andreotti. The podcast material adds Akomolafe's own framing: urgency is itself a colonial affect, a way that modernity recruits bodies to serve its reproduction.

## 2. Cracks and Tricksters: The Signature Metaphor

### Cracks as Generative

The crack metaphor is Akomolafe's most persistent image across these appearances (appearing in at least 10 of 13). A crack is not a failure -- it is where something new enters. He develops this most fully in the Beyond Fixing the World episode, the HITW Trickster episode, and the Animism conversation.

In the Beyond Fixing episode, he tells the story of his autistic son, who disrupts the linear narrative of normal development. The child on the autism spectrum becomes, for Akomolafe, the figure of the crack itself -- a rupture in what the system expects, and therefore a site where something unprecedented might emerge. He references Fernand Deligny, the French educator who refused to rehabilitate autistic children, instead creating "fugitive camps" where non-normative being could exist without being corrected.

In the HITW episode, he connects the crack directly to the figure of Eshu -- the Yoruba Orisha of the crossroads. Eshu is the crack in the ordered world, the trickster who disrupts pheromone trails and creates new pathways. This is not destruction for its own sake but generative disruption -- what cracks open can be received.

In the Animism conversation with Daniel Foor, he connects cracks to intergenerational failure and ancestral wounds. The cracks are not only in systems but in lineages -- in the places where transmission broke down, where stories were lost, where the thread between generations was severed by colonialism, migration, or trauma.

**Book relevance:** The crack-as-generative maps directly onto several arguments across the books:
- **Grammars ch09:** The crack in the solving reflex is where postactivist practice begins
- **Species ch02 (Darkness is the Medicine):** Darkness itself is a crack -- the absence of the expected, where new seeing becomes possible
- **Decolonization:** Colonial violence produced cracks in every knowledge system it encountered, and those cracks are simultaneously wounds and sites of emergence
- **Repair Deck:** The entire architecture of repair assumes that breaks are not mere failures but opportunities for a different kind of joining (kintsugi logic)

### Trickster Logic

The trickster appears in at least 8 of 13 podcasts. Akomolafe positions the trickster not as a character to emulate but as a force that acts through situations. In the HITW episode, he describes being called "trickster in residence" and embraces the designation while noting that trickster energy resists being claimed or institutionalized.

Key trickster characteristics across the podcasts:
- **Indirect path when the direct path fails:** The trickster does not confront the obstacle; it reroutes around it
- **Refusal of heroism:** The trickster is not the hero who saves the world but the disruptive agent who prevents the hero narrative from calcifying
- **Play as disruption:** Children at play, the fool, the one who does not know the rules -- these figures create openings precisely because they do not follow the expected script
- **The crossroads as site of power:** Not the destination but the intersection where multiple paths meet and multiply

**Book relevance:** For Fire ch10 (Not Pushing an Agenda), the trickster provides a counterpoint to Amodei's engineering-heroism. Amodei approaches AI safety as a problem to be solved through technical alignment; Akomolafe would say this is the ant death spiral of AI governance -- building better pheromone trails.

## 3. Fugitivity: The Refusal to Be Captured

### Fugitivity as Ontological

The Fugitivity lecture (Where is God Now?) provides the most sustained treatment of this concept. For Akomolafe, fugitivity is not merely political (the escaped slave) but ontological -- being itself is fugitive, always escaping the categories imposed on it. He draws on Fred Moten's concept of the undercommons, the Black radical tradition, and Fanon to argue that the fugitive is not someone who has failed to be captured but someone who reveals that capture was never complete.

In the Get More Lost episode, he extends this: getting lost is not failure but method. The modern obsession with orientation -- finding yourself, discovering your purpose, knowing where you are going -- is itself a colonial project. The fugitive moves without a map.

### Whiteness as Order (Not Race)

In the Fugitivity lecture, Akomolafe makes a careful move: he defines whiteness not as a racial category but as a mode of ordering. He states directly that whiteness is not evil but is rather a paradigm of organization, a way of stabilizing the world that necessarily excludes what does not fit. This allows him to critique the ordering impulse without reducing the critique to racial accusation.

This is strategically important: it means the postactivist critique applies to progressive institutions and movements that reproduce whiteness-as-order even while claiming to oppose whiteness-as-race. Identity politics, in his framing, can become another form of ordering that excludes the fugitive -- the one who does not fit the categories of oppressor or oppressed.

### Composting God

A striking development in the Fugitivity and Ten Laws episodes: Akomolafe speaks of "composting God" -- allowing the divine to decompose so that something new can grow from what remains. This connects to his work at an actual composting farm and his insistence that death, decay, and dissolution are not failures but necessary phases of regeneration.

**Book relevance:** This directly serves the Axiom book's Tillich argument. Tillich's "God above God" -- the God who appears when God has disappeared -- is structurally identical to Akomolafe's composted God. The theologian and the Yoruba-influenced philosopher arrive at the same place: the sacred survives its own decomposition.

## 4. Animism and Relationality

### Animism as Politics

In the dedicated Animism conversation with Daniel Foor and in the Sacred Sons episode, Akomolafe makes an important clarification: animism is not a belief system (everything is alive!) but a political disposition. It names a different way of being in relation to the world -- one that does not begin from the assumption of human sovereignty over inert matter.

In the Animism conversation, he notes that Yoruba tradition does not have a term equivalent to "animism" because the relational understanding is the default, not a special category. The word "animism" is itself a colonial artifact -- a way of naming from the outside what indigenous peoples simply live. He suggests it functions as useful shorthand in cross-cultural dialogue while remaining wary of its categorizing tendencies.

Foor (the co-speaker) contextualizes this within ancestral medicine practice, and the conversation becomes a live demonstration of how two different lineage-holders (Yoruba-influenced and Orisha-initiated) can speak across traditions without reducing either to the other.

### The More-Than-Human

Across multiple podcasts (Cracks/Tricksters, Sound Bath, Fugitivity), Akomolafe describes himself as working at the intersection of the human and the non-human. He insists that agency is not a human property but a distributed phenomenon -- the world acts through us, and we act through the world, in patterns that cannot be reduced to individual intention.

This is not abstract philosophy for Akomolafe. In the Beyond Fixing episode, his autistic son becomes the figure of more-than-human communication -- someone who relates to the world through patterns that the normative human framework cannot capture but that are not lesser, merely different.

**Book relevance:** The animist frame connects directly to:
- **Grammars ch01 (Biological Hypothesis):** If co-regulation is the organism's default, then the animist worldview (everything participates in everything) is not mysticism but neurobiology described in different terms
- **Species ch03 (What the Old Stories Knew):** Indigenous story traditions encode animist relationality in narrative form -- the stories themselves are technology for maintaining relational awareness
- **Decolonization:** The five-domain framework (Body, Mind, Spirit, Collective, Planet) maps onto Akomolafe's insistence that decolonization is not merely political but ontological

## 5. What This Means for the Books

### Strengthening the Argument

**The ant death spiral gives ch09 its missing concrete metaphor.** The existing draft operates at high abstraction (the colonial reflex, the five-move pattern, the neurochemical signature). Akomolafe's ant death spiral provides a visceral, memorable image for the trap of solving that can anchor the abstract argument.

**The autistic son story humanizes the theoretical.** Akomolafe is not merely theorizing about the crack -- he is living it. His son's existence outside normative developmental categories is the existential ground of his philosophy. This personal stake gives his work an authority that purely academic critique lacks.

**The Eshu/trickster connection provides mythological architecture.** The books already draw on multiple mythological traditions (Songlines, Jataka, Torah). Akomolafe's Eshu/trickster material adds a West African figure who embodies the logic of the crack -- the one who stands at the crossroads where ordered paths intersect and multiply.

**The composting/fugitivity language enriches the Tillich connection.** Research-04 (Akomolafe-Andreotti) identified the shared ground between Akomolafe and the books' philosophical framework. The podcast material deepens this with "composting God" -- a phrase that performs exactly what Tillich's "God above God" describes, but in Yoruba-inflected language that avoids the Western theological register.

### Challenging the Argument

**The 69/31 problem remains unresolved.** Akomolafe never provides criteria for distinguishing solvable from unsolvable problems. His consistent position is that the distinction itself is part of the trap -- that categorizing problems as "solvable" already presupposes the sovereign subject who will do the solving. The books' 69/31 frame (some things are perpetual problems, some are solvable) is a direct departure from Akomolafe, and the departure should be acknowledged rather than papered over.

**The practice gap.** Akomolafe is a magnificent diagnostician. What he is not -- and what listeners across these 13 podcasts repeatedly ask him about -- is a provider of practices. "What do we actually do?" comes up in nearly every Q&A segment. His answer is consistently a variation on: the demand for practice reproduces the colonial reflex. This is intellectually consistent but practically unsatisfying, and it is exactly where the books diverge. Working Architecture, the Repair Deck, and recursive.eco are all answers to the question Akomolafe refuses to answer.

**The risk of circularity.** Several interviewers (notably in the HITW and Contemplating Postactivism episodes) push Akomolafe on whether his critique is self-immunizing: if every response to the crisis is part of the crisis, then his own response (writing, speaking, running the Emergence Network) is also part of the crisis. He handles this gracefully -- acknowledging complicity, framing his work as gesture rather than solution -- but the structural problem remains. The books handle this with the Rowson formulation ("this move is forced, even if it loses"), which is more action-oriented than Akomolafe would be comfortable with.

**The technology question.** Akomolafe discusses technology, algorithms, and digital systems in several podcasts (Beyond Fixing, Contemplating Postactivism) but always as sites of concern, never as potential allies. His position is that technology is already captured by the ordering impulse (whiteness-as-order) and cannot be trusted to produce decolonial outcomes. This is in direct tension with the books' commitment to open-source technology as responsibility infrastructure. The tension is productive: it keeps the project honest about what it cannot guarantee.

### New Material Not in Existing Research

**The Deligny reference** (Beyond Fixing): Fernand Deligny's refusal to rehabilitate autistic children -- instead creating alternative spaces where non-normative being could exist -- is a powerful precursor to the books' container logic. A container that does not try to fix but creates conditions for emergence is exactly what the grammar-as-responsibility-structure is supposed to do. [RESEARCH NEEDED: Deligny primary sources for Working Architecture or Decolonization]

**The sanctuary concept** (Beyond Fixing, Cancel Culture, Sound Bath): Akomolafe's "making sanctuary" is distinct from safety. In the Sound Bath episode, he clarifies that sanctuary does not mean keeping us safe -- it means creating conditions where encounter becomes possible. This maps directly onto the books' distinction between containers that protect (heteronomous) and containers that hold (theonomous).

**The identity politics critique from inside** (Cancel Culture): Akomolafe critiques cancel culture and identity politics not from a conservative position but from a position further left than the left -- arguing that identity categories themselves are colonial artifacts that cannot produce liberation. This is a subtle and important argument for the Decolonization book: decolonization cannot simply invert the colonial hierarchy (centering the previously marginalized) because inversion preserves the structure.

**The psychology-as-policing argument** (Cracks/Tricksters/Collapse, multiple others): Akomolafe trained as a clinical psychologist in Nigeria and describes his disillusionment in the Cracks/Tricksters episode as discovering the politics embedded in therapeutic practice. He frames psychology as a disciplinary mechanism that constructs the normal subject and pathologizes everything else. This has direct bearing on the Social Working book's critique of the professional helping relationship.
