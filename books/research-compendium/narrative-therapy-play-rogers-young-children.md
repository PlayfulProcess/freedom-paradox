## 1. Narrative Therapy with Young Children

### Core Principle

Michael White and David Epston (late 1980s): **"The problem is the problem; the person is not the problem."** For children under seven, this unfolds through play, drawing, puppets, costumes, and dramatic enactment — not verbal conversation.

### Externalization

Born in work with a young child. White's landmark 1984 case of "Sneaky Poo" renamed encopresis as a mischievous character the child could outwit, transforming shame into agency.

Techniques for pre-literate children:
- Name problems as characters ("Mr. Mischief," "the Worry Monster," "Tricky Temper")
- Draw them, build them in sandtrays, embody them through puppets
- Freeman, Epston & Lobovits (*Playful Approaches to Serious Problems*, 1997) — foundational text — describe externalization with young children as "like playing a game of pretend"
- Children in Piaget's pre-operational stage naturally attribute personality and intention to objects, making externalized problems intuitively appealing

### Re-authoring for Pre-literate Children

Cannot rely on writing or verbal reflection. Adaptations:
- Five-year-old Rachel created a "Sleeping Soundly Handbook" of drawings chronicling victory over nighttime fears
- White's case of Gerry (severe feeding difficulties): Gerry donned a tiger costume and began eating "as a tiger" — re-authoring was entirely performative, documented in photo album, witnessed by cousins
- **Key insight**: Re-authoring with young children is fundamentally enacted rather than narrated

### Witnessing Practices

- White awarded children "Fear Busting and Monster Taming Certificates," inducted them into the fictional "Monster and Worm Tamers Society of Australia and New Zealand"
- Children share drawings, handbooks, puppet performances with invited audiences
- **Epston's finding**: Therapeutic letters (read aloud to pre-literate children) rated by clients as equivalent to **4.5 therapy sessions** on average

### Tree of Life Exercise

Created by Zimbabwean psychologist Ncazelo Ncube-Mlilo for children affected by HIV/AIDS, co-developed with David Denborough at the Dulwich Centre. Four-part structure: drawing the tree (roots as heritage, trunk as skills, branches as hopes), creating a forest, discussing "storms of life" at safe distance, celebration ceremony. Works best with children over six.

### Key Practitioners

- **Jennifer Freeman & Dean Lobovits**: Pioneered "puppet co-therapists" and expressive arts within narrative framework
- **David Epston**: Therapeutic letter-writing, "Temper Tantrum Parties," "Nightwatching," "Wonderfulness Interviews" (ask what is wonderful about a child before addressing problems)
- **David Marsten & Laurie Markham**: *Narrative Therapy in Wonderland* (2016) with Epston — connecting with children's imaginative know-how
- **Alice Morgan**: Co-authored *Narrative Therapy with Children and their Families* with White (2006)

### Evidence Gap

Research base for narrative therapy with children under seven specifically is **limited but growing**:
- 2024 qualitative study (Jørgensen): Danish therapists report externalization easy for young children, "well-nested within children's natural interest in play"
- Systematic reviews: small sample sizes, geographic concentration (many quantitative studies from Iran), absence of long-term follow-up
- Fundamental tension: narrative therapy's social constructionist philosophy sits uneasily with RCT assumptions

## 2. Evidence-Based Therapies for Children Under Seven

### Parent-Child Interaction Therapy (PCIT)

Developed by Sheila Eyberg, 1970s. California Evidence-Based Clearinghouse's highest rating.

- **Effect size**: d = **1.65** for reducing externalizing behavior in ages 2–7
- Two phases: Child-Directed Interaction (PRIDE skills: Praise, Reflect, Imitate, Describe, Enthusiasm) and Parent-Directed Interaction
- Delivered through live coaching via wireless earpiece behind one-way mirror
- One of few interventions specifically designed and tested for 2–7 age range

### Child-Parent Psychotherapy (CPP)

Developed by Alicia Lieberman, UCSF. Gold standard for trauma-exposed children birth to age five.

- Three published RCTs: significant reductions in traumatic stress (effect size d = **0.64**) and behavior problems
- Co-constructs developmentally appropriate trauma narrative with parent-child dyad
- Addresses "ghosts in the nursery" (parents' unresolved trauma) and "angels in the nursery" (positive intergenerational influences)
- Now used in **39 U.S. states and 6 countries**

### Child-Centered Play Therapy (CCPT)

Rooted in Virginia Axline (1947), systematized by Garry Landreth.

- **Meta-analysis** (Bratton et al., 2005): 93 controlled studies, overall effect size **0.80 SD**
- Critical finding: **parental involvement produced the largest effects**; humanistic/non-directive approaches significantly outperformed directive ones
- Mean age across studies: 6.7–7.0 years — squarely in under-seven population
- Play itself is the therapeutic language; verbal articulation not required

### Filial Therapy / Child-Parent Relationship Therapy (CPRT)

Landreth & Bratton's 10-session model: parents learn play therapy skills, conduct weekly 30-minute "Special Play Times" at home with specific toy kit, share video recordings in supervision groups. Produced the **largest effect sizes** of all play therapy interventions in Bratton meta-analysis.

### Other Approaches

- **Theraplay** (Jernberg & Booth): Four dimensions — Structure, Engagement, Nurture, Challenge. 2021 systematic review found promising results but only 6 eligible studies.
- **Circle of Security** (Cooper, Hoffman, Powell, Marvin): Elegant visual map of attachment needs. Extraordinary dissemination (10,000+ facilitators in Australia by 2016) despite effects in RCTs that are "small and rarely significant."
- **Gestalt Play Therapy** (Violet Oaklander): Creative arts with Gestalt principles of awareness and contact
- **Jungian Sandplay** (Dora Kalff): Suited for pre-verbal processing through tactile engagement
- **Cognitive-Behavioral Play Therapy** (Susan Knell): CBT adapted for children as young as 2½ through puppets, modeling, story-based cognitive restructuring
- **Somatic approaches** (Peter Levine's Somatic Experiencing): For children who process trauma in body rather than words

### Comparative Evidence Hierarchy

| Presenting Problem | Best-Supported Approach | Effect Size |
|---|---|---|
| Behavioral problems (ages 2–7) | PCIT | d = 1.65 |
| Trauma (birth–5) | CPP | d = 0.64 |
| Broad applicability | CCPT | d = 0.80 |
| Parent-mediated change | CPRT/Filial therapy | Largest in Bratton meta-analysis |

## 3. Fred Rogers' Developmental Framework

### Intellectual Formation

Three converging streams:
- **Eight years** (1954–1962) completing Bachelor of Divinity at Pittsburgh Theological Seminary; ordained 1963 with charge "to continue his work with children and families through the mass media"
- Studied at University of Pittsburgh Graduate School of Child Development
- Most influential relationship: **Margaret McFarland** (1905–1988)

### Margaret McFarland

Associate professor, Dept. of Psychiatry, University of Pittsburgh School of Medicine. Co-founded Arsenal Family & Children's Center (1953) with **Benjamin Spock** and **Erik Erikson**.

- Erikson said: "Margaret McFarland knew more than anyone in this world about families with young children"
- Her foundational principle: **"Attitudes aren't taught, they're caught"**
- Rogers and McFarland met mid-1950s, continued meeting **nearly weekly for ~30 years**
- She served as chief psychological consultant for the show (1966–1988), reviewing every script
- Originated the phrase: **"Anything that is human is mentionable, and anything that is mentionable is manageable"**

### McFarland's Applied Insight — The Fire Example

When Rogers wanted to do episodes about fire, McFarland helped him realize he must first address children's anxieties about controlling fluids — because children's dreams about fire relate to potty-training fears about controlling body fluids. Rogers created segments about water flow before introducing fire. When fire episodes aired, **seven complaint calls came from parents of frightened children — and every one of those children was having urinary difficulties**.

### Eriksonian Architecture

Rogers' connection to Erikson was direct (Erikson was co-founder of the Arsenal Center where Rogers studied).

- **Trust vs. mistrust**: Same opening song, same sweater, same trolley, same closing, same promise: "I'll be back next time"
- **Autonomy vs. shame**: "I like you just the way you are" — autonomy-supporting without shaming
- **Initiative vs. guilt**: "I wonder..." approach modeling initiative without directing it
- Show's six "fundamentals" began with "a sense of trust in the adults, peers, and world around them"

### "Freddish" — Piaget-Informed Language

Writers formalized a nine-step process for translating ideas into child-appropriate language:
- "It is dangerous to play in the street" → nine iterations → "Your favorite grown-ups can tell you where it is safe to play. It is important to try to listen to them, and listening is an important part of growing."
- Nurse's "I'm going to blow this up" (blood pressure cuff) → re-dubbed "I'm going to puff this up with some air" because pre-operational children might hear explosion
- Song "You Can Never Go Down the Drain" addressed a fear adults wouldn't anticipate but pre-operational children genuinely harbor

### The Neighborhood of Make-Believe as Psychological Instrument

Purposes: processing difficult emotions at safe distance, teaching fantasy-reality distinction (Rogers was never present in Make-Believe — deliberate, informed by McFarland), providing practice with disappearance and reappearance through Trolley's departures and returns.

Puppet functions:
- **Daniel Striped Tiger**: Rogers' inner child — shy, vulnerable, afraid of being inadequate (Joanne Rogers confirmed: "Daniel was the real Fred")
- **King Friday XIII**: Authority that could be gently challenged
- **Lady Elaine Fairchilde**: Disruptive, insecure parts of personality
- **Henrietta Pussycat**: Anxiety and need for reassurance
- **Music**: Pianist Johnny Costa's key changes during Make-Believe transitions incorporated notes from old key into new — Rogers compared this to a child seeing mother and teacher together at new school

### Difficult Topics

- Two days after RFK assassination: special program on scary events
- Inaugural week: King Friday building border wall and drafting guards (war allegory)
- Addressed death, divorce, disability, race
- Shared foot-soaking pool with François Clemmons (first Black actor with recurring role on children's show) during period when Southern hotel owners poured bleach into pools
- Told children truth about Santa Claus despite angry parent mail

## 4. Deep Convergence Across All Three Frameworks

### Externalization Operates Everywhere Under Different Names

- Narrative therapy's formal technique = Rogers' puppets expressing difficult feelings = CPP's co-construction of trauma narratives = CCPT's symbolic play
- Mechanism is the same: what is outside can be examined, managed, and transformed

### The Mentionable-Manageable Principle Unifies All Approaches

McFarland's phrase captures what narrative therapy achieves through naming, play therapy through symbolic expression, CPP through trauma narratives, and Rogers through direct address of difficult topics. Feelings denied expression gain destructive power; feelings given form become manageable.

### Unconditional Regard Is the Shared Foundation

Rogers' "I like you just the way you are" parallels narrative therapy's non-pathologizing stance, CCPT's permissive acceptance, Theraplay's nurture dimension, and DDP's PACE attitude (Playfulness, Acceptance, Curiosity, Empathy).

### Every Effective Approach Involves Caregivers Centrally

- PCIT coaches parents in real time
- CPP treats parent-child dyad
- CPRT trains parents as therapeutic agents
- Narrative therapy positions parents as co-narrators and primary witnesses
- Rogers modeled parenting attitudes on camera
- **Texas Tech research**: Children watching *Daniel Tiger's Neighborhood* showed higher empathy and emotion recognition, **with outcomes improving further when parents reinforced lessons**
