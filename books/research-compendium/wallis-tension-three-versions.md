## The Contradiction Wallis Partly Sees

Wallis built an entire "Near Enemies" pedagogical framework — 14+ named ways his own tradition's teachings get distorted (spiritual bypassing, "you create your own reality," etc.). He's acutely aware the tradition can be weaponized. He's not naive.

But his self-awareness stops at the structural circularity. He sees misapplication problems clearly but not the deeper issue: how do you verify that consciousness is fundamental using a method (meditation) that presupposes consciousness is fundamental?

He oscillates — in the same episode he'll make a realist claim ("consciousness IS the ground") and then a pragmatist move ("people who studied this through deep introspection had valid insights" — judging by outcomes). This "pragmatist-realist oscillation" may be the inevitable result of articulating a nondual position within dualist language.

His "bet my life on it" certainty is the key tension. Either:
- That certainty IS what Tillich calls absolute faith (courage to be despite nonbeing) — and Wallis just doesn't name it as courage because he names it as recognition
- Or that certainty IS genuine recognition — and Tillich's framework can't account for direct experience because it operates entirely within existential-philosophical categories

The Axiom book's argument: you can't resolve this from within either framework. That's the unresolvable tension.

## Three Versions of "It Might Not Matter"

### 1. The Animist Version (Schrei / Emerald)
Schrei's animist approach maintains relationship with the world's aliveness without requiring a philosophical framework to justify that relationship. You don't need to resolve whether inherent value is truth or axiom to live as if the world is alive. You just... relate.

### 2. The Psychological Version (Almaas)
His "theory of holes": where we feel absence, we discover presence. The practice of relating to the hole IS the recognition — you don't need to first prove the ground exists before standing on it.

### 3. The Empirical Version (Gopnik)
Seeing your grandchild as infinitely valuable is "seeing clearly, not hallucinating." A scientific claim that inherent value is observable without metaphysics. Value perception is accurate perception, not projection. No theology needed.

## The Dissolution

All three suggest the Axiom book's deepest contribution is showing that the Tillich-Wallis debate, honestly held, dissolves into practice — which is what both traditions actually recommend.

The argument is the finger pointing at the moon, and the moon is relationship.
