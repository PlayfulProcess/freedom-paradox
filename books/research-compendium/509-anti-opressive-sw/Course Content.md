**Several of Dr. Beverly Daniel Tatum’s (1992) working assumptions strongly resonate with me, especially her framing of racism as a systemic structure of advantage based on race, rather than merely individual prejudice. I agree with her assertion that racism is a pervasive aspect of U.S. socialization and that it is virtually impossible to grow up in this society without absorbing misinformation about racialized groups. This assumption helps me locate racism beyond individual intent and understand it as something we are all shaped by, even when we consciously reject racist beliefs.**

**I also resonate with Tatum’s distinction between prejudice and racism, particularly her emphasis on power. While anyone can hold prejudices, racism in the U.S. context is reinforced through institutional and cultural systems that systematically benefit White people as a group. I appreciate that Tatum also assumes we are not to blame for what we learned as children, yet we are responsible as adults for interrupting cycles of oppression. This framing allows space for accountability without collapsing into shame or defensiveness.**

**When I reflect on which emotions prevent me from speaking my truth in interracial conversations about race, fear is often the most visible one—fear of using the wrong language, saying something harmful, or revealing my own blind spots. At a deeper level, however, guilt and grief emerge. Guilt arises from recognizing how deeply I remain entangled in systems that reproduce racial and economic inequality in everyday life, and grief comes from realizing that many of these systems cannot be easily dismantled at the individual level. These emotions can make silence feel safer than engagement. Conditions that make it safer for me to speak include spaces that normalize discomfort, allow for emotional processing, and resist the pressure to arrive quickly at solutions.**

**I have certainly experienced discomfort in conversations about race. Sometimes this discomfort was worked through when there was time to stay with it and when the group resisted rushing toward resolution. Other times, the discomfort remained unresolved, particularly when conversations shifted too quickly into problem-solving or reassurance. I am increasingly aware that modern professional and academic cultures often reward action, solutions, and “fixes,” which can make it difficult to remain present with problems that are complex, systemic, and not individually solvable.**

**This is where non-closure becomes essential. As Singleton (2021) emphasizes, courageous conversations about race require accepting that there may be no immediate resolution. Vanessa Andreotti’s work deepens this insight by cautioning against “world savior” approaches that frame justice as something individuals can fix through good intentions or heroic action. Drawing on scholars like Pogge and Dobson, Andreotti reminds us that responsibility often arises not from charity or beneficence, but from our participation in global systems—*chains of cause and effect*—that produce harm. From this perspective, the impulse to rush into problem-solving can function as a way to avoid sitting with complicity, grief, and uncertainty.**

**As a future social worker, I am learning that courageous conversations are not only about breaking silence, but about resisting the pressure to resolve what is structurally unresolved. Breaking the silence is hard, but so is remaining present without saviorism. Accepting non-closure allows space for humility, relational accountability, and sustained engagement with systemic injustice—rather than retreating into silence or premature solutions.**

It offers firm boundaries and permission to wrestle with ambiguity. As I continue this journey, I am learning that ethical, antiracist, and anti-oppressive practice may require slowing down, being willing to change course, and prioritizing care over certainty.

Vanessa Andreotti (2014) calls instead for "analeptic" thinking—holding contradictions without resolution, without synthesis. What if I hold: I have implicit bias AND I'm committed to LGBTQ+ affirmation AND my bias will likely affect my practice in ways I can't fully control AND awareness alone won't eliminate the bias AND the frameworks I'm using to understand bias may themselves be inadequate. Nigerian philosopher Bayo Akomolafe talks about "the crack"—the place where frameworks break down, where something doesn't fit. My uncertainty feels like a crack. Problematising Jane Addams reveals a complex legacy where she acted as both a pioneer for women's entry into public life and a sanction for a professionalised form of colonialism. While her work at Hull House is often "lionized," this narrative frequently ignores her role in upholding white supremacy and the settler state.

**1\. Heteropatriarchy and the "Helper" Niche**

Addams and her contemporaries were governed by the "separate spheres" ideology, which dictated that respectable women belong in the private domain while men occupy the public sphere of politics and paid work. To enter the public arena, middle-class white women had to frame their roles as a continuation of domesticity—moving into "female-gendered" professions like nursing, teaching, and "social welfare activities".

• The Constraint: This model allowed women to work as long as they remained "helpers" who surveilled the private lives of others.

• The Moral Hierarchy: This professionalisation valued the "men's sphere" over the women’s and relied on moral economies that accumulated "moral worth" for white women by differentiating them from the "morally inferior" poor and racialised women they were meant to "save".

**2\. The Transfer and Monetisation of Care**

From an oppressed perspective, the professionalisation championed by Addams functioned to delegitimise Indigenous and community practices of caring.

• Co-option of Mutual Aid: Before social work was a paid profession, Black and Indigenous communities practiced unmonetized mutual aid and communal care, such as parallel welfare systems and extended kinship networks.

• "Taking a Cut": The rise of agencies like the Charity Organization Society (now the United Way) transformed these community burdens into monetised relationships. Instead of direct support, funds are diverted to professional salaries and administrative costs—often taking a cut of roughly 15%—while requiring poor people to adopt "middle-class values" to be deemed "deserving".

• Community Destruction: This "help" often resulted in the dismantling of communities; for instance, the destruction of Africville was justified by white social workers as a "rescue" that forcibly separated families and imposed nuclear family norms.

**3\. Opening and Closing the Door**

Addams is often framed as "almost unparalleled," a narrative that naturalises white people in the role of the helper and renders social workers of colour as "aberrational".

• Opening the Way: She created a path for white women to exert "private power" in the public sphere.

• Closing the Way: By centering the "white savior" model, she helped close the door on alternative, radical epistemologies. Her model of "doing the right thing" was often intrinsically colonial, involving the "mingling" of races to facilitate assimilation into white civility.

• Sanctioning Violence: Addams also helped close the door to true liberation by sanctioning eugenic practices; she endorsed eugenicists like Katherine Davis, who led the mass incarceration of women deemed "unfit" or "defective" during their childbearing years. Also, even though she opposed lynching, she did not opposed the view that black man were indeed a threat to white woman; which were the stated reasons behind most lynching. 

Ultimately, Addams’s model turned care into a commodity of privilege. Following her model means participating in a "helping imperative" that requires one to maintain a distance from "underprivileged communities" to preserve their own status as the "expert" provider of care.

In couples therapy, perpetual conflicts aren't obstacles to relationship—they're the texture OF relationship. They're what makes you need each other. If my partner and I were identical, there'd be no friction, no learning, no adaptation.

Now apply this to disability and society. **What if neurodivergence, chronic illness, different ways of moving and sensing aren't problems but the very differences that make us need each other—that create society as relationship?**

Forest ecosystems teach this. Monocultures collapse. Diverse forests—multiple species, different root systems, symbiotic relationships—are resilient. The differences ARE what creates adaptive capacity.

DBT's dialectical thinking helps here. Linehan (1993) teaches that **behaviors are adaptive or maladaptive depending on context.** Stimming isn't "abnormal behavior"; it's adaptive sensory regulation. The question isn't "what's wrong with this person?" but "what environment is this person adapting to?"

**Creating Adaptive Relationships**

What would it look like to approach disability the way good couples therapy approaches perpetual differences? Not trying to solve or eliminate them, but building relationships and systems that can work with them?

In couples work, we don't try to make the introvert become extroverted. We help them build a relationship flexible enough to hold both ways of being. We recognize that the tension between different needs is generative.

Slayter et al. (2022) call for moving beyond accommodations toward "celebrating Disability culture, affirming Disabled identities" (p. 90). This isn't just adding ramps—it's fundamentally reframing difference as essential rather than problematic.

What would an adaptive society look like? Not "How do we fix people to fit our structures?" but "How do we build structures flexible enough to include all ways of being human?" Not monoculture but ecosystem. Not problem-solving but relationship-building.

Maybe the question isn't "how do we help Disabled people adapt to society?" Maybe it's: "How does society become capable of relationship with all the different ways of being human?" Just like in couples therapy, where we're not trying to fix either partner but to create a relationship that can hold them both.

Slayter et al. (2022) quote bell hooks: "What we do is more important than what we say" (p. 92). So what am I going to do? Stop approaching difference as problem. Question every impulse to "fix" rather than include. Recognize that we become adaptive—as couples, as communities, as societies—not by becoming the same, but by cultivating relationships that can hold difference.

**When Statistics Shatter Charitable Interpretations**

**When I first read about racial disproportionality in child welfare, I looked for explanations other than racism. Maybe it was poverty, lack of services, intersecting risk factors.**

**Assimilation as "Protection": Judging From the Outside**

**Chapman and Withers (2019) document how assimilation policies devastated Indigenous communities through residential schools and forced adoption. Social workers promoted solving the "Indian Problem" by removing children to "rescue" them from their culture (p. 322). The CSWE Statement acknowledges social workers saw their role as rescuing Indigenous children from poverty created by colonization itself (CSWE, n.d., p. 15).**

**This connects directly to last week's discussion of rehabilitation and ableism. In both cases, people from one context judge what people from another context need. Ableism is "normal" people calling themselves "normative" so that different people feel they need to fit. Child welfare is "normal" families being defined as normative so that different families' children get removed from their "abnormal" homes to be made "normal."**

**We're still operating under the problem-solving mental model instead of working with difference and trying to bring justice for all. Rehabilitation tries to "fix" disabled bodies to fit abled norms. Assimilation tried to "civilize" Indigenous children to fit white norms. Child welfare tries to "rescue" Black and Brown children from poor families to fit middle-class norms. All assume deviation requires correction rather than recognizing difference as natural human variation requiring just systems.**

**Replacement, Not Extinction**

**Dettlaff et al. (2020) aren't proposing extinction of support for families—they're proposing replacement. The article identifies what this means: expanding safety net programs like subsidies and paid medical leave, creating universal child allowances, and eliminating racist policies that restrict access to concrete supports (p. 510). Research shows that restrictions on safety net programs increase child welfare involvement, while policies expanding subsidies decrease it (p. 510).**

**This is about investing in communities rather than surveilling them. Just as we could address ableism by building accessible environments instead of rehabilitating bodies, we could address child safety by providing housing, healthcare, and income support instead of investigation and removal.**

**Other Systems That Produce Inequity**

**Dettlaff et al. (2020) use Kendi's framework: policies are racist if they produce racial inequity (p. 18). By this definition, systems requiring medical insurance are racist, since minorities are underrepresented in insured populations. Mental health services that oversaturate care for white middle-class clients while underserving Black communities produce inequity.**

**Chapman and Withers (2019) argue these systems operate on the assumption that some lives are more valuable than others (p. 322). When therapists preferentially serve populations that look like them, privileged groups receive abundant services while oppressed communities have least access.**

**Alignment with Social Work Ethics**

**The NASW Code of Ethics (2021) calls social workers to challenge social injustice, particularly where vulnerable populations are affected. A system removing Black children at 77% higher rates than white children with identical risk isn't broken—it's working as designed to maintain inequality.**

**Justice means recognizing we've been judging from the outside, defining ourselves as normative and everyone else as deviant. It means replacing problem-solving with working with difference. It means what Dettlaff calls for: community-based supports designed by and for families themselves, not imposed by people who've decided what "normal" looks like.**
