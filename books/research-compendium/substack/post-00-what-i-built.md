# Post 0: What I Built and Why I'm Reading It Slowly

**Draft for Substack — by PlayfulProcess**

---

A few months ago I wrote a post about tarot. I promised to build the tools. I built them.

recursive.eco is live — grammars for meaning-making through make-believe. Tarot decks, ancient texts, kids' stories, wellness tools. Created by people, not algorithms. Free and open.

flow.recursive.eco delivers grammars to your phone — using the device against its own logic. The card interrupts the scroll. Five seconds of looking at an image you didn't choose, before the habitual narrative reasserts itself, is a parasympathetic micro-shift. That's the practice.

kids.recursive.eco is a locked-down viewing space for children. No algorithm, no recommendations, no data collection. The parent builds the playlist. The child watches the same story over and over — because repetition is how nervous systems learn, and the algorithm is designed to prevent it.

The Campfire Stories — ten bilingual bedtime stories from six continents — are published on GitHub under CC BY-SA 4.0. Written with AI, inside human-designed constraints. The constraint is what made them meaningful.

The courses are free. The grammar format is portable. The books are open source.

And somewhere in the middle of building all of this, I also wrote a book. Or rather, I wrote six books that turned out to be one book.

---

## The book

It's called *The Species That Got Fire*.

42 chapters. 8 parts. 2 appendices. About 125,000 words. It connects open source politics, AI governance, nervous system science, bedtime stories, Kashmir Shaivism, invasion ecology, contemplative practice, and the question of whether a species smart enough to burn down the world is also wise enough to tend the fire.

The central claim: the only way to hold fire without being consumed by it is to identify with the whole — with the living system that the fire belongs to, not with the part that wields it. Offering to the whole is not sacrifice. It is the most natural and ethical process of life. What we call responsibility is, at root, participation in what is already happening.

The full book is already online, free, at [books.recursive.eco](https://books.recursive.eco). You can read it all now. You can highlight text, take notes, export them, share links to specific passages.

---

## Why I'm reading it slowly

I could automate this. Schedule the chapters. Let them post while I build the next thing.

But the book argues that the sympathetic interrupt — the pause between stimulus and response — is the practice. The algorithm wants speed. The grammar wants repetition. The book I wrote says that you cannot practice a grammar in a reactive state. You can only consume content.

So I'm going to practice what I wrote.

Starting next week, I'll publish one chapter per week on this Substack. Not because the book isn't finished — it is. But because I want to read it again, slowly, in public. I want to sit with each chapter the way the book says a child sits with a story: letting it land, noticing what it does in the body, coming back to it.

I built the tools because I am amazed by what AI makes possible. I am slowing down now because I want to do the manual work — the reading, the digesting, the tending — that no tool can do for me.

Fred Rogers built a television show for the same reason. Not because he loved the medium. Because the medium existed and children were watching, and someone had to build a version that served their inner life rather than exploiting their attention.

The tools are built. The book is written. Now the practice begins.
