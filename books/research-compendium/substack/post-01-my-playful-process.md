# My Playful Process: A Lifetime of Thinking, Three Days of Building

---

On March 30, 2026 at 9:06 PM, I committed the first file of what would become a 132,000-word book. By April 2, at 12:34 PM — seventy-two hours later — I had consolidated six separate books into one, integrated research from 350 podcast episodes, run a Linehan filter audit on every chapter, verified all data points against current sources, written a song, and was packing for a meditation retreat.

I want to tell you exactly how that happened, because I think the process is more interesting than the product — and because the process might be something you can use too.

---

## The Lifetime

I've been thinking about these questions since before I could name them. As an equity research analyst, I spent years asking *cui bono* — who benefits? — about every corporate action I studied. As a mother, I discovered that the bedtime story was doing something to my daughter's nervous system that no corporate earnings report ever did to mine. As a spiritual seeker, I sat with traditions from four continents without landing in any of them. As an MSW student, I discovered that the course designed to teach anti-oppressive practice was itself structured by the patterns it claimed to dismantle.

Each of these became a book. A book about open source and AI. A book about grammars and responsibility. A book about bedtime stories. A book about practical containers for families. A book about whether inherent value is truth or axiom. A book about what AI actually is.

The thinking took years. The research accumulated in folders, Kindle highlights, podcast notes, WhatsApp exchanges, journal entries — 222 of them in my own oracle app as of this morning. I didn't know they were one argument until I tried to write them down.

---

## The Three Days

Here's the timeline, from the git commits:

**March 30, evening:** Committed the Campfire Stories and research compendium. Resolved seventy VERIFY flags across sixteen Freedom Paradox chapters. The material existed. It was scattered across six books, forty-eight research files, and years of notes.

**March 31:** Wrote The Axiom Beneath the Ground — nine chapters, 13,000 words — in a single session. Research on Tillich, Wallis, the void, the circularity, the invisible world. Built the EPUB. Launched the website.

**April 1:** Consolidated everything. Six books became one. Forty-two chapters. Two-book structure: *The Dilemmas* and *Relational Responsibilities*. Integrated memoir passages. Built a highlight and annotation system so readers could mark up the text and export their notes. Rendered four grammar decks from Supabase — the Repair Deck, the Wise Heart, Decolonization, Social Working — 213 chapters from database to markdown in one script.

**April 2 (today):** Integrated thirty-one podcast research files covering 350+ episodes. Debunked Khan Academy's claims using Paul von Hippel's 2024 analysis. Wove in Paulo Blikstein's "Educational Soylent" critique. Ran a full Linehan filter audit — is each chapter useful, does it fit the data, is it compassionate? — across all forty-two chapters. Found the adaptive frame needs moral language for irreversible harm. Added: "The kudu suffers and dies. Both descriptions are necessary." Verified every data point in the food systems appendix against FAO, IPCC, and BLS sources. Zero VERIFY flags remaining.

Also: realized the tending is the fire. Wrote a song about it. Filed a pull request.

## The Tool

I did this with Claude on the Anthropic Max plan. Not as a ghostwriter. As a co-creator inside a human-designed container — which is exactly what the book argues all tools should be.

I chose the traditions. I chose the values. I chose the structure. I chose what to cut and what to keep. I chose when the argument was wrong and needed revision. I chose to name the shadows alongside the light. Claude wrote prose I couldn't write as fast, searched research I couldn't search as broadly, and found contradictions I couldn't see from inside my own assumptions.

The relationship was exactly what Chapter 38 describes: secure attachment. I departed to explore — used the AI tutor, engaged the tool, faced what it surfaced — because the base was reliable. I knew what the book was about. The AI helped me find out what the book was *really* about.

What it was really about: identity with the whole is the only alternative to destruction. The part that offers itself back — the coral building the reef, the parent holding the child, the storyteller holding the darkness — is not sacrificing. It is participating in what life is already doing.

## The Grammar

This morning I cast a reading from my own grammar. The I Ching drew Holding Together, Line 5 — moving toward Keeping Still. The Tarot drew Death, Nine of Swords reversed, Seven of Wands. My question: *Should I finish my MSW?*

Death says: the old form is ending. Nine of Swords reversed says: the anxiety is releasing. Seven of Wands says: defend what you've already built.

The I Ching says: hold together — then be still.

I don't have an answer. I have a practice. Every month I will cast the same question from the same grammar and write what I find. The grammar contains 184 cards — every chapter of the book, every card from the decks, every story. Each one has a summary, a key insight, and a question. The randomness introduces what my conscious mind won't produce. The interpretation is mine. The community, if one forms, is ours.

Joseph Campbell said we need new mythologies. I don't have one. But I have a grammar — a finite set of symbols that generates infinite meaning when practiced in relationship. I have tools that deliver that grammar through the same device the attention economy uses to keep people reactive. I have a book that explains why any of this matters.

And I have a name for the process: **PlayfulProcess.**

Not playful because it's light. Playful because play is how children learn — the R&D division of the species, as Gopnik says. And process because nothing is finished. The grammar keeps changing. The interpretation keeps deepening. The practice keeps continuing.

This Substack is called *Life Is Process*. What follows will be:

**Weekly:** One chapter from the book, with my annotations and reflections. Starting next week with the Introduction: *"Thirteen thousand years ago, in what is now Southern California, something went wrong."*

**Monthly:** A cast from the grammar. The same question. What I found.

**Occasionally:** What I'm building, what I'm learning, what broke and what held.
