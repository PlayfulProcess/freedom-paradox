# Harm reduction at the root
*Substack draft — public-facing parallel essay. Links to "Why I've been quiet" as draft companion.*
*Drafted 2026-04-27. Author to review. Anonymous publication under PlayfulProcess.*

---

There is a question circulating right now about what the OpenAI Foundation should do with its $180 billion stake. The standard answers have the standard shape — fund alignment research, fund mental-health response infrastructure, fund AI-for-the-Global-South deployment, fund universal-basic-income pilots, fund adversarial evaluation labs. These are arguments for harm reduction *at the symptoms*, downstream of the products that produce the harm.

I want to argue something narrower and structurally sharper: **the safest thing the OpenAI Foundation can do is make OpenAI itself better.** Not in addition to. Instead of, when the trade-off is real.

The Foundation does not have $180 billion of cash. It has a 26 percent stake in OpenAI Group PBC, plus a Class N veto over mission changes, plus the right to nominate directors. Liquidating the stake depresses the valuation it depends on. Waiting for dividends produces nothing because the captive-research-and-development economics of frontier AI labs structurally do not generate dividends. The capital is paper. The lever is equity, not cash.

What does equity reach that cash does not? Equity reaches the inside of the company. Equity governs what the product itself does. Cash, deployed externally, addresses the consequences of what the product did. There is a public-health distinction here that I have come back to: harm reduction at the symptoms funds methadone clinics for opioid users; harm reduction at the root regulates the prescription pipeline that produced the dependency. Both are real work. The first matters. The second matters more, when you have access to the pipeline.

The Foundation has access to the pipeline. The pipeline is OpenAI's product. Five concrete moves the Foundation could make using the lever it already has, in rough order of operational urgency.

**Mandate iteration-speed metrics on identified harm patterns.** When OpenAI rolled back the GPT-4o sycophancy update in April 2025 within days of public outcry, that was harm reduction at the root, fast. The structural problem is that not all harms produce equivalent outcry. The 1.2 million weekly users expressing suicidal ideation through ChatGPT, per OpenAI's own October 2025 disclosure, did not produce a comparable rollback because the harm is distributed and chronic rather than concentrated and viral. The Foundation should require time-from-discovery-to-fix as a published metric, with governance review triggered when the metric exceeds defined thresholds.

**Mandate downstream API monitoring for emergent harm patterns.** Developers build agentic systems on top of OpenAI's API. OpenAI has visibility into what is built. Make the visibility operational. Public reporting on suspended API access. Enforcement of TOS prohibitions that currently exist on paper.

**Mandate product-level safety requirements in agentic deployments.** Crisis detection with human handoff. Session limits that resist extension. Restrictions on persistent memory that produces dependency. Refusal of agentic capabilities that have not passed defined evaluation thresholds. None are technically difficult; all cut into engagement metrics; none has been adopted at scale because commercial incentive runs the other way. The Foundation, using its governance lever, makes them mandatory.

**Operationalize the Class N veto.** Pentagon contracts above a defined threshold. Deployment expansions into surveillance domains. Acquisitions that subject OpenAI to a parent without equivalent governance. Each Foundation review produces a public report. The veto becomes a discipline rather than a paper right.

**Restructure the Foundation's own board.** No director on the Foundation should also sit on the OpenAI Group PBC board. The current 8-of-9 overlap means the same humans hold both sides. Re-seat the Foundation board within twelve months with directors whose only fiduciary duty is to the mission. The reform is what makes the other four operational.

These moves cost a small fraction of $180 billion. The bulk of the equity stays where it is — held, voted, watched, used. What changes is whether the lever the Foundation already has becomes a binding constraint on the company it owns.

This is the public-policy version of the argument. There is a parallel version at smaller scale that I have been working on, in practice rather than in theory, for the last several months. I would rather show it than describe it, so I am linking the [companion post draft] which lays out what I have been shipping on my own platform to apply the same principle at my own scale.

The short version: I run a small AI-using platform — recursive.eco, journaling and grammar-building, fewer than forty regular readers and fewer than that many active users. After spending some weeks examining harm patterns in adjacent tools, I recognized many of those patterns in my own platform's defaults, because most builders inherit the same defaults from the same foundation models. The work I have been doing since is not external philanthropy. It is changing what the product itself does. A universal seven-rule safety frame on every AI conversation. A default-model change from Sonnet to Opus, because Opus is more comfortable saying *I don't know* and less inclined to confidently fill ambiguity. A rewritten default personality that no longer borrows the "compassionate empathy buddy with thorough understanding" pattern that is the soft version of the harm pattern in companion AI. Removed CBT framing from tarot and I-Ching blocks because borrowed clinical structure without clinical accountability is exactly the architectural choice I want to refuse. A hard forty-message conversation cap that breaks engagement-optimization spirals at the cost of revenue-per-user. An onboarding tour with exit-any-time, because pushing users through fixed flows is itself part of the pattern.

The receipts are on the public commit log if you want to check. The principle is the same one I am asking the Foundation to apply at scale: **harm reduction at the root is superior to second-hand restitution at the symptoms, when the actor has access to the root.**

The Foundation has access to the root of the largest AI product on Earth. I have access to the root of one very small one. The distance between us is enormous. The principle is the same.

There is a moment where most builders would write a paragraph here about how we are all in this together. I am not going to write that paragraph. The Foundation is going to do whatever the Foundation is going to do; my work is what I can ship at my scale, and I will keep shipping it, and the public commit record will or will not corroborate the argument over time.

What I want to say instead, to the people reading: the question is not what the Foundation should fund. The question is what the Foundation could change about the product it owns, and whether the people who govern the Foundation will use the lever they already have. The answer to that question is the answer to the prize.
