## Source

Chapman & Withers note: 'We can just pause to consider what gets included or excluded in normative social work history' (60). Wells critiqued Booker T. Washington's assimilative measures alongside W.E.B. Du Bois (48).

## Context

'Black anti-lynching activist and social worker' (60). In 1892—the same year Pratt spoke at the National Conference on Charities and Corrections—Wells 'published Southern Horrors: Lynch Law in All Its Phases, which is a very different kind of social working than that of McCulloch or Pratt' (60).
